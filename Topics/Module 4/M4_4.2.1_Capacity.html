Capacity Concepts
Capacity: The maximum output rate of a facility, department, machine, or worker — expressed in units per period, labor hours, or machine hours.

Types of capacity:

  • Design capacity: The theoretical maximum output under ideal conditions (24/7, no downtime)
  • Rated capacity (effective capacity): Practical maximum considering scheduled maintenance, shifts, and normal downtime
  • Demonstrated capacity: Actual output achieved — based on historical performance
  • Available capacity: Capacity available after planned production is subtracted
Utilization: Actual output / Design capacity × 100%. How much of the theoretical maximum is being used.
Efficiency: Actual output / Standard output × 100%. How well the facility performs vs. the expected standard.
Rated Capacity = Design Capacity × Utilization × Efficiency
Capacity is a constraint in manufacturing planning. Exceeding capacity leads to backlogs and late deliveries. Under-utilizing capacity wastes fixed cost investment.
Capacity Planning Levels
Capacity planning occurs at each level of the manufacturing planning hierarchy:
  • Resource planning: Long-term (years) — checks S&OP production plan against capacity. Decisions: build/buy facilities, major equipment, workforce
  • Rough-Cut Capacity Planning (RCCP): Medium-term — validates MPS against key resource groups. Fast check before MRP run
  • Capacity Requirements Planning (CRP): Detailed — checks every work center against MRP-generated work orders. Most detailed, most accurate
  • Input/Output control: Short-term — monitors actual vs. planned input and output at each work center in real time
Bottleneck: A work center or resource where demand equals or exceeds available capacity — constraining the throughput of the entire system.
Theory of Constraints (TOC): Identify the bottleneck → exploit it (maximize its use) → subordinate everything else to the bottleneck → elevate the bottleneck (add capacity) → repeat.

Capacity options to resolve overloads: overtime, additional shifts, subcontracting, cross-training, equipment purchase, demand management (delay/redirect orders).

What is a bottleneck?
ANSWER
A work center or resource where demand equals or exceeds available capacity — constraining the throughput of the entire system. The system's output rate is limited by its slowest/most constrained resource.
TERM / CONCEPT
Theory of Constraints (TOC) — 5 focusing steps
ANSWER
1) Identify the bottleneck 2) Exploit it (maximize utilization of the constraint) 3) Subordinate everything else to the bottleneck's pace 4) Elevate the bottleneck (add capacity) 5) Repeat — find the next constraint

Options to resolve a capacity overload
ANSWER
Overtime, additional shifts, subcontracting non-core work, cross-training workers, purchasing additional equipment, or demand management (delay/redirect orders to less constrained periods).

Q1 of 5
A machine has design capacity of 1,000 units/day. It operates 8 hours out of a possible 10 (utilization = 80%) and produces at 90% of its standard rate (efficiency). Rated capacity is:
Rated Capacity = Design Capacity × Utilization × Efficiency = 1,000 × 0.80 × 0.90 = 720 units/day. This is the practical expected output under normal operating conditions.
Q2 of 5
Rough-Cut Capacity Planning (RCCP) is performed to:
RCCP is a fast, high-level check — does the MPS exceed capacity at key resource groups? It runs BEFORE MRP to avoid generating a detailed material plan that can't be executed. It's less detailed than CRP but much faster.
Q3 of 5
According to the Theory of Constraints, after identifying the bottleneck, the NEXT step is:
TOC step 2 = EXPLOIT: get maximum output from the existing bottleneck before investing in additional capacity. Don't add shifts or equipment until the current capacity is fully utilized — a bottleneck that is idle represents lost throughput.
Q4 of 5
A factory's output is 800 units/day but its slowest machine can only process 800 units/day. Adding more workers or machines elsewhere will:
The bottleneck sets the system's maximum output. Adding capacity anywhere except the bottleneck cannot increase total throughput — it just creates more work-in-process piling up in front of the bottleneck. Only relieving the constraint increases output.
Q5 of 5
Which capacity planning level provides the most detailed check — down to individual work centers and specific work orders?
CRP is the most detailed capacity check — it evaluates every work center against every MRP-generated work order, accounting for routing, setup times, and run times. It is the only capacity check that can identify detailed, work-center-specific overloads.
