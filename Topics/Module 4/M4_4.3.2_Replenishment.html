Reorder Point (ROP) System
Reorder point (ROP): The inventory level at which a new order should be placed so that new stock arrives before inventory runs out.
ROP = Average demand during lead time + Safety stock ROP = (d × LT) + SS d = average daily demand, LT = lead time in days, SS = safety stock
The ROP system is a CONTINUOUS REVIEW (Q) system — inventory is monitored continuously, and an order is placed whenever inventory drops to the ROP level. Order quantity is fixed (usually EOQ).
Periodic review (P) system: Inventory is checked at fixed intervals (e.g., every week). Order quantity varies to bring inventory to a target level. Simpler to administer — no continuous monitoring needed.
Q system (ROP) vs. P system: Q system orders a FIXED QUANTITY at VARIABLE times. P system orders VARIABLE QUANTITY at FIXED times.
Other Replenishment Approaches
Min-max system: When inventory drops to the minimum level (reorder point), order enough to bring inventory back to the maximum level. A variant of the P/Q hybrid.
Vendor-Managed Inventory (VMI): The supplier monitors the customer's inventory levels and takes responsibility for replenishing stock — the supplier decides when and how much to order. Customer shares sales/inventory data with supplier.
VMI benefits: reduces buyer's administrative burden, enables supplier to plan better (visibility into actual consumption), reduces the Bullwhip Effect (supplier sees real demand).
Kanban: A lean pull-based replenishment signal — a card or container that triggers replenishment when consumed. Production or procurement happens only when a kanban is released. 'Make one, move one, signal one.'
Two-bin system: A simple visual replenishment system — two bins of inventory. When the first bin is empty, pull from the second and trigger replenishment for the first. No complex tracking needed.
Just-in-Time (JIT) philosophy: receive materials just as they are needed in production — minimizing all forms of inventory waste. Requires reliable, frequent supplier deliveries and very stable demand.

TERM / CONCEPT
Reorder Point (ROP) formula
ANSWER
ROP = (Average Daily Demand × Lead Time) + Safety Stock When inventory drops to ROP, place a new order. The order arrives when inventory = safety stock (under normal conditions).
TERM / CONCEPT
Q system vs. P system replenishment
ANSWER
Q system (continuous review): fixed order QUANTITY, variable ORDER TIME — order when inventory hits ROP. P system (periodic review): variable order QUANTITY, fixed ORDER INTERVAL — order at set intervals to hit target level.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Vendor-Managed Inventory (VMI)?
ANSWER
The supplier monitors the customer's inventory and decides when/how much to replenish. Customer shares real-time sales/inventory data. Benefits: reduced buyer admin, better supplier planning, reduced Bullwhip Effect.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a Kanban?
ANSWER
A lean pull-based replenishment signal (card or container). Triggers production or procurement only when a kanban is released (when inventory is consumed). Prevents overproduction — the cornerstone of lean 'pull' systems.
TERM / CONCEPT
Min-max replenishment system
ANSWER
When inventory drops to the MINIMUM level (reorder point), order enough to bring it back to the MAXIMUM level. Max − current inventory = order quantity. Simple and widely used for MRO and low-value items.
TERM / CONCEPT
Just-in-Time (JIT) core principle
ANSWER
Receive materials EXACTLY when needed in production — minimizing all inventory waste. Requires: reliable, frequent supplier deliveries; stable demand; tight supplier partnerships; near-perfect quality (no buffer for rework).

Q1 of 5
A product has average daily demand of 50 units, lead time of 4 days, and safety stock of 100 units. What is the reorder point?
ROP = (d × LT) + SS = (50 × 4) + 100 = 200 + 100 = 300 units. When inventory drops to 300 units, place a new order. Under normal conditions, the order arrives exactly as safety stock begins to be used.
Q2 of 5
A company checks inventory every Monday and orders enough to bring stock to a target of 500 units. This is an example of:
Fixed ORDER INTERVAL (every Monday) + variable order QUANTITY (whatever is needed to reach the 500-unit target) = Periodic Review (P) system. The Q system has a fixed quantity and variable timing.
Q3 of 5
Under Vendor-Managed Inventory (VMI), who is responsible for deciding when and how much to replenish?
VMI shifts the replenishment decision to the SUPPLIER. The customer shares real-time inventory and sales data; the supplier uses this to proactively replenish before stockouts occur — without the customer needing to place orders.
Q4 of 5
Kanban is associated with which production philosophy?
Kanban is the operational mechanism of lean PULL systems. A kanban card/container signals that inventory has been consumed and replenishment is needed — production or procurement happens only in response to actual consumption, not forecast.
Q5 of 5
The primary advantage of a Just-in-Time (JIT) replenishment approach is:
JIT's goal is to minimize WASTE in all forms — including inventory. By receiving material exactly when needed, JIT eliminates all forms of non-value-adding inventory: buffer stock, anticipation stock, and cycle stock excess. It requires extremely reliable supply and stable demand.
