Linking Operations to Financial Performance
Inventory as a financial asset: Inventory appears on the balance sheet as a current asset — but it ties up working capital and incurs holding costs. The goal: carry enough inventory to service demand, but not so much that it erodes financial performance.
Three ways inventory affects financial performance:
  • Cost of Goods Sold (COGS): Inventory consumed in production or sold directly reduces gross profit
  • Holding costs: Capital, storage, insurance, obsolescence — typically 20–30% of inventory value per year
  • Cash-to-cash cycle time: Days of inventory outstanding (DIO) is one of three components — reducing it frees cash
Cash-to-cash (C2C) cycle time: DPO (Days Payable Outstanding) − DIO (Days Inventory Outstanding) − DSO (Days Sales Outstanding). A shorter C2C means the business collects cash faster than it pays suppliers — a sign of operational health.
C2C = DIO + DSO − DPO
Key Operational Performance Metrics
Inventory turnover: COGS ÷ Average Inventory. Measures how many times inventory is sold and replenished per year. Higher = more efficient use of inventory investment.
Inventory Turnover = COGS ÷ Average Inventory
Days of Supply (DOS): Average Inventory ÷ (COGS ÷ 365). The number of days current inventory can cover demand.
Days of Supply = Average Inventory ÷ (COGS ÷ 365)
Fill rate: The percentage of customer orders shipped on time and in full from available stock. A key customer service metric — directly linked to operational performance.
Return on Assets (ROA): Net Income ÷ Total Assets. Inventory is part of total assets — reducing excess inventory improves ROA.
Operations performance connects to finance: lower inventory → lower assets → higher ROA. Lower DIO → shorter C2C → better cash flow.
Manufacturing Planning Hierarchy and Financial Impact
The manufacturing planning hierarchy (Business Plan → S&OP → MPS → MRP → PAC) exists to align operations with financial targets.
Business plan alignment: The S&OP process translates the business plan's revenue and margin targets into production volumes. If production is misaligned, the result is excess inventory (eroding margins) or shortages (lost revenue).
Standard cost: A predetermined cost for manufacturing a unit — used for budgeting, variance analysis, and financial reporting. Variances between standard and actual cost signal operational inefficiency.
Capacity utilization affects unit cost: underutilized capacity spreads fixed costs over fewer units (higher cost per unit). Overutilized capacity causes overtime, quality issues, and service failures.

TERM / CONCEPT
Cash-to-cash cycle time formula
ANSWER
C2C = DIO + DSO − DPO DIO: Days Inventory Outstanding (time inventory sits before sold) DSO: Days Sales Outstanding (time to collect after sale) DPO: Days Payable Outstanding (time before paying suppliers) Lower C2C = better cash flow. Reducing inventory (DIO) directly shortens C2C.
TERM / CONCEPT
Inventory turnover formula and meaning
ANSWER
Inventory Turnover = COGS ÷ Average Inventory Higher = more efficient. Industry benchmarks vary: grocery (20–30×) vs. aerospace (1–2×). Low turnover = excess inventory tying up capital. High turnover = lean, but risk of stockouts if not managed.
TERM / CONCEPT
Days of Supply formula
ANSWER
Days of Supply = Average Inventory ÷ (COGS ÷ 365) Tells you: at current consumption rate, how many days can inventory last before stockout? Used to set reorder points and evaluate safety stock adequacy.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does excess inventory hurt financial performance?
ANSWER
1) Ties up working capital (cash not available for other uses) 2) Holding costs = 20–30% of inventory value/year (storage, insurance, obsolescence) 3) Increases Days Inventory Outstanding → lengthens cash-to-cash cycle 4) Increases total assets → reduces ROA 5) Risk of obsolescence = direct write-off (loss)
TERM / CONCEPT
What is fill rate?
ANSWER
Percentage of customer orders shipped on time and in full from available stock. Fill rate = (Orders shipped complete from stock) ÷ (Total orders) × 100 Key customer service metric. Low fill rate = lost sales and customer dissatisfaction. High fill rate requires adequate inventory — so there's a trade-off with inventory reduction goals.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does the manufacturing planning hierarchy link to financial performance?
ANSWER
Business Plan sets revenue/margin targets → S&OP translates into production volumes → MPS creates detailed schedule → MRP triggers material procurement → PAC executes. Misalignment at any level causes either: excess inventory (erodes margins, ties up cash) OR shortages (lost revenue, premium freight costs). Standard cost variances signal where the disconnect occurred.

Q1 of 5
A company has COGS of $10M and average inventory of $2M. Its inventory turnover is:
Inventory Turnover = COGS ÷ Average Inventory = $10M ÷ $2M = 5×. This means inventory is sold and replenished 5 times per year — or about every 73 days.
Q2 of 5
Reducing Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) improves the cash-to-cash cycle by:
C2C = DIO + DSO − DPO. Reducing DIO directly reduces C2C (assuming DSO and DPO unchanged), meaning the company converts its inventory investment back to cash more quickly. This is a key lever for working capital improvement.
Q3 of 5
A company reduces its excess inventory significantly. This would most likely:
Reducing excess inventory: (1) increases inventory turnover (COGS divided by lower inventory = higher ratio), (2) reduces total assets, improving ROA (Net Income / Total Assets), and (3) reduces DIO, improving cash-to-cash cycle and cash flow.
Q4 of 5
Standard cost variances in manufacturing signal:
Standard cost = predetermined cost per unit. Actual cost vs. standard cost = variance. Variances signal where operations diverge from plan: material price variance (supplier cost), material usage variance (waste), labor efficiency variance (productivity), overhead variance (capacity utilization). Used to identify and correct operational inefficiencies.
Q5 of 5
The primary reason inventory is considered a financial liability as well as an asset is:
Inventory IS an asset on the balance sheet — but it's a two-edged sword. Holding inventory costs 20–30% of its value per year (capital cost, storage, handling, insurance, obsolescence risk). Too much inventory erodes margins, reduces ROA, and lengthens cash-to-cash cycle. The goal is the optimal level — enough to service demand, not so much that it becomes a drag on financial performance.
