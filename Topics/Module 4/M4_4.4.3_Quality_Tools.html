The 7 Basic Quality Tools
The 7 basic quality tools (Ishikawa's toolkit) are fundamental problem-solving instruments used in quality management — simple enough for shop-floor workers, powerful enough to solve most quality problems.
1. Cause-and-Effect Diagram (Fishbone / Ishikawa): A diagram that organizes potential causes of a problem by category — typically the 6 M's: Man, Machine, Method, Material, Measurement, Mother Nature. Helps teams brainstorm and visualize root causes.
2. Check sheet: A simple tally form for collecting and organizing real-time data at the point of occurrence. Converts observations into countable data.
3. Control chart: A time-series graph with Upper Control Limit (UCL) and Lower Control Limit (LCL) — showing whether a process is in statistical control (variation is random and predictable) or out of control (a special cause is present).
4. Histogram: A bar chart showing the frequency distribution of data — revealing the shape (normal, skewed, bimodal), spread, and center of a process.
5. Pareto chart: A bar chart ranking causes from most to least frequent, with a cumulative line — visualizing the 80/20 rule (80% of problems from 20% of causes). Prioritizes where to focus improvement effort.
6. Scatter diagram: A plot of two variables to reveal correlation (positive, negative, or none). Used to test whether a suspected cause actually relates to the effect.
7. Stratification (or flow chart): Separating data into subgroups (by shift, machine, supplier) to identify patterns that are hidden in the aggregate data. Sometimes the 7th tool is listed as a process flow chart.
Statistical Process Control (SPC)
Statistical Process Control (SPC): Using statistical methods (primarily control charts) to monitor and control a process — ensuring it operates at its best capability and detecting when special causes introduce unacceptable variation.
Two types of process variation:
  • Common cause variation: Inherent, random variation from normal process inputs — the process is 'in control.' Can only be reduced by changing the system itself.
  • Special cause variation: Variation from an identifiable, assignable cause (broken tool, wrong material, new operator) — the process is 'out of control.' Must be found and eliminated.
Process capability (Cp, Cpk): A measure of whether a process can consistently produce output within specification limits. Cp > 1.33 is generally considered capable; Cpk accounts for process centering.
Control chart rule: a point outside UCL or LCL = out-of-control signal → investigate and remove the special cause. The goal is not to react to every data point — only to signals.
Quality Tools in Practice
Quality tools follow a problem-solving sequence: Define the problem (Pareto, check sheet) → Analyze causes (fishbone, scatter) → Monitor the process (control chart, histogram) → Sustain improvement (check sheets, SPC).
Root cause analysis (RCA): A structured method for finding the underlying cause of a problem rather than treating symptoms. Common RCA tools: fishbone diagram, 5 Whys (repeatedly asking 'why?' to drill down to root cause).
5 Whys: A simple but powerful RCA technique — ask 'why?' five times in succession to move from symptom to root cause. Example: Why did the machine stop? → Why was the fuse blown? → Why was it overloaded? → etc.

TERM / CONCEPT
Control chart: UCL, LCL, and what they mean
ANSWER
UCL = Upper Control Limit; LCL = Lower Control Limit Calculated from process data (typically ±3 standard deviations from mean). In control: points between UCL and LCL in random pattern → only common cause variation → no action needed. Out of control: point outside UCL/LCL OR non-random patterns → special cause present → investigate and fix.
TERM / CONCEPT
Common cause vs. special cause variation
ANSWER
Common cause: inherent, random variation — process is IN CONTROL. Can only be reduced by redesigning the system (management's job). Special cause: assignable, identifiable source — process is OUT OF CONTROL. Must be found and removed (operator's job). SPC's purpose: distinguish between the two so you don't overreact to normal variation or miss real problems.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a Pareto chart and why use it?
ANSWER
Bar chart ranking causes from most to least frequent + cumulative % line. Based on the 80/20 rule (Pareto principle): ~80% of problems come from ~20% of causes. Used to prioritize: instead of attacking all problems equally, focus CI effort on the vital few causes that account for most of the impact. Maximum improvement with minimum effort.
TERM / CONCEPT
Fishbone diagram — 6 M categories
ANSWER
Man (people), Machine (equipment), Method (process/procedure), Material (inputs/components), Measurement (gauges/data), Mother Nature (environment/climate) Used to: brainstorm all possible causes of a defect organized by category. Prevents teams from fixating on one cause while missing others. Starting point for root cause analysis.
TERM / CONCEPT
5 Whys technique
ANSWER
Ask 'Why?' five times in succession to move from symptom to root cause. Example: Why late delivery? → Order was delayed in picking Why? → Wrong location in WMS Why? → Item moved without system update Why? → No scan required for internal moves Why? → Procedure not enforced Root cause: No enforcement of scanning procedure for internal moves → fix: update policy + retrain

Q1 of 5
A Pareto chart is primarily used to:
Pareto chart = 80/20 tool. It ranks causes by frequency (most to least) with a cumulative % line. The key insight: 80% of problems come from 20% of causes. Rather than spreading improvement effort across all issues equally, the Pareto chart tells you where to focus for maximum impact.
Q2 of 5
A control chart shows a data point outside the Upper Control Limit (UCL). This means:
A point outside UCL or LCL signals an out-of-control condition — a special (assignable) cause has entered the process. The correct response: investigate to find and eliminate the root cause. Note: UCL/LCL are NOT the same as specification limits — they're statistical limits based on the process itself.
Q3 of 5
Which quality tool would best help a team visualize all possible root causes of a machine breakdown?
The fishbone/Ishikawa diagram is specifically designed for brainstorming and organizing potential causes of a problem. It structures causes by category (6 M's: Man, Machine, Method, Material, Measurement, Mother Nature), ensuring the team considers all possible cause categories rather than fixating on the most obvious one.
Q4 of 5
Common cause variation in a process can be reduced by:
Common cause variation is inherent to the current process design — it's the 'noise' of the system running normally. It can ONLY be reduced by changing the system itself (new equipment, process redesign, different materials). This is management's job, not the operator's. Operators can only respond to special causes (out-of-control signals).
Q5 of 5
A scatter diagram showing a strong positive correlation between temperature and defect rate means:
Scatter diagrams show correlation (association) — not causation. A strong positive correlation (as temperature rises, defect rate rises) means there IS a relationship worth investigating further. The next step is root cause analysis (perhaps DOE — Design of Experiments) to confirm whether temperature is actually causing the defects, or both are driven by a third variable.
