Risk Identification Methods
Risk identification: The process of systematically finding and documenting all potential supply chain risks — the foundation of risk management. You cannot manage a risk you haven't identified.
Risk identification methods:
  • Risk register: A living document cataloging all identified risks, their probability, impact, and response strategies
  • Supply chain mapping: Visually mapping the supply chain from raw material to customer — revealing single points of failure, concentration risks, and geographic exposures
  • Supplier audits: On-site visits to assess supplier operational, financial, and compliance health
  • Scenario planning: Structured 'what-if' exercises imagining specific disruption scenarios (e.g., 'what if our main port is closed for 30 days?')
  • FMEA (Failure Mode and Effects Analysis): Systematic analysis of how each supply chain component can fail and what the consequences are
Risk register: A centralized document (or database) listing all identified risks with: description, category, probability rating, impact rating, risk score (probability × impact), risk owner, current mitigation status, and response plan.
Supply Chain Mapping for Risk
Supply chain mapping: Creating a visual representation of the entire supply chain — all suppliers (Tier 1, Tier 2, Tier 3), manufacturing locations, distribution centers, and transportation routes — to identify vulnerabilities.
Tier 1 vs. Tier 2/3 suppliers: Tier 1 = direct suppliers. Tier 2 = suppliers to Tier 1. Tier 3 = suppliers to Tier 2. Many companies focus only on Tier 1 — but Tier 2/3 disruptions (e.g., a single raw material supplier) can cascade through the whole chain. COVID-19 semiconductor shortage was largely a Tier 3+ problem.
Geographic concentration risk: Over-reliance on suppliers or facilities in a single geographic area — vulnerable to regional disasters, geopolitical events, or regulatory changes affecting that location.
Supply chain mapping reveals: single points of failure (one supplier, one route, one port), geographic concentration (all suppliers in one region), and dependency chains (one Tier 2 supplier providing components to multiple Tier 1 suppliers — an invisible shared risk).
Risk Identification Tools
FMEA (Failure Mode and Effects Analysis): A structured risk identification tool that systematically asks: for each supply chain process or component, what can go wrong (failure mode)? What is the effect? What is the likelihood, severity, and detectability? FMEA generates a Risk Priority Number (RPN) = Severity × Occurrence × Detection.
RPN = Severity × Occurrence × Detection
PESTLE analysis: A macro-environment scanning tool identifying Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, and Environmental factors that could affect the supply chain. Used for strategic risk identification at the market and regulatory level.
Risk identification is never complete — supply chains, markets, and geopolitics continuously evolve. Risk registers must be regularly reviewed and updated to capture emerging risks (e.g., new suppliers added, new geographies, new regulations).

TERM / CONCEPT
Risk register — what it contains
ANSWER
A living document cataloging all identified risks: • Risk ID and description • Risk category (supply, demand, operational, environmental, etc.) • Probability rating (1–5 or low/medium/high) • Impact rating (1–5 or low/medium/high) • Risk score = Probability × Impact • Risk owner (accountable person) • Current mitigation measures • Response plan (if risk materializes) • Review date Priority: highest-scoring risks get the most mitigation investment.
TERM / CONCEPT
FMEA — Failure Mode and Effects Analysis
ANSWER
Systematic tool asking: What can go wrong? What is the effect? How likely/severe/detectable? RPN = Severity × Occurrence × Detection • Severity (1–10): how bad is the effect? • Occurrence (1–10): how often does this failure mode happen? • Detection (1–10): how likely is it to be caught before causing harm? (10 = impossible to detect) Highest RPN = highest priority for risk mitigation. Originally from automotive/aerospace — widely applied in supply chain risk.
TERM / CONCEPT
Supply chain mapping — why Tier 2/3 matter
ANSWER
Most companies know their Tier 1 suppliers (direct). Fewer map Tier 2 (suppliers of suppliers) or Tier 3. Problem: a single Tier 2 or 3 supplier may supply multiple Tier 1 suppliers in your chain → if THAT supplier fails, it simultaneously disrupts multiple Tier 1 nodes. Example: COVID-19 semiconductor shortage. Many auto, electronics, and appliance manufacturers shared common Tier 2/3 chip manufacturers — when those failed, the disruption cascaded everywhere simultaneously. Lesson: map beyond Tier 1 to reveal hidden concentration and dependency.
TERM / CONCEPT
Scenario planning in supply chain risk
ANSWER
Structured 'what-if' exercise: imagine a specific disruption scenario and work through the response. Steps: 1. Define scenario (e.g., 'Port of Shanghai closed for 45 days') 2. Identify affected supply flows 3. Assess inventory coverage (how long can we serve customers?) 4. Identify response options (alternate routing, substitute sourcing, demand rationing) 5. Estimate cost and timeline of each option 6. Develop response playbook Value: pre-thinking responses means faster, better decisions when the scenario actually occurs.
TERM / CONCEPT
Geographic concentration risk — definition and mitigation
ANSWER
Over-reliance on suppliers, manufacturing, or distribution in a single geographic region. Vulnerabilities: regional natural disasters (Japan earthquake 2011), geopolitical events (trade war with China), regulatory changes (export restrictions), labor unrest, currency crises. Mitigation: • Geographic diversification: add suppliers/facilities in different regions • Nearshoring: move some production closer to consumption market • Maintain backup sourcing options in different geographies (even at premium cost normally)
TERM / CONCEPT
PESTLE analysis for supply chain risk
ANSWER
Macro-environment scanning for strategic risks: Political: tariffs, trade policy changes, political instability, sanctions Economic: recession, currency volatility, commodity prices, inflation Social: labor market changes, consumer preference shifts, ESG demands Technological: automation risk, cyber threats, technology disruption Legal: regulation changes (GDPR, EPR, FSMA), compliance requirements Environmental: climate change, natural disaster frequency, carbon regulations PESTLE identifies external risks the supply chain cannot control but must monitor and plan for.

Q1 of 5
Supply chain mapping beyond Tier 1 is important for risk management because:
Tier 2/3 mapping reveals hidden concentration: a raw material supplier or component manufacturer serving multiple Tier 1 suppliers in your supply chain. If that shared Tier 2/3 supplier fails, multiple Tier 1 suppliers fail simultaneously — even if each Tier 1 supplier was individually dual-sourced. COVID-19 semiconductor shortage revealed this: auto, electronics, and appliance companies discovered they shared common wafer and chip manufacturers several tiers deep, creating simultaneous, widespread disruption.
Q2 of 5
A Risk Priority Number (RPN) in FMEA is calculated as:
FMEA RPN = Severity (1–10: how bad if failure occurs) × Occurrence (1–10: how often does this failure mode happen) × Detection (1–10: how likely is the failure to go undetected — 10 = impossible to detect before causing harm). Highest RPNs get priority mitigation investment. Note: a highly detectable risk (low Detection score) gets a lower RPN — because you'll catch it before it cascades. A hard-to-detect risk gets a higher RPN even at low probability/severity.
Q3 of 5
A risk register is most valuable when it is:
Risk register value = completeness + currency. A one-time annual document becomes stale within weeks as supply chains change (new suppliers added, geopolitical events, new product launches). A living document: updated when new risks emerge, reviewed regularly (quarterly minimum), owned by specific individuals (accountable for mitigation progress), and drives action (not just documentation). Without ownership and regular review, it becomes a filing exercise rather than a risk management tool.
Q4 of 5
Scenario planning in supply chain risk management helps organizations by:
Scenario planning: imagine specific disruption scenarios and work through responses BEFORE the disruption happens. Value: when a real disruption occurs, decision-makers aren't starting from zero — they've already identified options, estimated costs and timelines, and possibly pre-positioned resources. Response speed improves dramatically vs. companies that plan responses ad hoc during a crisis. Examples: 'what if our main Asia supplier fails?' → pre-identified backup supplier, pre-qualified, ready to activate.
Q5 of 5
PESTLE analysis is used in supply chain risk management to:
PESTLE = macro-environment scanning for strategic risks. Supply chains are embedded in a political, economic, and regulatory environment that is constantly changing. A tariff change (Political) can make an entire sourcing strategy uneconomical overnight. A new environmental regulation (Environmental/Legal) can require supply chain redesign. PESTLE ensures the supply chain strategy accounts for these external factors — not just operational/supplier risks. It feeds the risk identification step of the SCRM process.
