6.1.3 · Customer Service Metrics

Measuring Customer Service Performance
Customer service metrics: Quantitative measures that track how well the supply chain is meeting customer requirements — enabling performance management, benchmarking, and continuous improvement.
The SCOR model's customer-facing metrics: Reliability (Perfect Order %), Responsiveness (Order Fulfillment Cycle Time), and Agility (Upside/Downside Adaptability). These three capture the customer's experience of the supply chain.
Net Promoter Score (NPS): A customer loyalty metric asking: 'How likely are you to recommend us to a colleague or friend?' (0–10 scale). Promoters (9–10) minus Detractors (0–6) = NPS. Widely used as a proxy for customer satisfaction and relationship health.
NPS = % Promoters − % Detractors
Key Customer Service KPIs
On-Time In-Full (OTIF): A combined metric measuring whether orders are delivered on time AND in full. OTIF = (On-time %) × (In-full %). Major retailers (Walmart, Target) impose financial penalties for OTIF failures.
OTIF = % On-Time × % In-Full
Order fill rate: The percentage of customer orders (or order lines) shipped complete from available stock at time of order. Measures inventory availability from the customer's perspective.
Order cycle time (OCT): Elapsed time from customer order placement to product receipt. A key responsiveness metric — shorter OCT = competitive advantage in fast-moving markets.
Customer complaint rate: Number of complaints per 100 or 1,000 orders — measures overall service quality. High complaint rate = underlying supply chain or product quality problem requiring root cause analysis.
Metrics should be reported at multiple levels: overall average, by customer segment, by product category, by region — to identify where performance gaps actually exist.
Balancing Service and Cost
The service-cost trade-off: every percentage point of improvement in fill rate, on-time delivery, or order cycle time costs progressively more. The last 1–2% of perfection is exponentially more expensive than the first 95%.
Cost-to-serve: The total supply chain cost associated with serving a specific customer or customer segment — including order processing, warehousing, picking, transportation, and returns. Cost-to-serve analysis reveals which customers are profitable vs. which consume more cost than they generate.
Low-margin customers with high service requirements may actually be unprofitable when full cost-to-serve is calculated. This insight drives customer portfolio rationalization and differentiated pricing.
Activity-Based Costing (ABC costing): A costing method that assigns costs to activities and then to cost objects (customers, products) based on their actual consumption of those activities — enabling accurate cost-to-serve calculation.

TERM / CONCEPT
OTIF — formula and importance
ANSWER
OTIF = % On-Time × % In-Full Example: 95% on-time × 92% in-full = 87.4% OTIF Importance: many major retailers (Walmart, Target, Amazon) require high OTIF and impose financial penalties (chargebacks, fines) for failures. Why multiplicative: a shipment must be BOTH on-time AND complete to count — partial credit not given. Misses on either dimension = 0 for that order in OTIF.
TERM / CONCEPT
Net Promoter Score (NPS) — formula and meaning
ANSWER
Survey question: 'How likely to recommend us?' (0–10) Promoters: 9–10 → loyal enthusiasts Passives: 7–8 → satisfied but not loyal Detractors: 0–6 → unhappy, may spread negative word NPS = % Promoters − % Detractors Range: −100 to +100 Interpretation: >50 = excellent; 0–50 = good; <0 = needs improvement Value: simple, benchmarkable, correlates with growth and customer retention.
TERM / CONCEPT
Cost-to-serve analysis — what is it and why does it matter?
ANSWER
Cost-to-serve = total supply chain cost of serving a specific customer or segment. Includes: order processing + picking labor + packaging + transportation + returns handling + customer service calls. Why it matters: Revenue − COGS = gross margin; but gross margin doesn't reveal TRUE profitability. A customer with high gross margin but complex orders, frequent returns, and premium delivery requirements may have a negative NET margin when full cost-to-serve is deducted. Action: rationalize unprofitable customers (minimum order size, service fees) or exit the relationship.
TERM / CONCEPT
Order fill rate vs. OTIF vs. Perfect Order
ANSWER
Fill rate: % of orders shipped complete from stock (availability dimension only) OTIF: % of orders both on-time AND in-full (combines delivery timing + completeness) Perfect Order: % of orders on-time × in-full × damage-free × correctly invoiced (adds quality and accuracy dimensions) Fill rate ≥ OTIF ≥ Perfect Order → each adds requirements → harder to achieve 100%.
TERM / CONCEPT
Service-cost trade-off — the last 1-2% problem
ANSWER
Moving from 90% to 95% fill rate: moderate cost increase. Moving from 95% to 99%: significant cost increase (more safety stock, faster transport, more responsive operations). Moving from 99% to 99.9%: extremely expensive — exponentially diminishing returns. Why: the last few percentage points require preparing for rare, extreme events (tail-risk inventory, emergency freight, dedicated capacity). Decision: optimal service level = where marginal cost of improvement = marginal value to customer retention.
TERM / CONCEPT
Order Cycle Time (OCT) — components and improvement levers
ANSWER
OCT = time from customer order → product receipt Components: 1. Order transmission (customer to seller system) 2. Order processing (picking, credit check) 3. Order picking and packing 4. Carrier pickup to delivery 5. Customer receiving Levers to reduce OCT: • EDI/API order transmission (eliminates manual) • Same-day pick/ship cutoff extension • Closer DC to customer (last-mile) • Faster carrier mode • Real-time order visibility (tracks where delays occur)

Q1 of 5
A supplier with 94% on-time delivery and 96% order fill rate has an OTIF of:
OTIF = % On-Time × % In-Full = 0.94 × 0.96 = 0.9024 = 90.24%. Both dimensions must be met for an order to count as OTIF compliant. Even if each individual metric is above 90%, the combined OTIF is lower because orders failing either dimension are counted as failures — regardless of which dimension failed.
Q2 of 5
Cost-to-serve analysis reveals that a high-revenue customer is unprofitable. The most appropriate response is:
Cost-to-serve reveals the TRUE profitability of serving a customer after all supply chain costs. Options when a customer is unprofitable: (1) diagnose which costs are high (many small orders? returns? special handling?); (2) address root causes (minimum order sizes, SKU rationalization, delivery consolidation); (3) add surcharges for costly services; (4) renegotiate pricing; (5) as a last resort, exit the relationship. 'Absorb the cost' is not a strategy — it funds losses indefinitely.
Q3 of 5
Net Promoter Score (NPS) is calculated as:
NPS: survey asks likelihood to recommend (0–10). Promoters (9–10) = loyal advocates. Passives (7–8) = satisfied but neutral. Detractors (0–6) = unhappy, risk negative word-of-mouth. NPS = % Promoters − % Detractors. Range: −100 to +100. NPS is widely used because it's simple, correlates with growth, and enables benchmarking across industries. Passives are excluded from the calculation.
Q4 of 5
Tracking customer service metrics at the overall average level only is problematic because:
An overall 95% on-time average might look fine — but if it hides 80% on-time for one region and 99% for others, the regional problem is invisible until a major customer complains. Metrics should be segmented by: customer tier, product category, origin, carrier, and DC — to expose where performance actually breaks down and focus improvement effort where it matters most.
Q5 of 5
Activity-Based Costing (ABC costing) is used in supply chain to:
ABC costing (not to be confused with ABC inventory classification) traces overhead costs to activities, then to the products or customers that consume those activities. Traditional costing spreads overhead evenly — hiding the true cost of complex, demanding customers or products. ABC reveals that some customers consume far more order processing, special handling, returns processing, and customer service than others — critical for accurate cost-to-serve and profitability analysis.
