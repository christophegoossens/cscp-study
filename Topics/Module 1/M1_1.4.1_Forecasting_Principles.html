What is Forecasting?
Forecast (ASCM): An estimate of future demand. A forecast can be constructed using quantitative methods, qualitative methods, or a combination of methods, and it can be based on extrinsic (external) or intrinsic (internal) factors. Various forecasting techniques attempt to predict one or more of the four types of demand: trend, seasonal, cyclical, and random.
All forecasts are wrong — the goal is to make them as accurate as possible while quantifying the expected error so the supply chain can plan buffers accordingly.

Forecasting is a core input to Sales and Operations Planning (S&OP), master production scheduling, procurement, and capacity planning.

Forecasting Principles
Six fundamental principles of forecasting:
  • Forecasts are almost always wrong — but are still necessary for planning. Quantify the expected error.
  • Forecasts should include a measure of error — safety stock and capacity buffers are sized to the error range, not just the point estimate.
  • Aggregate forecasts are more accurate — a forecast for a product family is more accurate than individual SKU forecasts (errors cancel out).
  • Forecasts are more accurate for shorter time horizons — the further out you forecast, the less accurate.
  • Every forecast should include a planning assumption — what market conditions, promotions, or events are assumed.
  • The supply chain drives the need for forecasts — forecast horizon should match the longest cumulative lead time in the supply chain.
Forecast horizon: How far into the future the forecast extends. Must be at least as long as the longest cumulative supply chain lead time.
Forecast bucket: The time period for each forecast data point — daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly. Longer buckets are more accurate than shorter ones.
Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Forecasting
Intrinsic forecasting: Based on internal data — primarily historical sales or shipment data. Assumes the future will look similar to the past. Uses statistical methods (moving average, exponential smoothing, regression).
Extrinsic forecasting: Based on external indicators correlated with demand — GDP growth, housing starts, consumer confidence index, competitor actions, industry trends. Also called 'causal forecasting.'
Extrinsic forecasting is more powerful when your demand is strongly correlated with external factors (e.g., housing materials = housing starts). Intrinsic is simpler but ignores market-level signals.

In practice, best forecasts combine BOTH — statistical (intrinsic) baseline + market intelligence adjustments (extrinsic).

Forecasting Process

A structured forecasting process typically follows these steps:

  • 1. Gather and clean data — remove outliers, adjust for promotions, returns, lost sales.
  • 2. Select forecasting method — based on data availability, demand pattern type, and required accuracy.
  • 3. Generate statistical baseline forecast — apply the chosen model to history.
  • 4. Apply market intelligence adjustments — add planned promotions, new product launches, competitive events.
  • 5. Review and consensus — S&OP process aligns sales, marketing, and supply chain on one agreed forecast.
  • 6. Measure accuracy — track forecast error and feed back into model improvement.
The output of this process is the DEMAND PLAN — a forward-looking view used by supply planners to make inventory, production, and procurement decisions.

ANSWER

TERM / CONCEPT
Why are aggregate forecasts more accurate?
ANSWER
Errors at the individual SKU level tend to cancel out when combined. A product family forecast aggregates many SKUs — some will be over, some under, making the total more stable and accurate than any single-item forecast.

ANSWER
1) Gather/clean data 2) Select method 3) Generate statistical baseline 4) Apply market intelligence adjustments 5) Review and consensus (S&OP) 6) Measure accuracy and feed back
TERM / CONCEPT
Why must the forecast horizon match supply chain lead time?
ANSWER
If the forecast horizon is shorter than the lead time, there is no time to act on the forecast — the material will not arrive in time. The supply chain needs advance notice equal to at least its cumulative lead time.

Q1 of 5
A company forecasts at the individual SKU level and finds high error rates. Switching to forecasting at the product family level would most likely:
Aggregate forecasts are more accurate — a core forecasting principle. When multiple SKUs are combined, the over-forecasts and under-forecasts of individual items partially cancel each other out, producing a more accurate total.
Q2 of 5
The forecast horizon should be at least as long as:
The forecast must extend far enough into the future for the supply chain to actually act on it. That means it must cover the longest cumulative lead time — from raw material procurement through production to finished goods delivery.
Q3 of 5
Which of the following is an example of EXTRINSIC (causal) forecasting?
Extrinsic (causal) forecasting uses EXTERNAL indicators correlated with demand. Housing starts is an external economic indicator that drives demand for building materials — a classic causal relationship. The other options all use internal historical data = intrinsic forecasting.
Q4 of 5
Which statement about forecast error is most consistent with ASCM forecasting principles?
A core principle: all forecasts are wrong, but they are still necessary. The right response is to MEASURE the expected error and USE it to size safety stock and capacity buffers — not to eliminate forecasting or pretend the error doesn't exist.
Q5 of 5
A company is launching a major promotion next quarter. Where in the forecasting process should this be accounted for?
Statistical baseline forecasts extrapolate from history. Planned promotions (which are FUTURE events not in history) should be added as market intelligence adjustments AFTER the baseline is generated. This is the standard 'override' step in structured demand planning.
