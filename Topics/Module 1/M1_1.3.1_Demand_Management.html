What is Demand Management?
Demand (ASCM): A need for a particular product or component. The demand could come from any number of sources — a customer order or forecast, an interplant requirement, a branch warehouse request for a service part, or the manufacturing of another product.
Demand management (ASCM): (1) The function of recognizing all demands for goods and services to support the marketplace. It involves prioritizing demand when supply is lacking. Proper demand management facilitates the planning and use of resources for profitable business results. (2) In marketing, the process of planning, executing, controlling, and monitoring the design, pricing, promotion, and distribution of products and services to bring about transactions that meet organizational and individual needs.
Demand management is the art of synchronizing supply and demand plans.
4 Components of Demand Management
The 4 components of demand management:
  • Planning demand: Forecasting future demand, combining statistical forecasts with market intelligence
  • Communicating demand: Sharing demand information with supply-side planners (S&OP)
  • Influencing demand: Using marketing/pricing tools (4Ps) to shift demand to match supply capabilities
  • Managing and prioritizing demand: When supply is constrained, allocating it to the highest-value demand

Demand management operates at all planning levels:

  • Strategic: Long-term forecasting, product development, capacity development
  • Tactical: Aggregate demand forecasting, S&OP
  • Operational: Item-level forecasting, master scheduling, order management

The unit of demand becomes more specific from highest to lowest level: total revenue → product families → individual SKUs.

Demand Communication
Demand plan: The output of the demand planning process — a forward-looking view of expected demand at various levels (strategic, aggregate, item) that is communicated to supply-side planners.
Effective demand communication requires: accurate data, regular cadence (S&OP cycle), collaboration between demand-side and supply-side teams, and clear escalation when demand exceeds supply.
S&OP (Sales and Operations Planning): The process that links demand plans with supply capabilities to create an agreed, executable plan.
Managing and Prioritizing Demand

When supply cannot meet all demand, organizations must PRIORITIZE which demand to fulfill.

Common demand prioritization criteria: strategic customer importance, profitability, contract commitments, delivery date, order size.
Available-to-Promise (ATP): The uncommitted portion of a company's current inventory and planned production. Used to promise delivery dates to customers without over-committing.
Capable-to-Promise (CTP): Goes beyond ATP — checks whether new capacity can be added to meet demand if current ATP is insufficient.

What is a demand plan?
ANSWER
The output of demand planning — a forward-looking view of expected demand at strategic, aggregate, and item levels — communicated to supply-side planners to enable production and procurement planning.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Available-to-Promise (ATP)?
ANSWER
The uncommitted portion of current inventory and planned production. Used by sales to promise delivery dates to customers without over-committing supply capacity.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Capable-to-Promise (CTP)?
ANSWER
Goes beyond ATP — assesses whether ADDITIONAL capacity can be added (e.g., overtime, extra shifts) to fulfill demand that exceeds current planned production. A more responsive commitment method.
TERM / CONCEPT
At what planning levels does demand management operate?
ANSWER
1) Strategic: long-term forecasting, product development, capacity 2) Tactical: aggregate S&OP, product family planning 3) Operational: item-level scheduling, order management Unit granularity increases from revenue → families → individual SKUs.
TERM / CONCEPT
How should demand be prioritized when supply is constrained?
ANSWER
Common criteria: strategic customer importance, contractual commitments, profitability, delivery date urgency, order size. The goal is to allocate limited supply to highest-value demand.

Q1 of 5
Which of the following is NOT one of the 4 components of demand management?
The 4 components are: Planning, Communicating, Influencing, and Managing/Prioritizing demand. 'Optimizing demand' is not a named component — though optimization is implied in all four activities.
Q2 of 5
Available-to-Promise (ATP) is used by sales teams to:
ATP = the uncommitted portion of current inventory and planned production. Sales uses ATP to make delivery date promises that the supply chain can actually keep — without double-committing the same inventory or capacity.
Q3 of 5
A supply chain manager decides to offer a price discount to move excess inventory from a slow-moving product. This is an example of:
Using the price lever (one of the 4Ps) to stimulate demand for excess inventory is INFLUENCING demand — actively shaping demand to better align with supply reality.
Q4 of 5
At which planning level does demand management work with individual SKU-level forecasts?
At the OPERATIONAL level, demand management works with individual item/SKU forecasts that drive master production scheduling. At strategic level = total revenue. At tactical = product families.
Q5 of 5
When supply cannot meet all customer demand, demand management's prioritization role involves:
When supply is constrained, demand management must PRIORITIZE — deciding which customer orders to fulfill first based on criteria: strategic importance, profitability, contractual obligations, delivery urgency.
