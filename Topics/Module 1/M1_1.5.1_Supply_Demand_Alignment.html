The Alignment Challenge
The central challenge of supply chain management is aligning supply with demand — having the right product, in the right quantity, in the right place, at the right time.

Supply and demand mismatches create two types of cost:

  • Demand > Supply: Stockouts → lost sales, lost customers, emergency production/procurement costs, expediting fees
  • Supply > Demand: Excess inventory → holding costs (capital tied up), obsolescence risk, storage costs, markdowns
Perfect order fulfillment: Delivering the complete order, on time, undamaged, correctly invoiced — the ideal supply-demand alignment outcome.
Push vs. Pull Systems
Push system: Production or procurement is driven by FORECASTS. Items are made or purchased in advance of demand and pushed through the supply chain. Risk: build-up of inventory if demand doesn't materialize as forecast.
Pull system: Production or procurement is triggered by ACTUAL DEMAND signals (customer orders). No production until a demand signal is received. Risk: lead time may be too long to satisfy customer service expectations.
Push-pull boundary: The point in the supply chain where the strategy switches from push (forecast-driven) to pull (demand-driven). Also called the 'decoupling point.'
The push-pull boundary is typically located at the point of inventory stocking. Items with stable, high-volume demand can be pushed further forward (close to the customer). Highly customized or variable items benefit from a pull closer to the raw material level.

Examples: Build-to-stock (BTS) = fully push. Assemble-to-order (ATO) = push components, pull assembly. Make-to-order (MTO) = mostly pull. Engineer-to-order (ETO) = fully pull.

Decoupling Inventory and Postponement
Decoupling inventory (buffer stock): Inventory held at the push-pull boundary to absorb the mismatch between the push supply chain and the pull demand signal. Acts as a buffer between forecast-driven supply and actual demand.
Postponement: Delaying final product differentiation (customization) until an actual customer order is received. Keeps inventory in a generic form as long as possible.
Postponement reduces forecast risk by allowing one undifferentiated SKU to serve multiple final product variants. Example: a printer sold in different languages — final language manual and power adapter added only when the order is placed.

Two types of postponement:

  • Manufacturing postponement: Delay final assembly/configuration until order receipt
  • Logistics postponement: Hold centralized inventory and delay shipment to specific locations until demand is confirmed
Demand Sensing
Demand sensing: Using real-time demand signals (point-of-sale data, customer orders, web analytics) to update the near-term forecast and enable faster supply response.
Demand-driven supply chain: A supply chain architecture where real demand signals (not forecasts) pull material through the supply chain. Enabled by technologies like IoT, POS integration, and advanced analytics.
The more a supply chain can sense and respond to actual demand (vs. relying purely on forecasts), the lower the safety stock requirements and the better the service levels.

TERM / CONCEPT
What are the two types of supply-demand mismatches and their costs?
ANSWER
Demand > Supply: stockouts → lost sales, emergency costs, expediting fees. Supply > Demand: excess inventory → holding costs, capital tied up, obsolescence, storage, markdowns.
TERM / CONCEPT
Push vs. pull supply chain system
ANSWER
Push: production driven by FORECASTS — make in advance. Risk: inventory buildup if demand misses. Pull: production triggered by ACTUAL DEMAND — make after order. Risk: lead time may be too long for customers.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the push-pull boundary (decoupling point)?
ANSWER
The point in the supply chain where strategy shifts from forecast-driven (push) to demand-driven (pull). Location determines the manufacturing strategy: BTS (fully push) → ATO → MTO → ETO (fully pull).
TERM / CONCEPT
Postponement definition and benefit
ANSWER
Delaying final product differentiation until an actual customer order is received. Benefit: reduces forecast risk by pooling demand across variants — one generic SKU serves many final configurations, reducing safety stock.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is decoupling inventory?
ANSWER
Buffer inventory held at the push-pull boundary to absorb the mismatch between forecast-driven supply and actual customer demand. Acts as a shock absorber between push and pull portions of the supply chain.
TERM / CONCEPT
Manufacturing vs. logistics postponement
ANSWER
Manufacturing postponement: delay final assembly/configuration until order. Logistics postponement: centralize inventory and delay shipment to specific locations until demand is confirmed (e.g., central DC ships to regional DCs after orders are placed).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is demand sensing?
ANSWER
Using real-time demand signals (POS data, customer orders, web analytics) to update the near-term forecast and enable faster supply response. Reduces reliance on statistical forecasting for short-term decisions.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is perfect order fulfillment?
ANSWER
Delivering the complete order, on time, undamaged, and correctly invoiced. The ideal alignment outcome. Measured as a % of orders meeting ALL four criteria simultaneously.

Q1 of 5
A company makes a decision to not start production until it receives a confirmed customer order. This is an example of:
Waiting for an actual customer order before starting production = PULL system. The demand signal (order) pulls production. No speculative inventory is built ahead of demand.
Q2 of 5
A cosmetics company produces a standardized base product globally and adds country-specific packaging (language, regulatory labels) only after receiving a customer order. This is an example of:
Postponement = delay final customization (country-specific packaging) until actual demand is known. This reduces forecast risk by pooling demand across markets in the generic base product, then differentiating at the last possible moment.
Q3 of 5
Where is the push-pull boundary most likely to be located for a build-to-stock (BTS) product?
Build-to-stock = the most push-forward strategy. Production happens ahead of demand based on forecasts, with finished goods inventory as the decoupling point. The boundary is at finished goods — downstream (to customer) is pull/order management.
Q4 of 5
A retailer installs point-of-sale scanners that send real-time sales data to its suppliers, enabling suppliers to adjust production within 24 hours of demand shifts. This is an example of:
Real-time POS data flowing to suppliers = demand sensing. This enables a demand-driven supply chain where actual demand signals (not forecasts alone) trigger supply responses, reducing inventory needs and improving responsiveness.
Q5 of 5
Excess inventory held at the push-pull boundary to absorb mismatches between forecast-driven supply and actual demand is called:
Decoupling inventory (also called buffer stock) specifically refers to inventory at the push-pull boundary that absorbs the uncertainty between the forecast-driven supply plan and the actual customer demand signal. It 'decouples' the upstream supply from downstream demand.
