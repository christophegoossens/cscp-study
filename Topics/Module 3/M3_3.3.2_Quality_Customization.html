Quality in Product Design
Quality (ASCM): Conformance to requirements or fitness for use. Has five approaches: (1) Transcendent — ideal/excellence, (2) Product-based — product attribute, (3) User-based — fitness for use, (4) Manufacturing-based — conformance to requirements, (5) Value-based — excellence at acceptable price.

Quality has two major components:

  • Quality of conformance: Absence of defects — the product consistently meets its specification
  • Quality of design: How well the design specification captures customer needs
Total Quality Management (TQM): A management philosophy that involves all employees in continuous quality improvement across all processes — from design through delivery.
Defects found early are cheap to fix. Defects found after production, or by the customer, are exponentially more expensive. The '1-10-100 rule': $1 to prevent, $10 to fix in production, $100 to fix after delivery.
Customization Strategies
Mass customization: Delivering customized products and services at mass-production cost and speed — through modular design, postponement, and flexible manufacturing.

Four levels of product customization (from most to least standard):

  • Standard product: No customization — one product for all customers
  • Customized standard: Standard product with options (color, size, features) selected from a menu
  • Tailored: Standard product modified to customer specification
  • Pure custom: Designed from scratch for each customer
Postponement: Delaying final product differentiation until as late as possible in the supply chain — keeping inventory generic and customizing only when an order is placed.
The further downstream customization happens, the lower the forecast risk and inventory investment. Push generic products forward; pull customized variants on demand.
Sustainability in Product Design
Sustainability: Meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs (Brundtland definition). Applied to supply chain: environmental, social, and governance (ESG) considerations.
Design for Environment (DfE): Designing products to minimize environmental impact throughout the product lifecycle — material selection, energy use, recyclability, and end-of-life disposal.
Sustainability design considerations:
  • Material selection: Recyclable, renewable, non-toxic materials
  • Energy efficiency: Reduced energy in production and use
  • Product longevity: Designed to last longer, reducing replacement frequency
  • End-of-life: Designed for disassembly, reuse, or recycling
Triple Bottom Line (TBL): Measuring business performance across three dimensions: People (social), Planet (environmental), Profit (economic). Sustainability means optimizing all three, not just profit.

Q1 of 5
According to the ASCM definition, 'user-based quality' means:
User-based quality = fitness for use. It's defined by the customer's actual use experience — not by the design specification or defect rate. A product can be defect-free (manufacturing quality) but still fail user-based quality if it doesn't satisfy the intended use.
Q2 of 5
A printer manufacturer produces a standard printer body globally, then adds the appropriate power cable, manual language, and regulatory labels only when a customer order is placed. This is an example of:
Postponement = delaying final differentiation (language, power specs, regulatory labels) until a customer order triggers the specific configuration. The generic printer body is pushed forward; customization is pulled on demand. This enables mass customization at low inventory cost.
Q3 of 5
The '1-10-100 rule' in quality management means:
The 1-10-100 rule quantifies the cost escalation of late defect detection. Prevention (design/process controls) is cheapest. In-process detection costs 10× more. Post-delivery defects (warranty, recalls, customer damage) cost 100× more — making upstream quality investment highly justified.
Q4 of 5
The Triple Bottom Line framework measures business sustainability across:
TBL = People, Planet, Profit — the three pillars of sustainability. Businesses are evaluated not just on financial performance (Profit) but also on social impact (People — labor, community) and environmental impact (Planet — emissions, resource use, waste).
Q5 of 5
A company designs a new product using recycled materials, reduces energy consumption in production by 30%, and enables easy disassembly for recycling at end of life. This approach is called:
Design for Environment (DfE) = designing products to minimize environmental impact across the full lifecycle — material selection (recycled/recyclable), energy efficiency, and end-of-life design (disassembly for recycling). All three elements in the question are DfE practices.
