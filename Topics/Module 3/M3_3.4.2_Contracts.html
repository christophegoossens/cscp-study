What is a Contract?
Contract (ASCM): An agreement between two or more competent persons or companies to perform or not to perform specific acts or services, or to deliver merchandise. A contract is enforceable by law.

Key contract-related terms:

  • Annualized contract: A negotiated agreement for one year — sets pricing, ensures supply continuity, allows for periodic adjustments
  • Bilateral contract: Each party makes a promise to the other (most commercial contracts)
  • Trading partner agreement: Describes all facets of the business relationship between partners — pricing, service levels, data sharing, IP, liability
  • CISG (Contracts for the International Sale of Goods): Governs international sales contracts — an international legal framework
The level of the buyer-supplier relationship determines the type of contract needed. Spot market buy → simple PO. Strategic partnership → comprehensive trading partner agreement.
Types of Contracts by Risk Allocation
The key distinction between contract types is WHO BEARS THE COST RISK if actual costs differ from estimates:
  • Fixed-price contract: The buyer pays a set price regardless of the supplier's actual cost. Supplier bears cost risk. Best when: scope is well-defined and stable.
  • Cost-plus contract: Buyer pays the supplier's actual costs plus an agreed profit margin. Buyer bears cost risk. Best when: scope is uncertain or evolving (R&D, large projects).
  • Time and materials (T&M): Buyer pays for actual time spent and materials used at agreed rates. Shared risk. Common for services and projects with uncertain scope.
  • Performance-based contract: Pricing linked to supplier achieving defined performance outcomes (e.g., uptime, delivery rate). Aligns supplier incentives with buyer outcomes.
Fixed-price = best for the buyer when scope is clear (supplier bears risk). Cost-plus = best for the supplier when scope is uncertain (buyer bears risk). Understanding who bears risk is critical for CSCP exam questions on contract types.
Key Contract Terms

Common terms that appear in supply contracts:

  • Incoterms: International commercial terms defining who is responsible for freight, insurance, and risk of loss at each point in transit (e.g., FOB, CIF, DAP, EXW)
  • Payment terms: When payment is due — Net 30, Net 60, 2/10 Net 30 (2% discount if paid within 10 days)
  • Force majeure: Exempts parties from performance obligations during unforeseeable events (natural disasters, pandemics)
  • Liquidated damages: Pre-agreed financial penalties for specific contract breaches (e.g., late delivery)
  • Intellectual property (IP) clause: Defines ownership of designs, tooling, and innovations developed during the relationship
Incoterms (ASCM): Standard terms of sale internationally recognized, defining the transfer of risk and responsibility for freight and insurance between buyer and seller.

TERM / CONCEPT
Fixed-price vs. cost-plus contract — who bears cost risk?
ANSWER
Fixed-price: supplier bears cost risk (price is set regardless of actual cost). Best when scope is clear. Cost-plus: buyer bears cost risk (pays actual cost + profit). Best when scope is uncertain (R&D, complex projects).
TERM / CONCEPT
What are Incoterms?
ANSWER
International commercial terms defining who (buyer or seller) is responsible for freight, insurance, and risk of loss at each point in transit. Examples: EXW (buyer takes risk at factory door), FOB (seller's risk until goods are on board ship), CIF (seller covers cost, insurance, freight to destination port).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a trading partner agreement?
ANSWER
A comprehensive contract between supply chain partners describing all aspects of their business: pricing, service levels, data sharing protocols, IP ownership, liability, and dispute resolution. Used for strategic, long-term relationships.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a performance-based contract?
ANSWER
A contract where pricing is linked to the supplier achieving defined performance outcomes (e.g., uptime %, on-time delivery rate, defect rate). Aligns supplier incentives with buyer outcomes — paid more for better performance.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are liquidated damages in a supply contract?
ANSWER
Pre-agreed financial penalties for specific contract breaches — typically late delivery or missed performance targets. Agreed upfront so both parties know the consequences of non-performance without needing to prove actual damages in court.

Q1 of 5
A buyer contracts with an engineering firm to develop a new product. The design requirements will evolve as development progresses. Which contract type is most appropriate?
Uncertain, evolving scope → cost-plus contract. Fixed-price forces the supplier to price in all uncertainty (high price) or take losses on scope creep. Cost-plus allows scope to evolve while paying actual costs + agreed profit — appropriate for R&D and complex projects.
Q2 of 5
In a fixed-price contract, who bears the risk if material costs rise unexpectedly after the contract is signed?
Fixed-price = the supplier commits to a set price. If their actual costs (materials, labor) rise after signing, the supplier absorbs the difference. This is why suppliers price in a risk premium on fixed-price contracts for uncertain scope.
Q3 of 5
Incoterms are primarily used in contracts to define:
Incoterms = internationally recognized terms that define the division of responsibilities (freight, insurance, risk of loss) between buyer and seller at specific transit points. Critical for international contracts to avoid ambiguity about who owns the goods when they are damaged or lost.
Q4 of 5
A supplier contract includes a clause stating the supplier will pay €500 per day for every day a shipment is delivered late. This is an example of:
Liquidated damages = pre-agreed financial penalties for specific breaches (late delivery, missed targets). Both parties agree upfront — the buyer doesn't need to prove actual damages in court; the penalty is automatic per the contract terms.
Q5 of 5
CISG (Contracts for the International Sale of Goods) is relevant when:
CISG is an international legal framework governing cross-border sale of goods contracts between companies in different countries (that have ratified the convention). It provides a standard legal basis, avoiding the need to choose one country's domestic law.
