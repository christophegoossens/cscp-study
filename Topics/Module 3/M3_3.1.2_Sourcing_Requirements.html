Sourcing Requirements

Sourcing requirements define what the organization needs from a supplier — beyond just the product price. Requirements differ widely across product types (commodities vs. engineered parts vs. services).

Key sourcing requirements to evaluate for any supplier:
  • Cost: Total cost to the organization (not just price) and target consumer price
  • Quality: Quality culture, product/service quality standards, defect rates
  • Delivery performance: On-time delivery rate, delivery window adherence, scheduling flexibility
  • Lead time: From order to delivery — longer lead time = more inventory needed
  • Capacity: Current capacity and ability to scale (up or down) with demand
  • Design expertise: Can the supplier collaborate on product development?
  • Time to market: How fast can the supplier support new product launches?
Request for Information (RFI): A document sent to potential suppliers to gather general capability and qualification information. Used early in the sourcing process to build a qualified supplier list.
Request for Proposal (RFP): A more detailed document sent to qualified suppliers soliciting specific proposals — price, technical approach, delivery terms, quality certifications.
Request for Quotation (RFQ): A document requesting a specific price for a defined specification. Used when the requirement is well-defined and price is the primary differentiating factor.
Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)
Total Cost of Ownership (TCO): A calculation methodology that assigns values to all costs associated with a product, service, or capital equipment purchase. Goes beyond purchase price to include all lifecycle costs.
TCO components include:
  • Acquisition costs: Purchase price, tooling, setup, samples, qualification testing
  • Possession costs: Inventory carrying cost (holding, insurance, obsolescence), receiving and inspection
  • Usage costs: Quality failure (scrap, rework, warranty), lead time buffer inventory
  • End-of-life costs: Disposal, decommissioning, switching to a new supplier

TCO example: A supplier in Brazil offers copper tubing at $5/unit vs. a US supplier at $7/unit. After adding freight ($1.50), duty (10%), inventory carrying cost (longer lead time), and quality inspection costs — the US supplier's TCO may be lower.

TCO is the correct framework for make-vs-buy, supplier selection, and insource-vs-outsource decisions. It reveals hidden costs that purchase price comparisons miss.
Cost of goods sold (COGS): An accounting classification for direct material, direct labor, and manufacturing overhead in the cost of products sold. A key input to TCO analysis.

TERM / CONCEPT
RFI vs. RFP vs. RFQ — what is each used for?
ANSWER
RFI (Request for Information): gather supplier capability info early — build qualified list. RFP (Request for Proposal): solicits full proposal (technical + price) from qualified suppliers. RFQ (Request for Quotation): requests specific price for a defined spec — price is the key differentiator.
TERM / CONCEPT
ASCM definition: Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)
ANSWER
A calculation methodology that assigns values to ALL costs associated with a product, service, or capital equipment — not just purchase price. Includes acquisition, possession, usage, and end-of-life costs.
TERM / CONCEPT
4 TCO cost categories
ANSWER
1) Acquisition: price, tooling, qualification 2) Possession: holding cost, inspection, receiving 3) Usage: quality failures, scrap, rework, warranty, buffer inventory 4) End-of-life: disposal, switching costs, decommissioning
TERM / CONCEPT
7 key sourcing requirements to evaluate in a supplier
ANSWER
1) Cost (TCO) 2) Quality (culture + defect rates) 3) Delivery performance (OTD, scheduling) 4) Lead time 5) Capacity (current + scalability) 6) Design expertise / collaboration 7) Time to market
TERM / CONCEPT
Why does TCO often favor closer suppliers over cheaper distant ones?
ANSWER
Distant suppliers have lower unit prices but add: freight, duties/tariffs, longer lead time (more safety stock), harder quality management, currency risk, and higher switching costs. TCO captures all these — often making the apparent price advantage disappear.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between an RFP and an RFQ?
ANSWER
RFP: open-ended — asks supplier to propose their technical approach, methodology, AND price. Used when the solution is not fully defined. RFQ: closed-ended — you define the exact spec and just ask for a price. Used when the requirement is fully specified and you're comparing on price.

Q1 of 5
A company receives quotes from four suppliers. Supplier A has the lowest unit price. Using TCO, which additional factors should be evaluated before selecting Supplier A?
TCO = unit price + ALL associated costs. Long lead time means more safety stock (inventory carrying cost). Distant supplier = freight + duties. Unknown quality = potential scrap/rework costs. All must be included for a valid TCO comparison.
Q2 of 5
A purchasing team sends a document to 10 potential suppliers asking about their manufacturing capacity, quality certifications, and financial stability — with no pricing requested yet. This is:
RFI = gathering capability and qualification information from potential suppliers — no pricing yet. It narrows the field to qualified suppliers before issuing an RFP or RFQ.
Q3 of 5
Which of the following is a POSSESSION cost in a Total Cost of Ownership analysis?
Possession costs = costs incurred while the company HOLDS the purchased item: inventory carrying cost (capital cost, storage, insurance, obsolescence risk). Rework is a usage cost. Purchase price and tooling are acquisition costs.
Q4 of 5
A commodity product has well-defined specifications and the company simply wants the lowest price from qualified suppliers. The most appropriate sourcing document is:
When specifications are fully defined and price is the key decision variable, an RFQ is most efficient — it asks qualified suppliers for a specific price for a specific specification. RFP is used when the solution or approach is not fully defined.
Q5 of 5
A clothing company sources a basic t-shirt from a Chinese supplier. Requirements include low cost, adequate durability, and standard sizing. Which sourcing requirement is LEAST critical for this product?
For a commodity/staple product (basic t-shirt), design expertise and rapid time-to-market are not differentiating factors — the design is standardized. Cost, adequate quality, and reliable delivery are the key requirements for a commodity sourcing decision.
