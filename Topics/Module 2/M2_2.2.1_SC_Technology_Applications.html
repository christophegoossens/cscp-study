Overview of SC Technology Systems

Supply chain technology applications range from broad enterprise-wide systems to specialized point solutions. Key categories:

  • ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning): Broad, organization-wide system integrating finance, HR, procurement, manufacturing, and logistics in a single database. The backbone of most large supply chains.
  • WMS (Warehouse Management System): Manages warehouse operations — receiving, putaway, picking, packing, shipping, inventory tracking.
  • TMS (Transportation Management System): Manages transportation planning, carrier selection, load optimization, freight audit, and tracking.
  • APS (Advanced Planning System): Constraint-based planning — demand planning, supply planning, production scheduling across the supply chain.
  • CRM (Customer Relationship Management): Manages customer data, sales pipeline, service history.
  • SRM (Supplier Relationship Management): Manages supplier performance, contracts, and collaboration.
No single system does everything. Best-in-class supply chains use an integrated architecture of complementary systems — not just one monolithic platform.
Three Supply Chain Perspectives

A comprehensive supply chain system can be viewed from three perspectives:

  • Process value supply chain: Translates demand channel information into products or services — the operational supply chain (make, source, deliver).
  • Value delivery supply chain: Configured to deliver the service component of a product-service offering — how value is delivered to the customer.
  • Demand channel: Collects, analyzes, and disseminates market intelligence — the information flow from market to supply chain planners.
Modular architecture: A system design where related tasks are grouped in self-contained packages (modules) that communicate with each other. Allows best-of-breed selection — choosing the best WMS, TMS, APS individually and integrating them.
Technology has allowed supply chains to continually reinvent themselves — cloud platforms, APIs, and modular architecture enable faster adoption of new capabilities without replacing the entire system.
EDI and System Integration
EDI (Electronic Data Interchange): The structured electronic exchange of business documents (purchase orders, invoices, advance ship notices) between trading partners in a standardized format (e.g., ANSI X12, EDIFACT).
EDI was the original B2B integration technology. Modern alternatives include APIs and cloud-based integration platforms — faster and more flexible than traditional EDI but EDI remains dominant in many industries (retail, automotive, healthcare).
API (Application Programming Interface): A software interface that enables two systems to exchange data in real time. Increasingly used for supply chain integration alongside or instead of EDI.

TERM / CONCEPT
ERP vs. WMS vs. TMS — what does each manage?
ANSWER
ERP: enterprise-wide (finance, HR, procurement, manufacturing, logistics) — the backbone. WMS: warehouse operations (receiving, putaway, pick/pack/ship, inventory). TMS: transportation (carrier selection, load optimization, freight audit, tracking).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is an APS (Advanced Planning System)?
ANSWER
Constraint-based planning system that handles demand planning, supply planning, and production scheduling simultaneously — accounting for real capacity constraints. More sophisticated than basic ERP planning modules.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is EDI and why is it important in supply chain?
ANSWER
Electronic Data Interchange: structured electronic exchange of business documents (POs, invoices, ASNs) between trading partners in a standardized format. Eliminates paper/manual processes and enables automated B2B transactions.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is modular (best-of-breed) system architecture?
ANSWER
A design where independent software modules handle specific functions and communicate via APIs. Allows selecting the best WMS, TMS, APS individually rather than being constrained to one vendor's full suite.
TERM / CONCEPT
The 3 supply chain technology perspectives
ANSWER
1) Process value SC: operational chain — make/source/deliver 2) Value delivery SC: how value/service is delivered to customer 3) Demand channel: captures and disseminates market intelligence to supply chain planners
TERM / CONCEPT
CRM vs. SRM — what does each manage?
ANSWER
CRM (Customer Relationship Management): manages customer data, sales pipelines, and service history — the customer-facing side. SRM (Supplier Relationship Management): manages supplier performance, contracts, and collaboration — the supplier-facing side.

Q1 of 5
A company wants to optimize truck loads, select the best carrier for each shipment, and track all outbound freight in real time. Which system is designed for this?
TMS (Transportation Management System) handles transportation planning — carrier selection, load optimization, route planning, freight audit, and shipment tracking. This is its core purpose.
Q2 of 5
A retailer sends its purchase orders electronically to 200 suppliers using a standardized business document format, which suppliers' systems process automatically. This is an example of:
EDI = structured electronic exchange of standard business documents (POs, invoices, ASNs) between trading partners. Standardized formats (ANSI X12, EDIFACT) allow automated processing without manual re-entry.
Q3 of 5
A supply chain manager selects the best-in-class WMS from one vendor, the best TMS from another, and the best APS from a third — integrating all three. This approach is called:
Best-of-breed (modular) architecture = selecting the best system for each function and integrating them. The advantage: best functionality. The challenge: integration complexity and data consistency across systems.
Q4 of 5
The 'demand channel' component of a comprehensive supply chain technology system is responsible for:
The demand channel is the information-gathering function — collecting demand signals (POS data, orders, forecasts) from the market and feeding them to supply chain planners to drive production and procurement decisions.
Q5 of 5
Why do best-in-class supply chains typically use multiple integrated systems rather than a single ERP?
ERP systems are broad but not always deep. Specialized systems (WMS, TMS, APS) offer superior functionality for their specific domain. Best-in-class supply chains integrate the best tools for each function via modular/API architecture.
