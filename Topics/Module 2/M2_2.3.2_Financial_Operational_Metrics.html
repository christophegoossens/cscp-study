Cost Accounting for Supply Chain Managers
Cost accounting (ASCM): The branch of accounting concerned with recording and reporting business operating costs. Includes reporting of manufacturing, selling, and administrative costs.
Standard costs (ASCM): The target costs of an operation, process, or product — including direct material, direct labor, and overhead charges. What the product SHOULD cost under expected conditions.
Standard costing system (ASCM): A cost accounting system that uses predetermined cost units to estimate the cost of an order or product before production. Variances between standard and actual costs are analyzed.
Supply chain managers must understand cost accounting to evaluate sourcing decisions, make-vs-buy decisions, and measure the financial impact of supply chain improvements.
COGS (Cost of Goods Sold): The direct costs of producing goods sold — direct materials + direct labor + manufacturing overhead. Used in profit margin calculations.
Key Financial Metrics for Supply Chain
Inventory turnover (turns): COGS / Average inventory value. How many times inventory is sold and replaced in a period. Higher = more efficient use of inventory investment.
Inventory Turns = COGS / Average Inventory Value Higher turns = less capital tied up in inventory, lower holding costs
Days of Supply (DOS): Average inventory on hand / Average daily demand. How many days of demand current inventory can cover.
Days of Supply = Average Inventory / Average Daily Demand Or: 365 / Inventory Turns
Gross margin: (Revenue − COGS) / Revenue × 100%. Measures profitability of the product after direct production costs.
Return on Assets (ROA): Net income / Total assets. Supply chain improvements that reduce inventory (an asset) improve ROA.
Supply chain improvements have direct P&L and balance sheet impact: lower inventory → lower assets → better ROA; faster cycle times → less WIP → lower COGS; fewer errors → lower returns → higher margin.
Operational Reports
Key operational reports supply chain managers use:
  • Inventory report: Current stock levels by location, item, and age (days on hand)
  • On-time delivery report: % of orders/shipments delivered on or before requested date
  • Fill rate report: % of demand fulfilled from available stock (without backorder or stockout)
  • Supplier scorecard: Supplier performance on quality, delivery, lead time, and cost
  • Aging report: Inventory past its expiry date or slow-moving inventory at risk of obsolescence
Fill rate (ASCM): A measurement of customer service. The percentage of orders filled on the first shipment — without back orders or lost sales.

Reports should enable exception management — flagging items/suppliers/orders that need attention rather than requiring managers to review everything manually.

Q1 of 5
A company has COGS of €10M and average inventory of €2M. What is the inventory turn rate?
Inventory Turns = COGS / Average Inventory = 10M / 2M = 5 turns. This means the company sells and replaces its inventory 5 times per year — or holds about 73 days of inventory (365/5).
Q2 of 5
Standard costs are used to:
Standard costing sets predetermined cost targets. After production, actual costs are compared to standard — variances are analyzed to identify inefficiency, material waste, or labor overruns. This drives continuous cost improvement.
Q3 of 5
A retailer achieves a 98% fill rate. This means:
Fill rate = % of orders filled completely on first shipment without backorders. A 98% fill rate means 2% of orders have at least one item backordered or unfilled at first shipment.
Q4 of 5
A supply chain improvement reduces average inventory from €5M to €3M with no change in sales or net income. The financial impact is:
Lower inventory = lower total assets → higher ROA (same net income, smaller asset base). Also improves C2C cycle time (less cash tied up in DIO). This is why supply chain efficiency has direct balance sheet impact.
Q5 of 5
An aging inventory report showing 30% of stock is over 180 days old is most useful for identifying:
An aging report highlights inventory that has been sitting for a long time — a sign of slow-moving or obsolete stock that may need to be discounted, written down, or disposed. Proactive action reduces the financial hit of inventory write-offs.
