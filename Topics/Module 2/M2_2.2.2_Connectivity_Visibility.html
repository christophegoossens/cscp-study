Information System Architecture
Information system architecture (ASCM): A model of how the organization operates regarding information. Considers four factors: (1) organizational functions, (2) information requirements, (3) data attributes, and (4) information technologies.

The core technologies of any information system architecture:

  • Database: Structured repository of data (e.g., SQL relational database)
  • DBMS (Database Management System): Software for organizing, storing, maintaining, and retrieving data
  • Network: Interconnection of computers and systems — LAN (local area network) for internal; WAN/internet for external
  • Middleware/Integration layer: Software that connects disparate systems so they can share data
LAN (Local Area Network) (ASCM): A high-speed data communication system linking computers and terminals in a limited geographic area (e.g., a building or campus).
Network (ASCM): The interconnection of computers, terminals, and communications channels to facilitate file and peripheral device sharing, as well as electronic data interchange.
Supply Chain Connectivity
Connectivity: Getting the organization's information systems to communicate with the information systems of other supply chain partners — suppliers, customers, logistics providers.
Connectivity is the foundation of supply chain visibility. Without system-to-system connectivity, data must be re-entered manually — creating delay, errors, and cost.

Connectivity methods:

  • EDI: Structured B2B document exchange (traditional, batch)
  • API: Real-time data exchange between systems
  • Web portals: Browser-based access for partners without system integration
  • Cloud platforms: SaaS-based networks where multiple partners share data on a common platform (e.g., supply chain control towers)
Data interchange standards: Common formats (ANSI X12, GS1, EDIFACT) that enable different systems to exchange data in a mutually understood structure.
Visibility, Data Sharing, and Legal
Supply chain visibility: The ability to track products, orders, and inventory status across the supply chain in real time — from supplier through to customer.
Visibility enables: proactive disruption management, better customer service (order status), reduced safety stock (less uncertainty), and faster exception resolution.

Data sharing raises important legal considerations:

  • Data privacy: GDPR (EU), CCPA (US) — regulations governing collection, use, and storage of personal data
  • Intellectual property: Sharing product specifications with suppliers creates IP exposure
  • Antitrust: Sharing pricing or competitive data across industry competitors raises antitrust risk
  • Data security: Protecting supply chain data from cyberattacks — especially critical as more systems are connected
Supply chain connectivity must be governed by data sharing agreements, security protocols, and compliance with privacy regulations — not just technical integration.

Q1 of 5
A company wants to see the real-time inventory levels of its key suppliers and customers on a single dashboard. This requires:
Real-time multi-party visibility requires: (1) technical connectivity between systems (APIs, EDI, cloud platform) AND (2) agreed data sharing protocols and legal agreements. Technology alone is not sufficient without the governance framework.
Q2 of 5
According to the ASCM definition, information system architecture considers which four factors?
The ASCM definition of IS architecture covers: (1) organizational functions, (2) information requirements, (3) data attributes, and (4) information technologies. This is a cross-functional, business-driven view — not just a technology inventory.
Q3 of 5
A European manufacturer shares customer order data with a US-based logistics partner. Which legal requirement is most likely to apply?
GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) applies to the transfer of EU personal data to third parties — including cross-border transfers to the US. Supply chain data sharing that includes personal data (customer names, addresses, order history) requires GDPR compliance.
Q4 of 5
A data interchange standard like ANSI X12 or EDIFACT is used to:
Data interchange standards define common document formats — so that a retailer's ERP and a supplier's ERP can exchange purchase orders, invoices, and ASNs automatically without custom programming for each trading partner relationship.
Q5 of 5
Why does connecting more supply chain partners increase both the value AND the risk of an IT system?
Connectivity paradox: more connections → more visibility → more value. But also more entry points for cyberattacks, more data privacy obligations, and more IP exposure. Each new connection must be governed by security protocols and legal agreements.
