What is Logistics?
Logistics: The planning, implementation, and control of the efficient, effective forward and reverse flow and storage of goods, services, and related information between the point of origin and the point of consumption in order to meet customer requirements (CSCMP definition).
Logistics is the 'place and time' utility creator in the supply chain — getting the right product to the right place at the right time in the right condition at the right cost.
Integrated logistics management: Treating all logistics activities (transportation, warehousing, inventory, order processing, packaging) as a unified system rather than separate functions — optimizing total logistics cost rather than minimizing each function independently.
Total logistics cost (TLC): The sum of all costs across logistics functions: transportation + warehousing + inventory carrying + order processing + packaging + administration. Trade-offs exist between functions — e.g., faster transport (higher cost) can reduce inventory carrying costs.
Logistics Functions
Primary logistics activities (directly add time and place value):
  • Transportation: Moving goods from origin to destination
  • Inventory management: Maintaining the right stock at the right location
  • Order processing: Managing customer orders from receipt to fulfillment
Secondary logistics activities (support primary activities):
  • Warehousing: Storage, handling, and distribution of goods
  • Materials handling: Physical movement within a facility
  • Packaging: Protecting goods and enabling efficient handling
  • Procurement: Sourcing materials and services
  • Logistics information systems: WMS, TMS, order management systems
Customer service: In logistics, customer service means meeting the order cycle requirements (availability, order fill rate, delivery speed, consistency) that customers need to plan their own operations.
Logistics Network Design
Logistics network design: Determining the optimal number, location, and size of warehouses and distribution centers to serve customers at target service levels while minimizing total logistics cost.
Trade-off in network design: Fewer DCs → lower facility costs but higher transportation costs and longer delivery times. More DCs → higher facility costs but lower transport costs and faster delivery. Optimal design balances both.
Cross-docking: A logistics strategy where incoming goods are transferred directly from receiving to outbound shipping — with minimal or no storage. Reduces inventory and handling while speeding product flow. Requires tight coordination with suppliers and customers.
Third-party logistics (3PL): Outsourcing logistics functions to a specialized provider — transportation, warehousing, fulfillment, customs brokerage. 3PLs provide expertise, scale, and flexibility without capital investment in assets.

TERM / CONCEPT
CSCMP definition of logistics
ANSWER
The planning, implementation, and control of the efficient, effective forward and REVERSE flow and storage of goods, services, and related information between the POINT OF ORIGIN and POINT OF CONSUMPTION to meet customer requirements. Key words: forward AND reverse (includes returns); point of origin to consumption (end-to-end); meet customer requirements (customer-driven).
TERM / CONCEPT
Total logistics cost — what's included?
ANSWER
Transportation + Warehousing + Inventory carrying costs + Order processing + Packaging + Administration Key principle: optimize the TOTAL, not each part individually. Trade-off example: faster (expensive) transport → lower inventory holding costs → may lower TOTAL cost even though transport cost is higher.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is cross-docking?
ANSWER
Transferring incoming goods directly from receiving to outbound shipping — little or no storage. Benefits: eliminates storage cost, reduces handling, speeds product to customer. Requirements: synchronized supplier deliveries, high volumes of the same product, close coordination between supply and demand. Example: Walmart's distribution centers use cross-docking to rapidly flow goods from multiple suppliers to stores without warehousing them.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a 3PL?
ANSWER
Third-party logistics provider — a company that manages logistics functions on behalf of another company. Services: transportation, warehousing, fulfillment, freight brokerage, customs clearance, reverse logistics. Why use a 3PL: access expertise and scale, avoid asset investment, flexible capacity, focus on core business. Example: UPS Supply Chain Solutions, DHL Supply Chain, XPO Logistics.
TERM / CONCEPT
Primary vs. secondary logistics activities
ANSWER
PRIMARY (directly create time/place value): • Transportation • Inventory management • Order processing SECONDARY (support primary): • Warehousing • Materials handling • Packaging • Procurement • Logistics information systems (WMS, TMS)
TERM / CONCEPT
Logistics network design trade-off
ANSWER
Fewer DCs: ✓ Lower facility/fixed costs ✗ Higher transportation costs (longer distances) ✗ Slower delivery to customers More DCs: ✓ Lower transport costs (closer to customers) ✓ Faster delivery ✗ Higher facility costs, inventory duplication Optimal: balance total cost (facility + transport + inventory) at target service level.

Q1 of 5
The concept of 'total logistics cost' is important because:
Total logistics cost includes: transportation + warehousing + inventory carrying + order processing + packaging. These functions have trade-offs: using faster (more expensive) transport can reduce inventory carrying costs by enabling leaner safety stocks. Minimizing only transportation or only warehousing in isolation will sub-optimize the total system. TLC thinking evaluates the combined impact of decisions across all logistics functions.
Q2 of 5
Cross-docking primarily benefits logistics operations by:
Cross-docking = direct transfer from inbound to outbound with minimal or no storage. Benefits: eliminates warehousing cost, reduces handling touches, speeds product to market. It's ideal for high-volume, fast-moving, time-sensitive goods (perishables, fashion) where speed matters more than storage. Requires tight supply-demand synchronization.
Q3 of 5
A company with warehouses near every major customer region would have:
More distribution centers: higher facility costs (more buildings, leases, staff) and potentially higher inventory costs (safety stock duplicated at each location). BUT: lower transport costs (shorter routes) and faster delivery. The trade-off depends on customer service requirements and the balance of transportation vs. facility costs. No single right answer — depends on the company's specific cost structure and service requirements.
Q4 of 5
What distinguishes a 3PL from a company's in-house logistics operation?
3PL = third-party logistics provider. The company outsources logistics functions (transport, warehousing, fulfillment) to a specialist. Key advantages: 3PLs have expertise, technology, and network scale that most companies can't match individually; avoid capital investment in trucks/warehouses; scale capacity up/down with demand; company can focus on its core competency.
Q5 of 5
Logistics is defined as including 'reverse flow' because:
The CSCMP definition explicitly includes REVERSE flow — goods don't only move forward. Reverse logistics covers: customer returns, product recalls, warranty repair, end-of-life recycling, remanufacturing, and packaging recovery. Reverse logistics has grown significantly with e-commerce (high return rates) and sustainability requirements (recycling, refurbishment).
