Waste in the Supply Chain
Supply chain waste: Any material, resource, or activity in the supply chain that does not add value to the customer — including product waste (scrap, defects), packaging waste, energy waste, and process waste (non-value-added activities).
Waste reduction is both a cost driver (less waste = lower cost) and a sustainability imperative (less waste = lower environmental impact). The two goals are aligned — efficient supply chains are typically more sustainable.
Industrial ecology: A systems approach where the waste of one company becomes the input of another — mimicking natural ecosystems where nothing is 'wasted.' Also called 'circular economy' thinking at the industrial level.
Sustainability and the Circular Economy
Circular economy: An economic model that eliminates waste by keeping materials in use as long as possible — through design for longevity, repair, reuse, remanufacturing, and recycling. Contrasts with the linear 'take-make-dispose' model.
Circular economy strategies (in order of value preservation):
  • Refuse/Reduce: Eliminate unnecessary materials/use
  • Reuse: Use again as-is
  • Repair: Fix and continue using
  • Remanufacture: Rebuild to original spec
  • Recycle: Break down and use materials again
  • Recover energy: Extract energy from waste (last resort before disposal)
Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR): Regulations requiring manufacturers to take back and manage their products at end of life — incentivizing design for recyclability and creating a reverse logistics obligation. Common in EU for electronics (WEEE directive), batteries, and packaging.
Waste Reduction in Logistics
Packaging waste: Excess or non-recyclable packaging is a major source of supply chain waste. Sustainable packaging goals: right-size (eliminate excess space/material), use recycled/recyclable materials, and minimize weight (reducing transport emissions).
Carbon footprint of logistics: Transportation is a major source of greenhouse gas emissions — freight accounts for ~8% of global CO2 emissions. Strategies to reduce: consolidation (fuller loads), mode shift (rail/ocean vs. truck/air), route optimization, and alternative fuels (electric, hydrogen, biodiesel).
Scope 3 emissions: indirect emissions in a company's supply chain — including purchased goods, transportation, business travel, and use of sold products. Increasingly reported by companies under ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) frameworks. Supply chain typically represents 70–90% of a company's total carbon footprint.
Green logistics: Integrating environmental considerations into logistics decision-making — mode selection, load optimization, packaging design, and reverse logistics planning to minimize environmental impact.

TERM / CONCEPT
Circular economy vs. linear economy
ANSWER
Linear economy: Take (raw materials) → Make (product) → Dispose (landfill). One-directional, creates waste. Circular economy: Design products for longevity, repair, reuse, remanufacturing, recycling. Materials stay in use as long as possible. Strategies (highest to lowest value preservation): Refuse → Reduce → Reuse → Repair → Remanufacture → Recycle → Recover energy → Dispose
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)?
ANSWER
EPR = regulations requiring manufacturers to take responsibility for their products at end-of-life. Examples: EU WEEE Directive (electronics take-back), battery regulations, packaging EPR. Effect: manufacturers must design for recyclability (or pay for disposal), fund take-back programs, or pay into recycling schemes. Supply chain impact: creates reverse logistics obligation; incentivizes eco-design; shifts end-of-life cost from government/consumer to producer.
TERM / CONCEPT
Scope 1, 2, 3 emissions — what's the difference?
ANSWER
Scope 1: Direct emissions from company-owned operations (company fleet, factory boilers) Scope 2: Indirect emissions from purchased energy (electricity, steam) Scope 3: All OTHER indirect emissions in the value chain: - Upstream: suppliers, transportation of purchased goods - Downstream: distribution, customer use, end-of-life disposal Scope 3 = 70–90% of most companies' total carbon footprint. Supply chain is the biggest lever for reducing corporate emissions.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does freight consolidation reduce environmental impact?
ANSWER
More goods per vehicle = fewer vehicle trips to move the same total volume. Full trucks produce lower emissions per unit shipped than half-empty trucks. Consolidation methods: FTL vs. LTL optimization, cross-docking, milk runs (one truck picks up from multiple suppliers), intermodal (shift long haul from truck to rail/ocean — lower emissions per ton-mile). Bonus: also reduces cost (fewer trips = lower freight spend).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is industrial ecology?
ANSWER
A systems approach where waste outputs from one company become inputs for another — mimicking natural ecosystems (nothing is 'wasted'; everything cycles). Example: a steel mill's slag becomes cement manufacturer's raw material; a food processor's organic waste becomes biogas for energy. Contrasts with traditional 'end-of-pipe' approach (manage waste after it's created) by designing systems where waste = resource from the start. Larger concept: industrial symbiosis (companies in geographic proximity exchange waste streams).
TERM / CONCEPT
Green logistics strategies
ANSWER
Mode shift: rail/ocean instead of truck/air → lower emissions per ton-mile Load optimization: full loads, reduced empty miles Route optimization: TMS finds shortest/most efficient routes Packaging right-sizing: less material, lighter weight = less fuel to transport Alternative fuels: electric, hydrogen, biodiesel vehicles Consolidation: more goods per trip Local sourcing: shorter transportation distances Return logistics design: reduce emissions of reverse supply chain

Q1 of 5
In the circular economy hierarchy, 'remanufacturing' is preferred over 'recycling' because:
Circular economy hierarchy: highest value preservation → lowest. Remanufacturing keeps the product structure intact — rebuilds using existing components, preserving the manufacturing energy and material value embedded in those parts. Recycling breaks everything down to raw materials — losing the value of previous manufacturing steps and requiring significant energy to process. Rule: keep materials at highest level of use possible for as long as possible.
Q2 of 5
Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) regulations change supply chain design by:
EPR = 'you made it, you're responsible for it.' Manufacturers must plan and fund product recovery at end-of-life (take-back programs, contributions to recycling schemes). This incentivizes design changes: modular design (easier disassembly), recyclable materials (lower disposal cost), fewer hazardous substances (lower compliance cost). It also creates a mandatory reverse logistics program — companies must build or outsource the reverse supply chain.
Q3 of 5
Supply chain transportation typically represents what percentage of a company's total carbon footprint (Scope 3)?
Scope 3 emissions (all indirect supply chain emissions) typically represent 70–90% of a company's total carbon footprint. Transportation (freight) is a large component of Scope 3 — along with purchased goods/services (the emissions from suppliers' production). This is why supply chain decarbonization has become a major focus: a company can't meaningfully reduce its total carbon impact without addressing its supply chain, which dominates total emissions.
Q4 of 5
A company that designs its packaging to be smaller, lighter, and made from recycled materials is pursuing:
Green logistics = integrating environmental considerations into logistics decisions. Packaging example: smaller (fewer packages per shipment? wrong — same volume. Actually: less space wasted → more product per truck → fewer trips), lighter (reduces vehicle weight → less fuel per trip), recycled materials (lower carbon intensity of materials). This is a case where environmental and economic goals align: less packaging material = lower material cost + lower shipping cost + lower environmental impact.
Q5 of 5
Industrial ecology differs from traditional waste management in that:
Traditional waste management: treat waste after it's created (end-of-pipe). Industrial ecology: design systems at the outset so waste doesn't exist — outputs from one process/company are inputs for another. Example: industrial symbiosis parks where companies cluster geographically to exchange waste streams (steam, heat, by-products). The goal is a closed loop where no material goes to landfill. This systems-level design is more effective and economical than treating waste as an afterthought.
