5.2.2 · Transport Mode & Carrier Selection

Carrier Types
Carriers are classified by ownership and service type:
  • Private carrier: Company-owned fleet — maximum control, high fixed cost, ideal when high volume and specialized requirements justify the investment
  • Common carrier: Offers service to any shipper at published tariff rates — regulated, no exclusivity (e.g., UPS, FedEx, major railroads)
  • Contract carrier: Serves specific shippers under contract — dedicated equipment/service, negotiated rates, more committed capacity than common carrier
  • Freight broker: Intermediary matching shippers with carriers — doesn't own equipment, earns commission, access to spot market capacity
Asset-based vs. non-asset-based 3PL: Asset-based 3PLs own trucks/warehouses (more control, higher cost). Non-asset-based (brokers, freight forwarders) rely on networks of contracted carriers — more flexibility, lighter capital.
Mode and Carrier Selection Criteria
Factors in transportation mode selection:
  • Cost: Total freight cost per unit or shipment
  • Transit time: How long delivery takes
  • Reliability/consistency: On-time performance and transit time variability
  • Cargo characteristics: Weight, dimensions, fragility, temperature sensitivity, hazmat classification
  • Destination accessibility: Rail doesn't reach all locations; air serves major airports only
  • Frequency: How often shipments are needed
Routing guide: A document (or system rule) specifying which carrier and mode a shipper prefers for each lane (origin-destination pair), based on pre-negotiated rates and service levels. Carriers are typically listed in priority order.
Spot market vs. contract rates: Contract rates are negotiated with carriers for a defined period (usually 1 year) — predictable cost. Spot market rates are transacted one shipment at a time — volatile, can be lower or much higher than contract rates depending on carrier capacity.
Carrier Performance Management
Carrier scorecard: A structured performance evaluation of carriers across metrics: on-time pickup, on-time delivery, claims ratio (damage), invoice accuracy, responsiveness. Used for carrier development, renewals, and routing guide prioritization.
Key carrier KPIs:
  • On-time delivery %: % of shipments delivered on or before committed date
  • Cargo claims ratio: Claims value ÷ freight revenue — measures damage/loss rate
  • Load acceptance rate: % of tender offers the carrier accepts (low = unreliable capacity)
  • Invoice accuracy: % of carrier invoices that match contracted rates without disputes
Preferred carrier program: A tiered carrier management strategy — preferred carriers (reliable, competitive rates) get first tender priority. Backup carriers get overflow. This ensures capacity during tight markets while maintaining cost discipline.

TERM / CONCEPT
Contract rates vs. spot market rates
ANSWER
Contract rates: negotiated with carrier for a set period (typically annual). Stable, predictable cost. Carrier commits to capacity. Shipper commits to volume. Spot market: rate set for a single shipment based on current supply/demand of carrier capacity. Volatile — can be 50% cheaper or 100% more expensive than contract rates depending on market conditions. Strategy: use contracts for base volume, spot/broker for surge capacity.

TERM / CONCEPT
What is a routing guide?
ANSWER
A document or system rule specifying which carrier and mode to use for each shipping lane (origin-destination pair). Typically: Carrier 1 (preferred, best rate) → Carrier 2 (backup) → Carrier 3 (last resort/spot market) Purpose: ensure shippers consistently use negotiated rates, comply with customer routing requirements, and protect preferred carrier relationships.

TERM / CONCEPT
Private fleet vs. common carrier — when to use each
ANSWER
Private fleet: company owns trucks. Use when: very high volume on consistent lanes, specialized handling (hazmat, refrigerated), exceptional service requirements, or driving competitive differentiation through delivery (e.g., same-day delivery as a brand promise). Common carrier: 3rd party trucks. Use when: variable volume, many destinations, specialized equipment needed occasionally, or capital is better deployed elsewhere. Lower fixed cost, scalable.

Q1 of 5
A shipper uses a routing guide with three carrier options listed in priority order. The purpose of the routing guide is to:
Routing guides specify: for this lane (origin-destination), use Carrier A first (best rate/service), then Carrier B if A declines, then Carrier C/spot market as last resort. Benefits: consistent application of negotiated rates, protection of preferred carrier relationships, and predictable freight cost. Without a routing guide, shippers might accidentally use expensive carriers or skip preferred arrangements.
Q2 of 5
A carrier with a low 'load acceptance rate' is problematic because:
Load acceptance rate = % of shipment tenders the carrier actually accepts. A carrier that accepts only 60% of tenders will leave 40% of loads needing urgent re-tendering — likely at spot market rates. Low acceptance means unreliable capacity commitment. Preferred carriers should have high acceptance rates (85%+) — that's the value of a contract relationship.
Q3 of 5
For a recurring, high-volume lane, a shipper is better served by:
Contract rates provide: price predictability (know your cost for the year), committed carrier capacity (carrier reserves trucks for you), and typically lower rates than spot market average (carrier values the guaranteed volume). Spot market is better for overflow or irregular lanes where volume doesn't justify a carrier committing dedicated capacity. Spot rates can swing dramatically (±50%) with market conditions.
Q4 of 5
A cargo claims ratio measures:
Cargo claims ratio = Claims value ÷ Freight revenue × 100%. It measures how often (and how much) the carrier damages or loses freight. A high ratio = the carrier is rough with cargo, improperly secures loads, or has theft issues. This metric drives carrier selection (don't use high-claims carriers for fragile or high-value goods) and annual contract renewal decisions.
Q5 of 5
A company ships refrigerated pharmaceutical products requiring precise temperature control throughout transit. The most important mode selection criterion is:
Pharmaceuticals require cold chain integrity — temperature excursions can render product worthless or dangerous. For temperature-sensitive cargo, the most critical criterion is the carrier's reefer (refrigerated) equipment reliability, temperature monitoring capability, and track record with pharma shipments. Cost matters, but a cheaper carrier that compromises the cold chain destroys far more value than the freight savings — and creates regulatory and safety liability.
