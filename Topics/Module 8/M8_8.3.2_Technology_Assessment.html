Evaluating Supply Chain Technology
Technology assessment: The process of evaluating supply chain technology options to determine which best fits the organization's needs, capabilities, and strategic direction — considering functionality, TCO, vendor stability, integration requirements, and implementation risk.
Technology selection criteria:
  • Functional fit: Does it do what we need it to do?
  • Total Cost of Ownership (TCO): License/subscription + implementation + training + maintenance + integration over 5 years
  • Integration: How does it connect with existing ERP, WMS, TMS, and partner systems?
  • Scalability: Will it grow with the business?
  • Vendor stability: Is the vendor financially viable and committed to the product long-term?
  • Implementation risk: How complex is deployment? What can go wrong?
Build vs. buy vs. subscribe: The make-or-buy decision for technology. Build (custom): highest cost, maximum fit, long timeline. Buy (license): faster, proven, less flexible. Subscribe (SaaS/cloud): lowest upfront cost, fastest deployment, dependent on vendor.
Technology Implementation
Change management: The structured approach to transitioning people from the current state (old processes, old tools) to a future state (new technology, new processes) — addressing the human side of technology adoption.
Technology implementations fail more often due to people/process issues than technology issues. Common failure causes: lack of executive sponsorship, insufficient user training, resistance to change, inadequate data quality, scope creep.
Phased implementation: Deploying technology in stages (by function, region, or capability) rather than all at once — reducing risk by limiting the scope of any single deployment failure. Allows learning and adjustment between phases.
Business process reengineering (BPR): Fundamentally redesigning supply chain processes before or during technology implementation — rather than automating existing inefficient processes. 'Don't automate a broken process; fix it first.'
Data quality is the foundation of any supply chain technology: a TMS routing the wrong way, a WMS directing to the wrong location, an AI making bad forecasts — all trace back to bad data. Data cleansing and governance are prerequisites, not afterthoughts.
ROI and Continuous Improvement of Technology
Technology ROI: Quantifying the financial return from a supply chain technology investment — typically measured as: (Benefits − Costs) ÷ Costs × 100. Benefits include: labor savings, inventory reduction, error reduction, faster cycle times, improved service.
Technology ROI = (Benefits − Costs) ÷ Costs × 100%
Continuous improvement of technology: Supply chain technology must be regularly updated, expanded, and optimized post-implementation — both to capture emerging capabilities (vendor updates, new modules) and to increase user adoption and process maturity.
Technology adoption curve: early adopters gain first-mover advantages; late adopters face competitive disadvantage but benefit from more mature, proven technology. Supply chain leaders balance innovation risk against competitive urgency.

TERM / CONCEPT
Build vs. buy vs. subscribe (SaaS) — supply chain technology
ANSWER
Build (custom development): ✓ Perfect functional fit ✗ Highest cost, longest timeline, ongoing maintenance burden When: truly unique requirement; core competitive differentiator Buy (license): ✓ Proven functionality, faster than build ✗ Requires customization, upgrade complexity When: well-defined requirements, standard process Subscribe (SaaS/cloud): ✓ Lowest upfront, fastest deployment, vendor maintains ✗ Vendor dependency, less customization, data in vendor cloud When: standard processes, need speed, limited IT resources
TERM / CONCEPT
Why supply chain technology implementations fail
ANSWER
Technology failure causes (in order of frequency): 1. Lack of executive sponsorship (no one pushing adoption) 2. Insufficient change management / user training 3. Resistance to change (people prefer the old way) 4. Poor data quality (garbage in = garbage out) 5. Scope creep (project grows beyond original plan) 6. Inadequate process redesign (automate broken processes) 7. Actual technology failure (least common) Key lesson: technology implementation = 30% technology + 70% people and process change.
TERM / CONCEPT
Change management in technology implementation
ANSWER
Structured approach to transitioning people from old to new ways of working. Key elements: • Sponsorship: executive champion who communicates WHY change matters • Stakeholder engagement: involve users in design, not just deployment • Training: adequate hands-on training before go-live • Communication: clear, regular updates on timeline, benefits, what changes • Resistance management: identify concerns early, address proactively • Reinforcement: measure adoption, recognize positive behaviors Rule: the technical solution is rarely the barrier — human adoption is.
TERM / CONCEPT
Phased vs. big-bang technology implementation
ANSWER
Big-bang: all features deployed simultaneously across all users/locations. ✓ Faster to complete; no parallel running ✗ High risk — if anything fails, everything fails simultaneously When: small scope; replacement of critically broken legacy system Phased: deploy by function, region, or capability in stages. ✓ Lower risk (limited scope per phase); learn and adjust between phases ✗ Longer timeline; integration complexity of parallel systems during transition When: large, complex implementations; high risk of disruption Best practice: almost always phase large supply chain system implementations.
TERM / CONCEPT
Data quality — why it's the foundation of supply chain technology
ANSWER
Every supply chain system is only as good as its data: • WMS sends pickers to wrong location → wrong location data • TMS calculates wrong route → incorrect transit time data • MRP plans wrong quantities → wrong BOM or inventory records • AI forecast is wildly off → inaccurate historical data • Control tower shows phantom inventory → disconnected systems Data governance: policies defining who owns data, how it's maintained, quality standards. Pre-implementation: data cleansing is critical (find and fix bad data BEFORE migrating to new system — don't migrate garbage).
TERM / CONCEPT
Technology ROI calculation
ANSWER
ROI = (Benefits − Costs) ÷ Costs × 100% Benefits to quantify: • Labor savings (fewer FTEs needed for task X) • Inventory reduction (lower safety stock from better forecast) • Error reduction (fewer mis-shipments, returns) • Faster cycle time (shorter order-to-delivery) • Improved service (higher OTIF → less lost revenue) Costs to include: • License/subscription fees • Implementation (consulting, internal time) • Training • Integration • Ongoing maintenance and support Typical TMS ROI: 5–15% freight savings; typical WMS ROI: 15–25% labor efficiency improvement.

Q1 of 5
The most common reason supply chain technology implementations fail is:
Technology implementation failures: the technology rarely fails — the adoption does. Most common causes: no executive sponsor (no one championing adoption when resistance appears), insufficient training (users revert to old ways), resistance to change (fear of job loss, comfort with familiar processes), poor data quality (system can't produce accurate output), and scope creep (project expands beyond manageable size). Rule of thumb: 30% of implementation effort = technical; 70% = change management, training, and process redesign.
Q2 of 5
A phased technology implementation reduces risk by:
Phased implementation: deploy module 1 → stabilize → deploy module 2. Benefits: (1) contained failure scope — if Phase 1 has problems, only a subset of operations is affected; (2) organizational learning — each phase teaches lessons applied to the next; (3) early wins build confidence and adoption; (4) course corrections are possible between phases. Trade-off: longer total timeline and complexity of parallel systems during transition — worth it for large, complex implementations.
Q3 of 5
Business Process Reengineering (BPR) should occur before technology implementation because:
'Don't automate a broken process.' If the current order management process has 12 unnecessary approval steps, implementing a new OMS that automates those 12 steps just makes bureaucracy faster — it doesn't eliminate it. The right sequence: (1) map the current state process; (2) identify waste and inefficiency; (3) design the improved future-state process; (4) configure/implement technology to enable the IMPROVED process. BPR + technology = transformation. Technology without BPR = digital lipstick on a flawed process.
Q4 of 5
SaaS (Software as a Service) supply chain technology is preferred when:
SaaS advantages: no infrastructure investment, fastest time-to-value (weeks vs. months), vendor handles maintenance and updates, pay-as-you-grow pricing. Best for: standard supply chain processes (WMS, TMS, procurement) where differentiation comes from business processes, not technology customization. Limitations: less customizable (must fit vendor's process model), vendor dependency (vendor's pricing and roadmap decisions affect you), data security considerations (data in vendor cloud). Most new supply chain technology investments favor SaaS unless there's a specific need for on-premise control.
Q5 of 5
Data quality is described as the 'foundation' of supply chain technology because:
Garbage in, garbage out. WMS directs pickers to wrong locations → wrong item data. MRP generates wrong material requirements → wrong inventory records. AI forecast is wildly wrong → corrupted historical data. TMS routes incorrectly → wrong transit times or costs in the database. The most sophisticated technology is only as good as its data. Pre-implementation data cleansing (finding and fixing bad data before migrating to a new system) is critical — migrating bad data into a new system just means operating a more expensive bad data system.
