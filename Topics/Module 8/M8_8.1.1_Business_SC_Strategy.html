Linking Business Strategy to Supply Chain
Business strategy: A company's plan for how it will achieve its goals — which markets to compete in, how to create value for customers, and how to sustain competitive advantage. Supply chain strategy must align with and enable the business strategy.
Two primary competitive strategies (Porter's generic strategies):
  • Cost leadership: Compete by being the lowest-cost producer — requires an efficient, high-volume, standardized supply chain focused on waste elimination and economies of scale (Walmart, IKEA, Amazon logistics)
  • Differentiation: Compete by offering unique value that customers will pay a premium for — requires a responsive, flexible, customer-centric supply chain that can handle variety and customization (Apple, Zara, luxury brands)
Supply chain strategy alignment: Ensuring that the supply chain's design, capabilities, and priorities (cost vs. responsiveness, efficiency vs. flexibility) match what the business strategy requires to serve its chosen markets.
Efficient vs. Responsive Supply Chains
Efficient supply chain: Optimized for lowest total cost — high asset utilization, low inventory, standardized products, long production runs, lowest-cost sourcing. Best fit for predictable demand, commodity products, price-sensitive customers.
Responsive (agile) supply chain: Optimized for speed and flexibility — ability to respond quickly to demand changes, customize, and meet tight delivery windows. Higher cost but better service. Best fit for volatile demand, short product life cycles, fashion, high-value products.
Fisher's model: Framework matching supply chain type to product type: Functional products (stable demand, long life cycle) → Efficient supply chain. Innovative products (uncertain demand, short life cycle) → Responsive supply chain. Mismatch between product type and supply chain type is a root cause of supply chain problems.
The 'zone of strategic fit': the alignment region where supply chain capabilities match the uncertainty and requirements of the served market. Operating outside this zone creates either excess cost (over-responsive for stable demand) or service failures (too efficient for volatile demand).
Strategic Supply Chain Decisions
Major strategic supply chain decisions:
  • Network design: Number/location of plants, DCs, and suppliers — long-term, capital-intensive
  • Make vs. buy: Which capabilities to own vs. outsource
  • Global vs. regional vs. local sourcing: Geographic footprint trade-offs
  • Push vs. pull: Where in the supply chain to decouple make-to-stock from make-to-order
  • Integration level: How much to integrate with suppliers and customers
Decoupling point: The point in the supply chain where the production/fulfillment strategy switches from push (make-to-forecast) to pull (make/assemble-to-order). Positioning the decoupling point determines customer lead time and inventory investment.
Moving the decoupling point downstream (closer to customer) requires more finished goods inventory but shorter customer lead time. Moving it upstream enables lower inventory but longer lead time. Postponement: delay the decoupling point as far downstream as possible.

TERM / CONCEPT
Cost leadership vs. differentiation supply chain
ANSWER
Cost leadership: compete on lowest price. Supply chain focus: efficiency — low cost, high volume, standardized, minimal waste, lowest-cost sourcing. Long production runs. Examples: Walmart (logistics efficiency), IKEA (flat-pack design + customer assembly), Amazon (scale + automation). Differentiation: compete on unique value (quality, speed, customization). Supply chain focus: responsiveness — flexible, fast, customizable, higher service level. Examples: Zara (fast fashion, 2-week design-to-shelf), Apple (premium, controlled supply chain).
TERM / CONCEPT
Fisher's model — functional vs. innovative products
ANSWER
Functional products: stable, predictable demand; long product life cycle; commodity-like. → Need: EFFICIENT supply chain (low cost, high utilization, low inventory) Innovative products: uncertain demand; short life cycle; high variety; fashion/tech. → Need: RESPONSIVE supply chain (speed, flexibility, ability to handle demand volatility) Mismatch = supply chain problems: • Responsive SC for functional product = too expensive • Efficient SC for innovative product = stockouts + markdowns
TERM / CONCEPT
Decoupling point — definition and strategic importance
ANSWER
The point in the supply chain where production switches from PUSH (make-to-forecast/stock) to PULL (make/assemble/configure-to-order). Upstream decoupling: make/buy components to forecast (push), assemble/customize only on order (pull). Strategic trade-off: • Decoupling point close to customer: more finished inventory, shorter lead time • Decoupling point far from customer: less finished inventory, longer lead time Postponement: push the decoupling point as far downstream as possible → carry generic/semi-finished inventory → customize on order.
TERM / CONCEPT
Efficient vs. responsive supply chain — key characteristics
ANSWER
EFFICIENT: • Primary goal: lowest total cost • Inventory strategy: low; minimize buffers • Lead time focus: reduce cost, not necessarily speed • Supplier selection: lowest cost • Manufacturing: high utilization, long runs • Best for: functional products, stable demand RESPONSIVE: • Primary goal: speed and flexibility • Inventory strategy: buffer to meet demand variability • Lead time: minimize for customer advantage • Supplier selection: fast, flexible, reliable • Manufacturing: flexible, fast changeovers • Best for: innovative products, volatile demand
TERM / CONCEPT
Zone of strategic fit
ANSWER
Fisher's framework: match the supply chain type to the market's demand uncertainty. Zone of strategic fit = alignment between supply chain capabilities (implied supply uncertainty) and market demands (implied demand uncertainty). Operating in the zone: supply chain capability matches market need → good performance. Outside the zone: • Responsive SC for predictable product → wasteful, expensive • Efficient SC for unpredictable product → stockouts, lost sales, markdowns Achieving strategic fit is the central task of supply chain strategy.
TERM / CONCEPT
Push vs. pull in supply chain
ANSWER
PUSH (make-to-forecast/make-to-stock): • Production driven by demand forecast • Goods manufactured before orders received • Risk: overstock if forecast wrong, stockout if under-forecast • Best for: stable, predictable demand; long production lead times PULL (make/assemble-to-order): • Production triggered by actual customer orders • No finished goods inventory until order placed • Risk: longer customer lead time • Best for: high variety, unpredictable demand; short production lead time Most supply chains combine both — push upstream, pull downstream of the decoupling point.

Q1 of 5
A company competing on price leadership requires a supply chain that prioritizes:
Cost leadership = compete by being the lowest-cost producer. Supply chain alignment: minimize total logistics and operations cost through standardization (fewer SKUs), high asset utilization (full trucks, high warehouse density), long production runs (economies of scale), lowest-cost sourcing, and waste elimination (Lean). Adding responsiveness capability (customization, fast response, buffers for variety) adds cost that the cost-leadership model can't absorb without losing the price advantage.
Q2 of 5
According to Fisher's model, a fashion clothing company should use:
Fisher's model: Innovative products (short life cycles, uncertain demand, high variety) → Responsive supply chain. Fashion is the quintessential innovative product: trends change every season, demand is hard to predict, wrong inventory = markdowns, too little = lost sales. Responsive SC: fast suppliers, flexible manufacturing (Zara makes in Portugal and Spain, not just Asia), buffer inventory, quick design-to-shelf cycles. Efficient SC (optimized for lowest cost with long lead times) is wrong for fashion — you need speed over cost.
Q3 of 5
The decoupling point in a supply chain determines:
Decoupling point: push production upstream (build components to forecast), pull/configure downstream (only finish/customize on actual order). Positioning determines: customer lead time (everything downstream of decoupling point is in the order fulfillment cycle) AND inventory form (finished goods vs. semi-finished/components). Postponement strategy moves decoupling point downstream: carry fewer finished configurations, customize later. Dell's original model: push components, pull final PC assembly to customer order.
Q4 of 5
Operating an efficient supply chain for products with volatile, unpredictable demand results in:
Efficient SC for volatile demand: the SC is built around a demand forecast (low inventory, high utilization, long lead times). When volatile demand deviates from forecast — as it will — the efficient SC has no flexibility buffer: demand spike → stockout (can't respond quickly with low inventory and long supplier lead times); demand drop → excess inventory that must be marked down. Fisher's model: innovative/volatile products REQUIRE responsive supply chains. Applying an efficient SC here is a fundamental strategic misalignment.
Q5 of 5
A company that makes products to a forecast and then customizes them to specific customer orders only when orders are received is using:
Push-pull: push standard components/semi-finished goods to forecast (economical, large batches); pull final customization/assembly only when order arrives (responsive, no finished goods overstock). The decoupling point is between the generic semi-finished and the customized finished product. Classic example: Dell (components pushed, PCs pulled/assembled to order). Or Benetton (garments produced undyed/generic, dyed to specific colors only when orders reveal demand). Combines efficiency (long-run component production) with responsiveness (customization on order).
