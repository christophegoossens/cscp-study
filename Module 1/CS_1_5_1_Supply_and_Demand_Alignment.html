Operations Planning & Control Road Map

Supply and demand are aligned through a process called operations planning and control. The road map runs from strategic planning down to shop floor execution.

Master Planning: a group of business processes that includes demand management (forecasting, order entry, order promising), production planning, master scheduling, and the master production schedule (MPS).
Exhibit 1-47: Operations Planning and Control
Steps in Operations Planning & Control
  • 1. Gather & cleanse demand history; run statistical forecast; identify outliers.
  • 2. Review with marketing, product management, and sales — add qualitative overlay.
  • 3. Finalize demand plan with demand-side teams; hand off to supply.
  • 4. Constrain demand plan based on capacity and material availability.
  • 5. S&OP meeting with finance and executive management — one consensus plan.
  • 6. Production plan → master scheduling → MPS (Master Production Schedule).
  • 7. MRP uses BOM, inventory data, and MPS to calculate material requirements.
  • 8. Production activity control executes the plan on the shop floor.
💡 Key Point: The S&OP output is a single plan — sales, manufacturing, and finance all work from the same agreed numbers.
Strategic, Business & Master Planning
  • Strategic plan: 5–10+ year horizon. Focuses on how to marshal resources to meet goals. Sets the direction for master planning and S&OP.
  • Business plan: translates strategy into specific goals and financial targets. Stated in dollars, grouped by product family. Input to S&OP.
  • Business plan purposes: clarify strategy, provide reference point for S&OP, describe long-term strategies for shorter-term tactical plans.
  • Master plan: long-term resource plan and aggregate production plan. Built after the business plan.
Resource Planning
Resource Planning (RP): capacity planning conducted at the business plan level. The process of establishing, measuring, and adjusting limits or levels of long-range capacity. Also called resource requirements planning (RRP).
  • Takes the longest view of capacity — determines need for capital investments, new equipment, or new facilities.
  • Planning horizon = lead time of the needed resource (can be years).
  • Sits above rough-cut capacity planning and capacity requirements planning in the hierarchy.

TERM / CONCEPT
ASCM def: Master Planning
ANSWER
A group of business processes including demand management (forecasting, order entry, order promising), production planning, master scheduling, and the master production schedule (MPS).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the operations planning and control process?
ANSWER
The end-to-end process that aligns supply and demand — from strategic/business planning down through S&OP, master scheduling, MRP, and production activity control on the shop floor.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 8 steps of operations planning and control?
ANSWER
1. Gather/cleanse demand data, run statistical forecast. 2. Review with marketing/sales (qualitative overlay). 3. Finalize demand plan. 4. Constrain for capacity/materials. 5. S&OP executive meeting — one consensus plan. 6. Production plan → MPS. 7. MRP calculates material needs. 8. Production activity control executes.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the key output of the S&OP meeting?
ANSWER
A single consensus plan: sales sells to the plan, manufacturing builds to the plan, and finance funds the plan. All functions aligned to the same numbers.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Master Production Schedule (MPS)?
ANSWER
The output of master scheduling (which takes the production plan as input). The MPS drives MRP by specifying what to build, in what quantities, and when.
TERM / CONCEPT
What inputs does MRP use?
ANSWER
Bill of Materials (BOM), inventory data, and the MPS. MRP calculates what materials are needed, in what quantities, and when to order or produce them.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the strategic plan?
ANSWER
A long-term plan (5–10+ years) focused on how to marshal resources to meet strategic goals. Foundation for master planning and S&OP. Sets direction for shorter-term plans.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the business plan?
ANSWER
Translates organizational strategy into specific goals and financial targets. Stated in dollars, grouped by product family. Input to S&OP. Provides reference point for the sales and operations plan.
TERM / CONCEPT
Three purposes of the business plan
ANSWER
1. Clarify strategy — state an explicit vision for the business. 2. Provide a reference point for the S&OP plan. 3. Describe long-term strategies to guide shorter-term tactical plans.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the master plan?
ANSWER
A long-term resource plan and aggregate production plan. Developed after the business plan. Feeds into S&OP and drives capacity and production decisions.
TERM / CONCEPT
ASCM def: Resource Planning (RP)
ANSWER
Capacity planning conducted at the business plan level. The process of establishing, measuring, and adjusting limits or levels of long-range capacity. Also called resource requirements planning (RRP).
TERM / CONCEPT
What does resource planning determine?
ANSWER
The need for capital investments, new machinery, or new facilities. It takes the longest view of capacity. Planning horizon = lead time of the resource needed (often years).
TERM / CONCEPT
Where does resource planning sit in the capacity planning hierarchy?
ANSWER
At the top — above rough-cut capacity planning (RCCP) and capacity requirements planning (CRP). Resource planning is tied to the business plan; RCCP to the MPS; CRP to MRP.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is production activity control?
ANSWER
The final execution layer. Receives output from MRP and detail planning and controls the actual work on the shop floor (sequencing, dispatching, monitoring).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the production plan?
ANSWER
An output of S&OP. Provides the rate of production at the product family level. Input to master scheduling, which converts it into the more detailed MPS.
TERM / CONCEPT
Demand-side vs. supply-side in the operations planning road map
ANSWER
Demand-side: forecasting, demand management, demand sensing, demand creation. Supply-side: resource planning, production planning, master scheduling, MRP, production activity control.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is demand sensing?
ANSWER
Monitoring actual incoming demand signals (orders, POS data) in near real-time to detect changes from the forecast. Part of demand management.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the relationship between strategic plan and business plan?
ANSWER
The strategic plan sets the long-term direction (5–10+ years). The business plan operationalizes it in financial terms (dollars, product families) with specific goals and time frames. Business plan is the input to S&OP.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is a single consensus plan from S&OP important?
ANSWER
It prevents each function (sales, operations, finance) from working to different numbers, which leads to excess inventory, shortages, or missed financial targets. Alignment = one version of truth.
TERM / CONCEPT
What role does strategic planning play in the master planning process?
ANSWER
It is a key input — master planning uses both the strategic/business plan and information from customers and the marketplace to drive the demand management and supply planning processes.

Question 1
What is the primary goal of supply and demand alignment?
A.To maximize plant utilization regardless of demand levels
B.To match supply capacity and inventory with actual demand requirements, minimizing both stock-outs and excess inventory
C.To eliminate all inventory from the supply chain
D.To ensure the sales team always meets its revenue targets
Supply and demand alignment aims to synchronize what customers want with what the supply chain can provide — meeting service level targets while minimizing the cost of excess inventory and capacity.
Question 2
Which planning tool is primarily used to align aggregate supply and demand at the product family level?
A.Material Requirements Planning (MRP)
B.Sales and Operations Planning (S&OP)
C.Economic Order Quantity (EOQ)
D.Vendor Managed Inventory (VMI)
S&OP operates at the aggregate (product family) level to balance demand forecasts with supply plans — including production capacity, procurement, and inventory. It produces the agreed production plan.
Question 3
Rough-Cut Capacity Planning (RCCP) is used to:
A.Calculate exact labor requirements for each work center
B.Quickly check whether the master production schedule is feasible given key resource constraints (bottlenecks) — without the detailed analysis of full CRP
C.Determine the optimal safety stock level for finished goods
D.Set supplier delivery schedules for raw materials
RCCP provides a high-level feasibility check on the MPS by testing it against key (bottleneck) resources. It is less detailed than Capacity Requirements Planning (CRP) but much faster to run.
Question 4
What is 'available to promise' (ATP)?
A.The total production capacity of all facilities
B.The uncommitted portion of current inventory and planned production that can be promised to new customers without impacting existing orders
C.The minimum safety stock level for a finished good
D.The lead time required to fulfill a customer order from stock
ATP is the quantity a company can commit to a new customer order without jeopardizing existing commitments. It is calculated by comparing planned production to existing customer orders.
Question 5
When supply is constrained (less than demand), which action helps align supply and demand?
A.Building additional inventory of all SKUs
B.Demand shaping — using pricing or promotions to shift demand from constrained items to available alternatives
C.Expanding the sales force to increase demand
D.Reducing safety stock targets to free up capacity
Demand shaping uses price incentives, product substitution, or promotion of alternatives to redirect demand toward what is available, helping to balance supply and demand without requiring additional supply.
Question 6
What is 'postponement' and how does it help supply-demand alignment?
A.Delaying customer deliveries when supply is short
B.Deferring final product differentiation (customization) until actual demand is known, reducing the risk of producing the wrong mix
C.Postponing payment to suppliers to improve cash flow
D.Delaying the S&OP meeting to gather more demand data
Postponement (e.g., Dell's build-to-order model) defers differentiation to the last moment, allowing the supply chain to hold generic inventory and respond to actual mix demand — reducing inventory risk.
Question 7
Which metric directly measures supply-demand alignment performance?
A.Total cost of goods sold
B.Fill rate — the percentage of demand that is satisfied from available inventory when requested
C.Return on assets
D.Supplier on-time delivery percentage
Fill rate measures how well supply matches demand at the point of customer request. A high fill rate indicates good supply-demand alignment; a low fill rate indicates misalignment (stock-outs or wrong mix).
Question 8
What does a 'demand-supply mismatch' cost the company?
A.Only the cost of excess inventory
B.Only the cost of lost sales from stock-outs
C.Both the cost of excess inventory (overstock) and lost sales/expediting costs (understock) — the total cost of imbalance
D.Nothing if safety stock levels are set correctly
Misalignment has costs on both sides: excess supply = carrying costs, obsolescence, markdowns; insufficient supply = lost sales, expediting costs, customer service failures. Both must be managed.
Question 9
Which planning horizon is typical for a Sales and Operations Plan?
A.1–2 days ahead
B.1–3 weeks ahead (rolling)
C.3–18 months ahead (rolling, monthly review cycle)
D.3–5 years ahead (strategic)
S&OP operates in the tactical horizon — typically 3–18 months out, with monthly review cycles. It bridges strategic planning (business plan) and operational execution (MPS/MRP).
Question 10
'Inventory pooling' improves supply-demand alignment by:
A.Combining all inventory into one product type
B.Centralizing inventory so that variability of total demand is lower than the sum of individual location variabilities — reducing safety stock needs
C.Outsourcing inventory management to a 3PL
D.Using one supplier for all inventory categories
Risk pooling: when demand is aggregated across locations, the coefficient of variation of total demand is lower, requiring less total safety stock. Centralized warehousing and virtual inventory pooling leverage this principle.
Question 11
What happens during the 'reconciliation' step of a typical S&OP process?
A.Invoices from suppliers are reconciled with purchase orders
B.The unconstrained demand plan is compared to the supply plan, gaps are identified, and cross-functional trade-offs are resolved to produce a single agreed plan
C.The accounting team reconciles inventory records to the general ledger
D.Customer orders are matched against available inventory
Reconciliation in S&OP brings together the demand plan and supply plan, identifies gaps (e.g., demand exceeds capacity), and drives cross-functional decisions about how to close those gaps (e.g., capacity investment, demand shaping, lead time changes).
Question 12
Why is it important to align supply chain planning with financial planning?
A.Because IT systems require financial data to run MRP calculations
B.To ensure that the supply chain plan (inventory, capacity, sourcing) reflects realistic financial constraints and that financial budgets account for actual supply chain cost drivers
C.To give the CFO control over all supply chain decisions
D.Because tax authorities require integrated supply and financial plans
Integrated financial and SC planning ensures that capacity investments, inventory levels, and sourcing decisions are financially feasible, and that financial budgets reflect real supply chain requirements rather than being set in isolation.
