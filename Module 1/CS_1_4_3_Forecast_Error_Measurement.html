Forecast Error, Accuracy & Bias
Forecast Error: the difference between actual and forecast demand.
  • Absolute error: |Actual − Forecast|
  • APE (Absolute Percentage Error): |Actual − Forecast| ÷ Actual × 100%
  • Forecast accuracy: 100% − APE
Bias: a consistent deviation from the mean in one direction (high or low). A good forecast is not biased.
  • Bias: cumulative sum of errors ≠ 0 (consistently positive = under-forecast; negative = over-forecast)
  • Random variation: errors cancel out — cumulative sum ≈ 0
MAD & Tracking Signal
Mean Absolute Deviation (MAD): the average of the absolute values of the deviations of observed values from some expected value. MAD = Σ|actual − forecast| ÷ n
  • MAD of 1.68 = forecasts are off by ±1.68 units on average
  • Used to calculate safety stock: multiply MAD by the safety factor for your target service level
  • ±1 MAD ≈ 60% of outcomes; ±2 MAD ≈ 89%; ±3 MAD ≈ 98%

Tracking Signal: the ratio of the cumulative algebraic sum of the deviations between the forecasts and the actual values to the mean absolute deviation. Signals when a model needs revision.
  • Tracking Signal = Cumulative algebraic errors (NOT absolute) ÷ MAD
  • Rule of thumb: if tracking signal exceeds ±4, review and revise the model
  • Stays within ±4 = method is appropriate for the data

Standard Deviation & Safety Stock
Standard Deviation: a measurement of dispersion of data or of a variable. Computed by finding differences from the mean, squaring them, summing, dividing by n−1, and taking the square root.
  • ±1 SD = 68.26%; ±2 SD = 95.44%; ±3 SD = 99.74%
  • SD ≈ 1.25 × MAD — interchangeable for safety stock calculations
  • Example: SD = 2.1, 98% service level (factor 2.05) → safety stock = 2.1 × 2.05 ≈ 4.3 units

MSE & MAPE
  • MSE (Mean Squared Error): squares each error before averaging. Magnifies large errors — more sensitive than MAD to occasional big deviations. Harder to interpret (squared units).
  • MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error): average of all APE values. Scale-independent — compare accuracy across products, SKUs, or regions regardless of volume.

💡 Key Point: MAD = absolute units (needs context). MAPE = percentage (universally comparable). Tracking signal detects bias. MSE highlights outlier errors. All four serve different diagnostic purposes.

What is MSE and when is it useful?
ANSWER

TERM / CONCEPT
MSE vs. MAD: when to prefer MSE?
ANSWER
When occasional large errors matter more than many small ones. MSE highlights outliers strongly. MAD treats all errors equally regardless of size.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is MAPE?
ANSWER
Mean Absolute Percentage Error = average of all APE values. Expressed as %, so scale-independent — you can compare forecast accuracy across products of very different volumes.
TERM / CONCEPT
MAD vs. MAPE: key difference?
ANSWER
MAD is an absolute number (e.g. 1.68 units) — only meaningful in the context of demand scale. MAPE is a % — inherently comparable across products, locations, or time periods.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the complement of APE?
ANSWER
Forecast accuracy = 100% − APE. Example: APE of 5% → accuracy of 95%.
TERM / CONCEPT
What does a consistently negative tracking signal mean?
ANSWER
The model is systematically over-forecasting (actual demand is consistently lower than the forecast). Action: review the model for bias.
TERM / CONCEPT
Name the four main forecast error metrics and their purpose.
ANSWER
MAD: average absolute error — safety stock basis. Tracking Signal: detects bias (algebraic errors ÷ MAD). MSE: sensitive to large outliers (squares errors). MAPE: % error — comparable across scales.

Question 1
What does MAD stand for in forecast error measurement?
A.Maximum Allowable Deviation
B.Mean Absolute Deviation — the average of absolute forecast errors over a period
C.Minimum Adjusted Demand
D.Monthly Adjusted Difference
MAD (Mean Absolute Deviation) is calculated as the average of |Actual - Forecast| over multiple periods. It measures average forecast error magnitude in the same units as demand.
Question 2
What does MAPE stand for, and what is its key advantage over MAD?
A.Maximum Absolute Percentage Error; it is always positive
B.Mean Absolute Percentage Error; it expresses error as a percentage of actual demand, enabling comparison across products with different volumes
C.Moving Average Percentage Error; it smooths the error over time
D.Minimum Allowable Period Error; it sets the acceptable error threshold
MAPE = average of |Error/Actual| × 100%. Its key advantage is scale-independence — a 10% MAPE means the same thing regardless of whether the product sells 100 or 100,000 units per month.
Question 3
What does 'forecast bias' mean?
A.Random fluctuation in forecast errors with no pattern
B.A systematic tendency to consistently over-forecast or under-forecast; the cumulative error is not close to zero
C.The range of possible forecast error values
D.The lag time between creating a forecast and receiving actual demand
Bias is a non-random, systematic error. If the sum of errors is consistently positive, the forecast is chronically under-forecasting. If consistently negative, it is over-forecasting. Both must be corrected.
Question 4
The tracking signal is used to:
A.Calculate the economic order quantity
B.Monitor whether forecast bias is within acceptable limits — a tracking signal outside ±4 to ±6 MADs signals systematic bias requiring review
C.Measure on-time delivery performance of suppliers
D.Track the progress of an S&OP action plan
The tracking signal = cumulative sum of errors / MAD. It detects when bias has drifted outside acceptable limits, triggering a review and correction of the forecast model.
Question 5
Which is WORSE from an operations standpoint: consistent over-forecasting or consistent under-forecasting?
A.Consistent over-forecasting is always worse because it wastes money on excess inventory
B.Consistent under-forecasting is always worse because it causes stock-outs
C.Both are equally problematic — each has different but significant costs (excess inventory vs. lost sales and service failures), and both indicate a biased forecast that should be corrected
D.Neither matters if safety stock is set correctly
Both types of bias cause harm. Over-forecasting leads to excess inventory and write-offs; under-forecasting leads to stock-outs, lost sales, and poor customer service. A good forecast process monitors and corrects both.
Question 6
Why is MAPE a poor metric for slow-moving or intermittent demand items?
A.MAPE cannot be calculated for items with seasonal demand
B.When actual demand is zero or near zero, dividing by actual creates very large or undefined percentage errors — MAPE becomes meaningless or misleading
C.MAPE requires too much data to calculate for slow movers
D.MAPE does not account for lead time variability
When actual demand is zero, MAPE is undefined (division by zero). For near-zero demand, even small absolute errors produce enormous percentage errors. Alternative metrics like MAD or RMSE are preferred for intermittent items.
Question 7
What is the formula for calculating forecast error for a single period?
A.Forecast ÷ Actual
B.Actual demand minus Forecast (Actual − Forecast)
C.(Forecast + Actual) ÷ 2
D.Forecast × Lead time
Error = Actual − Forecast. A positive error means demand exceeded the forecast (under-forecast); a negative error means the forecast exceeded actual demand (over-forecast).
Question 8
A forecast has MAD of 50 units and average demand of 500 units. What does this imply?
A.The forecast is perfect — 50 units of error is negligible
B.The average absolute forecast error is 50 units, or about 10% of average demand — a reasonable starting benchmark depending on product type
C.The product should be discontinued due to poor forecastability
D.Safety stock should be set to exactly 50 units
MAD of 50 on average demand of 500 = roughly 10% MAPE. Whether this is acceptable depends on the product and business context, but it provides a useful baseline for evaluating forecasting performance.
Question 9
Which metric would you use to compare forecast accuracy across products with very different sales volumes?
A.MAD (Mean Absolute Deviation)
B.MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error) — because it normalizes error as a percentage, making cross-product comparison meaningful
C.Cumulative forecast error
D.Absolute forecast error for the most recent period
MAPE is scale-independent — it expresses error as a percent of actual demand, so a product selling 10 units and one selling 10,000 units can be compared on the same scale.
Question 10
What action should be taken when the tracking signal moves outside ±4 MADs?
A.Increase safety stock immediately and do nothing to the forecast model
B.Investigate for a systematic change in demand pattern or forecast assumption — review and recalibrate the forecast model
C.Decrease the forecast by exactly 4 MADs
D.Archive the forecast error data and restart the measurement cycle
A tracking signal outside ±4 MADs signals systematic bias — the model is no longer tracking actual demand. This triggers investigation: has demand shifted? Is there a new trend? The model should be recalibrated.
Question 11
Root mean square error (RMSE) differs from MAD because:
A.RMSE ignores positive errors and only counts negative errors
B.RMSE squares the errors before averaging, giving disproportionately more weight to large errors — making it sensitive to outliers and spikes
C.RMSE is measured in percent rather than units
D.RMSE is only applicable to seasonal demand
Squaring errors penalizes large errors more heavily than small ones. RMSE is therefore a better metric when large errors (e.g., a stock-out event) are disproportionately costly compared to small errors.
Question 12
What is the relationship between MAD and standard deviation of forecast errors?
A.There is no mathematical relationship between the two
B.Standard deviation ≈ 1.25 × MAD — this approximation is used to calculate safety stock from MAD when the error distribution is approximately normal
C.MAD is always larger than standard deviation
D.Standard deviation is only used for quality control, not forecasting
For normally distributed forecast errors, σ ≈ 1.25 × MAD. This allows safety stock formulas (which use standard deviation) to be applied when only MAD has been calculated.
