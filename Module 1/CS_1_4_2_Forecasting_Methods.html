Qualitative & Combination Methods

Forecasting methods are qualitative (judgment-based) or quantitative (math/data-based). Good forecasting typically combines both.

  • Expert/judgmental forecasting: executives, salespeople, and market analysts use product and customer knowledge to estimate demand. Useful when no historical data exists (new products).
  • Delphi method: experts are surveyed independently; responses are collated and sent back anonymously for re-evaluation. Iterative process converges toward consensus without group-think.
  • Combination methods: apply qualitative adjustments on top of a quantitative base forecast. Both versions (with and without qualitative overlay) can be measured for accuracy separately.
💡 Key Point: Bias is a major risk in qualitative forecasting. Estimators may be motivated to inflate or deflate forecasts (e.g., sales optimism, fear of stockouts). Document and track qualitative adjustments to identify systematic biases.
Time-Series Forecasting: Overview & Visualizing

Time-series methods use historical demand data to project the future. Range from simple (naive) to complex. All require deseasonalizing before applying the model when seasonality is present.

  • Naive forecasting: last period’s actual = next period’s forecast. Simplest possible; useful baseline.

Deseasonalizing & Seasonal Index

Step 2 — Deseasonalize. Remove seasonal patterns before forecasting so the underlying trend is visible. Process (using monthly buckets, 3 years of data):

  • Calculate month average for each month (sum of that month across years ÷ number of years).
  • Calculate year average (sum of 12 month averages ÷ 12).
  • Calculate seasonal index = month average ÷ year average.
  • Deseasonalize raw data = raw data ÷ seasonal index for that month.

SMA, WMA & Exponential Smoothing

Applied to the deseasonalized data:

  • Simple Moving Average (SMA): average of demand from N preceding periods (commonly 3 or 6 months). Each period drops the oldest and adds the most recent. Best when demand is relatively constant.
  • Weighted Moving Average (WMA): assigns higher weights to more recent periods. Divide by the sum of weights (not number of periods). More responsive to recent trends than SMA.
  • Exponential smoothing: uses smoothing constant α (alpha, 0–1) plus last period’s forecast and actual demand. Higher α = more weight on recent data (more responsive but more volatile). Lower α = smoother, less reactive to short-term variation.

Associative (Causal) Forecasting

Associative (causal/correlation/extrinsic) forecasting uses an independent variable (predictor) that has a measurable effect on the dependent variable (demand). Best for long-term forecasting.

💡 Key Point: Correlation ≠ Causation. Just because two variables move together does not mean one causes the other. Selected predictors must make logical sense to decision makers.
Leading Indicator: a specific business activity index that indicates future trends. Examples: building permits, initial unemployment claims, manufacturers’ orders for durable goods, money supply (M2), S&P 500, yield curve spread, consumer confidence index.

Lagging indicators reflect changes that have already occurred in the economy (e.g., outstanding loans, inventory-to-sales ratio, CPI, average unemployment duration). Useful for confirming trends rather than predicting them.

Simple regression: y = a + bx. One predictor (x) forecasts one outcome (y). Assessed by coefficient of correlation (r): –1.0 = perfect negative, 0 = none, +1.0 = perfect positive. R² (coefficient of determination) shows % of variation explained by the predictor.

Multiple regression: extends simple regression with multiple predictors (e.g., housing starts AND interest rates to forecast lumber sales).

TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Delphi method?
ANSWER
A qualitative forecasting method where experts are surveyed independently, responses are collated and shared anonymously, and the process iterates until convergence. Avoids group-think while harnessing expert knowledge.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the main risk of qualitative forecasting?
ANSWER
Bias — estimators may be motivated to inflate (sales optimism) or deflate forecasts (fear of stockouts). Qualitative adjustments should be documented and tracked to identify systematic biases over time.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are combination forecasting methods?
ANSWER
Methods that apply qualitative judgment adjustments on top of a quantitative base forecast. Both the unadjusted and adjusted forecasts can be measured for accuracy separately to assess the value added by the qualitative overlay.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is naive forecasting?
ANSWER
The simplest time-series method: last period's actual demand = next period's forecast. Useful as a baseline benchmark to compare other methods against.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the purpose of visualizing data before forecasting?
ANSWER
To detect seasonality, trends, cycles, or unusual spikes in historical data. This determines whether deseasonalizing is needed and which forecasting method is appropriate.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a seasonal index and how is it calculated?
ANSWER
A seasonal index measures the relative demand for a period vs. the annual average. Calculated as: month average ÷ year average. Month average = sum of that month across all years ÷ number of years. Year average = sum of 12 month averages ÷ 12.
TERM / CONCEPT
How do you deseasonalize data?
ANSWER
Divide the raw demand data by the seasonal index for the corresponding period. This removes the seasonal pattern so the underlying trend and base demand can be analyzed cleanly.
TERM / CONCEPT
How do you reseasonalize a forecast?
ANSWER
Multiply the deseasonalized forecast by the seasonal index for each period to restore the seasonal pattern. Always done after the model produces a deseasonalized forecast.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Simple Moving Average (SMA)?
ANSWER
The average of demand from N preceding periods (commonly 3 or 6 months). Recalculated each period by dropping the oldest and adding the newest. Best when demand is relatively constant.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Weighted Moving Average (WMA)?
ANSWER
Like SMA but assigns higher weights to more recent periods. Divide by the SUM of weights (not the number of periods). More responsive to recent demand changes than SMA.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is exponential smoothing?
ANSWER
A time-series method using: New forecast = α x Last actual + (1–α) x Last forecast. α (alpha) is the smoothing constant (0–1). Higher α = more reactive to recent data. Lower α = smoother output. Used to minimize lag when trends shift.
TERM / CONCEPT
What happens if you use forecasted data (instead of actuals) in exponential smoothing for future periods?
ANSWER
The forecast quickly becomes repetitive — after a few periods it produces the same value repeatedly. Always use actual demand when available; only use forecasted demand when projecting into truly unknown future periods.
TERM / CONCEPT
When is time-series forecasting best used vs. associative forecasting?
ANSWER
Time-series: best for short- to medium-term forecasting based on historical demand patterns. Associative (causal): best for long-term forecasting when a reliable external predictor (leading indicator) is available.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the ASCM definition of a leading indicator?
ANSWER
A specific business activity index that indicates future trends. Examples: building permits, initial unemployment claims, manufacturers' orders for durable goods, money supply (M2), S&P 500, yield curve, consumer confidence.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are lagging indicators?
ANSWER
Economic/financial factors that reflect changes that have ALREADY occurred. Used to confirm trends, not predict them. Examples: outstanding business loans, inventory-to-sales ratio, CPI, average unemployment duration, company profits.
TERM / CONCEPT
Correlation vs. causation in associative forecasting?
ANSWER
Correlation = change in an independent variable has a measurable effect on a dependent variable — an observed statistical relationship. Causation = one variable actually CAUSES the other to change. Correlation does not imply causation. Predictors must make logical sense.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is simple regression (linear regression)?
ANSWER
y = a + bx. Uses one predictor variable (x) to forecast an outcome (y). The coefficient of correlation (r) measures strength: -1.0 = perfect negative, 0 = none, +1.0 = perfect positive. R² = % of variation in y explained by x.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is multiple regression?
ANSWER
Extension of simple regression using multiple predictor variables (e.g., housing starts AND interest rates to forecast lumber sales). Produces a more accurate forecast when demand is driven by multiple independent factors.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are service-sector forecasting challenges?
ANSWER
Service demand can fluctuate hour by hour (not just month to month). Example: restaurants must forecast by hour of day, day of week, AND season to determine staffing, equipment, and capacity requirements. Point-of-sale automation makes detailed tracking feasible.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the coefficient of determination (R²) in regression?
ANSWER
The square of the coefficient of correlation (r). Represents the percentage of variation in the dependent variable (y) that is explained by the independent variable (x). Example: r = +0.79 → R² = 0.62 → 62% of demand variation is explained by the predictor.

Question 1
Which forecasting method calculates the forecast as the simple average of the N most recent periods?
A.Exponential smoothing
B.Simple moving average
C.Regression analysis
D.Delphi method
A simple moving average averages the most recent N periods. It is easy to calculate but gives equal weight to all periods in the window and reacts slowly to trend changes.
Question 2
In exponential smoothing, the smoothing constant alpha (α) controls:
A.The number of periods included in the moving average
B.How much weight is given to the most recent actual demand versus older history — higher α reacts faster to recent changes
C.The number of seasonal periods in a year
D.The confidence interval around the forecast
Alpha (α) ranges from 0 to 1. High α (close to 1) puts most weight on recent demand — reactive but volatile. Low α (close to 0) smooths more — stable but slow to react to genuine demand shifts.
Question 3
Which method is most appropriate for forecasting a product with a strong seasonal pattern and an upward trend?
A.Simple moving average
B.Simple exponential smoothing
C.Holt-Winters (triple exponential smoothing) — which captures level, trend, AND seasonality
D.Linear regression alone
Holt-Winters (triple exponential smoothing) has three smoothing equations: one for the level, one for the trend, and one for the seasonal index — making it ideal when both trend and seasonality are present.
Question 4
A 'causal forecasting' model (e.g., linear regression) predicts demand based on:
A.Historical demand patterns only
B.The relationship between demand and one or more independent variables (e.g., GDP, advertising spend, price)
C.The average of the last 12 months of sales
D.Expert panel consensus opinions
Causal models link demand to explanatory variables — for example, housing starts predicting appliance demand, or advertising spend predicting product demand. They require identifying and validating the causal relationship.
Question 5
What is the Delphi method?
A.A statistical time-series forecasting algorithm
B.A qualitative method where a facilitator collects and anonymously aggregates opinions from a panel of experts through iterative rounds until consensus emerges
C.A technique for calculating the moving average of demand
D.A simulation approach for supply chain scenario planning
The Delphi method collects expert opinions anonymously (to avoid groupthink), shares aggregated results, and repeats until consensus is reached. Used for long-range or new-product forecasts where data is absent.
Question 6
When is a 'focus forecasting' approach useful?
A.When the product has a very stable and predictable demand pattern
B.When trying multiple simple forecasting methods on recent data and selecting the one that performed best in the recent past
C.When only qualitative inputs are available
D.When the forecast horizon exceeds 5 years
Focus forecasting tests multiple simple methods against recent actuals and uses whichever method performed best recently. It adapts automatically to changing demand environments without manual model selection.
Question 7
Which time series component represents demand changes tied to the calendar (monthly, quarterly, or annual)?
A.Trend
B.Cyclical
C.Seasonal
D.Random
The seasonal component reflects demand changes that repeat on a calendar cycle — holiday peaks, summer buying patterns, school-year effects. Seasonal indices quantify the deviation from the average by period.
Question 8
What is 'disaggregation' in forecasting?
A.Removing outliers from the historical data
B.Breaking a high-level aggregate forecast (e.g., product family) down to detailed SKU/location level forecasts using historical ratios or profiles
C.Combining all product forecasts into a single company-wide total
D.Adjusting the forecast for known supply constraints
Disaggregation converts the aggregate forecast to the item/location level needed for operational planning. Ratios from historical data (e.g., SKU A = 30% of family demand) are applied to allocate the aggregate forecast.
Question 9
A new product forecast is typically most challenging because:
A.New products are always expensive to manufacture
B.There is no historical demand data, so time-series methods cannot be used — the forecast must rely on analogies with similar products, market research, or management judgment
C.Regulatory approval requirements make forecasting unnecessary
D.Supply chain systems cannot handle new product codes
New products have no demand history, making statistical forecasting impossible. Analogous product analysis (how did similar products sell in their first year?), market research, and management judgment are the primary inputs.
Question 10
Which forecasting method is most appropriate when demand is driven by a known upcoming event (e.g., a sports championship)?
A.Time series exponential smoothing
B.Event-based or causal forecasting that explicitly models the demand impact of the event
C.Simple moving average over 12 months
D.Delphi expert panel method
Event-driven demand (concerts, sporting events, weather events) is best forecast by modeling the event's expected impact directly — either through causal regression or by overlaying event-specific adjustments on the statistical baseline.
Question 11
What is the key advantage of exponential smoothing over a simple moving average?
A.It is computationally simpler to calculate
B.It gives more weight to recent observations, making it more responsive to recent demand changes, while still using all historical data
C.It requires no software to implement
D.It eliminates random variation in the demand data
Exponential smoothing weights all historical data but with exponentially declining weights — recent periods have more influence. This makes it more responsive than a simple moving average while using all available history.
Question 12
'Collaborative forecasting' means:
A.Using computer algorithms to automatically generate the best statistical forecast
B.Trading partners jointly develop a shared demand forecast, combining internal data with external market intelligence from customers or suppliers
C.The finance team provides the forecast to operations
D.Sales teams forecast independently for each product category
In collaborative forecasting (as in CPFR), the manufacturer and retailer share forecasts, align on promotional plans, and resolve exceptions — producing a single, more accurate forecast than either could produce alone.
