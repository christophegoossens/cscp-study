Overview

Supply chain maturity refers to the level of integration and coordination with supply partners relative to competing supply chains. Organizations that want to create value must assess and develop their supply chain maturity. Maturity evolves through five stages as technology and organizational culture advance.

The 5 Stages of Supply Chain Evolution

Experts agree on five typical stages of development. An organization may be sheltered from some changes depending on its industry:

  • Stage 1 — Multiple Dysfunction
  • Stage 2 — Semifunctional Enterprise
  • Stage 3 — Integrated Enterprise
  • Stage 4 — Extended Enterprise
  • Stage 5 — Orchestrated Supply Chain
💡 Key Point: Early-stage supply chains are stable with predictable supply/demand, low costs, and minimal technology needs. As maturity increases, focus shifts from internal efficiency to cross-company collaboration and digital integration.
Stage 1 — Multiple Dysfunction

A reactive supply chain that fulfills demand without concern for costs. Perceived as a cost center.

  • Activities are ad hoc rather than planned; management direction is vague.
  • Forecasting is guesswork, often inflated by marketing optimism.
  • Products designed without input from manufacturing or marketing.
  • Warehouses overstocked; trucks loaded/unloaded reactively with no advance planning.
  • Material Requirements Planning (MRP) at basic level only: bill of materials, master schedule, on-hand/on-order data.
  • Purchasing is ad hoc and transactional — no consistent preferred suppliers.
Exhibit 1-4: Multiple Dysfunction
Stage 2 — Semifunctional Enterprise

A reactive efficient supply chain. Functional areas are defined but each department operates in isolation (silos). Supports competitive positioning as an efficient, low-cost unit.

  • Focuses on total delivered cost of finished goods.
  • Invests in connectivity technology and automation to reduce labor costs and improve throughput.
  • Inventory management reduces stock levels within the company.
  • Procurement pursues lowest-price strategies; traffic department reduces transport costs.
  • Marketing develops more reliable forecasting techniques.
  • Closed-loop MRP software may be in place; some cross-functional planning begins.
Exhibit 1-5: Semifunctional Enterprise
Stage 3 — Integrated Enterprise

A proactive efficient supply chain. The company shifts from individual functions to companywide business processes. Proposes design and material changes to lower complexity and costs.

  • Sales and Operations Planning (S&OP) — replaces silo planning; cross-functional teams periodically align sales, marketing, and production plans.
  • Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) — extends closed-loop MRP to link all functional areas; advanced versions reach through the corporate supply chain.
  • Just-in-Time (JIT) — combined with better demand planning and logistics to make fulfillment more efficient and reliable.
  • Product design becomes cross-functional (production, marketing, purchasing, and design engineers collaborate).
  • Improved customer service through better market segmentation and replenishment policies per segment.
  • The nucleus company may begin contracting external logistics providers (e.g., UPS, FedEx).
Exhibit 1-6: Integrated Enterprise
Stage 4 — Extended Enterprise

A strategic driver supply chain. The nucleus company extends at least one business process beyond its own boundaries to collaborate with partners. The supply chain actively contributes to corporate strategy.

  • Demand generation and fulfillment are fully integrated.
  • Forecasting, planning, and replenishment are fully integrated and visible across partners.
  • Partners synchronize ERP systems across corporate boundaries to share data.
  • Collaborative Planning, Forecasting, and Replenishment (CPFR) — cross-organizational process enabling synchronized planning with partners.
  • E-commerce advances: customers can order, track shipments, and communicate with customer service online.
  • Technological improvements, knowledge, and real-time information are shared with chain partners.
Exhibit 1-7: Extended Enterprise
Stage 5 — Orchestrated Supply Chain

Also called supply chain digital transformation or (in Europe) Industrie 4.0. A consistent and systematic supply chain.

  • Improvement teams are cross-functional, technically and socially skilled.
  • Change management is embedded in organizational culture.
  • Data has integrity, is secure, and drives all decision making.
  • End-to-end visibility is achieved; technology gaps are resolved.
  • Sourcing is right-sized and resilient (quickly adaptable).
  • Supply chain inventory and asset data is truly real-time, feeding into predictive analytics (e.g., predictive maintenance scheduling).
  • Processes are shared, policies enforced, and automation is common.
💡 Key Point: Stage 5 goes beyond digitization (converting documents to digital) or digitalization (automated sensors triggering responses). It is a full transformation where real-time data reshapes product development, sourcing, and operations across the entire network.
Exhibit 1-8: Orchestrated Supply Chain
Maturity as an Input to Strategy
  • Higher-stage supply chains focus more on creating net value than on minimizing costs.
  • The goal: identify gaps preventing full connectivity and visibility, then build a roadmap to close them.
  • Supply chain maturity must be assessed before setting strategy — gaps determine priorities.
Supply Chain Examples

Manufacturing Supply Chain Model

Shows two tiers of suppliers, a manufacturer, distribution centers (DCs), and customers. The nucleus firm may be a designer or brand owner rather than a manufacturer. Tier 1 suppliers have their own Tier 2 suppliers — even raw material extractors have suppliers of their own.

Exhibit 1-9: Manufacturing Supply Chain Model

Service Industry Supply Chain

Service Industry Company: 1) In its narrowest sense, an organization that provides an intangible product (e.g., medical or legal advice). 2) In its broadest sense, all organizations except farming, mining, and manufacturing.

Service-oriented supply chains also require sophisticated management. Example: an electric utility receives products, services, and raw materials and delivers electricity to customers.

Exhibit 1-10: Electric Utility Supply Chain

Specialized Supply Chains

  • Humanitarian/disaster relief — must be built quickly and be resilient against theft, misappropriation, and transport disruptions.
  • Hospitals — cost cutting is vital, but safe and effective patient care is always the primary goal; visibility and traceability are critical.
  • Retail — under pressure to compete with online retailers (e.g., Amazon); must redesign supply chains for speed, cost, and omnichannel fulfillment.

What does supply chain maturity refer to?

What are the 5 stages of supply chain evolution?
ANSWER
Stage 1: Multiple Dysfunction — Stage 2: Semifunctional Enterprise — Stage 3: Integrated Enterprise — Stage 4: Extended Enterprise — Stage 5: Orchestrated Supply Chain.
TERM / CONCEPT
Describe Stage 1: Multiple Dysfunction.
ANSWER
A reactive supply chain that fulfills demand without concern for costs. Activities are ad hoc, forecasting is guesswork, purchasing is transactional, and there is no cross-functional planning. Perceived as a cost center.
TERM / CONCEPT
Describe Stage 2: Semifunctional Enterprise.
ANSWER
A reactive efficient supply chain where functional areas are defined but operate in silos. Each department improves independently — inventory, procurement, transport, forecasting — with little cross-functional overlap.
TERM / CONCEPT
Describe Stage 3: Integrated Enterprise.
ANSWER
A proactive efficient supply chain using S&OP, ERP, and JIT. Cross-functional product design and improved customer segmentation. May begin contracting external logistics providers.

Describe Stage 4: Extended Enterprise.
ANSWER
A strategic driver supply chain extending processes beyond company boundaries. Partners synchronize ERP systems, use CPFR, and share real-time information. Supply chain actively drives corporate strategy.
TERM / CONCEPT
Describe Stage 5: Orchestrated Supply Chain.
ANSWER
A consistent and systematic supply chain (also called digital transformation or Industrie 4.0). Real-time data drives all decisions, end-to-end visibility is achieved, and change management is embedded in culture.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is CPFR and in which stage does it appear?
ANSWER
Collaborative Planning, Forecasting, and Replenishment (CPFR) — a cross-organizational process enabling synchronized planning with supply chain partners. Appears in Stage 4: Extended Enterprise.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between digitization, digitalization, and digital transformation?
ANSWER
Digitization = converting documents to digital format. Digitalization = automated sensors triggering responses. Digital transformation (Stage 5) = real-time data reshaping entire operations and strategy across the network.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is S&OP and when does it first appear?
ANSWER
Sales and Operations Planning (S&OP) — periodic cross-functional meetings where sales, marketing, and production align plans. First appears in Stage 3: Integrated Enterprise, replacing silo-based planning.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is ERP and how does it relate to MRP?
ANSWER
Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) extends closed-loop MRP by adding modules for each functional area and crossing corporate boundaries. MRP appears in Stage 1; closed-loop MRP in Stage 2; ERP in Stage 3.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Just-in-Time (JIT) and when does it emerge?
ANSWER
JIT is an approach aimed at minimizing inventory by receiving goods only when needed. Emerges in Stage 3 alongside better demand planning and logistics.
TERM / CONCEPT
ASCM Definition: Service Industry Company

1) In its narrowest sense, an organization that provides an intangible product (e.g., medical or legal advice). 2) In its broadest sense, all organizations except farming, mining, and manufacturing.

What is the goal of assessing supply chain maturity?
ANSWER
To identify gaps preventing full connectivity and visibility, then build a roadmap to close those gaps and advance toward greater maturity and net value creation.
TERM / CONCEPT
What makes Stage 5 different from Stage 4?
ANSWER
Stage 5 features truly real-time data feeding predictive analytics, full end-to-end visibility, embedded change management culture, and cross-functional teams — not just bilateral partner collaboration.
TERM / CONCEPT
What unique challenges do humanitarian supply chains face?
ANSWER
Must be built quickly and be resilient against theft, misappropriation, and transportation disruptions.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Industrie 4.0?
ANSWER
The European term for Stage 5 (Orchestrated Supply Chain / digital transformation). Refers to the fourth industrial revolution: fully integrated, automated, and data-driven operations.
TERM / CONCEPT
What type of supply chain does Stage 3 represent, and why?
ANSWER
Proactive efficient — it proposes new materials or product designs to lower complexity and costs, and uses integrated information systems (ERP, S&OP) to facilitate cross-functional sharing.
TERM / CONCEPT
What type of supply chain does Stage 2 represent, and why?
ANSWER
Reactive efficient — supports competitive positioning by serving as an efficient, low-cost unit focused on total delivered cost of finished goods, but without cross-functional integration.
TERM / CONCEPT
In which stage does CPFR first appear, and what does it enable?
ANSWER
Stage 4 (Extended Enterprise). CPFR enables synchronized planning across corporate boundaries, aligning forecasts and replenishment plans between the nucleus company and its supply chain partners.

B.Stage 2: Internal integration
C.Stage 3: External integration / collaboration

External integration (stage 3 in most models) involves sharing information and coordinating plans with upstream suppliers and downstream customers, going beyond internal cross-functional integration.
Question 5

B.A single integrated S&OP process aligning sales, operations, and finance internally

Internal integration means functions within the company — sales, operations, procurement, finance — work from a single set of shared data and plans (e.g., S&OP).

The Gartner Top 25 uses a composite score of return on assets, inventory turns, revenue growth, and peer/analyst opinion to identify supply chain leaders.
Question 7
Which of the following is a characteristic of a 'demand-driven' supply chain at high maturity?
A.Orders are batched monthly and sent to suppliers
B.Production and replenishment are triggered by real-time customer demand signals
C.Inventory is pushed into the channel based on quarterly forecasts
D.All decisions are made by a central planning team
Demand-driven supply chains use actual demand data (point-of-sale, consumption) to trigger replenishment, reducing the bullwhip effect and inventory waste.
Question 8
What is a key risk of remaining at a low supply chain maturity level?
A.Too much collaboration with suppliers that reduces bargaining power
B.Sub-optimal total supply chain cost and poor responsiveness to market changes
C.Over-investment in technology that is not needed
D.Excessive information sharing with competitors
Low maturity (silos, poor integration) leads to higher total cost (local optimization vs. system optimization), slower response to demand changes, and vulnerability to disruptions.
Question 9
Which term describes the practice of companies working together to jointly plan, forecast, and replenish inventory?
A.Vertical integration
B.CPFR — Collaborative Planning, Forecasting, and Replenishment
C.Master data management
D.S&OP (Sales and Operations Planning)
CPFR is a structured collaboration process where trading partners jointly develop a single agreed demand forecast and use it to drive replenishment decisions.
Question 10
At the highest level of supply chain maturity, what is the primary focus?
A.Achieving lowest unit manufacturing cost
B.Orchestrating the entire supply network for maximum responsiveness, sustainability, and customer value
C.Reducing the number of suppliers in each category
D.Automating all repetitive tasks within the company
The highest maturity level involves end-to-end network visibility and orchestration — managing the entire ecosystem of partners to dynamically optimize for cost, service, and sustainability.
Question 11

A.Dividing the workforce into specialized teams
B.Designing different supply chain strategies for different product/customer segments based on their distinct needs
C.Separating domestic and international supply chains
D.Splitting the S&OP process into monthly and annual cycles
Supply chain segmentation (also called multi-channel or differentiated SC strategy) recognizes that one supply chain does not fit all products or customers — different segments require different supply chain designs.
Question 12

A.Reduced number of SKUs in the product range
B.Elimination of all safety stock

Internal integration produces a single set of shared data and plans — a common forecast, shared inventory visibility, and aligned objectives across functions — reducing internal conflicts and improving total cost.
