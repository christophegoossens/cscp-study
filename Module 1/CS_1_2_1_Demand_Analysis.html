Overview

Demand analysis looks at the organization in relation to its external environment. It can be done over the long, medium, or short term, using tools such as SWOT analysis, market research, and product assessments. The goal is to develop strategies flexible enough to respond quickly to a changing environment.

Macro Environment: the environment external to a business including technological, economic, natural, and regulatory forces that marketing efforts cannot control.
💡 Key Point: At the strategic (long-term) level, demand can only be understood and reacted to — not controlled. At the tactical and operational levels, demand can be influenced (prices, promotions) or managed (prioritized).
6 Key Processes for Procuring and Delivering Goods and Services
  • Developing supply chain personnel knowledge, skills, and abilities — goal setting, coaching, training, certifications.
  • Developing supply chain infrastructure — design, make-vs-buy analysis, staffing, change management; for buy: supplier/customer contracts and relationship management.
  • Performing supply and demand planning and scheduling — demand management, forecasting, S&OP, master scheduling, MRP, DRP, capacity planning.
  • Identifying logistical requirements — warehousing, materials handling, transportation, and logistics objectives.
  • Developing logistical capabilities — internal logistics (projects, staffing, training) or outsourced logistics (selecting providers, contracts).
  • Executing the plans — production activity control, capacity control, inventory control, monitoring transportation and third-party providers, regulatory compliance.
SWOT Analysis

SWOT (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats) is used for strategic planning and long-term demand analysis. The analysis is a quadrant distinguishing internal vs. external focus and positive vs. negative factors.

  • Strengths & Weaknesses (internal) — derived from data on skills, facilities, reputation. Customer and supplier input substantiates weaknesses.
  • Opportunities & Threats (external) — based on market trends and risk analyses; requires environmental scanning (market forces, demographics, competition, technology, regulations).
💡 Key Point: Opportunities ignored or poorly developed can become threats (e.g., IBM letting Bill Gates market DOS). Threats are external risks that can diminish productivity or market share if unaddressed (e.g., Ever Given blocking the Suez Canal in 2021).

Market Research & the Market Plan
Market Plan: the current market position, opportunity and issue analysis (SWOT results), marketing objectives and strategies, action plans, programs, projects, budgets, and pro forma profit and loss statement and management controls.

The market plan is a subset of the organization's strategic business plan. It drives finance, engineering, marketing, and production. The marketing strategy (shown in Exhibit 1-12) is based on market position, demand patterns, customer satisfaction, pricing, and service level agreements.

💡 Key Point: Marketing translates the external perspective for internal audiences ("What does the market need?"). Sales translates the internal perspective for external audiences ("Why do you need what we offer?").
Exhibit 1-12: Marketing Strategy and Plan

Market research is "the systematic gathering, recording, and analysis of data about problems relating to the marketing of goods and services." It can be done by impartial agencies, internal staff, or market research agents.

Types of Market Research
  • Market analysis — study of the size, location, nature, and characteristics of markets; results in market segmentation (distinct lines of business).
  • Sales analysis — systematic study and comparison of sales data and market share.
  • Consumer research — discovery and analysis of consumer attitudes, reactions, and preferences; results in customer segmentation.
Market Share: the actual portion of current market demand that a company or product achieves.
Purposes of Market Research
  • Finding potential markets — answering "Does anyone care?" Quantify unmet needs identified by sales or engineering.
  • Analyzing markets — segment the market (Who? Where? When? Why? What? How many?). Determine best channels, timing of purchase, and product life cycle expectations.
  • Refining product design — incorporate customer features into design. Limit SKU proliferation (too many SKUs increases cost and dilutes marketing). Consider reverse supply chain: support, documentation, return policy.
💡 Key Point: Marketing has a natural bias toward optimism in demand forecasting (magnified by top management). Overly optimistic plans cause excess capacity investment or inventory hangover. Cross-functional collaboration and CPFR (Collaborative Planning, Forecasting, and Replenishment) help correct this bias.
Competition & Global Perspectives

Organizations must scan the market for competitor offerings, pricing, and market share. Getting a foothold where competition is entrenched requires a clear competitive advantage.

Global forces reshaping supply chains:

  • Global expansion — longer, more complex chains requiring formal coordination and collaboration.
  • Increased project complexity and scope — larger teams, remote sites, more information to manage.
  • Greater market volatility — demand is harder to predict as information speed increases for both consumers and competitors.
💡 Key Point: Disruption of one link in a globally interconnected chain can impact the whole (e.g., 2011 Japan earthquake/tsunami). Global strategies must account for local customer requirements while maintaining standardized measurements (e.g., SCOR® model: flexibility, velocity, predictability).
Product Assessments — Overview

The product portfolio must be reviewed to determine whether the organization's products and services are still appropriate for the market. The product portfolio is the mix of product classifications, families, products, and services the organization offers.

Exhibit 1-13: Product Portfolio Management
Product Classification Review

Broadest categories: durable goods (long-lasting), non-durable goods (deteriorate quickly), and services (intangible, non-inventoriable).

Next level: industrial vs. consumer goods — each with subcategories and different strategic priorities (speed, dependability, flexibility, quality, cost).

Exhibit 1-14: Product Classifications and Typical Strategic Priorities
Product Portfolio Complexity Management

Involves reviewing the number of stock keeping units (SKUs). More SKUs means more manufacturing variations, higher purchasing/inventory/service costs, and diluted marketing. But too few SKUs stifles revenue because customers don't find what they need.

Exhibit 1-15: Portfolio Complexity Impact

Staying in the "sweet spot" requires:

  • Reviewing complexity separately per product category or family
  • Using holistic metrics covering total cost of ownership and profit (not just revenue or cost alone)
  • Balancing marketing demands (more SKUs) vs. operations/supply chain demands (fewer SKUs)
  • Evaluating modular design to shift the cost curve
Product Life Cycle Review

No product is immune to changes in demand over time. Life cycle stages: Introduction, Growth, Maturity, and Decline.

  • Introduction — low availability, volume, and sales. High marketing spend; rely on qualitative forecasting (no data). Risk: over- or under-estimating demand.
  • Growth — availability and quality are competitive priorities; customer testimonials drive further growth.
  • Maturity — competitive price and dependability minimize defections.
  • Decline — need innovative ways to provide availability at low cost.
Exhibit 1-16: Product Life Cycle and Market Growth versus Market Share
Services Review & Brand Strategy / NPI

Services Review

Determine whether customer perceptions of services (warranties, technical support, repairs, trade-in allowances, training, transportation, bulk discounts) have been changing. When a service reaches saturation (all competitors offer it), it becomes an order qualifier rather than an order winner.

Brand Strategy & New Product Introduction (NPI)

Results from portfolio, life cycle, and services reviews determine how to invest limited marketing resources. NPI requires market analysis, R&D, marketing, and phase-in/phase-out plans.

  • Entirely new products: qualitative forecasting required (no historical data). Risk of over/under supply.
  • Upgraded products: base forecasts on prior model's sales (more reliable).
💡 Key Point: Order qualifier = minimum requirement to compete. Order winner = the differentiating factor that wins the sale. When a service becomes saturated (offered by all competitors), it shifts from order winner to order qualifier.

What does SWOT stand for and what is its purpose?

TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between strengths/weaknesses and opportunities/threats in SWOT?
ANSWER
Strengths and Weaknesses are INTERNAL (skills, facilities, reputation). Opportunities and Threats are EXTERNAL (market trends, competition, regulations, economic conditions).
TERM / CONCEPT
At which level can demand be influenced, and how?
ANSWER
At the tactical level (by changing prices, promotions, customer/employee policies) and operational level (by managing and prioritizing demand). At the strategic level demand can only be understood and reacted to, not controlled.

What is market research?
ANSWER
The systematic gathering, recording, and analysis of data about problems relating to the marketing of goods and services. Done by agencies, internal marketing staff, or research agents.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the three types of market research?
ANSWER
1) Market analysis — size, location, characteristics of markets; results in market segmentation. 2) Sales analysis — comparison of sales data and market share. 3) Consumer research — consumer attitudes and preferences; results in customer segmentation.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the three purposes of market research?
ANSWER
1) Finding potential markets (quantify unmet needs). 2) Analyzing markets (segmentation: Who? Where? When? Why? What? How many?). 3) Refining product design (features, pricing, SKU management, reverse supply chain).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the marketing bias problem in demand forecasting?
ANSWER
Marketing has a natural bias toward optimism, often magnified by management pressure. Overly optimistic forecasts cause excess capacity investment or inventory hangover, and may cause supply areas to distrust the demand plan and create their own competing forecasts.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does CPFR address the marketing bias problem?
ANSWER
Collaborative Planning, Forecasting, and Replenishment (CPFR) shares forecasts with cross-functional and intercompany teams so everyone works from the same information, reducing the impact of optimism bias from any one function.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the three global forces reshaping supply chains?
ANSWER
1) Global expansion — longer, more complex chains requiring formal coordination. 2) Increased project complexity and scope. 3) Greater market volatility — demand harder to predict due to information speed.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is SKU proliferation and why is it a problem?
ANSWER
SKU (stock keeping unit) proliferation is having too many product variants. It increases purchasing, inventory, and service costs at a steadily increasing rate, dilutes marketing messages, and confuses customers.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why can having too FEW SKUs also be a problem?
ANSWER
Too few SKUs stifles revenue and profit because customers don't find the varieties that suit their needs, especially if competitors offer more variety.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the four product life cycle stages?
ANSWER
Introduction, Growth, Maturity, and Decline. Product availability, volume, and sales volume are low in Introduction, increase in Growth, level in Maturity, and low in Decline.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the competitive priority in each life cycle stage?
ANSWER
Introduction: availability (qualitative forecasting). Growth: availability and quality (testimonials drive growth). Maturity: competitive price and dependability. Decline: innovative ways to provide availability at low cost.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is product saturation?
ANSWER
When a service or feature reaches maturity because all competitors offer it. At that point it becomes an ORDER QUALIFIER (minimum to compete) rather than an ORDER WINNER (what wins the sale).
TERM / CONCEPT
Distinguish: order qualifier vs. order winner.
ANSWER
Order qualifier = minimum requirement a product/service must have to be considered by the customer. Order winner = the competitive attribute that wins the sale over competitors.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is New Product Introduction (NPI)?
ANSWER
NPI attempts to produce new demand or build on previous demand by focusing on new product features that differentiate from past models or the competition. Requires market analysis, R&D, marketing, and phase-in/phase-out plans.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is demand estimation especially problematic during NPI?
ANSWER
Entirely new products have no historical data. The organization must rely on qualitative forecasting methods. Over-estimating demand leads to excess supply and capital losses; under-estimating leads to inadequate capacity.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between durable and non-durable goods?
ANSWER
Durable goods are expected to last for an extended time period. Non-durable goods deteriorate quickly and may need to be consumed quickly. Both have physical substance; services are intangible and non-inventoriable.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is product portfolio complexity management?
ANSWER
Reviewing and managing the number of SKUs to stay in a 'sweet spot' — enough variety to drive revenue without the cost increases and marketing dilution that come from too many SKUs.
TERM / CONCEPT
What tools are used in demand analysis at each planning horizon?
ANSWER
Long-term (strategic): SWOT analysis, environmental scanning. Medium-term (tactical): market research, demand influence (pricing, promotions). Short-term (operational): demand management and prioritization.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the SCOR® model and how is it used?
ANSWER
SCOR (Supply Chain Operations Reference) is a framework for supply chain management. Used to standardize measurements — flexibility, velocity, predictability — so organizations can compare performance across regions.

What does 'customer segmentation' enable in supply chain demand management?
A.Reducing the total number of customers served
B.Differentiating service levels and supply chain strategies based on distinct customer groups
C.Eliminating low-volume customers from the supply chain

Customer segmentation groups customers by similar needs, behaviors, or value, allowing the company to tailor service offerings and supply chain strategies to each segment.
Question 2
Which type of demand represents replenishment orders placed by a distribution center, not by the end consumer?
A.Independent demand
B.Dependent demand
C.Derived demand
D.Latent demand
Derived demand (also called dependent or downstream demand) originates from demand further down the supply chain. A DC's order is derived from the end consumer's purchase.
Question 3
'Independent demand' in a supply chain context refers to demand that is:
A.Driven by a parent item's production schedule
B.Sourced from customer orders or forecasts and not directly tied to another item's demand
C.Shared across multiple product lines
D.Fully seasonal and predictable
Independent demand comes directly from customers (external demand) and is not mathematically derived from another item's demand. It must be forecasted. Examples: finished goods, service parts.
Question 4
A company's most profitable customer segment should typically receive:
A.Standard service at list price
B.A differentiated, higher-touch service level aligned with their strategic value
C.Reduced lead times at additional cost
D.The same service level as all other customers
Customers with the highest strategic or financial value should receive service levels commensurate with that value — not necessarily a single standard service level applied to all.
Question 5
What is the purpose of an ABC analysis of customers or products?
A.To alphabetically sort products in the warehouse
B.To prioritize resources and attention by classifying items by their relative importance (A = most important, C = least)
C.To calculate safety stock for each item
D.To determine optimal order quantities
ABC analysis classifies items by value or contribution: A-items are the vital few that deserve the most attention; C-items are the trivial many. This focuses limited resources where they matter most.
Question 6
Which demand type describes a product that consumers want but do not yet know exists?
A.Derived demand
B.Latent demand
C.Seasonal demand
D.Inelastic demand
Latent demand is unfulfilled demand that exists but is not yet expressed — customers would buy if they knew the product existed or if it were available at the right price. Market research helps uncover it.
Question 7
What is 'price elasticity of demand'?
A.The degree to which quality improvements drive higher sales
B.The sensitivity of demand quantity to changes in price — highly elastic demand falls sharply when price rises
C.The relationship between lead time and customer order frequency
D.The variability of demand caused by promotions
Price elasticity measures how much quantity demanded changes in response to a price change. Elastic demand (>1) is very sensitive to price; inelastic demand (<1) is not.
Question 8
A demand signal from a customer's point-of-sale (POS) system is most useful for:
A.Setting annual production capacity
B.Calculating depreciation of warehouse equipment
C.Generating real-time replenishment signals that reflect actual consumption
D.Determining supplier payment terms
POS data represents actual consumer pull — the closest thing to true demand — and is highly valuable for triggering real-time replenishment and improving forecast accuracy.
Question 9
Which term describes demand that is not affected by promotional activities or pricing changes?
A.Elastic demand
B.Inelastic demand
C.Derived demand
D.Latent demand
Inelastic demand does not respond much to price or promotion changes — utility services, medicines, and essential staples often have inelastic demand.
Question 10
The 'Pareto principle' (80/20 rule) applied to supply chain demand analysis suggests:
A.80% of supply chain problems come from 20% of suppliers
B.80% of revenue or demand is typically generated by 20% of products or customers
C.80% of inventory costs come from 20% of warehouse locations
D.80% of transportation costs come from 20% of carriers
The Pareto principle (basis of ABC analysis) states that roughly 80% of effects come from 20% of causes — in SC, ~80% of sales typically come from ~20% of SKUs or customers.
Question 11
Why is understanding demand patterns important for supply chain planning?
A.To justify increasing the size of the planning team
B.To enable accurate forecasting, appropriate inventory positioning, and efficient capacity planning
C.To eliminate all safety stock
D.To negotiate longer payment terms with suppliers
Understanding demand patterns (trend, seasonality, cyclicality, randomness) is the foundation of good forecasting, which in turn drives inventory, capacity, and sourcing decisions.
Question 12
What is 'channel demand' in a multi-tier supply chain?
A.Demand generated exclusively from online retail channels
B.Total demand from all customers at a given tier of the distribution channel
C.Demand created by advertising campaigns
D.The demand a company places on its direct suppliers
Channel demand is the aggregated demand flowing through a particular distribution channel tier — e.g., the combined orders placed by all distributors on a manufacturer.
