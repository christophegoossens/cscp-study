Overview & ASCM Definitions
Demand: a need for a particular product or component. The demand could come from any number of sources (e.g., a customer order or forecast, an interplant requirement, a branch warehouse request for a service part, or the manufacturing of another product).
Demand Management: 1) The function of recognizing all demands for goods and services to support the marketplace. It involves prioritizing demand when supply is lacking. Proper demand management facilitates the planning and use of resources for profitable business results. 2) In marketing: planning, executing, controlling, and monitoring the design, pricing, promotion, and distribution of products and services.
Demand Management Process: a process that weighs both customer demand and a firm's output capabilities and tries to balance the two. Made up of four components: planning demand, communicating demand, influencing demand, and prioritizing demand.
💡 Key Point: Demand management is the art of synchronizing supply and demand plans. It operates at all three planning horizons: long-term (forecasting, capacity development), medium-term (S&OP, aggregate forecasting), and short-term (item-level master scheduling).
4 Components of Demand Management
Exhibit 1-20: Four Components of Demand Management
  • Planning demand — forecasting + moving beyond prediction to what quantities to produce. Output: the consensus demand plan.
  • Communicating demand — sharing demand information internally (supply, finance) and externally (customers, suppliers).
  • Influencing demand — shaping demand via pricing, promotions, and product/brand management.
  • Managing and prioritizing demand — controlling demand when supply is constrained; allocating limited supply to highest-priority customers.

These components are cyclical and iterative. Exhibit 1-21 shows how the cycle can be shortened at the operational level (e.g., using point-of-sale data directly instead of forecasts — shifting from push to demand-pull).

4 Organizational Capacity Strategies
  • Fixed high capacity (planning focus) — meet demand to the maximum extent possible; keep capacity high to avoid stockouts.
  • Highly variable capacity (communicating focus) — match supply to demand; flex capacity up/down rapidly based on communicated demand.
  • Moderately variable capacity (influencing focus) — level production and use promotions/pricing to shift demand to fill capacity.
  • Fixed average capacity (managing/prioritizing focus) — control demand allocation; prioritize customers/orders when supply is constrained.
Planning Demand & the Demand Plan

The demand plan is a consensus forecast — a single, agreed-upon view of future demand used by the entire organization (sales, finance, supply, production). It represents demand both in units and monetary value.

Exhibit 1-22: Demand Plan Inputs

Key inputs: forecasting, product/brand commitments, marketing activities, customer orders, assumptions, and lessons learned.

Uses of the demand plan:

  • Drives production scheduling, procurement, and capacity planning.
  • Provides sales with availability information (types and quantities).
  • Controls and validates departmental plans — holds demand side accountable for inventory consequences.
  • Reveals biased inputs or unrealistic assumptions.

Planning horizon: minimum 18 months, revised monthly (rolling re-planning).

  • Ensures each period is reviewed multiple times with increasing accuracy.
  • Aligns with product/brand management timelines (typically 18+ months).
  • Provides enough lead time to approve and execute capacity changes.
  • By midyear, feeds the next year’s annual business planning process.
Communicating Demand

Effective demand communication reduces bullwhip effect variability by sharing timely, accurate demand information with all supply chain partners.

Order Qualifiers: competitive characteristics that a firm must exhibit to be a viable competitor in the marketplace.
Order Winners: competitive characteristics that cause a firm’s customers to choose that firm’s goods and services over those of its competitors.

3 Principles of Effective Communication (Exhibit 1-23):

Exhibit 1-23: Principles of Effective Communication
  • Communicate soon — timely information has far greater value than delayed information. Bad news is especially important to communicate early.
  • Structure communications — formalize recurring communication flows so they cannot be skipped. Exhibit 1-24 shows required two-way communications in the demand planning process.
  • Focus communications — each person receives only what they need to make informed decisions (no overload, no gaps).

Demand Manager & Demand Dashboards

The demand manager is a full-time role that serves as the focal point for all demand communications. Responsibilities:

  • Gather demand volume and timing data by product, family, and/or customer segment.
  • Perform analytical work on the demand plan.
  • Build consensus on the demand plan.
  • Communicate demand information to/from all stakeholders (supply, finance, marketing, key customers).
  • May lead the S&OP process by creating demand scenarios.
Exhibit 1-25: Use of Demand Manager as Communications Focal Point

Demand dashboards are software presentations of key performance indicators used to focus communications. Key elements to include:

  • Historical demand (3+ months) with KPIs and metrics.
  • Demand plan for next 18+ months (vs. prior plan and statistical forecast).
  • Assumptions and pricing assumptions.
  • Planned branding, marketing, and promotions activities.
  • Key risks, opportunities, economic trends, and competitor actions.
  • Subtleties, uncertainties, events, and decisions made.

What is the bullwhip effect and how does demand communication counteract it?
ANSWER
The bullwhip effect is amplifying demand variability upstream in the supply chain. Communicating timely, accurate demand data to all partners (via CPFR or similar) reduces the distortion by giving everyone the same demand signal.

TERM / CONCEPT
How can short-term demand management bypass the planning component?
ANSWER
By using point-of-sale data transferred directly to all supply chain partners, replacing short-term forecasting with actual demand data — shifting from a push system to a demand-pull system. Timely communications also reduce the need for managing and prioritizing demand.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is CPFR and how does it relate to demand management?
ANSWER
Collaborative Planning, Forecasting, and Replenishment — a system where organizations and supply chain partners share demand forecasts and plans. It supports both planning demand (collaborative forecasting) and communicating demand (shared information with supply chain partners).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between influencing demand and managing/prioritizing demand?
ANSWER
Influencing demand: shaping demand proactively through pricing, promotions, and product management to make it more favorable. Managing/prioritizing demand: reactive — allocating limited supply when demand exceeds capacity, deciding which customers/orders get served first.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is demand management necessary at all three planning levels?
ANSWER
At strategic level: long-term forecasting, capacity development, product development. At tactical level: aggregate forecasting and S&OP. At operational level: item-level master scheduling. Demand becomes more specific (total revenue → units per SKU) as horizon shortens.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the role of a consensus demand plan in S&OP?
ANSWER
It is the demand-side input to the S&OP process — a single agreed-upon view that integrates with finance plans and supply plans. The demand manager often creates multiple demand scenarios for the S&OP meeting.
TERM / CONCEPT
What makes communicating bad news especially important in demand management?
ANSWER
Bad news (e.g., demand shortfall, lost customer, forecast drop) is most useful when communicated early because it allows supply and capacity adjustments before commitments become irreversible. Delayed bad news leads to excess inventory or capacity stranded costs.

Question 2
Which best describes 'demand shaping'?
A.Using mathematical algorithms to improve forecast accuracy
B.Proactively influencing demand through pricing, promotions, and product availability to move it toward supply capabilities
C.Increasing production capacity to meet any level of demand
D.Eliminating seasonal demand peaks through product line changes
Demand shaping uses marketing and pricing levers to shift demand in time or across products — for example, using promotions to pull forward demand during slow periods or discounts on overstocked items.
Question 3
What does 'demand sensing' refer to in modern supply chain planning?
A.Annual market research surveys to understand customer preferences
B.Using high-frequency, short-horizon data (POS, shipments, social media) to detect actual demand trends in near real time
C.The process of setting annual sales targets
D.Monitoring competitor pricing to adjust demand forecasts
Demand sensing uses real-time or near-real-time signals (daily POS, syndicated data, social trends) to improve the short-term forecast — usually the first 1–4 weeks — beyond what statistical models alone can achieve.
Question 4
A company that matches its supply capabilities to demand (rather than trying to match supply to any level of demand) is practicing:
A.Demand fulfillment
B.Demand shaping
C.Supply-driven planning
D.Vendor-managed inventory
Demand shaping deliberately influences demand to fit supply constraints — for example, running promotions on items where inventory is plentiful, or de-emphasizing products where supply is constrained.
Question 5
Collaborative demand planning between a manufacturer and retailer typically involves:
A.The manufacturer setting retail prices unilaterally
B.Sharing point-of-sale data, promotional plans, and jointly developing a single agreed demand forecast
C.The retailer forecasting independently and submitting orders monthly
D.Outsourcing all planning to a 3PL
Collaborative planning (as in CPFR) involves sharing POS data, joint forecasting, and aligning promotional plans so both parties work from the same demand picture rather than independent, often conflicting, forecasts.
Question 6
What is 'available to promise' (ATP)?
A.The maximum production capacity of a facility
B.The quantity of a product that can be committed to a customer without compromising existing customer orders or the master schedule
C.The minimum order quantity a supplier will accept
D.The safety stock level set for a finished good
ATP is the uncommitted portion of inventory or planned production — the quantity a company can promise for delivery to a customer on a given date without affecting other committed orders.
Question 7
Which process is responsible for resolving conflicts between customer demand and available supply capacity?
A.Accounts payable reconciliation
B.Sales and Operations Planning (S&OP)
C.Transportation management
D.Supplier development
S&OP is the monthly business process that balances demand (sales/marketing) with supply (operations/procurement) and resolves conflicts at the aggregate level, producing an approved plan.
Question 8
An effective demand management process should include which of the following?
A.Only historical sales data to drive forecasts
B.Input from sales, marketing, finance, and supply chain — plus external data — to generate the best possible demand plan
C.Forecasts generated exclusively by the IT department
D.Orders only — no forward-looking forecasts
Best-practice demand management is cross-functional: it combines statistical forecasts with market intelligence from sales/marketing, new product plans from R&D, and promotional data to produce the most accurate demand plan.
Question 9
'Order promising' is the process of:
A.Creating a supplier purchase order
B.Committing to a customer on when and how much product will be delivered, using ATP and CTP logic
C.Setting safety stock levels for finished goods
D.Approving customer credit limits
Order promising uses Available to Promise (ATP) or Capable to Promise (CTP) logic to determine and commit delivery dates to customers, balancing their requests against existing plans and capacity.
Question 10
What is 'capable to promise' (CTP)?
A.An ATP check that only looks at current finished goods inventory
B.A check of available raw materials, work in process, and finished goods to determine if an order can be committed — possibly requiring additional capacity
C.A guarantee that any customer order will be fulfilled within 24 hours
D.A supplier's commitment to deliver within lead time
CTP goes beyond ATP by checking the full supply chain (materials, capacity, lead times) to determine whether a new order can be committed, and if so, what delivery date is achievable.
Question 11
Which metric directly measures how well demand management is performing in the customer-facing dimension?
A.Inventory turnover ratio
B.Order fill rate (or perfect order rate)
C.Total landed cost per unit
D.Return on assets
Order fill rate (and the perfect order rate) measures how well the company meets customer orders — the most direct measure of demand management's customer-facing effectiveness.
Question 12
Why is safety stock used in demand management?
A.To reduce the number of SKUs in the product range
B.To buffer against forecast error and supply variability so that service level targets can be met even when demand or supply deviates from plan
C.To eliminate the need for a demand forecast
D.To allow the company to delay production until actual orders are received
Safety stock is held precisely because demand is uncertain and forecasts are imperfect. It protects service levels against the gap between planned and actual demand/supply.
