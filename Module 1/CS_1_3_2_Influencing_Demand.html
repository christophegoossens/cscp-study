PDCA Model for Influencing Demand
PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Action): a four-step process for quality improvement. Plan: develop a plan to effect improvement. Do: carry out the plan (preferably small scale). Check: observe the effects. Action: study results to determine what was learned and what can be predicted.
  • Plan: product/brand management, marketing, and sales research and develop strategies and tactics.
  • Do: execute the plans — launch campaigns, run promotions, adjust pricing.
  • Check: demand manager reviews metrics vs. plan; documents variances and their causes.
  • Action: demand manager leads replanning to respond to variances and address underlying issues.

Demand Generation

Demand generation translates latent demand ideas into actual sales. Critical for new product introductions — most new products fail. Key marketing responsibilities:

  • Educate customers: buyers must know the product exists. Use appropriate media for the audience (print, TV, internet). Know who the actual buyer is (not always obvious).
  • Educate supply chain partners: distributors and retailers need to understand the product to design, source, stock, and sell it effectively.
💡 Key Point: Demand-influencing activities may include convincing customers to accept substitutions or change purchase timing. Influence over marketing and operations is not automatic — especially in cross-company supply chain relationships.
The Four Ps & Demand Shaping
Four Ps: a set of marketing tools to direct the business offering to the customer. The four Ps are product, price, place, and promotion.
Demand Shaping: the use of tactics such as price incentives, advertising, product positioning, product modifications and substitutions, or trade programs to entice customers to purchase.

Customer-focused marketing has shifted focus from product-out to customer-in. Key tools:

  • Product: starts with customer need. Increasingly customizable by segment. Must meet quality expectations and minimize challenges for customer use.
  • Price: strategic decision based on competition, perceived value, and brand identity. In customer-focused model, price and product are tightly linked. Must cover costs, return profit, and remain competitive. Price is often the major demand variable.
  • Placement (distribution): getting the product into the customer’s hands via the right channel. Customer-focused = contact channel strategy. Channels must be: accessible, reliable, complete, secure, direct, convenient, fast, and flexible. From the business side: must allow control/consistency and profitability.
  • Promotion: consumer research, segmentation, advertising, publicity, sampling, and customer relationship management (CRM).
Branding: the use of a name, term, symbol, design, or a combination of these to identify a product.
💡 Key Point: Brand image can take years to build and be destroyed in an instant. Customer loyalty is increasingly transferring from products to brands. Packaging also serves a marketing purpose: commands attention, reinforces features/benefits, and can promote sustainability goals.
Product Life Cycle Stages

Exhibit 1-28 shows 6 stages of the product life cycle (including pre-launch development):

  • Development: incubation stage — no sales, focused on R&D. Costs are high. Product gets designed, sourced, and built for launch.
  • Introduction: sales low until customers become aware. Marketing spend is high. Qualitative forecasting required.
  • Growth: rapid revenue growth. More customers and distributors. Must commit resources to both satisfying market and managing growth.
  • Maturity: most profitable stage. Sales still increase but at a slower rate. Competition intensifies; price and dependability become key.
  • Decline: sales begin to fall. Market becomes saturated, product becomes outdated or is superseded.
Life Cycle Analysis: a quantitative forecasting technique based on applying past patterns of demand data. Covers introduction, growth, maturity, saturation, and decline of similar products to a new product family.
Product Life Cycle Management (PLM)
Product Life Cycle Management: the process of facilitating the development, use, and support of products that customers want and need. Helps professionals envision the creation and preservation of product information, both to the customer and along the reverse-logistics portion of the supply chain.

PLM manages new product introductions, existing product life cycles, and end-of-life activities (phase-out, reverse logistics). PLM software provides tools for the full lifecycle.

Exhibit 1-29: Product Life Cycle Management

NPI schedule phases:

  • Innovation: market research, idea generation.
  • Concept: vision vetted for customer demand and financial promise.
  • Development: engineering, manufacturing, and other functions add detail.
  • Prototyping: working model developed; proves feasibility.
  • Engineering: product structure, bills of material, drawings, tolerances.
  • Supply: supply requirements defined, capable suppliers identified, footprint mapped.
  • Manufacturing: production capability confirmed.
  • Launch: marketing and sales launch product and foster awareness.
💡 Key Point: Innovative products require compressed timelines to shorten time to market. Functional products have low margins, making cost the primary factor.
NPI Frequency vs. Demand Uncertainty

New products range from innovative (high uncertainty, short life cycles) to functional (stable demand, long life cycles). Design and supply chain strategy must align with product type.

Exhibit 1-30: NPI Frequency Versus Demand Uncertainty

4 categories (clockspeed = rate of product change):

  • Unstable demand, low clockspeed: long life cycles, integral design. Can use make-to-stock with safety stock.
  • Unstable demand, high clockspeed: short life cycles, high rate of change. Use pull manufacturing; agile/lean supply chains.
  • Stable demand, low clockspeed: staple items, low margins. Produce economically and efficiently; focus on cost.
  • Stable demand, high clockspeed: few finished goods but many component types fit here. High volume, lean manufacturing.

TERM / CONCEPT
What is clockspeed in the context of NPI?
ANSWER
The rate of product change — how quickly products in a category are replaced by new versions. High clockspeed = short life cycles and frequent new product introductions. Low clockspeed = long-lived products with infrequent redesigns.
TERM / CONCEPT
How has customer-focused marketing changed the Four Ps?
ANSWER
Shifted from product-out (we make it, then sell it) to customer-in (customer need drives product design). Product starts with customer need; placement uses a contact channel strategy; price and product are tightly linked; promotion uses CRM to build individualized relationships.
TERM / CONCEPT
What criteria must a customer-facing channel meet (from the customer perspective)?
ANSWER
Accessible, reliable, complete, secure and error-free, direct (decision authority), convenient, fast, and flexible (accommodates non-native speakers, non-computer users, etc.).
TERM / CONCEPT
What criteria must a channel meet from the business perspective?
ANSWER
Control and consistency (promotes intended values), and profitability (minimizes expensive human resources via automation where possible).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the role of packaging in demand influencing?
ANSWER
Packaging serves a marketing purpose: commands attention, reinforces features and benefits, differentiates on the shelf, and can promote organizational goals such as sustainability. Services also have 'packaging' (presentation, environment design, uniforms).
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is brand image described as fragile?
ANSWER
Brand image takes years to build but can be destroyed in an instant by a scandal, quality failure, or public relations crisis. Organizations take great pains to protect brand image because it represents a significant portion of market value.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between demand shaping and demand prioritization?
ANSWER
Demand shaping (influencing): proactively using pricing, promotions, and positioning to shift demand toward what the organization can profitably supply. Demand prioritization: reactive — allocating limited supply when constrained, deciding which customers/orders get served.

Question 1
Which of the following is a 'demand influencing' strategy available to supply chain managers?
A.Building a new distribution center to increase capacity
B.Offering promotional pricing to stimulate demand for slow-moving items
C.Hiring more production workers to increase output
D.Renegotiating supplier contracts to reduce material costs
Demand influencing uses marketing and pricing levers — promotions, discounts, advertising — to shift demand in time or volume. It is a pull on the demand side, not a push on the supply side.
Question 2
What is the 'marketing mix' (4Ps)?
A.Product, Plant, Personnel, Procurement
B.Product, Price, Place, Promotion — the four levers used to influence consumer demand
C.Plan, Purchase, Produce, Place
D.Product, Process, People, Performance
The 4Ps (Product, Price, Place, Promotion) are the classic marketing mix levers that shape demand. Supply chain managers must understand these because they directly affect demand patterns and supply chain requirements.
Question 3
How do promotions typically affect a supply chain?
A.They smooth demand and reduce inventory requirements
B.They create demand spikes that require surge capacity and often lead to post-promotion demand troughs
C.They have no impact on upstream supply chain planning
D.They only affect retail shelf placement, not inventory
Promotions cause demand spikes (customers buying ahead) and subsequent troughs. This creates the 'forward buying' problem — a major driver of the bullwhip effect and one of the biggest challenges in demand planning.
Question 4
What is 'revenue management' in a supply chain context?
A.Maximizing supplier payments through extended payment terms
B.Using pricing and availability controls (e.g., dynamic pricing) to maximize revenue from a fixed or perishable capacity
C.Calculating total supply chain cost
D.Managing accounts receivable from customers
Revenue management (yield management) uses pricing, availability, and timing to optimize revenue from a constrained resource — airlines, hotels, and even retailers use it to fill capacity at maximum revenue.
Question 5
A company shifts demand from a stock-out product to an available substitute by offering a discount on the substitute. This is an example of:
A.Supply allocation
B.Demand substitution or demand shaping
C.Inventory pooling
D.Order promising
Offering a discount on a substitute product to steer customers away from an out-of-stock item is demand shaping — proactively influencing what customers buy to match available supply.
Question 6
What is 'new product introduction' (NPI) planning important for in demand management?
A.It has no impact on supply chain planning since new products start with zero sales
B.It creates demand ramping requirements (materials, capacity, inventory build) that must be anticipated in the supply chain plan
C.It only affects marketing budgets, not supply chain operations
D.It automatically cancels demand for old products
NPI launches create complex supply chain requirements: initial inventory builds, phase-in of new components, phase-out of old products, and uncertain demand forecasts — all must be planned proactively.
Question 7
Which approach helps reduce artificial demand variability caused by trade promotions?
A.Increasing safety stock levels across the supply chain
B.Shifting from trade promotions to everyday low pricing (EDLP) strategies
C.Using quarterly instead of monthly S&OP cycles
D.Reducing supplier lead times
EDLP (Everyday Low Pricing, as used by Walmart) eliminates forward buying and demand spikes caused by periodic promotions, resulting in smoother, more predictable demand patterns.
Question 8
'Channel stuffing' is a problematic practice that involves:
A.Placing too many products on a retail shelf
B.Pushing excessive inventory into distribution channels to artificially inflate reported sales
C.Using too many distribution tiers between manufacturer and consumer
D.Overstocking safety stock at all levels of the supply chain
Channel stuffing is the unethical (and often illegal) practice of shipping product to distributors or retailers beyond what they can sell, inflating short-term revenue while creating excess inventory problems downstream.
Question 9
What is the role of 'trade promotion management' (TPM) in demand management?
A.Managing import/export duties on traded goods
B.Planning, executing, and evaluating promotional spending to ensure promotions deliver ROI and are factored into the demand plan
C.Negotiating supplier contracts for traded commodities
D.Managing the company's foreign exchange exposure
TPM systems help companies plan promotional events, estimate their demand impact, share plans with supply chain, and measure actual ROI — improving both demand accuracy and marketing effectiveness.
Question 10
Price elasticity is an important concept for demand management because it helps predict:
A.How much inventory is needed at each distribution center
B.How much demand will change in response to a price increase or decrease
C.How quickly suppliers can respond to purchase order changes
D.The lead time required to fulfill a customer order
Knowing price elasticity helps demand managers predict the demand impact of planned price changes or promotions — essential input for the demand plan and inventory decisions.
Question 11
What is the key risk of using heavy promotions as the primary demand influencing tool?
A.Promotions always reduce long-term brand value
B.Promotions create demand spikes/troughs, forward buying, and supply chain volatility — potentially costing more in supply chain disruption than the promotion revenue generates
C.Promotions are illegal in most markets
D.Promotions only work for low-cost products
Heavy promotional activity creates the forward buying problem — customers buy large quantities during promotions and then stop buying, creating supply chain volatility that can cost more to absorb than the promotional revenue is worth.
Question 12
How does 'everyday low pricing' (EDLP) benefit supply chain management?
A.It increases margins for all products across the board
B.It smooths demand by eliminating periodic promotional spikes, reducing forecasting error and inventory requirements
C.It eliminates the need for safety stock entirely
D.It allows companies to charge different prices to different customers
EDLP produces stable, predictable demand by eliminating buy-on-deal behavior. Walmart's adoption of EDLP is a classic example of a supply chain-friendly pricing strategy that reduces the bullwhip effect.
