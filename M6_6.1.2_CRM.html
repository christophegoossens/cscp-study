CRM Systems and Capabilities
Customer Relationship Management (CRM) system: Technology that centralizes customer data — contact information, interaction history, purchase records, service issues, and preferences — enabling sales, marketing, and customer service teams to provide consistent, informed service.
CRM supports supply chain by providing demand signals: order history, contract terms, and customer behavior patterns inform demand forecasting and inventory planning.
Order management system (OMS): Software that manages the entire order lifecycle — from customer order placement through fulfillment and delivery confirmation. Interfaces with CRM (customer data), WMS (warehouse execution), and TMS (transportation).
Customer portals: Web-based interfaces allowing customers to place orders, track shipments, view invoices, and manage returns — reducing order management cost and improving customer self-service. A form of supply chain collaboration.
Key Account Management
Key Account Management (KAM): A dedicated approach to managing the most important customer relationships — assigning a dedicated team, providing tailored services, and building deep integration with the customer's operations.
Key account characteristics: high revenue, high strategic importance, high switching cost (deeply embedded in their operations), and high potential for growth. Key accounts often receive customized supply chain services.
Joint planning: Collaborative supply chain planning between a supplier and its key customer — sharing forecasts, inventory data, and capacity information to improve supply chain efficiency for both parties. Reduces the Bullwhip Effect.
Vendor-Managed Inventory (VMI): The supplier is responsible for monitoring and replenishing the customer's inventory — using shared inventory and sales data. Reduces customer ordering burden, improves supply continuity, and gives the supplier better demand signal visibility.
Customer Collaboration Models
CPFR (Collaborative Planning, Forecasting, and Replenishment): A collaborative supply chain framework where trading partners jointly develop forecasts and replenishment plans — sharing data and aligning on a single, agreed forecast. Reduces forecast error and inventory while improving service.
CPFR reduces the Bullwhip Effect by replacing independent, redundant forecasting with shared information — one data source flowing through the supply chain rather than each tier adding its own forecast error.
EDI (Electronic Data Interchange): Computer-to-computer exchange of business documents (purchase orders, invoices, advance ship notices) in a standardized format — enabling automated, paperless transactions between trading partners.
Collaboration maturity ladder: Transactional (buy/sell, no sharing) → Information sharing (share data) → Coordinated (align plans) → Synchronized (joint decision-making, CPFR). Higher maturity = lower total supply chain cost but more investment in relationship and systems.

TERM / CONCEPT
What is VMI (Vendor-Managed Inventory)?
ANSWER
Supplier manages the customer's inventory replenishment: • Supplier monitors customer inventory levels (via shared data) • Supplier decides when and how much to replenish • Customer doesn't place orders — supplier ships proactively Benefits for customer: reduced ordering cost, better in-stock, no stockouts from ordering delays. Benefits for supplier: sees real consumption data (not orders distorted by Bullwhip Effect), can plan production better, stronger customer relationship. Requires: data sharing, trust, agreed inventory targets.
TERM / CONCEPT
CPFR — what is it and how does it reduce the Bullwhip Effect?
ANSWER
Collaborative Planning, Forecasting, and Replenishment: trading partners share data and jointly develop a single agreed forecast and replenishment plan. How it reduces Bullwhip: instead of each supply chain tier making an independent forecast (adding error at each step), partners use ONE shared forecast. Demand signal is not distorted as it passes through the chain. Result: lower forecast error → lower safety stock → lower total inventory → lower cost, with maintained or improved service levels.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is EDI?
ANSWER
Electronic Data Interchange: computer-to-computer exchange of standard business documents between trading partners. Common EDI transactions: • 850: Purchase Order • 856: Advance Ship Notice (ASN) • 810: Invoice • 940/945: Warehouse shipping orders Benefits: eliminates manual data entry (cost, errors), speeds order cycle (orders processed automatically), required by many large retailers (Walmart, Target). Modern alternative: API-based integration (faster, more flexible than EDI, but same principle).
TERM / CONCEPT
Key Account Management (KAM) — what makes it different from standard sales?
ANSWER
KAM: dedicated team managing your most strategically important customers. Differences from standard sales: • Dedicated resources (account manager, supply chain team, technical support) • Joint business planning (shared annual plan with customer) • Customized supply chain services (dedicated inventory, tailored delivery schedules) • Deep operational integration (system connections, joint KPIs) • Long-term partnership focus (not transactional) Why invest in KAM: key accounts generate disproportionate revenue (Pareto 80/20); losing one = major financial impact.
TERM / CONCEPT
Collaboration maturity stages in supply chain
ANSWER
Level 1 — Transactional: buy/sell, no information sharing. High Bullwhip Effect. Level 2 — Information sharing: share demand data, inventory levels. Reduces some distortion. Level 3 — Coordinated: align production/delivery plans. Better synchronization. Level 4 — Synchronized (CPFR): joint forecasting and replenishment. Single shared plan. Lowest Bullwhip, lowest total cost. Higher maturity: requires more trust, data sharing, and system integration — but generates significantly lower total supply chain cost.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does a CRM system support supply chain operations?
ANSWER
CRM centralizes customer data → supply chain uses it for: 1. Demand signals: order history → better forecasting 2. Contract terms: service level commitments → operational targets 3. Customer segmentation: tier customers by value → differentiated service 4. Order management integration: CRM feeds orders to WMS/TMS for fulfillment 5. Issue tracking: service failures → root cause → process improvement CRM connects the commercial relationship (sales, marketing) to the operational execution (supply chain).

Q1 of 5
Vendor-Managed Inventory (VMI) benefits the supplier primarily by:
VMI: supplier monitors customer inventory and replenishes proactively. For the supplier, the key benefit is demand signal visibility — instead of seeing customer purchase orders (inflated/deflated by ordering behavior), the supplier sees actual consumption. This removes one layer of the Bullwhip Effect, enabling more accurate production planning and lower safety stock. The customer benefits from better in-stock and lower ordering burden.
Q2 of 5
CPFR reduces the Bullwhip Effect primarily by:
Bullwhip Effect: each tier's independent forecasting adds error; ordering patterns amplify demand variability up the chain. CPFR solution: partners jointly develop ONE agreed forecast using shared POS data, inventory, and promotion plans. With a single source of truth flowing up the chain, each tier doesn't need to independently forecast and buffer — dramatically reducing forecast error and safety stock at every tier.
Q3 of 5
EDI (Electronic Data Interchange) improves supply chain performance by:
EDI = standardized, automated document exchange (purchase orders, advance ship notices, invoices). Benefits: eliminates manual data entry (cost, error, delay), enables automatic order processing (order placed at supplier system the moment the buyer sends an 850 PO), enables automatic receiving (856 ASN tells WMS what's arriving before truck arrives). EDI can compress order cycle time from days (manual) to hours (automated).
Q4 of 5
A company assigns a dedicated cross-functional team to manage its relationship with a customer representing 25% of total revenue. This is:
Key Account Management (KAM): dedicating specialized resources to the most important customer relationships. A customer representing 25% of revenue is clearly a key account — losing them would be a major financial event. KAM investment (dedicated team, joint planning, custom services) is justified by the account's strategic and financial importance. This is how Pareto principle applies to customer management: 20% of customers = 80% of revenue → focus disproportionate resources on those 20%.
Q5 of 5
A customer portal in supply chain management primarily serves to:
Customer portals = B2B self-service. Instead of customers calling/emailing for order status, invoice copies, or return requests, they access a portal directly. Benefits: (1) Reduced inbound contact volume (supplier labor cost); (2) 24/7 availability (customer convenience); (3) Consistent, accurate information (system data, not human-retrieved); (4) Faster resolution (customer finds own answer immediately). Customer portals are a form of supply chain collaboration — sharing information to improve joint efficiency.
