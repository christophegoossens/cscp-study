Warehousing Functions
Warehousing: The physical storage and handling of goods as they move through the supply chain — providing time utility by making products available when customers need them, even when production timing doesn't match consumption timing.
Primary warehousing functions:
  • Receiving: Accepting and verifying inbound shipments
  • Putaway: Moving goods to their designated storage location
  • Storage: Holding inventory in an organized, accessible manner
  • Order picking: Selecting items to fulfill customer orders
  • Packing & shipping: Preparing and dispatching outbound orders
  • Cross-docking: Direct transfer inbound to outbound without storage
Value-added services (VAS): Activities performed at the warehouse beyond basic storage — labeling, kitting, assembly, customization, returns processing. VAS allows postponement of product differentiation closer to demand.
Warehouse Design and Layout
Warehouse layout: The arrangement of storage locations, aisles, receiving, shipping, and equipment to minimize travel time and maximize throughput — critical for picking efficiency.
Slotting strategies for warehouse efficiency:
  • Fast-movers (A items) near shipping: Reduces travel for high-frequency picks
  • Heavy/bulky items at floor level: Reduces ergonomic risk
  • Similar items stored together: Reduces multi-location trips for related orders
  • Seasonal rotation: Move items to best slots based on current demand patterns
Warehouse Management System (WMS): Software that manages and controls day-to-day warehouse operations — receiving, putaway, picking, packing, shipping, and inventory tracking. Directs workers via pick lists, barcode scanning, and RF devices.
Pick-to-light / voice picking: Technologies that direct pickers to locations using light displays or voice instructions — improving accuracy and speed vs. paper pick lists.
Types of Warehouses
Common warehouse types:
  • Private warehouse: Owned/operated by the company — full control, high fixed cost
  • Public warehouse: Third-party facility rented by space/time — flexible, low fixed cost, less control
  • Contract warehouse: Dedicated 3PL space under a long-term agreement — balance of control and flexibility
  • Distribution center (DC): High-throughput facility optimized for rapid product flow rather than long-term storage
  • Fulfillment center: DC focused on direct-to-consumer e-commerce orders — small, frequent shipments
Cross-docking facility: A warehouse-like facility with little or no storage — goods arrive, are sorted, and immediately dispatched to outbound vehicles. Minimizes dwell time and storage cost.
Economic trade-off: Private warehouse = high fixed cost + high control + scalability risk. Public warehouse = low fixed cost + less control + scalable. Most companies use a mix.

TERM / CONCEPT
What is a WMS?
ANSWER
Warehouse Management System — software that directs and tracks all warehouse operations. Capabilities: receiving guidance, directed putaway, pick path optimization, wave planning, barcode/RFID integration, labor management, inventory tracking, shipping confirmation. Benefit: replaces paper-based processes, reduces errors, improves throughput, provides real-time inventory visibility.
TERM / CONCEPT
Private vs. public vs. contract warehouse
ANSWER
Private: company owns/operates. High fixed cost, full control, inflexible to volume changes. Public: rented space from 3PL by time/space. Low fixed cost, flexible, but less control over operations. Contract: dedicated 3PL space under long-term agreement. More control than public, more flexible than private. Middle ground. Choice depends on: volume stability, capital availability, control requirements, market served.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are value-added services (VAS) in warehousing?
ANSWER
Activities beyond basic storage performed at the warehouse: • Labeling and price tagging • Kitting (assembling components into sets) • Light assembly • Customization (adding logos, accessories) • Returns processing and refurbishment • Repacking VAS enables POSTPONEMENT: standard products are stored and customized only when an order is placed, reducing inventory of finished configurations.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is slotting in a warehouse?
ANSWER
Assigning products to optimal storage locations based on movement frequency, weight, size, and order profiles. Best practice: Fast movers (A items) near shipping → reduces picker travel Heavy/large items at ground level → ergonomics and safety Items frequently ordered together → stored near each other Good slotting can reduce picking labor 10–20% with no capital investment.
TERM / CONCEPT
Distribution center vs. fulfillment center
ANSWER
Distribution Center (DC): high-throughput facility serving retail stores or B2B customers. Receives large quantities, ships pallets/cases to stores. Fulfillment Center: variant of DC optimized for direct-to-consumer (e-commerce). Receives bulk, ships individual units to end customers. Higher complexity: more SKUs, smaller orders, more packaging options, higher return rates.

Q1 of 5
Order picking is the most critical warehousing operation to optimize because:
Order picking = selecting items from storage to fill customer orders. It's labor-intensive, involves lots of travel in the warehouse, and is prone to errors (wrong item, wrong quantity, wrong location). At 50–60% of total warehouse labor cost, improving picking efficiency (slotting, routing, technology like pick-to-light) has the highest financial return of any warehousing improvement.
Q2 of 5
A company that wants flexible warehouse capacity that can grow and shrink with seasonal demand would best use:
Public/contract warehouses: company pays for space as needed — scale up in peak season, scale down in off-peak. No long-term capital commitment. Private warehouses have fixed capacity and fixed costs regardless of volume — poor fit for volatile demand. This is a core reason companies use 3PLs: to get flexible, scalable capacity without owning fixed assets.
Q3 of 5
Value-added services (VAS) in a warehouse support which supply chain strategy?
VAS (labeling, kitting, customization at the warehouse) enables postponement: keep generic/standard product in inventory and differentiate it (add labels, assemble kits, customize) only when a real order arrives. This reduces the number of finished SKUs that must be forecasted and stocked, lowering forecast error and inventory investment while still delivering a customized product quickly.
Q4 of 5
A cross-docking facility differs from a conventional warehouse in that:
Cross-docking: inbound vehicles arrive → goods unloaded, sorted, and immediately loaded onto outbound vehicles → no long-term storage. Reduces: inventory holding costs, product damage (fewer touches), total cycle time. Works best for: fast-moving consumer goods, perishables, and where supplier deliveries can be coordinated to match outbound shipment schedules.
Q5 of 5
A WMS (Warehouse Management System) improves warehouse performance primarily by:
A WMS manages the operational execution layer of the warehouse: tells workers exactly where to put stock, where to pick from, in what sequence, and confirms all transactions via barcode/RFID scan. Benefits: higher inventory accuracy (real-time tracking), faster picking (optimized routes), fewer errors (system-directed vs. memory-based), better labor visibility (productivity tracking), and faster shipping (wave management).
