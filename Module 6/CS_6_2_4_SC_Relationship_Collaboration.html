6.2.4 Overview: CRM and SRM as Two Sides of One Coin

CRM (managing customer relationships) and SRM (managing supplier relationships) are two sides of the same relationship coin. Together they enable:

  • Organizing supplier chains to suit specific customer segment needs
  • Customers or suppliers collaborating to add value for all parties
  • Customer/supplier participation in product development (co-design)

Process for managing customer relationships (CRM): change management to build customer-centric culture; CRM software; customer segmentation; understanding segment needs (data → analysis → tailored packages); lifetime relationship building; sharing customer info with supply chain partners; capturing satisfaction metrics; feedback loop for CRM improvement.

Process for managing supplier relationships (SRM): change management for internal alignment; SRM software; TCO calculation; partner selection; goal development; contract/ground rules; alliance managers; long-term relationship development; realign processes; measure performance; periodic course corrections; periodic strategic fit reevaluation.

Virtual Organizations & Collaboration
Virtual organization: short-term alliances between independent organizations in a potentially long-term relationship to design, produce, and distribute a product. Organizations in virtual organizations share costs, skills, and market access.

Virtual organizations characteristics:

  • Use POS data to replace make-to-stock (push) with make-to-order (pull)
  • Build trust by sharing information — the more shared, the more robust the relationship
  • Speed of adaptation to new business situations is key
  • May need to weaken formal hierarchy to empower individuals/teams in partner-facing roles

Building Collaborative Relationships

Requirements for partner selection (Jordan Lewis framework): adding value, improving market access, strengthening operations, adding technological strength, enhancing strategic growth, sharing insights and learning, increasing financial strength.

Effective partnerships: shared risks + resources + rewards + vision + values. Building them requires: auditable information exchange, deterrence-based arrangements (contracts), incentive-based arrangements, process-based trust-building, leadership with authority, focus on entire supply chain, networkwide visibility, sharing of knowledge (not just data).

Three management tasks to initiate collaboration:

  • Jointly discuss/designate goals and next steps
  • Define roles for each party (avoid redundancy)
  • Create conflict resolution policy and maintain management involvement

Eight barriers to collaboration: suboptimization, individual incentives conflicting with organizational goals, working with competitors, bottlenecks from weak/slow partners, technology barriers, regulations/legal issues, power-based relationships, underestimated benefits, culture conflicts.

Four levels of communication in collaboration:

  • Transactional with information sharing (single data source access)
  • Shared processes and partnership (shared knowledge, collaborative design)
  • Linked competitive vision and strategic alliance (virtual entity, joint strategy)
  • Mergers and acquisitions (backward/forward integration)

What is organizational learning in CRM/SRM context?
ANSWER

TERM / CONCEPT
What is “saving face” and its supply chain implication?
ANSWER
An Asian/Latin cultural concept of retaining good image/pride despite being wrong or losing in conflict. In SC negotiations and relationships, managers must avoid situations that cause partners to “lose face” to maintain trust and relationship health.
TERM / CONCEPT
What two major shifts drive customer-centric businesses today?
ANSWER
1) Greater focus on the customer (both internal and external); 2) Greater integration (internally via process reengineering AND externally via supply chain collaboration with partners).

Question 1
What is 'supply chain collaboration' and what are the main forms it takes?
A.Working with the same supplier for many years
B.Partners sharing information, planning processes, and resources to achieve better collective outcomes than possible independently — forms include: information sharing (forecasts, inventory data), joint planning (S&OP integration), process integration (synchronized replenishment), and joint investment (capacity, technology, innovation)
C.Merging supply chain companies together
D.A regulatory framework for supply chain data sharing
Collaboration creates mutual value: information sharing reduces uncertainty (and thus safety stock) for both parties; joint planning aligns supply with demand; synchronized replenishment reduces bullwhip effect; joint investment creates shared capabilities that neither could develop alone.
Question 2
What is the 'bullwhip effect' and how does collaboration reduce it?
A.The whipping motion used in quality control testing
B.The amplification of demand variability as orders move upstream in the supply chain — small customer demand variations become large order swings for suppliers. Collaboration reduces it by sharing actual demand data (POS, sell-through) upstream, replacing order-based forecasting
C.The effect of overstocking at distribution centers
D.The impact of transportation delays on inventory levels
The bullwhip effect is caused by: demand signal distortion (ordering based on orders, not sales), lead time variability, batch ordering, and shortage gaming. Collaboration fixes the root causes: sharing POS data with upstream suppliers eliminates demand distortion; VMI eliminates batching.
Question 3
What is 'vendor-managed inventory' (VMI) and what are its benefits?
A.A system where vendors manage their own warehouse inventory
B.An arrangement where the supplier (vendor) takes responsibility for managing the buyer's inventory levels — using the buyer's inventory data to decide when and how much to replenish. Benefits: reduced stockouts, lower buyer inventory management cost, higher vendor fill rates, and reduced order variability
C.A system where customers manage their own orders
D.A government program for managing strategic inventory reserves
VMI shifts replenishment responsibility to the party with the best visibility into demand and supply: the supplier sees actual consumption data and manages replenishment within agreed min/max levels. Buyer gains: lower inventory, fewer stockouts, less ordering effort. Supplier gains: stable, visible demand, better production planning.
Question 4
What is 'collaborative planning, forecasting, and replenishment' (CPFR) and what problem does it solve?
A.A government program for emergency supply planning
B.A standardized industry framework where buyer and seller jointly develop a shared sales forecast, identify exceptions, collaboratively resolve them, and generate synchronized replenishment orders — solving the problem of misaligned plans causing demand amplification and inventory imbalance
C.A financial planning process between companies
D.A software tool for supply chain planning
CPFR (Voluntary Interindustry Commerce Solutions standard) aligns plans: buyer and supplier share forecasts, identify where they diverge, collaboratively determine the right forecast, and use it to drive replenishment — eliminating the plan misalignment that causes over- and under-stocking.
Question 5
What is 'co-managed inventory' (CMI) and how does it differ from VMI?
A.Inventory managed by two suppliers jointly
B.A collaboration model where supplier and buyer share responsibility for replenishment — the supplier sees inventory data and proposes order quantities, but the buyer approves before orders are placed. A middle ground between full VMI and buyer-managed inventory
C.Inventory owned jointly by two companies
D.A system where customers can see inventory levels
CMI gives buyers control while leveraging supplier visibility: the supplier's proposal benefits from demand/supply knowledge, but the buyer retains approval authority — useful when buyers want collaboration benefits but aren't ready to fully delegate replenishment decisions.
Question 6
What are 'supply chain information sharing' barriers and how can they be overcome?
A.Network bandwidth limitations preventing data transfer
B.Barriers include: trust concerns (sharing data = sharing competitive intelligence), IT system incompatibility, data ownership and security concerns, and perceived power asymmetry. Overcome through: trust-building (starting with limited sharing), EDI/API integration standards, data sharing agreements, and demonstrating mutual benefit
C.Language barriers between supply chain partners
D.Geographic distance between partners
Information sharing barriers are primarily behavioral and organizational (trust, power dynamics) rather than technical. Overcoming them requires: demonstrating ROI from sharing (show the mutual benefit), starting small (share less sensitive data first), and establishing formal governance (what data, who accesses it, how is it protected).
Question 7
What is 'joint business planning' (JBP) in supply chain partnerships?
A.Two companies planning to merge their businesses
B.A collaborative process where buyer and supplier jointly develop strategic and operational plans — aligning on growth targets, promotional calendars, new product launches, capacity investments, and supply chain improvements — creating a shared roadmap for the partnership
C.A procurement planning process between departments
D.A financial planning alignment between companies
JBP is the highest form of commercial collaboration: companies share strategic plans (sales growth targets, new markets, product launches), align supply chain investment (capacity for growth, technology for efficiency), and jointly commit to mutual goals — creating aligned incentives across the supply chain.
Question 8
What is 'supplier co-location' or 'resident engineering' in strategic relationships?
A.Building supplier factories next to the buyer's factories
B.Placing supplier engineers or representatives at the buyer's facility (or vice versa) — to enable real-time collaboration on engineering, quality, and supply issues, reduce communication delays, and build deeper integration between the companies' teams
C.A supplier delivery consolidation strategy
D.A shared office space arrangement between companies
Supplier co-location deepens integration: Toyota's supplier association (kyohokai) includes resident engineers at Toyota facilities who can immediately respond to quality or supply issues, participate in design reviews, and develop deep knowledge of Toyota's production system — reducing response time and deepening partnership quality.
Question 9
What is 'supplier diversity collaboration' and how does it differ from standard supplier diversity programs?
A.Collaborating with many different types of suppliers
B.Moving beyond simply tracking diverse supplier spend to actively developing diverse suppliers — providing mentoring, technical assistance, access to larger contracts, and supply chain connections to help diverse businesses grow into full supply chain partners
C.Having suppliers of different product types
D.A program for suppliers in different geographic regions
Advanced supplier diversity programs don't just buy from diverse suppliers — they develop them: mentoring programs, technical assistance, bundled opportunities (pairing a small diverse supplier with a large prime supplier), and access to executive relationships accelerate diverse supplier development into strategic partners.
Question 10
What is 'open book costing' in supply chain collaboration?
A.A pricing model where all books are sold without markup
B.A collaboration model where a supplier shares its detailed cost structure (materials, labor, overhead, profit margin) with the buyer — enabling joint cost reduction (the buyer can help identify efficiency improvements) and building trust through transparency
C.A public financial disclosure requirement for suppliers
D.A cost accounting method used in manufacturing
Open book costing moves from adversarial negotiation (supplier hides costs, buyer probes) to collaborative optimization: when cost structures are transparent, buyer and supplier can jointly identify cost reduction opportunities — redesign specs, improve processes, aggregate materials purchasing — and share the savings.
Question 11
What is 'supply chain visibility' and what technologies enable it?
A.The ability to see inside supply chain facilities
B.Real-time awareness of inventory, order, and shipment status across the entire supply chain — from raw material through customer delivery. Enabled by: EDI/API data sharing, IoT track-and-trace, RFID, GPS fleet tracking, and supply chain control tower platforms
C.The physical layout of a supply chain facility
D.A management reporting system for supply chain executives
Supply chain visibility enables proactive management: knowing a shipment will be late before it arrives enables advance customer notification and contingency planning. End-to-end visibility requires data sharing across company boundaries — collaborative relationships are the prerequisite for the technology to add value.
Question 12
What is the difference between 'transactional' and 'strategic' supply chain relationships?
A.Transactional relationships use paper; strategic relationships use digital systems
B.Transactional: arms-length, price-focused, easily switched, minimal integration, short-term. Strategic: trust-based, mutually invested, deeply integrated (systems, processes, people), long-term commitment, with shared goals and joint value creation — justified when the supplier is critical and switching is costly
C.Transactional relationships are with domestic suppliers; strategic are with international
D.Transactional means manual processes; strategic means automated
Relationship type should match supplier segment (Kraljic): strategic relationships for critical/unique suppliers (deep integration, mutual dependency, shared success); transactional for commodity/leverage suppliers (efficiency, competitive tension, easy switching). Over-investing in transactional suppliers or under-investing in strategic ones both reduce value.
