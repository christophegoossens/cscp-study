6.2.3 Overview: Supplier Performance Measurement

Effective supplier performance measurement is compulsive — tracking all suppliers to some extent, focusing on critical or problematic ones. Best-practice systems:

  • Track all suppliers; focus on critical component suppliers and those with prior quality issues
  • Collaborate with suppliers on measurements, reporting, and improvements
  • Automate key supplier performance measurement activities
  • Standardize procedures across the organization

When evaluating supplier performance, check: promptness and flexibility of response, consistency of schedule, quality assurance processes, financial stability, technology investment, and reliability of their own supply chain partners.

SCOR metrics can be used to judge individual supplier performance.

Supplier Certification
Supplier certification: certification procedures verifying that a supplier operates, maintains, improves, and documents effective procedures that relate to the customer’s requirements. When a supplier’s products or services are certified, there is no inspection required when the goods arrive.

Certification: extensive on-site evaluation against agreed performance levels in cost, quality, delivery, technical support, and environmental performance.

Exhibit 6-14: Certification Process

Nine-step supplier certification process:

  • Step 1: Define Requirements, Process, and Roles
  • Step 2: Evaluate Alternative Suppliers (screen via conversations, RFIs)
  • Step 3: Select Suppliers (pilot with a few good candidates)
  • Step 4: Conduct Joint Quality Planning
  • Step 5: Cooperate and Build Partnership (formal commitment agreement)
  • Step 6: Conduct Measurements (against cost, quality, delivery, technical support)
  • Step 7: Certify Suppliers (award certification)
  • Step 8: Conduct Quality Improvement Programs (bring non-certified suppliers up)
  • Step 9: Reevaluate Suppliers Periodically (recertification/benchmarking)

Benefits of Certification (Exhibit 6-13)

For customers: ensures development/supply of safer/cleaner products; safeguards consumers; extends CSR to suppliers; assists supplier selection and ongoing evaluation; may consolidate to fewer suppliers; builds trust to share information.

For suppliers: access to wider market; helps market capabilities; higher quality reduces costs (fewer defects, returns, lost customers); learn more about customer needs; best practices improve throughput; possible single-source opportunity; demonstrates commitment.

Supplier Rating Systems & Controlling Errors

Supplier rating systems set performance measures and standards — monitoring quality costs, tracking timeliness of incoming materials, improving supplier performance over time.

Four data sources for rating systems:

  • Conformation rates (inspection/test data)
  • Number of floor failure events (defects found after shipment)
  • Levels of conditionally accepted materials
  • Time line performance (scheduled vs. actual receipt dates)

Three methods of sharing ratings with suppliers:

  • Scorecards: quantitative + qualitative data; historical, current, predictive views
  • Performance alerts: real-time automated alerts on areas of concern
  • Surveys: standardized surveys for qualitative information
Controlling errors: “Do not ship unless in most recent PO release.” “Do not ship +/– days outside delivery request date.” These control rules minimize rejected shipments at the receiving dock. When supply chain disruptions occur, flexibility in controls must be balanced against the cost of deviations.

Freight claim: a form submitted to the carrier requesting financial reimbursement for loss or damage — can be filed for visible damage, shortages, concealed damages, and delays.

Question 1
What is 'supplier performance management' (SPM) and what does it enable?
A.The process of managing supplier invoices and payments
B.A systematic process of measuring, analyzing, and communicating supplier performance against agreed metrics — enabling objective assessment, identification of improvement opportunities, early warning of deteriorating performance, and data-driven supply base decisions
C.The management of supplier production schedules
D.A process for auditing supplier financial statements
SPM provides the data foundation for supplier relationship management: objective performance data replaces opinion-based assessments, enables performance-based rewards/consequences, identifies top performers deserving more business, and flags underperformers requiring improvement action.
Question 2
What metrics are typically included in a supplier scorecard?
A.Only price and lead time
B.Quality (defect rate, rejection rate), delivery (on-time %, fill rate), service (responsiveness, issue resolution), and cost performance (vs. target/market) — plus optionally: sustainability, innovation contribution, and risk profile
C.Only quality and cost
D.Only on-time delivery and customer satisfaction
A balanced supplier scorecard captures performance across dimensions that matter to the buyer: quality (affects production reliability), delivery (affects order fulfillment), service (affects problem resolution efficiency), and cost (affects competitiveness) — with weights reflecting the buyer's priorities.
Question 3
What is 'supplier corrective action request' (SCAR) and when is it triggered?
A.A request to a supplier to change their pricing
B.A formal notification to a supplier that a performance deficiency has been identified, requiring a root-cause analysis and documented corrective action plan — typically triggered when quality or delivery performance falls below agreed minimum thresholds
C.A request to add new products to a supplier's offering
D.A financial claim against a supplier for late delivery
SCARs formalize the corrective action process: the buyer identifies the deficiency, the supplier must identify root cause (using 8D, 5-Why, fishbone), implement corrective actions, and demonstrate sustained improvement. SCAR closure requires verified, sustained performance improvement.
Question 4
What is 'supplier audit' and what types of supplier audits are performed?
A.An audit of the buyer's purchasing processes
B.A formal assessment of a supplier's processes, facilities, systems, and capabilities against defined standards — types include: quality system audits (ISO 9001 compliance), manufacturing process audits, financial audits, social responsibility audits (labor practices), and security audits
C.An audit of supplier financial invoices for accuracy
D.A legal inspection of supplier contracts
Supplier audits verify that suppliers have the processes, systems, and facilities claimed — and that products/services actually conform to requirements. Audits can be first-party (supplier self-audits), second-party (customer audits supplier), or third-party (independent certification body audits).
Question 5
What is 'supplier risk assessment' and what factors does it evaluate?
A.An assessment of the risk that a supplier will raise prices
B.A structured evaluation of the risks associated with a specific supplier — financial stability (bankruptcy risk), geographic risks (natural disasters, geopolitical), operational risks (single-site, no backup), quality risks, and compliance risks (regulatory, ethics)
C.An assessment of the risk of having too many suppliers
D.An analysis of market price risk for supplied materials
Supplier risk assessment identifies vulnerabilities before they cause disruptions: a financially stressed supplier (high debt, declining margins) may be unable to invest in quality or may fail; a single-site supplier in a flood zone is a geographic risk; a supplier using conflict minerals is a compliance risk.
Question 6
What is 'supplier development program' vs. 'supplier disqualification' — how is the choice made?
A.Both options are always used together
B.Development: invest resources to help an underperforming strategic supplier improve — appropriate when the supplier is hard to replace, has a correctable performance gap, and is motivated to improve. Disqualification: remove the supplier from approved list — appropriate when issues are uncorrectable, represent ethical violations, or where qualified alternatives exist
C.Development is always preferred; disqualification is never used
D.Disqualification is always preferred as it creates urgency
The choice depends on: How replaceable is the supplier? How serious and correctable is the problem? Is the supplier motivated to improve? For strategic, hard-to-replace suppliers, development makes sense. For commodity suppliers with correctable problems, the threat of replacement often motivates improvement without requiring heavy investment.
Question 7
What is 'preferred supplier program' and what criteria are typically used?
A.A program where suppliers pay to receive preferred status
B.A formal recognition and reward program for top-performing suppliers — earned through sustained performance excellence across quality, delivery, service, and cost metrics, often granting preferred suppliers first-right-of-refusal on new business, longer contract terms, and reduced audit frequency
C.A government program for certifying suppliers
D.A program for suppliers who offer the lowest prices
Preferred supplier programs incentivize excellence: suppliers who achieve and maintain preferred status get business growth opportunities (new categories, new markets), reduced administrative burden, and closer access to buyer product roadmaps — creating a positive incentive to outperform.
Question 8
What is 'supplier self-assessment' and how does it complement buyer-conducted evaluations?
A.Suppliers assessing their own prices
B.A process where suppliers evaluate their own performance and capabilities against defined criteria before formal buyer assessment — serving as a data collection mechanism, a tool for supplier self-awareness, and a way to scale assessment across a large supply base efficiently
C.A survey completed by supplier employees
D.A process for suppliers to identify their own quality defects
Supplier self-assessments extend performance management reach: with hundreds of suppliers, buyers cannot audit everyone. Self-assessments collect data at scale; buyer-conducted assessments focus on high-risk and strategic suppliers where objective, independent assessment is most critical.
Question 9
What is 'supplier innovation' and how does performance management encourage it?
A.New products invented by the supplier
B.Suppliers proactively identifying and proposing improvements — to product design (value engineering), process (cost reduction, quality improvement), technology (new capabilities), or sustainability — that benefit the buyer. Performance management enables it by building trust, sharing strategic information, and formally recognizing contributions
C.Suppliers developing new IT systems
D.New sourcing approaches developed by the procurement team
Innovation from suppliers is a competitive advantage: Toyota's supplier partnerships consistently produce cost and quality improvements; Apple's supplier relationships enabled manufacturing innovations. Long-term relationships with performance-based trust, shared roadmap information, and formal innovation recognition programs enable supplier innovation.
Question 10
What is 'escalation process' in supplier performance management?
A.The process of escalating supplier prices
B.A structured process for progressively escalating response to supplier performance problems — from supplier self-correction → buyer-supplier working meeting → formal corrective action → executive escalation → business at risk → potential disqualification — with defined trigger thresholds at each level
C.A process for escalating purchase orders
D.The process of moving a supplier from one category to another
Escalation processes ensure problems are resolved: working-level relationship managers address routine issues; serious or persistent issues escalate to senior management (both sides); strategic relationship issues involve executive sponsorship. Clear triggers prevent either over-reacting (executive attention to minor issues) or under-reacting (allowing serious problems to persist).
Question 11
What is 'total landed cost' and how does it apply to supplier performance evaluation?
A.The total weight of goods delivered by the supplier
B.All costs from supplier to final destination — purchase price + freight + duties/tariffs + inventory carrying cost (safety stock due to lead time and variability) + quality costs (inspection, defects, warranty). Used to evaluate the true cost of alternative supply sources
C.The total distance goods travel from supplier to customer
D.The total cost of supplier management activities
Total landed cost reveals the true cost of supply choices: a supplier with a 10% higher unit price but higher quality (lower defect cost), shorter lead time (lower safety stock), and better reliability may have lower total landed cost than the cheaper alternative.
Question 12
What is 'supplier relationship review' (business review) and what agenda items should it cover?
A.An annual meeting where suppliers present their financial results
B.A formal, periodic (quarterly/annual) structured meeting between buyer and supplier leadership to review performance data, discuss strategic alignment, identify opportunities and risks, plan next period, and strengthen the relationship beyond day-to-day transactions
C.A review of the supplier contract's legal terms
D.A supplier presentation of new products and services
Business reviews are the backbone of strategic supplier relationships: they create the right level to discuss performance trends (not individual shipments), strategic direction (buyer's product roadmap, supplier's investment plans), shared opportunities (joint cost reduction, innovation), and relationship health — preventing relationships from becoming purely transactional.
