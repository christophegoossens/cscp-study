6.1.2 Overview: Scope of CRM and CRM Strategy

A CRM strategy defines how an organization initiates, develops, and sustains customer relationships. The scope of CRM covers four areas:

  • Sales Operations: order taking, invoicing, call centers, returns — optimizing customer experience and collecting transaction data
  • Analysis: data validation, customer segmentation, performance measurement
  • Customer Information Dissemination: getting focused information to the right groups (supply chain partners included)
  • Relationship Building and Collaboration: developing lifetime customers through staff training and experience optimization

Lifetime Customers & CRM Components
Lifetime customers: lower total marketing costs (acquisition costs are front-loaded), easier to satisfy (technology enables deeper knowledge), and higher revenue/profit opportunities as relationships mature.
Exhibit 6-3: Development of Lifetime Value

Six components of CRM strategy (Exhibit 6-4):

  • EBS (Enterprise Business Systems): technological backbone — customer database, transaction maintenance, order status, sales support, financial details
  • Web systems: enables online browsing, ordering, payment, tracking
  • Marketing: ensures customers know about products/services
  • External data: supports collaborative partnerships with suppliers, resellers, channel partners
  • CRM application: three elements — operations CRM, collaborative CRM, analytical CRM
  • Service: post-sale follow-up and support

CRM and Product Life Cycle
Exhibit 6-5: Product Life Cycle
  • Development: VOC to shape product design; test with selected customer segments
  • Introduction: high advertising spend; CRM supports promotional programs
  • Growth: sustain customer care; use CRM to identify strong/weak segments
  • Maturity: entice competitors’ customers to switch; retain existing base
  • Decline: critical customer care; assure availability of service/parts for existing customers
CRM Core Technologies
Data warehouse: a repository of data specially prepared to support decision-making applications.

A Customer Data Warehouse (CDW) stores customer data separately from transactional systems for deeper analysis.

CDW benefits: strategic marketing, new product development, channel management, sales productivity, one-to-one marketing.

CRM technology tools (Exhibit 6-7):

  • Account management: managing customer information for marketing and care
  • Sales Force Automation (SFA): collects data from transactions/call centers; contact management, account management, opportunity management, quotation management
  • Business intelligence: information about customers, competitors, products — transformed into insights
  • Marketing automation: media-based marketing, direct marketing, e-commerce, campaign management by customer type (prospecting, vulnerable, win-back, loyalty)

Keys to Successful CRM Implementation
  • Architecture determined early: avoid disconnected silos; aim for multi-enterprise integration
  • System must enhance efficiency: make CRM tasks faster and easier, not more complex
  • Coordinated implementation: cross-functional teams from every area
  • Everyone trained: job processes redrawn; all staff trained at appropriate depth
  • Measured against customer needs: will customers use it? Does it improve their experience? Does it allow personalization?

Four maturity levels of CRM integration: Disconnected → Interfacing → Internally integrated → Multi-enterprise integrated.

Question 1
What are the three main functional areas of a CRM system?
A.Sales, marketing, and finance
B.Operational CRM (automating customer-facing processes), Analytical CRM (analyzing customer data), and Collaborative CRM (enabling sharing of customer information across channels and partners)
C.Customer service, billing, and shipping
D.ERP, WMS, and TMS integration
CRM systems span three domains: Operational CRM automates sales force, marketing, and service functions; Analytical CRM mines customer data for insights (segmentation, CLV, churn prediction); Collaborative CRM enables multi-channel customer interaction data sharing across the organization and partners.
Question 2
What is 'customer analytics' in the context of CRM?
A.The study of how many customers a company has
B.The use of data analysis techniques to understand customer behavior, segment customers, predict future purchasing, identify churn risk, and determine the most effective ways to serve and grow customer relationships
C.Analytics performed by customers themselves
D.Financial analysis of customer payment records
Customer analytics transforms raw data into actionable intelligence: RFM analysis (Recency, Frequency, Monetary), churn prediction models, next-best-offer recommendations, and customer lifetime value calculations — enabling data-driven decisions on service investment and customer strategy.
Question 3
What is 'available-to-promise' (ATP) and why is it important in customer service?
A.The amount of inventory a company has in stock
B.A real-time calculation of when the supply chain can promise delivery of a customer's order — based on current inventory, scheduled production, and existing commitments. Enables reliable delivery commitments rather than promising based on guesses
C.A measure of supplier delivery reliability
D.A calculation of the maximum order size for a customer
ATP enables accurate delivery promising: by checking uncommitted inventory and supply schedule, the system can tell a salesperson (or customer) exactly when an order can ship. Promising from ATP data reduces late deliveries and customer complaints caused by over-promising.
Question 4
What is 'customer data management' and what are the key data quality requirements?
A.Storing customer contact information in a spreadsheet
B.The process of collecting, maintaining, and ensuring the accuracy, completeness, and consistency of customer data across all systems — with key requirements: single customer view (no duplicates), accurate contact data, and clean transactional history
C.Backing up customer order databases
D.Protecting customer data from cybersecurity threats
Customer data quality underpins CRM effectiveness: duplicate records (same customer in system twice) corrupt analytics; inaccurate addresses cause delivery failures; incomplete contact data prevents service recovery. A 'golden record' — a single, accurate customer profile — is the foundation.
Question 5
What is 'sales force automation' (SFA) and how does it relate to supply chain?
A.A robot that performs sales tasks
B.Technology that automates sales process activities — opportunity tracking, quote generation, order entry, and forecast collection — with supply chain integration enabling salespeople to check inventory availability and lead times while with customers
C.Automated telemarketing systems
D.Software that replaces the sales team
SFA connects the sales process to supply chain: salespeople can check ATP, lead times, and pricing in real time during customer conversations — enabling accurate commitments. SFA also aggregates salesforce opportunity data that feeds demand planning.
Question 6
What is 'customer segmentation analytics' (RFM analysis)?
A.A geographic segmentation method
B.A customer segmentation technique using three dimensions: Recency (how recently they purchased), Frequency (how often they purchase), and Monetary value (how much they spend) — to identify best customers, at-risk customers, and lapsed customers
C.A method for analyzing customer complaint patterns
D.A technique for predicting product demand
RFM analysis scores customers on three dimensions: Recency (recent purchasers are more likely to buy again), Frequency (frequent buyers are loyal), and Monetary value (high spenders have high CLV). Combined scores segment customers for targeted marketing and service differentiation.
Question 7
What is 'churn prediction' analytics in CRM?
A.Predicting when ice cream will melt in the supply chain
B.Using historical data patterns to identify customers showing signs of leaving — such as declining order frequency, decreasing order size, or increased complaints — enabling proactive retention actions before the customer actually churns
C.Predicting product spoilage in transit
D.A method for forecasting which customers will grow their business
Churn prediction models identify at-risk customers before they leave: early warning signals (reducing purchase frequency, first-time complaint, competitor inquiry) trigger proactive outreach. Intervening with service recovery, pricing adjustments, or account attention is cheaper than re-acquiring a lost customer.
Question 8
What is 'demand sensing' in CRM-supply chain integration?
A.The ability of sales staff to sense when demand will change
B.Using real-time signals from customer systems (POS data, order patterns, web activity) to improve short-term demand forecasts — detecting demand shifts faster than traditional statistical forecasting
C.Monitoring customer warehouse inventory levels
D.A technique for sensing customer satisfaction
Demand sensing uses high-frequency data (daily POS, syndicated data, order signals) to update short-horizon forecasts dynamically — replacing weekly or monthly statistical forecast updates with near-real-time sensing of actual demand patterns.
Question 9
What is 'customer scorecard' reporting in supply chain management?
A.A report card given to customers about their behavior
B.A regular performance report provided to customers summarizing supply chain performance metrics (OTIF, fill rate, order accuracy, lead time) — enabling transparency, joint performance review, and data-driven discussion of improvement opportunities
C.A financial scorecard tracking customer profitability
D.A quality report submitted to regulators
Customer scorecards build trust and collaboration: sharing performance data openly (even when performance is below target) demonstrates accountability, enables joint root-cause analysis, and builds the foundation for improvement — stronger relationships are built on transparency.
Question 10
What is 'omnichannel fulfillment' and what CRM data does it require?
A.Fulfilling orders through multiple channels without coordination
B.Delivering a consistent, seamless customer experience across all channels (online, store, phone) with the same product availability, pricing, and service — requiring a unified customer profile, real-time inventory visibility, and order management across all channels
C.Shipping from multiple warehouses simultaneously
D.Fulfilling orders for multi-product customers
Omnichannel requires unified CRM: the same customer order history, preferences, and service interactions visible across channels — so a customer who ordered online can return in-store, and a customer service agent has full context regardless of channel. Inventory visibility and order routing must also be unified.
Question 11
What is 'net promoter score' (NPS) and how does supply chain affect it?
A.A measure of how many new customers a company is acquiring
B.A customer loyalty metric asking customers how likely they are to recommend the company (0-10 scale) — supply chain reliability (stockouts, delivery failures, order errors) directly depresses NPS, as negative experiences are the top reason customers don't recommend
C.A financial measure of net profit
D.A score used to prioritize new product launches
NPS (% promoters minus % detractors) is directly impacted by supply chain performance: stockouts, late deliveries, and damaged goods create 'detractors' who actively dissuade others. Supply chain reliability improvement is often the highest-ROI lever for NPS improvement.
Question 12
What is 'customer portal' technology and what supply chain functions does it enable?
A.A physical customer entrance to a distribution center
B.A web-based self-service platform where customers can place orders, track shipments, view invoices, access product information, and submit service requests — reducing the cost of customer interactions while improving convenience
C.A data sharing agreement between companies
D.A payment processing system for customers
Customer portals provide self-service supply chain visibility: order status, shipment tracking, invoice access, and returns initiation — reducing call volume for routine inquiries, improving customer satisfaction (24/7 access), and freeing customer service staff for complex issues.
