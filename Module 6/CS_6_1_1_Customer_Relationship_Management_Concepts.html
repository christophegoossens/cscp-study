6.1.1 Overview: CRM Philosophy and Segmentation

CRM is a philosophy that recognizes all companies are part service companies — quality of relationship with the customer is a key differentiator.

Customer Relationship Management (CRM): a marketing philosophy based on putting the customer first. Involves collection and analysis of information designed for sales and marketing decision support, and the execution of transactions and services to retain and expand the customer base.
  • CRM is broad (covers every customer interaction) and deep (focuses on long-term profitability of each relationship)
  • Customer-centric organizations: easy to do business with, add value, innovate, design from the customer’s perspective, and share customer insights throughout the supply chain
Customer Segmentation
Customer segmentation: the practice of dividing a customer base into groups of individuals that are similar in specific ways relevant to marketing. Traditional segmentation variables include geographic, demographic, psychographic, and behavioral data.
  • Intermediate customers: not at end of supply chain (e.g., manufacturer’s customers are retailers)
  • Ultimate customers: final recipients of the product or service
  • Segmentation by: customer value, customer needs, preferred contact channel
Customer driven: a company’s response to meet customer expectations and requirements.
Segmentation by customer value: Pareto principle — roughly 20% of customers generate 80% of profit. High-value customers warrant more investment to retain. Exhibit 6-1: Pareto Effect in Customer Segments

Sources of Customer Information & Voice of the Customer
  • Transaction records, sales representatives, service representatives, distribution points, purchased data
Voice of the Customer (VOC): actual customer descriptions in words for the functions and features customers desire for goods and services. In a broader context, a research and measurement tool to identify unmet needs that businesses can address.

VOC lets customers talk freely, reveals surprises, and allows organizations to anticipate needs.

CRM Strategies by Customer Segment & Relationship Type

Customer segment strategies: demographic/attitudinal, value-based, service-minded, retail, and B2B.

B2B customer priorities: complementary core competencies, knowledge of their business requirements, continuous improvement suggestions.

Four Customer Relationship Types:

  • Prospective customers: potential customers — focus on market research, pricing, promotions
  • Vulnerable customers: at risk of leaving; targeted retention programs
  • Win-back customers: ex-customers to be reacquired quickly
  • Loyal customers: least vulnerable; cross-sell and up-sell opportunities
Churn: the process of customers changing their buying preferences because they find better and/or cheaper products and services from another company. A high churn rate indicates poor retention.
CRM campaign impact study (PricewaterhouseCoopers): reducing churn in vulnerable customers = 15–20% profit increase; winning back lost customers = 10–20%; cross/up-selling loyal customers = 2–3%; increasing prospective customers = 3–4%.

Question 1
What is 'Customer Relationship Management' (CRM) in supply chain context?
A.A software system for storing customer contact information
B.A strategy for managing all interactions with current and potential customers to improve satisfaction, loyalty, and profitability — integrating sales, marketing, customer service, and supply chain operations around the customer
C.A program for resolving customer complaints
D.A pricing strategy for key customers
CRM is a customer-centric strategy that integrates all customer-facing functions: understanding customer needs, delivering the right product/service, resolving problems quickly, and building long-term relationships — supply chain capabilities are a key enabler of the customer experience.
Question 2
What is 'customer segmentation' and why is it important in supply chain management?
A.Dividing customers alphabetically
B.Categorizing customers into groups based on shared characteristics (volume, profitability, service needs, strategic importance) to tailor supply chain service levels, pricing, and relationship investment to each segment's value and requirements
C.Separating domestic from international customers
D.Grouping customers by their payment method
Segmentation enables differentiated service: high-value customers get premium service levels, dedicated inventory, and direct relationships; standard customers receive standard service. Without segmentation, a one-size-fits-all approach over-serves low-value customers and under-serves high-value ones.
Question 3
What is 'customer lifetime value' (CLV) and how does it affect supply chain decisions?
A.The total revenue from a single customer transaction
B.The total net profit a company expects to earn from a customer over the entire duration of their relationship — a long-term view that justifies investment in service quality, inventory availability, and relationship management for high-CLV customers
C.The value of all orders in a calendar year
D.The credit limit extended to a customer
CLV shifts decision-making from transaction-level cost to relationship-level value: a customer who orders $50K/year for 10 years has a $500K+ CLV. Investing $10K/year in premium service and inventory availability for such a customer delivers strong ROI.
Question 4
What is 'customer order fulfillment' and what are the key performance dimensions?
A.The process of accepting customer orders only
B.The end-to-end process of receiving, processing, preparing, and delivering customer orders — key performance dimensions: on-time delivery, order fill rate (completeness), order accuracy, invoice accuracy, and lead time
C.The billing and invoicing process
D.The customer's product selection process
Order fulfillment encompasses the complete order-to-cash cycle. Perfect Order performance — OTIF (on-time in-full), damage-free, accurate documentation — is the gold standard metric capturing all key fulfillment dimensions in a single composite measure.
Question 5
What is 'demand visibility' and why does it matter in customer relationship management?
A.The ability to see customer demand data in real time
B.Access to customers' forward-looking demand data (forecasts, order projections, planned promotions) — enabling suppliers to plan production, build inventory, and allocate capacity ahead of orders, improving service and reducing costs
C.The process of making products visible to customers
D.A marketing strategy for product launches
Demand visibility reduces the bullwhip effect: when suppliers see downstream customer demand directly (POS data, customer forecasts), they can plan ahead rather than reacting to erratic orders — improving service levels while reducing safety stock.
Question 6
What is 'collaborative planning, forecasting, and replenishment' (CPFR)?
A.A software platform for customer service agents
B.A supply chain collaboration initiative where trading partners share data and jointly develop plans, forecasts, and replenishment orders — replacing sequential, siloed planning with synchronized, jointly owned plans to improve service and reduce inventory
C.A financial planning process between buyers and finance departments
D.A government program for supply chain development
CPFR (VICS standard) is a structured process for buyer-supplier collaboration: sharing sales data and forecasts, identifying and resolving exceptions, and developing joint replenishment plans — proven to improve in-stock rates and reduce inventory simultaneously.
Question 7
What is 'service level agreement' (SLA) in customer-supplier relationships?
A.A financial agreement about service pricing
B.A formal contract defining the specific service performance standards (fill rate, lead time, delivery accuracy) that a supplier commits to provide — with defined measurement methods, reporting frequency, and consequences for non-performance
C.A training agreement for customer service staff
D.A legal agreement about product liability
SLAs formalize service expectations: they define what 'good' service means (e.g., 98% OTIF), how it's measured (weekly/monthly), who reports (supplier self-reporting or buyer measurement), and what happens when targets are missed (corrective action, penalties).
Question 8
What is 'voice of the customer' (VOC) and how is it used in supply chain management?
A.Customer feedback captured through social media monitoring only
B.The systematic capture and analysis of customer needs, requirements, and expectations — through surveys, interviews, complaints analysis, and data analysis — to drive supply chain improvements aligned with what customers actually value
C.Recorded customer service calls
D.A marketing research technique for new product development only
VOC in supply chain: understanding what service attributes customers value most (on-time delivery vs. lead time vs. order accuracy) enables targeted improvement investment. VOC findings should drive performance targets, process improvement priorities, and technology investment.
Question 9
What is 'customer profitability analysis' and what does it typically reveal?
A.A simple calculation of revenue per customer
B.An analysis of the profit (revenue minus all costs to serve) generated by each customer — typically revealing that a small percentage of customers (20%) generate the majority of profits (80%), while some customers are actually unprofitable
C.A comparison of customer satisfaction scores
D.An analysis of customer payment history
Customer profitability analysis (applying ABC/activity-based costing to customers) typically reveals the Pareto distribution: 20% of customers = 80% of profit, and some customers have negative profitability due to high service costs (small orders, frequent returns, customization requirements).
Question 10
What is 'order management' and what systems support it?
A.The process of managing inventory orders from suppliers
B.The business process that handles the complete lifecycle of customer orders — from receipt through credit check, inventory commitment, picking/packing, shipping, and invoicing — supported by OMS (Order Management Systems), ERP, and WMS
C.The management of purchase orders only
D.A customer portal for checking order status
Order management encompasses order-to-cash: order entry (EDI, web, manual), credit check, available-to-promise check, order confirmation, fulfillment instructions to WMS, shipment tracking, and invoice generation. OMS integrates with ERP (finance) and WMS (execution).
Question 11
What is 'key account management' (KAM) in supply chain context?
A.Managing the company's most important financial accounts
B.A dedicated approach for managing strategic, high-value customer relationships — with dedicated account teams, customized supply chain solutions, joint business planning, and closer integration of systems and processes between the supplier and key customer
C.A management system for large inventory accounts
D.The process of handling VIP customer complaints
KAM recognizes that strategic customers warrant different treatment: dedicated teams with supply chain expertise, tailored service agreements, joint planning processes, shared data visibility, and supplier resources invested specifically in the key account's success.
Question 12
What is 'customer churn' and what is its supply chain impact?
A.Product waste that occurs during customer fulfillment
B.The rate at which customers stop doing business with a company — supply chain failures (poor OTIF, stockouts, order errors) are a leading cause. High churn requires expensive customer acquisition to replace lost revenue
C.The physical mixing of products in a warehouse
D.Customer returns that occur after product use
Supply chain performance is a major driver of customer churn: stockouts, late deliveries, and order errors directly drive customers to switch suppliers. Since acquiring a new customer costs 5-7× more than retaining one, supply chain investment in service reliability creates measurable financial returns.
