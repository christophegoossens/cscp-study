What is a Supply Chain?
ASCM definition of Supply Chain: The network of suppliers that deliver products from raw materials to end customers through either an engineered or transactional flow of information, goods, and money.
ASCM definition of Supply Chain Management: The design, planning, execution, control, and monitoring of supply chain activities with the objective of creating net value, building a competitive infrastructure, leveraging worldwide logistics, synchronizing supply with demand, and measuring performance globally.

Supply chains can take many forms — from a simple linear chain to a complex global network. They apply equally to product companies and service companies.

Echelon: A level of supply chain nodes. Example: a supply chain with factory warehouses → wholesale warehouses → retail stores has three echelons between the factory and the end customer.
Channel master: The dominant company that organizes and leads the supply chain network.
The 4 Flows of a Supply Chain
Every supply chain has four primary flows that connect its entities:
  • Information flow — travels in BOTH directions (upstream and downstream)
  • Strategic requirements flow — from customers upstream (needs drive the chain)
  • Goods & services flow — moves DOWNSTREAM from suppliers to customers
  • Cash flow — moves UPSTREAM from customers back to suppliers
Reverse supply chain: The upstream flow of products returned for refund, replacement, repairs, recycling, remanufacturing, resale, or disposal. Managed by reverse logistics.
Cash-to-cash cycle time (cash conversion cycle): Measures how long it takes for cash invested in the supply chain to return as revenue. Shorter is better for working capital.
Vertical vs. Lateral Integration
Vertical integration: Bringing supply chain activities inside one organization (ownership of multiple supply chain stages). Primary benefit: CONTROL. Example: Henry Ford's River Rouge complex.
Lateral (horizontal) integration: Each entity in the supply chain is a separate, independent company linked by contracts. The dominant modern approach. Benefits: economies of scale, business focus, specialization.

Lateral integration replaced vertical as the favored approach because: no single company can excel at all supply chain activities, and independent specialists achieve greater scale economies.

Why lateral integration dominates: (1) Economies of scale through specialization, (2) Better business focus/expertise, (3) Reduced complexity, (4) Leveraged communication technology
Keiretsu: A Japanese hybrid form — companies remain legally and economically independent but work closely through financial bonds, cross-shareholding, and deep collaboration. Examples: Toyota supplier networks.
Outsourcing: Contracting an internal function out to an external provider — a key enabler of lateral integration.
Supply Chain Entities

A basic supply chain includes: Supplier → (Consolidation Warehouse) → Producer → (Distribution Centers / Wholesalers / Retailers) → End Customer

Supply chain value: The goal of supply chain management is to create NET VALUE — not just reduce cost. Early SC management focused too heavily on lean/cost reduction, sometimes reducing customer value.

Managing supply chains requires balancing competing interests between entities — each with their own goals, incentives, and capabilities.

Supply chain management is organized by a channel master that designs, plans, and coordinates the network activities.

Q1 of 8
The ASCM definition of supply chain management emphasizes which primary objective?
The full ASCM definition: 'the design, planning, execution, control, and monitoring of supply chain activities with the objective of creating net value, building a competitive infrastructure, leveraging worldwide logistics, synchronizing supply with demand, and measuring performance globally.' Net value creation is central.
Q2 of 8
In a supply chain, cash flows in which direction?
Cash flows UPSTREAM — from the end customer, back through retailers, distributors, and manufacturers, toward raw material suppliers. Goods flow downstream; cash flows upstream.
Q3 of 8
A supply chain with factory warehouses, then wholesale warehouses, then retail stores has:
An echelon = one level of supply chain nodes. Factory warehouses = Echelon 1. Wholesale warehouses = Echelon 2. Retail stores = Echelon 3. So three echelons between the factory and the end customer.
Q4 of 8
The primary benefit of vertical integration is:
Vertical integration's primary benefit is CONTROL — the organization controls every stage of the supply chain. The main drawbacks are complexity and inability to achieve the scale economies that independent specialists can.
Q5 of 8
Keiretsu is best described as:
Keiretsu = companies remain legally and economically independent but collaborate through financial bonds, cross-shareholding, personnel exchanges, and long-term business relationships. Toyota's supplier network is a classic example.
Q6 of 8
Information flows in a supply chain move:
Information flows in BOTH directions — customer requirements and orders flow upstream; supply status, pricing, and availability flow downstream. Unlike goods (downstream) and cash (upstream), information is bidirectional.
Q7 of 8
Why did lateral integration become the dominant supply chain model?
Lateral integration dominates because: (1) independent specialists achieve scale economies impossible for a single company, (2) focus improves expertise, (3) modern communication makes coordination feasible without ownership.
Q8 of 8
The goal of supply chain management goes beyond cost reduction to:
ASCM emphasizes NET VALUE creation — not just cost reduction. Early SC management over-focused on lean/cost, sometimes reducing customer service and overall value. The goal is a supply chain that creates value for all stakeholders.
