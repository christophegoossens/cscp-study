Sustainability in the Supply Chain
Sustainable supply chain management: Integrating environmental, social, and economic (triple bottom line) considerations into supply chain decisions — to meet today's needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet theirs.
Triple bottom line (TBL): A framework measuring corporate performance on three dimensions: Profit (economic), People (social responsibility), and Planet (environmental impact). Sustainable supply chains optimize all three, not just profit.
Why sustainability matters for supply chains: regulatory requirements (carbon reporting, EPR, deforestation laws), customer/investor ESG demands, cost reduction opportunities (energy efficiency, waste reduction), risk mitigation (climate risk, regulatory risk), and talent attraction.
ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance): A framework used by investors and companies to evaluate business sustainability performance. Supply chains are a major focus of ESG assessment — Scope 3 emissions, labor standards in supply chains, and governance of supplier relationships.
Environmental Sustainability
Carbon footprint: The total greenhouse gas emissions caused directly or indirectly by a supply chain — measured in CO2-equivalent (CO2e). Supply chain transportation and production are major emission sources.
Scope 3 emissions: Indirect emissions across the entire value chain — from suppliers (Scope 3 upstream) and from product use and disposal (Scope 3 downstream). Typically 70–90% of a company's total carbon footprint. Reducing Scope 3 requires supply chain collaboration.
Supply chain carbon reduction levers: mode shift (rail/ocean vs. truck/air), load optimization (fuller loads = fewer trips), nearshoring (shorter distances), renewable energy at facilities, supplier emission requirements, product eco-design (lighter, less material).
Science-based targets (SBTs): Emission reduction targets aligned with climate science — specifically the Paris Agreement goal of limiting global warming to 1.5°C. Companies with SBTs commit to measurable, time-bound reduction in both direct and supply chain (Scope 3) emissions.
Social Sustainability
Supply chain social responsibility: Ensuring that labor standards, human rights, and health and safety requirements are met throughout the supply chain — at direct suppliers and deeper tiers.
Social responsibility risks in supply chains: child labor, forced labor, unsafe working conditions, inadequate wages, discrimination. These risks are most acute in Tier 2/3 suppliers in low-cost countries — beyond where most company audits reach.
Supplier code of conduct: A document specifying the social and environmental standards that suppliers must meet to do business with the company — covering labor practices, human rights, health & safety, environmental management, and anti-corruption.
Supply chain transparency: The ability to see and verify conditions at all levels of the supply chain — including Tier 2/3 and beyond. Increasingly required by regulation (EU Supply Chain Act, US Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act) and demanded by consumers.

TERM / CONCEPT
Triple bottom line (TBL) — 3 dimensions
ANSWER
Profit (Economic): financial performance, profitability, economic value creation People (Social): labor standards, community impact, human rights, diversity Planet (Environmental): carbon footprint, resource use, waste, biodiversity Sustainable supply chain: optimizes all three simultaneously — not maximizing profit at the expense of people or planet. Why it matters commercially: ESG performance affects investor capital access, customer preference, regulatory compliance, and talent attraction.
TERM / CONCEPT
Scope 1, 2, 3 emissions — quick reference
ANSWER
Scope 1: Direct emissions from OWNED operations (own factories, own fleet) Scope 2: Indirect from PURCHASED energy (electricity, steam) Scope 3: ALL other indirect — supply chain and customer use • Upstream (Scope 3): suppliers, purchased goods, transportation • Downstream (Scope 3): product use, end-of-life disposal 70–90% of corporate carbon footprint is Scope 3 → supply chain decarbonization is essential for meaningful corporate carbon reduction.
TERM / CONCEPT
Supplier code of conduct — what it covers
ANSWER
A document specifying standards suppliers must meet: Labor: no child/forced labor, fair wages, reasonable hours, right to organize Health & Safety: safe working conditions, PPE, emergency procedures Environmental: waste management, emissions, hazardous substances compliance Ethics: anti-bribery, anti-corruption, confidentiality Subcontracting: standards cascade to sub-suppliers Enforcement: supplier self-assessment, third-party audits, corrective action requirements. Regulatory backing: increasingly supported by law (EU Supply Chain Act, UK Modern Slavery Act, US UFLPA).
TERM / CONCEPT
Science-based targets (SBTs) — what they mean
ANSWER
Emission reduction targets aligned with the Paris Agreement (limit global warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels). SBTs require companies to commit to: • Measurable % reduction in Scope 1+2 emissions by a target year • Measurable Scope 3 (supply chain) reduction targets • Validated by the Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) Why Scope 3 is included: supply chain emissions (70–90% of total footprint) cannot be excluded if targets are to be meaningful. Forces companies to engage suppliers on emissions.
TERM / CONCEPT
Supply chain transparency — why it's difficult
ANSWER
Challenges: • Most companies audit only Tier 1 — labor violations are concentrated in Tier 2/3 • Subcontracting: Tier 1 subcontracts to uncertified Tier 2/3 without buyer knowledge • Audit fatigue: suppliers overwhelmed with multiple customer audits • Remote locations: difficult and expensive to audit • Structural causes: root cause often in pricing pressure (too-low prices force subcontracting) Solutions: mandatory due diligence laws (EU), blockchain for traceability, shared audit platforms, responsible purchasing practices (pay fair prices).
TERM / CONCEPT
ESG and supply chain — key connection
ANSWER
ESG = Environmental, Social, Governance framework. Supply chain is central to all three: E: Scope 3 emissions (70–90% in supply chain), resource use, packaging waste S: Labor standards in supply chain (child/forced labor, safety), community impact G: Supplier governance, anti-corruption, conflict minerals Investors use ESG scores to allocate capital — poor ESG performance → higher cost of capital or exclusion from funds. Regulation growing: EU CSRD (Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive) requires large companies to disclose supply chain ESG impacts.

Q1 of 5
The 'triple bottom line' framework measures supply chain sustainability performance across:
Triple bottom line (TBL): Profit (economic performance), People (social responsibility — labor, community), Planet (environmental impact — emissions, resource use, waste). Sustainable supply chain management: optimize all three dimensions, not just financial performance. Why commercially relevant: ESG performance now affects investor capital access (institutional investors apply ESG screens), customer preference (B2B buyers demand supplier ESG compliance), regulatory compliance (growing mandatory reporting), and employer brand (talent increasingly prioritizes sustainable employers).
Q2 of 5
Scope 3 emissions are the most critical for supply chain sustainability because:
Scope 3 = all indirect value chain emissions. Scope 1 (own operations) and Scope 2 (purchased energy) are important but typically represent only 10–30% of total footprint. Scope 3 upstream (purchased goods, supply chain transport) = the vast majority. A manufacturer's Scope 3 includes: the emissions from mining the metals in its components, manufacturing the parts at suppliers, and transporting goods. Without Scope 3 reduction (requiring supplier engagement), corporate carbon targets are largely symbolic.
Q3 of 5
A supplier code of conduct primarily serves to:
Supplier code of conduct: the buyer's baseline requirements for doing business. Covers: labor standards (no child/forced labor, fair wages, safe conditions), environmental practices, ethics (anti-corruption), and subcontracting standards. Increasingly backed by law: EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive, UK Modern Slavery Act, US Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act require companies to have and enforce supply chain standards. The code is a starting point — enforcement through audits, corrective actions, and ultimately supplier selection/de-selection is what gives it teeth.
Q4 of 5
Supply chain transparency at Tier 2 and deeper is challenging primarily because:
Supply chain visibility: most companies know their Tier 1 (direct suppliers). Tier 2/3 are often invisible — especially since Tier 1 may subcontract to Tier 2 without the buyer's knowledge or approval. The most severe labor violations (forced labor, child labor, unsafe factories) are often concentrated in informal Tier 2/3 suppliers in remote locations that no one audits. EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive is forcing companies to map and take responsibility for deeper tiers — creating significant compliance complexity.
Q5 of 5
Science-based targets (SBTs) differ from general carbon reduction commitments in that:
Science-based targets: reduction targets validated by the SBTi (Science Based Targets initiative) as sufficient to keep global warming to 1.5°C. Key difference from generic pledges: (1) scientifically calibrated — based on remaining global carbon budget; (2) include Scope 3 — can't claim science-based status while ignoring 70–90% of your footprint; (3) independently validated — not self-declared. Companies with SBTs must engage their supply chains on emissions reduction, since Scope 3 targets are impossible to meet without supplier collaboration.
