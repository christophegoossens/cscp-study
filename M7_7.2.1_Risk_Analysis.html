7.2.1 · Risk Analysis

Qualitative Risk Analysis
Risk assessment: Evaluating identified risks to determine their probability of occurrence and the magnitude of their potential impact — enabling prioritization of mitigation effort.
Qualitative risk analysis: Rating risks on descriptive scales (Low/Medium/High or 1–5) for probability and impact — then plotting on a risk matrix to prioritize. Fast and easy; less precise than quantitative methods.
Risk matrix (heat map): A 2D grid with probability on one axis and impact on the other — each risk is plotted as a dot, creating a visual 'heat map' of the risk portfolio. High probability + high impact = red (priority risks). Low probability + low impact = green (acceptable).
Risk matrix quadrants: (1) High probability, high impact = CRITICAL — mitigate urgently; (2) Low probability, high impact = MONITOR — develop contingency plans; (3) High probability, low impact = REDUCE — process improvement; (4) Low probability, low impact = ACCEPT — monitor, no action needed.
Quantitative Risk Analysis
Quantitative risk analysis: Using numerical methods to estimate risk probability and financial impact — converting risk assessments into dollar values and probabilities.
Expected value (EV): The probability-weighted outcome of a risk event. EV = Probability × Impact (in dollars). Enables ranking risks by expected financial impact for investment prioritization.
Expected Value = Probability × Financial Impact
Monte Carlo simulation: A computational technique that runs thousands of simulated scenarios using probability distributions — producing a probability distribution of possible outcomes rather than a single point estimate. Used for complex supply chain risk modeling.
Value at Risk (VaR): A statistical measure of the maximum expected loss at a given confidence level over a specified time period. Used in financial supply chain risk (currency, commodity price).
Risk Prioritization
Not all risks deserve equal attention — risk analysis enables prioritization of the finite mitigation budget and management attention on the highest-impact, highest-likelihood risks.
Inherent risk: The risk level before any mitigation measures are applied — the 'raw' exposure. Assessing inherent risk establishes the baseline and shows where mitigation investment is needed most.
Residual risk: The risk remaining after mitigation measures are applied — the 'net' exposure. Risk management objective: reduce residual risk to within the organization's risk appetite through cost-effective mitigation strategies.
Residual risk vs. risk appetite: If residual risk > risk appetite → additional mitigation required. If residual risk ≤ risk appetite → mitigation is sufficient, risk is accepted. This comparison drives the risk treatment decision.

Risk matrix — 4 quadrants and appropriate responses
ANSWER
HIGH probability + HIGH impact = CRITICAL: prioritize immediate mitigation LOW probability + HIGH impact = MONITOR: develop contingency plans; accept risk but prepare response HIGH probability + LOW impact = REDUCE: process improvement to reduce frequency LOW probability + LOW impact = ACCEPT: monitor periodically; no significant action needed Risk matrix: visual prioritization tool — plots all identified risks to show which need urgent action vs. monitoring.
TERM / CONCEPT
Expected Value (EV) — formula and use
ANSWER
EV = Probability × Financial Impact Example: 20% probability of a supply disruption causing $500,000 in lost sales and emergency costs. EV = 0.20 × $500,000 = $100,000 Use: compare risk EVs to prioritize mitigation investment. A risk with EV of $100K is more urgent than one with EV of $10K. Also use to evaluate mitigation options: if a $30K investment reduces EV by $80K → worth doing.
TERM / CONCEPT
Inherent risk vs. residual risk
ANSWER
Inherent risk: risk level BEFORE any mitigation. The 'raw' exposure if nothing is done. Residual risk: risk remaining AFTER mitigation measures are applied. Target: Residual risk ≤ Risk appetite If residual risk > risk appetite → need more mitigation. If residual risk ≤ risk appetite → acceptable; accept and monitor. Process: Identify inherent risk → Apply mitigations → Assess residual risk → Compare to appetite → Decide if sufficient.
TERM / CONCEPT
Monte Carlo simulation — what it is and why it's useful
ANSWER
Runs THOUSANDS of simulated scenarios, each time randomly sampling from probability distributions for each risk variable → produces a range of possible outcomes with probabilities. Instead of: 'lead time will be 3 weeks' (point estimate) Monte Carlo gives: '10% chance lead time < 2 weeks; 60% chance 2–4 weeks; 30% chance > 4 weeks' Value for supply chain: models complex interactions between multiple uncertain variables → quantifies probability of stockout, cost overrun, or service failure → enables better risk-informed decisions.
TERM / CONCEPT
Qualitative vs. quantitative risk analysis
ANSWER
Qualitative: • Descriptive scales (Low/Medium/High) • Fast, easy, accessible to non-analysts • Risk matrix / heat map • Less precise — subjective ratings • Good for initial prioritization Quantitative: • Numerical probabilities and dollar impacts • Expected Value, Monte Carlo, VaR • More accurate but requires data and expertise • Better for major investment decisions and financial risk In practice: start qualitative to prioritize, then quantitative for top risks.
TERM / CONCEPT
How to evaluate whether a risk mitigation investment is worthwhile
ANSWER
Mitigation ROI analysis: Without mitigation: EV = Probability × Impact = $100K Mitigation cost: $25K one-time With mitigation: reduces probability 50% → new EV = $50K Net benefit of mitigation: $100K − $50K = $50K reduction in expected loss Mitigation cost: $25K ROI: $50K benefit ÷ $25K cost = 2:1 → worthwhile Principle: invest in mitigation up to the point where mitigation cost = reduction in expected loss.

Q1 of 5
A risk with high probability and high impact should be treated with:
High probability + high impact = critical risk. It's likely to occur AND it will cause significant harm when it does. This quadrant demands immediate mitigation investment — either to reduce the probability (prevent the event), reduce the impact (limit the damage when it occurs), or both. These are the risks most likely to threaten business continuity and should be at the top of the risk management action list.
Q2 of 5
The Expected Value (EV) of a risk with a 15% probability of causing $400,000 damage is:
EV = Probability × Financial Impact = 0.15 × $400,000 = $60,000. Expected value converts risk into a comparable financial basis — enabling prioritization of mitigation investment across different risks. Any mitigation investment costing less than $60,000 that eliminates this risk entirely would have a positive ROI. EV doesn't tell you the risk WILL cost $60K — it tells you, on average across many repetitions of this situation, you'd expect this cost.
Q3 of 5
Residual risk represents:
Inherent risk (before mitigation) → apply mitigations → Residual risk (after mitigation). The residual risk is what the organization is actually living with. If residual risk > risk appetite → more mitigation needed. If residual risk ≤ risk appetite → acceptable and can be monitored. Risk management objective: reduce residual risk to within risk appetite through cost-effective mitigations.
Q4 of 5
Monte Carlo simulation is valuable for supply chain risk analysis because:
Real supply chains have MULTIPLE uncertain variables simultaneously (lead time, demand, yield, carrier reliability) — each with a range of possible outcomes. Monte Carlo: randomly samples from each variable's probability distribution → runs thousands of simulations → produces a probability distribution of combined outcomes (stockout probability, total cost range). Far more realistic than single-point estimates, which give a false sense of precision and miss the tails of the distribution where bad outcomes live.
Q5 of 5
A qualitative risk matrix (heat map) is most useful for:
Risk matrix: plots all identified risks by probability (Y-axis) × impact (X-axis) → creates a visual 'heat map.' Upper right (high prob, high impact) = red/critical. Lower left (low prob, low impact) = green/acceptable. Instantly communicates the risk portfolio to leadership: which risks need urgent attention vs. monitoring vs. acceptance. Not precise (qualitative scales are subjective) — but fast, accessible, and communicates priorities clearly to non-specialists.
