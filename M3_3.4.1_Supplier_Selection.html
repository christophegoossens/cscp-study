The Supplier Selection Process

Supplier selection occurs after strategic sourcing decisions (make-vs-buy, category strategy) are made. It is the first step in the purchasing function.

Supplier evaluation: A formal process of assessing potential suppliers against predefined criteria before awarding business.

Traditional supplier selection focused primarily on: lowest price, quality, and delivery. Modern supply chain thinking requires a broader view — long-term partnership potential, innovation capability, financial stability, and ESG performance.

Modern supplier selection focuses on TOTAL VALUE — not just lowest price. A supplier that is slightly more expensive but delivers higher quality, better flexibility, and innovation capability may have a lower TCO and higher strategic value.
Supplier Evaluation Criteria
Common supplier evaluation criteria (weighted by category importance):
  • Cost: Unit price, TCO, payment terms, cost reduction roadmap
  • Quality: Defect rates (PPM), quality certifications (ISO 9001, IATF 16949), quality systems
  • Delivery: On-time delivery rate, lead time, scheduling flexibility
  • Capacity: Current capacity and ability to scale with demand
  • Financial stability: Credit rating, years in business, ownership structure
  • Technology/innovation: R&D capability, product development collaboration
  • Sustainability/ESG: Environmental certifications, labor practices, ethical sourcing
  • Location/logistics: Geographic proximity, transportation costs, customs risk
Supplier scorecard: A structured evaluation tool that rates suppliers across multiple criteria with defined weights. Enables objective, comparable assessment of multiple suppliers.
Total Cost of Ownership (TCO): Used as a selection criterion to compare the true all-in cost of sourcing from each supplier — not just the quoted price.
Supplier Approval and Qualification
Approved Vendor List (AVL): A list of suppliers that have been formally evaluated and approved to supply specific items or categories. Purchasing should only buy from approved suppliers.

Supplier qualification steps typically include:

  • Financial and business review (stability, references)
  • Technical capability assessment (facility audit, process capability)
  • Quality system audit (ISO certifications, corrective action process)
  • Sample/prototype approval (first article inspection)
  • Trial production run and performance monitoring
Qualification is a one-time gate — ongoing performance management (via supplier scorecards and reviews) determines whether the supplier maintains approved status.

TERM / CONCEPT
Traditional vs. modern supplier selection criteria
ANSWER
Traditional: price, quality, delivery (transactional focus). Modern: TCO, financial stability, innovation capability, flexibility, ESG performance, strategic partnership potential. Modern = long-term total value, not just lowest price.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a supplier scorecard?
ANSWER
A structured evaluation tool rating suppliers across multiple weighted criteria (cost, quality, delivery, flexibility, innovation, ESG). Enables objective comparison and ongoing performance tracking — not just one-time selection.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is an Approved Vendor List (AVL)?
ANSWER
A formally maintained list of suppliers that have been evaluated and approved to supply specific items/categories. Purchasing should only place orders with approved suppliers — the AVL enforces supply base governance.

Supplier qualification steps
ANSWER
1) Financial/business review 2) Technical capability assessment (facility audit) 3) Quality system audit (ISO certs, corrective action) 4) Sample/prototype approval (first article inspection) 5) Trial production + ongoing performance monitoring
TERM / CONCEPT
Why does modern supply chain thinking require more than lowest-price selection?
ANSWER
Low price may come with hidden TCO costs (quality failures, long lead times, inflexibility, financial risk). A slightly higher-priced supplier with better quality, flexibility, and innovation may have lower TCO and create more long-term competitive value.

Q1 of 5
A supplier quotes the lowest price but has a history of 5% defect rates and frequent late deliveries. Using TCO-based selection, this supplier:
TCO-based selection: low price + 5% defect rate = high rework/scrap costs + quality inspection costs + customer service failures. Late deliveries = expediting costs + customer satisfaction damage. These hidden costs may far exceed the price savings.
Q2 of 5
An Approved Vendor List (AVL) is used to:
The AVL is a governance tool — it defines which suppliers are qualified to supply each item/category. Purchasing should not buy from non-approved suppliers without going through the qualification process first.
Q3 of 5
A company is selecting a supplier for a critical aerospace component. Which evaluation criteria should be given the HIGHEST weight?
For safety-critical components (aerospace), quality is paramount. Quality system maturity (AS9100 certification), process capability (Cpk), and corrective action processes take highest weight — a defective component can cause catastrophic failure regardless of cost or delivery speed.
Q4 of 5
First article inspection (FAI) is typically performed during:
First article inspection (FAI) = evaluating a sample/prototype produced by the supplier to verify it meets all specifications before approving the supplier for full production orders. It is a qualification gate, not an ongoing monitoring activity.
Q5 of 5
Which of the following best represents a STRATEGIC supplier relationship approach?
Strategic supplier relationships = long-term, collaborative, mutual investment. Both parties share information (forecasts, roadmaps), co-develop innovations, and align processes. This drives lower TCO and competitive advantage that purely transactional, lowest-price relationships cannot achieve.
