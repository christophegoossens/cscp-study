What is Continuous Improvement?
Continuous improvement (CI): An ongoing, systematic effort to improve products, services, or processes. The philosophy: there is always a better way — improvement never stops.
Kaizen: Japanese term meaning 'change for better' — the philosophy of small, incremental improvements made continuously by all employees. Kaizen is the cultural foundation of CI; tools like Lean and Six Sigma are the methods.
PDCA cycle (Plan-Do-Check-Act): Also called the Deming Cycle. The universal CI framework: Plan (identify the problem and design a solution) → Do (implement on a small scale) → Check (measure results) → Act (standardize if successful, or restart the cycle).
CI is not a one-time project — it is an ongoing discipline. The PDCA cycle repeats continuously, each loop driving the process to a higher level of performance.
Material Requirements Planning (MRP) as a CI Enabler
MRP (Material Requirements Planning): A computer-based planning system that calculates the quantity and timing of material requirements based on: (1) the Master Production Schedule (MPS), (2) the Bill of Materials (BOM), and (3) current inventory records.
MRP inputs → outputs:
  • Inputs: Master Production Schedule (what to make, when), Bill of Materials (what parts each product needs), Inventory records (what's on hand/on order)
  • Outputs: Planned order releases (when to order), Order recommendations (purchase orders, work orders), Exception messages (overdue orders, capacity conflicts)
Dependent demand: Demand that is derived from the demand for a higher-level item. Example: demand for car tires is dependent on demand for cars. MRP manages dependent demand — it calculates exactly how many components are needed based on the parent item's production schedule.
Bill of Materials (BOM): A structured list of all components, subassemblies, and raw materials required to produce one unit of a finished product — including quantities at each level. MRP explodes the BOM to generate material requirements.
MRP advantage: replaces manual calculations and gut feel with a systematic, data-driven approach to material planning — reducing both shortages and excess inventory.
Lean as a CI Method
Lean manufacturing: A production philosophy focused on eliminating all forms of waste (non-value-added activities) while maximizing customer value. Derived from the Toyota Production System (TPS).
The 8 wastes of Lean (TIMWOODS):
  • Transportation (moving items unnecessarily)
  • Inventory (excess stock)
  • Motion (unnecessary movement of people)
  • Waiting (idle time)
  • Overproduction (making more than needed)
  • Overprocessing (more work than required)
  • Defects (errors requiring rework)
  • Skills (underutilizing people's talents)
Value stream mapping (VSM): A Lean tool that maps the current flow of materials and information from supplier to customer — identifying waste and non-value-added steps. The 'future state' map shows the improved process.
5S: A Lean workplace organization method: Sort (remove unneeded items), Set in order (organize what remains), Shine (clean), Standardize (document the standard), Sustain (maintain discipline). 5S creates the visual workplace foundation for other CI methods.

Dependent demand vs. independent demand
ANSWER
Independent demand: demand for finished goods — driven by customer orders/forecasts (MPS manages this) Dependent demand: demand derived from a higher-level item — component parts needed to build the finished good. MRP manages dependent demand by exploding the BOM. Example: demand for bicycles = independent; demand for bicycle tires = dependent (2× bicycle demand)
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the BOM and how does MRP use it?
ANSWER
Bill of Materials (BOM): structured list of ALL components, subassemblies, and raw materials needed to produce one finished unit — with quantities at each level. MRP 'explodes' the BOM: takes MPS quantity × BOM quantities → gross requirements for each component at each level → nets against inventory → planned orders.
TERM / CONCEPT
8 wastes of Lean (TIMWOODS)
ANSWER
T — Transportation (unnecessary movement of goods) I — Inventory (excess stock) M — Motion (unnecessary movement of people) W — Waiting (idle time) O — Overproduction (making more than needed NOW) O — Overprocessing (more steps/quality than required) D — Defects (errors, rework, scrap) S — Skills (unused employee talent) Memonic: TIM WOODS
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Kaizen?
ANSWER
Japanese: 'change for better' Philosophy of small, continuous improvements made by ALL employees — not just specialists or management. Key principles: everyone improves something every day; small improvements compound; workers closest to the process know best how to improve it. Kaizen is the culture; Lean/Six Sigma/PDCA are the tools that support it.

Q1 of 5
MRP calculates material requirements based on which three inputs?
MRP's three inputs: (1) MPS — what finished goods to produce and when; (2) BOM — what components are needed per finished unit; (3) Inventory records — what's already on hand or on order. MRP combines these to calculate exactly what to order, in what quantity, and when.
Q2 of 5
Demand for engine parts at an auto manufacturer is an example of:
Dependent demand = demand derived from a parent item. Engine parts are needed because cars are being produced — the demand is entirely dependent on the production schedule for cars (the parent). MRP manages this by exploding the BOM: if we're making 1,000 cars, and each needs 1 engine, we need 1,000 engines (less whatever's on hand).
Q3 of 5
Overproduction is considered the worst waste in Lean thinking because:
Overproduction = making more than is needed right now. It's the 'root waste' because it generates all others: excess inventory (I), unnecessary transportation (T) to store it, waiting while it sits, overprocessing to rework it later. Lean's antidote is JIT (Just-in-Time) — make only what's needed, when needed, in the quantity needed.
Q4 of 5
The PDCA cycle's 'Check' phase involves:
PDCA: Plan (design solution) → Do (implement pilot) → CHECK (measure results vs. expected) → Act (standardize or restart). The Check phase is critical — it determines whether the improvement actually worked before committing to full-scale rollout. Without it, organizations implement changes without knowing if they helped.
Q5 of 5
5S is a Lean tool for:
5S = Sort, Set in order, Shine, Standardize, Sustain. It creates a clean, organized, visually managed workplace — a foundational Lean practice. Benefits: reduce time searching for tools/materials, expose abnormalities (problems become visible in an organized space), improve safety, and create the discipline needed for other CI tools (Kanban, standard work) to work effectively.
