Spend Analysis
Spend analysis: A review of all purchases made by the organization during a period — typically retroactive. Used to identify where money is being spent, with which suppliers, and in which categories. The foundation for supply base rationalization.

Steps in a spend analysis:

  • 1. Data preparation: Consolidate supplier databases (across business units), cleanse data, standardize naming
  • 2. Categorize spend: Classify all purchases into spend categories
  • 3. Pareto analysis by spend: Rank categories from highest to lowest spend — identify the 20% of categories that represent 80% of spend
  • 4. Pareto analysis by supplier count: Rank categories by number of suppliers — categories with many suppliers may be candidates for consolidation
  • 5. Pareto analysis by spend per supplier: Categories with low spend-per-supplier suggest fragmented spend with low leverage
A spend analysis answers: Where is the money going? Are we getting volume leverage? Where are we over-supplierized? Where is risk concentrated?
Supply Base Right-Sizing
Supply base: The total set of suppliers a company uses. Supply base management involves continuously evaluating and optimizing the number and mix of suppliers.
Supply base rationalization: The process of reducing the number of suppliers to improve leverage, simplify management, and deepen relationships with the remaining suppliers.
Supply base right-sizing is about finding the OPTIMAL number of suppliers — not the minimum or maximum. Too few = resilience risk. Too many = diluted leverage, high management cost.

Signs of an oversized supply base:

  • Many suppliers per category with low individual spend
  • Duplicate suppliers for the same items across different sites
  • No formal supplier performance management due to volume
  • Inconsistent pricing for same items across sites

Signs of an undersized supply base:

  • Single-source dependency on critical items
  • No competitive pressure on supplier pricing
  • Unable to switch suppliers if one fails
  • All supply risk concentrated in a few relationships
Preferred supplier: A supplier that has been pre-qualified and is given priority for new business in their category, based on demonstrated performance.

Q1 of 5
A spend analysis reveals that a company uses 47 different suppliers for standard fasteners across 6 manufacturing plants. The recommended action is:
47 suppliers for a commodity category = fragmented spend with very low volume leverage per supplier. Rationalization — consolidating to fewer preferred suppliers — increases volume, improves leverage for pricing, and reduces management complexity.
Q2 of 5
In a Pareto analysis of spend by category, the primary goal is to:
Pareto/80-20 principle: a small number of categories typically represent the majority of spend. Spend analysis identifies them so the sourcing team focuses strategic effort where it creates the most financial impact.
Q3 of 5
Which of the following is a RISK of excessive supply base rationalization (too few suppliers)?
Reducing to too few suppliers increases resilience risk — if the remaining supplier fails, there is no fallback. The optimal supply base balances leverage (fewer suppliers) against resilience (multiple options).
Q4 of 5
The first step in a spend analysis is:
Spend analysis starts with data preparation — consolidating supplier databases across business units, standardizing naming conventions (the same supplier may appear under different names), and cleansing errors. Without clean data, the analysis produces unreliable results.
Q5 of 5
A preferred supplier designation means:
Preferred supplier = pre-qualified + priority access to new business. It doesn't necessarily mean exclusive — other suppliers may still be used. The designation rewards good performance and simplifies sourcing by skipping a full RFP for new orders in the same category.
