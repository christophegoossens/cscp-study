Overview of Demand Analysis
Demand analysis: The examination of a firm's demand in relation to its external environment. Demand can be analyzed over long, medium, or short terms.

Demand analysis is needed because strategic, tactical, and operational plans can be derailed by economic shifts, competitor actions, changing customer tastes, or regulatory changes.

Demand analysis time horizons:
  • Long-term: Macro environment analysis — industry trends, GDP, economic cycles
  • Medium-term: Market research, competitive landscape, product lifecycle
  • Short-term: Historical demand data, promotions, seasonality

At long-term strategic level: SWOT analysis, PEST analysis. At medium-short term: market research, internal demand data.

The Macro Environment (PEST)
Macro environment (ASCM): The environment external to a business including technological, economic, natural, demographic, political/legal, and social/cultural factors.
PEST analysis: A framework scanning Political, Economic, Social, and Technological factors in the macro environment. Sometimes extended to PESTLE (adding Legal and Environmental).
Organizations cannot control the macro environment — they can only adapt their strategies in response to it.

Examples of macro factors: currency exchange rates, interest rates, trade policies, demographic shifts, environmental regulations, technological disruption.

SWOT Analysis
SWOT analysis: A strategic tool examining Strengths (internal), Weaknesses (internal), Opportunities (external), and Threats (external).
SWOT in supply chain context: Use strengths to exploit opportunities; use strengths to counter threats; address weaknesses before threats exploit them.

SWOT is most useful at the long-term strategic planning level — when setting supply chain strategy in response to the competitive environment.

Demand Analysis Processes
Key processes supply chain managers perform related to procuring and delivering goods/services:
  • Developing SC personnel's knowledge, skills, and abilities
  • Developing supply chain infrastructure
  • Performing supply and demand planning and scheduling
  • Identifying logistical requirements
  • Managing SC performance
Independent demand: Demand for a finished product not derived from any other item — driven by end-customer decisions. Examples: retail consumer goods, spare parts ordered directly by customers.
Dependent demand: Demand derived from the demand for a higher-level item. Example: demand for tires depends on demand for cars. Calculated through MRP/BOM explosion — not forecasted independently.
Derived demand: Similar to dependent demand — demand for a product or service is derived from demand for another product (e.g., steel demand derived from automotive demand).
Correctly identifying whether demand is independent or dependent is critical for choosing the right planning methodology (forecast-driven vs. MRP-driven).
Market Research Tools
Market research: Systematic collection and analysis of data about target markets, customers, and competitors to inform demand planning.
Market research methods include: customer surveys, focus groups, sales force input, trade data analysis, POS (point-of-sale) data analysis.
Product lifecycle: The stages a product passes through from introduction → growth → maturity → decline. Demand analysis should account for the current lifecycle stage when forecasting.
SKU (Stock Keeping Unit): A unique identifier for each distinct product and its attributes (size, color, packaging). The fundamental unit of demand management.

Independent demand vs. Dependent demand
ANSWER
Independent: demand driven by end customers, not derived from another item (must be forecasted). Dependent: demand derived from a higher-level item — calculated via MRP/BOM, not forecasted independently.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is derived demand?
ANSWER
Demand for a material or component that is derived from demand for a higher-level product. Example: steel demand is derived from automotive production demand. Related to dependent demand.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 3 time horizons of demand analysis?
ANSWER
1) Long-term: macro environment, GDP, industry trends, SWOT/PEST 2) Medium-term: market research, competitive dynamics, product lifecycle 3) Short-term: historical demand, promotions, seasonality patterns
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a product lifecycle and why does it matter for demand analysis?
ANSWER
The 4 stages: Introduction → Growth → Maturity → Decline. Demand characteristics differ at each stage. Forecasting methods and supply chain strategies should adapt accordingly.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is an SKU?
ANSWER
Stock Keeping Unit — a unique identifier for each distinct product variant (including attributes like size, color, packaging). The fundamental unit of demand management and inventory tracking.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why must organizations analyze the macro environment?
ANSWER
Because strategic and operational plans can be derailed by external factors the company cannot control — economic shifts, regulatory changes, competitor actions, demographic changes. Analysis enables proactive adaptation.

Q1 of 6
Demand for automobile tires at a manufacturer is an example of:
Tire demand at an auto manufacturer is DEPENDENT demand — it is derived from (depends on) the production schedule for finished cars. It is calculated through BOM explosion via MRP, not forecasted independently.
Q2 of 6
SWOT analysis is most useful at which planning level?
SWOT analysis examines internal strengths/weaknesses and external opportunities/threats — making it most appropriate for long-term STRATEGIC planning, where organizations set direction in response to their competitive environment.
Q3 of 6
The PEST framework helps supply chain managers analyze:
PEST (Political, Economic, Social, Technological) is a macro-environment scanning tool. It helps SC managers understand external forces that could affect demand — trade policies, economic cycles, demographic shifts, technology disruption.
Q4 of 6
A consumer products company wants to forecast demand for a new product. This is an example of:
Finished consumer products face INDEPENDENT demand — driven by end-customer choices, not derived from any other item. Independent demand must be forecasted (qualitatively for new products, quantitatively for established ones).
Q5 of 6
Why is correctly identifying independent vs. dependent demand critical in supply chain planning?
Independent demand must be FORECASTED using statistical/qualitative methods. Dependent demand is CALCULATED via BOM explosion in MRP — no forecasting needed. Using the wrong method leads to planning errors.
Q6 of 6
A company notices that demand for its components follows the production schedule of its major customer. This is:
When a component supplier's demand follows a customer's production schedule, it is DERIVED/DEPENDENT demand. The correct approach is to collaborate with the customer (e.g., through CPFR or VMI) rather than independently forecasting.
