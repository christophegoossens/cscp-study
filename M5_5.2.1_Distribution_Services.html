Distribution Network Design
Distribution: The activities involved in delivering products from manufacturer to end customer — encompassing warehousing, transportation, order management, and channel management.
Distribution channel: The path a product takes from producer to end consumer. Can be: direct (manufacturer → consumer), or indirect (manufacturer → distributor/wholesaler → retailer → consumer). More channel steps = more cost but potentially broader reach.
Distribution network design decisions: number and location of DCs, make-or-buy (own vs. outsource), inventory positioning (where to hold stock), and transportation mode mix.
Direct distribution: Manufacturer ships directly to end customer — eliminating intermediaries. Reduces markup cost, provides direct customer relationship, but requires logistics infrastructure and is challenging at scale.
Indirect distribution: Uses intermediaries (distributors, wholesalers, retailers) — sacrifices margin for reach, reduced logistics complexity, and faster market penetration.
Order Fulfillment and Service Levels
Order cycle time: The elapsed time from when a customer places an order to when they receive it. A key customer service metric — shorter = competitive advantage.
Order cycle time components: order transmission + order processing + order picking/packing + transportation + customer receiving. Each step can be a source of delay or variability.
Perfect Order: An industry metric measuring the percentage of orders that are delivered on time, in full, damage-free, with correct documentation, and correctly invoiced. Perfect Order % = (On Time %) × (In Full %) × (Damage Free %) × (Invoice Accuracy %).
Perfect Order % = % On Time × % In Full × % Damage Free × % Invoice Accurate
The Perfect Order metric is multiplicative — four 99% scores give only ~96% perfect order performance. Small improvements across all dimensions compound significantly.
Omnichannel and E-Commerce Distribution
Omnichannel distribution: Seamlessly fulfilling orders from multiple channels (store, website, mobile, marketplace) using the same inventory pool — customers can order from any channel and receive or return through any channel.
Omnichannel challenges: inventory visibility across all locations, faster fulfillment expectations (same-day/next-day), higher return rates (e-commerce), and more complex transportation (individual parcels vs. pallets).
Last-mile delivery: The final leg of delivery from a distribution point to the end customer. The most expensive and complex part of e-commerce logistics — parcel carriers, gig delivery, and locker networks are competing solutions.
Ship-from-store: Using retail store inventory to fulfill online orders — reduces last-mile distance and cost by leveraging proximity to customers, but adds operational complexity to stores.

Direct vs. indirect distribution channels
ANSWER
Direct: Manufacturer → Consumer ✓ Higher margin (no intermediary markup) ✓ Direct customer relationship/data ✗ Requires own logistics infrastructure ✗ Harder to reach scale Indirect: Manufacturer → Distributor → Retailer → Consumer ✓ Broader reach and scale ✓ Reduced logistics complexity ✗ Lower margin (intermediary takes markup) ✗ Less direct customer visibility
TERM / CONCEPT
Order cycle time — components
ANSWER
1. Order transmission (customer to seller) 2. Order processing (system + credit check) 3. Order picking and packing 4. Carrier pickup / transportation 5. Customer receiving and acceptance Total = order cycle time. Reducing any component shortens cycle time. Consistency (low variability) in each step is as important as speed.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is omnichannel distribution?
ANSWER
Fulfilling customer orders from multiple channels (physical store, website, app, marketplace) using a shared, visible inventory pool — with flexible fulfillment and return options. Customer can: buy online / pick up in store (BOPIS), buy in store / ship to home, return online purchase to store. Requires: real-time inventory visibility across all nodes, unified OMS (Order Management System), flexible fulfillment execution.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is last-mile delivery and why is it expensive?
ANSWER
Last mile = final delivery from a distribution point to the end customer's door. Why expensive: (1) Many small individual stops vs. few bulk stops; (2) Failed deliveries (no one home); (3) Urban congestion; (4) Labor-intensive; (5) Customer expectation of speed (same/next day). Solutions: parcel carriers (UPS, FedEx), crowd-sourced delivery (DoorDash, Instacart), package lockers, ship-from-store (reduces distance).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a distribution channel and why does it matter strategically?
ANSWER
The path a product follows from producer to end consumer (including all intermediaries). Strategic importance: • Channel choice affects cost, reach, margin, and customer relationships • Disintermediation (cutting out intermediaries) = higher margin + direct relationships, but requires own logistics • Adding channels = more reach but complexity • Channel conflict: if selling direct AND through retailers, retailers may object to competing with the manufacturer

Q1 of 5
If a company's order fulfillment performance is: 95% on time, 98% in full, 99% damage-free, and 97% invoice accurate, its Perfect Order rate is approximately:
Perfect Order % = 0.95 × 0.98 × 0.99 × 0.97 = approximately 0.895 = ~89.5%. This illustrates the multiplicative effect: even though no individual metric is catastrophically bad, the combined result is that roughly 1 in 10 orders has something wrong. Improving each dimension by even 1–2% significantly lifts the combined score.
Q2 of 5
A manufacturer that sells directly to consumers through its own website is using:
Direct distribution = manufacturer to end consumer with no intermediary (no distributor, wholesaler, or retailer). Benefits: higher margin (no intermediary markup), direct customer data and relationship, control over brand experience. Challenges: requires building own logistics/fulfillment capability, harder to achieve the physical reach of retail chains.
Q3 of 5
The most expensive component of e-commerce logistics is typically:
Last-mile delivery (final delivery to customer's door) is the most expensive part of e-commerce logistics — typically accounting for 40–50% of total delivery cost. Why: many individual stops vs. bulk delivery, failed delivery attempts, customer expectation of speed, labor-intensive. It's why Amazon invested heavily in its own delivery network and why drone/autonomous delivery innovation is focused on last-mile.
Q4 of 5
Omnichannel fulfillment requires which critical operational capability?
Omnichannel requires a single, real-time view of inventory across all locations (DCs, stores, in-transit) — so any order from any channel can be fulfilled from the most efficient location. Without unified inventory visibility, orders are accepted that can't be filled, or inventory is siloed per channel (inefficient). An Order Management System (OMS) typically provides this unified view.
Q5 of 5
Adding an intermediary (distributor) to a distribution channel typically:
Intermediaries (distributors, wholesalers) take a margin for their services — reducing the manufacturer's per-unit profit. But they provide value: they cover more geographic territory, carry inventory (reducing manufacturer's inventory), handle customer relationships, and aggregate demand from many end customers. The manufacturer trades margin for reach and reduced logistics complexity. Whether that trade-off is worth it depends on the market, volume, and competitive dynamics.
