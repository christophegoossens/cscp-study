The Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA) Model
PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Act/Action): A four-step iterative process for quality improvement. Also called the Deming Cycle. Used in demand management to continually adapt influencing activities.
  • Plan phase: Develop detailed strategies and tactics for influencing demand. Set budgets, schedules, and accountability. Define measurable targets.
  • Do phase: Execute the plans — launch products, run promotions, activate sales force, implement pricing changes.
  • Check phase: Review metrics vs. plan. Determine root causes of variances. Collect customer feedback on pricing, features, service.
  • Act/Action phase: Replan to address variances and root causes. Increase or decrease investment in activities based on what is and isn't working.
PDCA is iterative — after the Action phase, the cycle restarts with a new or adjusted Plan. It drives continuous learning and improvement.

PDCA applies not just to demand influencing but to any process improvement — including supply chain process management.

The 4Ps of Marketing (Demand Levers)
The 4Ps — also called the marketing mix — are the primary levers for influencing demand:
  • Product: What you offer — features, quality, design, branding, packaging, lifecycle management. New features or better quality can stimulate demand.
  • Price: What you charge — list price, discounts, payment terms, financing options. Price promotions can spike demand; premium pricing can restrict it.
  • Placement (Distribution/Place): Where and how the product is available — channels (online, retail, direct), geographic availability, lead times, convenience.
  • Promotion: How you communicate — advertising, sales promotions, PR, social media, trade shows. Drives awareness and purchase intent.
Demand generation: Activities that create new demand where none existed or stimulate latent demand — primarily driven by Product and Promotion.
Demand shaping: Activities that redirect existing demand — shifting customers to different products, time periods, or channels — often using Price and Placement.
Demand Influencing in Practice
Influencing demand helps align demand with supply capabilities — reducing the cost of mismatches.

Examples of demand influencing: Offering discounts to stimulate demand during slow periods. Raising prices or discontinuing promotions to reduce demand when supply is constrained. Offering product substitutes when primary SKU is unavailable.

Price elasticity: How sensitive demand is to price changes. High elasticity = demand changes significantly with price. Low elasticity = demand is relatively unchanged. Critical input for demand influencing decisions.

Trade promotion management: Managing promotions with retail partners (deal structures, timing, lift expectations) to maximize ROI on promotional spend.

Risk of demand influencing: It can create the Bullwhip Effect if promotions drive artificial demand spikes. Supply planners must be informed of planned promotions in advance.

What are the 4 steps of PDCA?
ANSWER
Plan: develop strategies, set targets and budgets Do: execute the plans Check: review metrics vs. plan, find root causes of variance Act: replan to address root causes and variances. Then repeat.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 4Ps of Marketing?
ANSWER
Product (what you offer), Price (what you charge), Placement/Place (where/how it's available), Promotion (how you communicate). Together = the marketing mix for influencing demand.
TERM / CONCEPT
Demand generation vs. demand shaping
ANSWER
Demand generation: creates NEW demand where none existed (typically via Product and Promotion). Demand shaping: redirects EXISTING demand — different time, product, or channel (typically via Price and Placement).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the 'Check' phase of PDCA used for?
ANSWER
Review metrics against the plan. Identify root causes of variances (why did results differ from plan?). Collect customer feedback on pricing, features, service. This feeds the Act phase with insights for replanning.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does PDCA help with demand influencing?
ANSWER
It creates a structured, iterative improvement cycle: plan campaigns → execute → measure results → diagnose what worked/didn't → adjust the plan. Prevents repeating the same mistakes and drives continuous improvement.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why must supply planners be informed of planned promotions?
ANSWER
Promotions drive artificial demand spikes that look like the Bullwhip Effect if not anticipated. Supply planners need advance notice to build inventory, adjust production schedules, and secure carrier capacity.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is price elasticity and why does it matter for demand influencing?
ANSWER
How sensitive demand is to price changes. High elasticity = a 10% price cut drives large demand increase (use pricing to shape demand effectively). Low elasticity = pricing has little demand impact (focus on other levers).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is trade promotion management?
ANSWER
Managing promotional deals with retail partners — deal terms, timing, expected demand lift, financial ROI analysis. Goal: maximize the effectiveness of promotional spend while minimizing supply chain disruption.

Q1 of 5
A company runs a '20% off' promotion that drives a massive spike in customer orders but leaves the company with a stockout afterward. To prevent this in the future, which PDCA phase should improve?
The PLAN phase should have involved better coordination between marketing (promotion) and supply chain (capacity/inventory readiness) BEFORE execution. The supply chain should have been informed and inventory built up in advance.
Q2 of 5
A company wants to shift customers from its most popular (but constrained) product to a similar in-stock product. Which demand influencing approach is most appropriate?
Demand shaping redirects EXISTING demand — in this case, using pricing (discount on alternative) or placement (featuring the alternative prominently) to move customers from the constrained SKU to available inventory.
Q3 of 5
In the 4Ps model, which 'P' primarily concerns how, where, and through which channels products are made available to customers?
Placement (also called Place or Distribution) determines where and how products are available — retail channels, online, direct, geographic reach, convenience. It is a key lever for demand shaping.
Q4 of 5
A company's marketing team runs a promotion without telling the supply chain team. What is the most likely consequence?
Uncoordinated promotions create demand spikes the supply chain cannot absorb — leading to stockouts, emergency production, overtime costs, expedited shipping, or poor service. Marketing and supply chain MUST communicate planned promotions in advance.
Q5 of 5
The 'Act' phase of PDCA in demand management involves:
The Act phase = REPLAN based on insights from the Check phase. It involves adjusting strategies, reallocating budget from ineffective to effective tactics, and addressing root causes of performance gaps. Then the cycle restarts.
