Free Trade Agreements and Trade Blocs
Free Trade Agreement (FTA): A treaty between two or more countries eliminating or reducing tariffs, quotas, and trade barriers on goods exchanged between them — promoting trade and economic integration.
Major trade blocs and agreements relevant to CSCP:
  • USMCA (United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement): Replaced NAFTA in 2020 — free trade between the US, Canada, and Mexico
  • EU (European Union): Single market — free movement of goods, services, capital, and people among 27 member states; common external tariff
  • RCEP (Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership): World's largest FTA — 15 Asia-Pacific nations including China, Japan, South Korea, ASEAN
  • CPTPP (Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership): 11 Pacific Rim countries excluding the US
Rules of origin: Criteria that determine whether a product qualifies for preferential tariff treatment under an FTA — typically requiring that a minimum percentage of the product's value is added within the FTA partner countries.
Trade Zones
Free Trade Zone (FTZ) / Foreign Trade Zone: A designated geographic area within a country where goods can be imported, stored, handled, manufactured, or reconfigured without paying customs duties — until goods enter domestic commerce.
Export Processing Zone (EPZ): A special zone focused on manufacturing for export — companies in EPZs receive tax and regulatory benefits in exchange for exporting the majority of their production. Common in developing countries to attract foreign investment.
Special Economic Zone (SEZ): A broader version of EPZ — offers tax holidays, reduced regulation, and business incentives across multiple industries. China's SEZs (Shenzhen, Shanghai Pudong) played a key role in China's industrial development.
Bonded warehouse: A secure storage facility where imported goods can be held without paying duties until they are either entered into domestic commerce (duties paid) or re-exported duty-free. Provides duty deferral and cash flow benefits.
Impact on Supply Chain Strategy
Trade agreements shape supply chain design: companies locate manufacturing in FTA partner countries to qualify for preferential tariff rates, reducing product landed cost.
Trade policy risk: tariffs and trade agreements change — supply chains built around specific trade preferences become vulnerable when agreements change or tariffs are imposed. Companies must monitor trade policy as a strategic risk.
Country of origin determination: Complex rules govern where a product 'comes from' for tariff purposes — substantial transformation test (was the product fundamentally changed in the last country?) or specific percentage of value addition. Critical for FTA qualification.
Nearshoring trend: companies shifting manufacturing from Asia to USMCA countries (Mexico) to reduce tariff exposure, improve supply chain resilience, and lower transportation costs and lead times — driven partly by trade policy risk.

TERM / CONCEPT
USMCA, EU, RCEP — what are they?
ANSWER
USMCA: US-Mexico-Canada Agreement (replaced NAFTA 2020) — free trade across North America EU: 27-member single market — free movement of goods/services/capital/people; common external tariff to non-members RCEP: Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership — 15 Asia-Pacific nations (China, Japan, S. Korea, ASEAN + others); world's largest FTA by GDP For CSCP: know these shape where companies manufacture to minimize tariffs.
TERM / CONCEPT
Rules of origin — what are they and why do they matter?
ANSWER
Rules of origin = criteria defining whether a product qualifies for preferential FTA tariff rates. Typically require: minimum % of product value added within FTA partner countries, OR specific manufacturing process performed in-country. Why it matters: A Chinese company can't just ship goods through Mexico to get USMCA tariff rates — the product must have sufficient North American content. Supply chain implication: sourcing decisions are influenced by rules of origin to preserve FTA benefits.
TERM / CONCEPT
Free Trade Zone (FTZ) — key benefits
ANSWER
Goods can be imported into an FTZ without paying customs duties. Duties only paid when goods leave FTZ and enter domestic commerce. Benefits: 1. Duty deferral (pay later = cash flow benefit) 2. Inverted tariff benefit: if components have higher duty than finished goods, manufacture in FTZ and pay finished goods rate 3. Re-export duty-free (if goods are exported from FTZ, no duty paid) 4. Duty reduction on scrap/waste (pay duty only on what enters commerce, not on waste from production)
TERM / CONCEPT
EPZ vs. SEZ
ANSWER
Export Processing Zone (EPZ): manufacturing zone — tax/regulatory benefits in exchange for exporting most production. Common in developing nations to attract foreign manufacturing investment. Special Economic Zone (SEZ): broader concept — tax holidays, reduced regulation across multiple industries and activities. China's SEZs (Shenzhen 1980) were a key tool for economic development by attracting FDI with favorable conditions. Both: reduce cost of doing business in specific geographic areas.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a bonded warehouse?
ANSWER
A licensed, secure facility where imported goods can be held without paying customs duties. Goods can: (1) Enter domestic commerce upon payment of duties, OR (2) Be re-exported without paying any duties, OR (3) Be held while awaiting customs processing or sale Benefit: duty deferral until goods are actually sold. Useful for: seasonal goods, goods awaiting sale/distribution decisions, goods that may be re-exported. Difference from FTZ: bonded warehouse is a storage-only facility; FTZ allows manufacturing/processing.
TERM / CONCEPT
How do trade agreements shape supply chain design?
ANSWER
Companies locate manufacturing in FTA partner countries to qualify for preferential (zero or low) tariff rates → lower product landed cost. Example: USMCA benefit → many companies manufacture in Mexico rather than Asia to sell to US market at zero tariff. Risk: trade policy changes. If tariffs are imposed or FTA renegotiated, the supply chain built around those preferences becomes expensive to restructure. Trend: nearshoring to USMCA countries driven by BOTH tariff benefits AND supply chain resilience (shorter lead times, reduced risk).

Q1 of 5
Rules of origin in a Free Trade Agreement determine:
Rules of origin prevent 'tariff shopping' — a non-FTA country routing goods through an FTA partner to get preferential rates. FTAs require that goods have sufficient 'local' content (% of value added, specific processing, or substantial transformation) to qualify for the lower tariff. Supply chain implication: where you source components and manufacture affects whether you can claim FTA tariff benefits.
Q2 of 5
A company imports components into a Free Trade Zone, assembles finished goods, and re-exports to customers in other countries. The customs duty impact is:
FTZ benefit: goods enter the zone without paying duties. If finished goods are re-exported (never entering domestic commerce), no duty is paid at any point. If goods are sold domestically, duties are paid on the finished goods (not components) when they exit the FTZ — potentially at a lower rate than if each component was dutiable upon import (inverted tariff benefit).
Q3 of 5
A company moves manufacturing from China to Mexico primarily to reduce import duties when selling to US customers. This is an example of:
USMCA (formerly NAFTA) provides zero or near-zero tariffs for qualifying goods produced in the US, Canada, or Mexico. By manufacturing in Mexico (and meeting USMCA rules of origin), the company eliminates the tariff it would pay importing from China — reducing product landed cost in the US. This is 'trade agreement-driven supply chain design' — a legitimate and common supply chain strategy.
Q4 of 5
A bonded warehouse differs from a regular warehouse in that:
Bonded warehouse = licensed facility where imported goods are held under customs supervision without paying duties. Duties are only assessed when goods leave the warehouse to enter domestic commerce. If re-exported, no duty is paid. Benefit: defer duty payment until goods are actually sold (cash flow improvement) and avoid paying duty on goods that will ultimately be re-exported.
Q5 of 5
The RCEP (Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership) is significant because:
RCEP (effective 2022): 15 nations — China, Japan, South Korea, Australia, New Zealand, and 10 ASEAN members. Covers ~30% of global GDP and 30% of world population — the largest FTA by economic scale. It reduces tariffs across the Asia-Pacific region, facilitating supply chains among its member countries. The US is NOT a member. Significant for companies with Asia-Pacific manufacturing or sourcing.
