Sourcing Requirements & Timing

Once make-vs-buy is decided, the organization identifies requirements for potential suppliers. Key sourcing requirements include:

  • Cost to organization and target price to consumer
  • Quality culture and product/service quality
  • Delivery performance (due dates, scheduling, delivery windows)
  • Lead time (slow and cheap to fast and expensive)
  • Available capacity (and ability to change capacity)
  • Design expertise or ability to collaborate
  • Time to market (weeks or months)
  • Sustainability

Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)
Total Cost of Ownership (TCO): in supply chain management, the sum of all the costs associated with every activity of the supply stream.

Cost terminology hierarchy: Purchase Price → + Transportation + Customs + Inventory = Landed Cost → + Process Change + Ongoing Costs = TCO

Exhibit 3-5: Relationship between Cost Terminology (Purchase Price → Landed Cost → TCO)

Landed costs (most important in TCO studies):

  • Purchase price / production cost
  • Transportation cost (including special packaging)
  • Customs and related costs (tariffs, duties, taxes, intermediary fees)
  • Inventory costs (carrying, ordering, backorder)
  • Outsourcing cost
  • Monitoring and control costs (higher when outsourcing)

Process change costs (costs of evaluating and implementing supply chain changes):

  • Requirements identification and research
  • Product development
  • Contract sourcing (search, selection, qualification, legal review)
  • Process change and training
  • Plant openings/closings, hiring/layoffs
  • Supplier education and integration (including IT systems)

Ongoing costs (post-transaction, throughout product life):

  • Life cycle costs (quality, durability, maintainability)
  • Maintenance, repair, and operations (MRO) costs
  • Costs of quality (defects, rework, returns, warranties, field repairs)
  • Sustainability costs (recycling, material recovery)
  • Reputation costs (customer loyalty vs. lost customers)
Exhibit 3-6: Economic Tradeoffs in TCO for Distribution — lowest total cost is often at a different point than lowest unit cost

TCO measures the net effect of all cost increases and reductions. It uses incremental (marginal) costs — only the cost differences between alternatives that matter.

TCO includes both tangible costs (objective measures: market value) and intangible costs (e.g., reputation, relationship quality).

Should-Cost Estimate

A should-cost estimate is an estimate of the cost drivers for a product or service that leads to an estimate of what it should cost. Used when suppliers won’t share actual costs.

  • Cost drivers are elements that strongly contribute to how much something costs: materials, labor, overhead, profit margin.
  • Supply market scanning provides typical cost driver rates by country/region (e.g., government publications on labor rates).
  • Results are compared to supplier quotes or internal costs to assess fairness and identify negotiation leverage.

TERM / CONCEPT
ASCM def: Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)
ANSWER
In supply chain management, the sum of all the costs associated with every activity of the supply stream. Broader than purchase price or landed cost.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is landed cost?
ANSWER
Purchase price + transportation + customs/duties + inventory costs + outsourcing + monitoring costs. The total cost to get goods to the buyer’s facility. A subset of TCO.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 3 categories of TCO costs?
ANSWER
1. Landed costs (purchase price, transportation, customs, inventory, outsourcing). 2. Process change costs (research, training, supplier integration, plant openings). 3. Ongoing costs (life cycle, MRO, quality costs, sustainability, reputation).
TERM / CONCEPT
What are process change costs in TCO?
ANSWER
One-time costs of evaluating and implementing SC changes: requirements research, product development, contract sourcing, training, plant openings/closings, supplier education and IT integration.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are ongoing (post-transaction) costs in TCO?
ANSWER
Life cycle costs, MRO costs, costs of quality (defects, rework, returns, warranties, field repairs), sustainability costs, reputation costs.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a should-cost estimate?
ANSWER
An estimate of the cost drivers for a product/service to determine what it should cost. Used when suppliers won’t share actual costs. Compared to supplier quotes to assess fairness and identify negotiation leverage.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are cost drivers?
ANSWER
Elements that strongly contribute to cost: materials, labor, overhead, profit margin. Used in should-cost estimates and overhead allocation.
TERM / CONCEPT
What does TCO use incremental costs for?
ANSWER
TCO compares the differences between incremental (marginal) costs of alternative SC solutions — only cost differences between alternatives matter, not absolute costs.
TERM / CONCEPT
What types of costs does TCO include?
ANSWER
Both tangible costs (objective measures: market value, purchase price) and intangible costs (reputation, customer loyalty, relationship quality, risk).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the key insight from the TCO economic tradeoff exhibit?
ANSWER
The lowest total cost is often at a different point than the lowest unit cost. Adding distribution points may lower transportation cost but raise total cost through inventory and facility costs.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are key sourcing requirements to evaluate in a supplier search?
ANSWER
Cost, quality, delivery performance, lead time, available capacity, design expertise, time to market, and sustainability.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is TCO difficult to implement across a supply chain?
ANSWER
Organizations have long-standing departmental cost structures. Collecting costs across multiple SC partners is challenging. Intangible costs are hard to quantify. Total costs may span multiple departments.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is monitoring and control cost in landed cost?
ANSWER
The cost of overseeing outsourced or offshore operations, e.g., sending employees abroad to manage relationships, auditing compliance, performance measurement systems. Generally higher when outsourcing.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are costs of quality (in ongoing/TCO)?
ANSWER
Line fallout, defects, in-house or field repairs, rework, returns, warranties. Poor quality creates significant ongoing costs beyond the purchase price.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is reputation an ongoing cost in TCO?
ANSWER
Customer loyalty lost due to poor quality or sustainability practices creates revenue loss. Brand damage from supplier misconduct can have large financial consequences not visible in purchase price.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are customs and related costs in landed cost?
ANSWER
Tariffs (taxes on imported goods), duties, import taxes, and fees for intermediary services (customs brokers, freight forwarders). Significant in global sourcing.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does TCO support the make-vs-buy decision?
ANSWER
TCO makes clear that a lower foreign purchase price may still result in a higher total cost after transportation, customs, inventory, quality, and ongoing costs are included.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a sourcing requirements search?
ANSWER
After recognizing the need for a new supplier, the organization searches the supply market. Search intensity depends on strategic importance: more critical = more intensive research (see Exhibit 3-4).
TERM / CONCEPT
What costs may be excluded from a TCO analysis?
ANSWER
Costs that don’t differ between alternatives may be omitted to simplify analysis (e.g., financing, sales & marketing, insurance, taxes if identical for both options).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is “net cost” in TCO?
ANSWER
TCO measures the net effect of all cost increases and cost reductions — including benefits (cost reductions) achieved by the alternative being evaluated, not just costs added.

Question 1
What is 'strategic sourcing'?
A.Buying whatever is cheapest at the time of need
B.A systematic, data-driven process for analyzing spend, understanding supply markets, and developing supplier strategies that align with business objectives — beyond tactical purchasing
C.Sourcing only from strategic (large) suppliers
D.Purchasing based on historical supplier relationships
Strategic sourcing is a cross-functional, data-driven methodology for managing an organization's external spend — analyzing categories, evaluating supply markets, and developing strategies that deliver optimum value (cost, quality, risk, innovation).
Question 2
What does 'spend analysis' involve in strategic sourcing?
A.Analyzing employee salary expenditure
B.Collecting, cleansing, classifying, and analyzing all purchasing spend data by supplier, category, and location — to identify consolidation opportunities, maverick spend, and strategic categories
C.Auditing supplier invoices for billing errors
D.Calculating the cost of running the procurement department
Spend analysis provides visibility into where money is being spent — identifying the top suppliers, maverick spend (outside contracts), opportunities to consolidate, and which categories warrant strategic sourcing efforts.
Question 3
What is a 'request for information' (RFI) used for?
A.Formally requesting a supplier to submit a price quote
B.Gathering general information about a supplier's capabilities, experience, and market offerings — typically an early step before an RFP, used to qualify suppliers
C.Requesting a supplier to confirm delivery dates
D.Requesting a quality certificate from a supplier
An RFI is an information-gathering document sent to potential suppliers to understand their capabilities, capacity, experience, and technology — used to short-list suppliers before issuing a more detailed RFP.
Question 4
What is the purpose of supplier qualification?
A.Setting the final price for a purchase contract
B.Evaluating whether a supplier has the financial stability, operational capability, quality systems, and ethical practices to be approved as a supply partner before placing significant business with them
C.Determining the quantity to order from a supplier
D.Measuring existing supplier performance
Supplier qualification assesses a potential supplier's ability to meet requirements — financial health, quality systems (ISO 9001), capacity, lead times, labor standards — before committing to a supply relationship.
Question 5
What does 'total cost of ownership' (TCO) include in a sourcing decision?
A.Only the purchase price per unit
B.All costs associated with acquiring and using a good or service: price, quality costs, transportation, duties, inventory costs, switching costs, and supply risk
C.Only the transportation cost
D.Only the quality inspection cost
TCO captures the full cost of a sourcing decision, not just the quoted price. Low-price suppliers with poor quality, long lead times, or high supply risk often have higher TCO than slightly more expensive but more reliable alternatives.
Question 6
What is 'category management' in procurement?
A.Managing products by their storage temperature in the warehouse
B.Organizing procurement around distinct groups of related goods/services (categories), developing category-specific strategies, and managing all spend within the category holistically
C.Categorizing suppliers by their size
D.Managing product categories in retail stores
Category management groups similar spend into categories (e.g., packaging, MRO, logistics services) and develops a tailored strategy for each — rather than managing each purchase independently. It enables more strategic, value-creating supplier relationships.
Question 7
What is 'maverick spend' (or 'rogue spending')?
A.Spending on new, innovative product development
B.Purchases made outside approved contracts, channels, or processes — bypassing procurement controls, typically resulting in higher prices and compliance risks
C.Emergency procurement during supply disruptions
D.Spending on non-core business activities
Maverick spend occurs when users purchase outside contracted channels — losing volume discounts, contract compliance, quality assurance, and spend visibility. It is a key target for procurement process improvement.
Question 8
Which of the following best describes 'supplier diversity'?
A.Sourcing from suppliers in many different countries
B.A procurement strategy that intentionally includes small businesses, minority-owned, women-owned, veteran-owned, and other diverse suppliers in the supply base
C.Having multiple suppliers for every commodity
D.Sourcing a variety of different products from one supplier
Supplier diversity programs intentionally develop and purchase from diverse-owned businesses (minority, women, veteran, LGBTQ+-owned, small businesses) — driven by social responsibility, customer requirements, and often government mandates.
Question 9
What is the difference between a 'single source' and a 'sole source' supplier?
A.They are the same — both mean only one supplier is available
B.Single source = company chooses to buy from one supplier (though alternatives exist); sole source = only one supplier exists in the market (no alternatives available)
C.Single source is used for commodities; sole source is used for specialty products
D.Sole source is always preferred because it simplifies procurement
Single-source is a deliberate business decision (possibly for cost or relationship reasons) with alternatives available. Sole-source is a market reality — no alternative supplier exists, creating significant supply risk.
Question 10
What does 'competitive bidding' (RFQ/RFP process) achieve in sourcing?
A.Guarantees the lowest total cost
B.Introduces market competition among suppliers, providing market price benchmarks, potentially reducing cost, and ensuring the selection is not based on relationship alone
C.Eliminates the need for supplier qualification
D.Ensures the highest quality supplier is always selected
Competitive bidding forces suppliers to compete, revealing market pricing and ensuring the selection decision is informed by market data — not just inertia or relationships. However, lowest bid doesn't always mean best TCO.
Question 11
What is 'supply market analysis' in strategic sourcing?
A.Analyzing customer market demand for products
B.Understanding the supply market for a category: number of suppliers, their capabilities and strategies, competitive dynamics, cost drivers, and market trends — to inform sourcing strategy
C.Analyzing the company's market share
D.Studying competitor pricing strategies
Supply market analysis provides intelligence about the external supply landscape: who the suppliers are, their strengths and weaknesses, market concentration, cost structures, and trends — enabling more informed sourcing strategy.
Question 12
Which sourcing strategy minimizes supply risk for a critical, sole-source component?
A.Switching entirely to a different component immediately
B.Qualifying one or more alternative suppliers to create competition and reduce dependency on the sole source — even if they are not used for routine orders
C.Increasing safety stock to cover 6 months of demand
D.Negotiating a long-term contract with the sole source
Qualifying alternative suppliers for critical sole-source items is the primary risk mitigation strategy. Even maintaining a qualified backup supplier (used for a small % of volume) provides leverage and protection against supply disruptions.
