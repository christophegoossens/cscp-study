Purchase Orders & Invoicing
Purchase Order (PO): the purchaser’s authorization used to formalize a purchase transaction with a supplier. When given to a supplier, it becomes a legally binding contract for both parties.
Blanket Purchase Order: a long-term commitment to a supplier for material against which short-term releases will be generated to satisfy requirements. Often a year, covering multiple deliveries.

PO process: Purchase requisition → approval → PO creation → supplier acceptance → delivery → receiving → 3-way match (PO + receipt + invoice) → payment approval.

Blanket PO benefits: eliminates repeated competitive bidding; negotiates quantity discounts, delivery lead times, quality terms upfront; releases are made against the blanket as needed.

Order tracking functions:

  • Purchasing: primary party tracking open orders; report exceptions.
  • Sales: notify customers of delays.
  • Finance/Treasury: forecast future AP obligations.
  • Accounting: ensure financial record accuracy.
  • Receiving: forecast inbound workload and space.
  • Traffic: schedule carriers to avoid bottlenecks.

Expediting: rushing or chasing orders needed in less than normal lead time. Should be rare in well-managed systems — frequent expediting signals poor forecasting and increases costs. Achieved by asking suppliers to prioritize your orders above others.

E-Procurement: Portals & Trading Exchanges
Portal: a multiservice website providing access to data secured by user role. Users can aggregate data and perform basic analysis. Provides faster access to information.

Types of portals:

  • Consumer portals: personalized home pages, email, shopping — for B2C.
  • Business portals: aggregate and analyze job-relevant data — intranets, extranets.
  • SRM portals: view/react to production changes on supplier availability and capacity — supplier collaboration.

Trading Exchanges: online B2B platforms where buyers and sellers transact. 4 ownership models:

  • Independent Public Trade Exchange (ITX): public marketplace, used for indirect materials and commodities, open to all. Many buyers, many sellers.
  • Private Trading Exchange (PTX): ASCM def: “trade exchange hosted by a single company to facilitate collaborative e-commerce with its trading partners. As opposed to public exchanges, data is secure.” Formed by market-dominant companies. High security, high cost.
  • Consortia Trade Exchange (CTX): ASCM def: “an online marketplace, usually owned by a third party, that allows members to trade with each other. Lowers members’ search and transaction costs.” Some-to-many — within same industry.
  • Virtual Trading Exchange: enables both information integration and e-commerce — deepest integration of systems.

Horizontal marketplace: buyers/sellers from multiple industries (lowers prices through broad competition). Vertical marketplace: buyers/sellers within same industry (lowers prices through industry-specific knowledge).

Auction Types & Exchange Benefits/Risks

5 types of auctions:

  • Classic (forward) auction: one seller, multiple buyers bid UP the price. Highest bid wins.
  • Reverse auction: suppliers bid DOWN the price to win the buyer’s business. Company identities may be concealed. Used for commodities.
  • Dutch auction: one seller, multiple buyers for multiple finite quantities. Price starts high and drops until all units are sold.
  • Demand management auction: clearinghouse to liquidate excess supply (e.g., hotel rooms, airline seats); buyers set their own prices.
  • Stock-market-style auction: dynamic pricing based on buy and sell offers; multiple buyers and sellers for same item simultaneously.

Buyer benefits of trading exchanges: better control over contracts, standard specifications, lower administrative costs, lower unit costs, faster response, improved catalog accuracy.

Buyer risks: lower quality goods, nonconformance to specifications, more rework/returns, long-term loss of skilled suppliers, adversarial relationships.

Supplier benefits: automatic connections, wider market access, faster payment, volume leverage, better catalogs.

Supplier risks: option contracts lock capacity without guaranteed business, tighter margins reduce process investment, risk of being driven out of business, buyer may use seller’s data for lower bids elsewhere.

Question 1
What is a purchase order (PO) and what does it legally represent?
A.An informal request for goods sent via email
B.A formal commercial document issued by the buyer to the supplier authorizing purchase of specific goods or services at agreed prices and terms — it becomes a legally binding contract when accepted by the supplier
C.A forecast of expected purchases for the year
D.A goods receipt confirmation from the warehouse
A purchase order is the formal authorization to purchase. Once accepted by the supplier (by acknowledgment or shipment), it becomes a binding contract committing both parties to the specified terms.
Question 2
What is the '3-way match' in purchase order processing?
A.Matching purchase orders with three different suppliers
B.Verifying that the purchase order, goods receipt note, and supplier invoice agree in terms of quantities, prices, and descriptions before authorizing payment — preventing payment errors and fraud
C.Matching three different quality standards
D.Comparing three supplier bids
3-way match compares: PO (what was ordered), Goods Receipt (what was received), and Invoice (what the supplier claims). All three must agree before payment — preventing overpayment, duplicate payments, and fraud.
Question 3
What is an 'acknowledgment' (PO acknowledgment) from a supplier?
A.A supplier's rating of the buyer's purchasing process
B.The supplier's confirmation that they have received the PO and can fulfill it as specified — may include confirmed delivery dates, or may note exceptions (quantity changes, alternate delivery dates)
C.A receipt for payment of an invoice
D.A quality inspection certificate
PO acknowledgment confirms the supplier received and accepts the order. It may confirm all terms or flag discrepancies — giving the buyer early warning if there are issues with availability, pricing, or delivery dates.
Question 4
What is a 'blanket purchase order' (BPO)?
A.A PO that covers all purchases from all suppliers
B.A pre-negotiated commitment to purchase specified goods from a supplier over a defined period at agreed prices — individual deliveries are called off against the blanket without issuing new POs each time
C.A PO with no quantity limits
D.A purchase order for blankets and other textiles
Blanket POs (frame orders) establish the commercial terms upfront; buyers then issue release orders (call-offs) against the blanket as needed. This reduces administrative cost and provides the supplier with volume visibility.
Question 5
What is 'maverick purchasing' and why is it a problem?
A.Emergency purchasing during supply disruptions
B.Purchasing outside approved contracts and channels — bypassing procurement controls, losing contracted discounts, creating compliance risks, and fragmenting spend visibility
C.Purchasing from international suppliers
D.Large, strategic purchases requiring CEO approval
Maverick purchasing bypasses negotiated contracts, losing discounts, creating compliance and audit risks, fragmenting spend data (making strategic sourcing harder), and exposing the company to liability if unapproved suppliers are used.
Question 6
What does 'e-procurement' mean?
A.Purchasing only through e-commerce websites
B.Using electronic systems (procurement portals, supplier catalogs, workflow tools) to automate and streamline the purchasing process — from requisition through payment — reducing cost and cycle time
C.Electronic communication with procurement staff
D.A type of ERP module
E-procurement automates the procure-to-pay process: electronic catalogs (replacing paper/phone ordering), electronic approval workflows, EDI order transmission, electronic invoicing, and automated payment — reducing cost and cycle time significantly.
Question 7
What is 'purchase order change management'?
A.A process for changing suppliers on existing orders
B.The process of managing modifications to issued POs — quantity, delivery date, specification changes — including impact assessment, supplier notification, and ERP/MRP updates to maintain planning accuracy
C.Canceling orders that are no longer needed
D.Managing the approval process for large purchases
PO changes are common (engineering changes, demand shifts, capacity problems) but must be carefully managed: the supplier must be notified promptly, MRP/ERP must be updated, and impacts on other orders and production plans must be assessed.
Question 8
What is an 'advance ship notice' (ASN) and when is it sent?
A.A notice sent before placing a purchase order
B.An electronic document sent by the supplier shortly before or at time of shipment, detailing the exact contents, quantities, and expected arrival time of the shipment — enabling the receiver to prepare for arrival
C.A shipping request sent by the buyer to the supplier
D.A notification of product quality issues
The ASN (EDI 856) is sent when goods ship, giving the receiver advance notice of exactly what's coming. It enables dock scheduling, pre-allocation of storage locations, and advance receiving — dramatically reducing receiving time.
Question 9
What is 'invoice matching tolerance' in purchase-to-pay?
A.The maximum time allowed for a supplier to submit an invoice
B.A defined percentage or absolute amount by which an invoice can differ from the PO/GR before requiring manual review — allowing small, acceptable discrepancies to process automatically
C.The interest rate charged for late invoice payment
D.The minimum invoice amount that requires management approval
Invoice matching tolerances allow the automated 3-way match to approve minor discrepancies (e.g., ±2% or ±$10) without manual intervention — improving processing efficiency while maintaining controls for material discrepancies.
Question 10
What is 'dynamic discounting' in purchase order payment?
A.Automatically discounting prices based on order volume
B.An arrangement where buyers offer to pay suppliers earlier than standard terms in exchange for a discount — providing working capital benefit to the buyer and early payment to the supplier
C.Changing prices dynamically based on market conditions
D.A discount for electronic invoice submission
Dynamic discounting allows buyers to take early payment discounts on supplier invoices at any time (not just at the 2/10 net 30 standard) — beneficial for both parties when the buyer has excess cash and the supplier needs early payment.
Question 11
What is a 'purchasing card' (P-card) and when is it used?
A.A loyalty card for purchasing department staff
B.A corporate credit card used by authorized employees to make small, low-risk purchases directly — bypassing the formal PO process for low-value transactions, reducing administrative cost
C.A card that lists approved suppliers
D.A card given to suppliers for receiving payments
P-cards are used for small, tactical purchases (office supplies, travel, small MRO) where the formal PO process is disproportionately expensive. They maintain spend controls through transaction limits and category restrictions while reducing paperwork.
Question 12
What is 'consignment inventory' in a purchasing context?
A.Goods shipped by air on consignment insurance
B.Inventory supplied by the vendor to the buyer's facility but not paid for until consumed — the supplier retains ownership until the buyer uses the goods, reducing the buyer's inventory investment and working capital
C.Inventory that has been rejected and returned to the supplier
D.A type of purchase order with no agreed quantity
Consignment inventory transfers the inventory holding risk to the supplier: goods are at the buyer's facility but owned by the supplier. The buyer pays only when consuming them — improving cash flow and reducing inventory investment.
