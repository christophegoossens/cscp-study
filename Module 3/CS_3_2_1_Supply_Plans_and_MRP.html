Supply Planning

A supply plan is the strategic design of a supply network based on TCO, make-vs-buy decisions, and category strategies. Supply plans answer:

  • Centralized global sourcing, decentralized, or hybrid?
  • Seek local sources over cheapest sources?
  • What are our contingency plans for supply risks?
  • Sole-source vs. single-source vs. multi-source suppliers?
  • How do supply plans differ for strategic vs. nonstrategic materials?

Plan validation considerations: corporate strategy alignment, mission/culture alignment, risk assessment, centralized vs. autonomous sourcing tradeoffs, additional optimization, flexibility for future growth.

Flexibility test: measure baseline customer service, output capacity, and quality under different scenarios (increase safety stock = higher cost but flexible; add warehouses = high fixed cost, less flexible).

Category Strategy & Portfolio Analysis

A category is a grouping of purchased goods or services and their suppliers. A portfolio analysis segments sourcing into 4 quadrants based on strategic importance and supply chain difficulty:

Exhibit 3-7: Portfolio Analysis — 4 categories of goods
  • Commodity (low strategic importance, low difficulty): price-driven, many suppliers, buy on market. Strategy: leverage competition.
  • Bottleneck (low strategic importance, high difficulty): few suppliers, risk of supply disruption. Strategy: ensure supply continuity (build inventory, qualify alternatives).
  • Leveragable (high strategic importance, low difficulty): high purchase volume, many sources. Strategy: use purchasing leverage to get best TCO (competitive bidding).
  • Direct/Core Competency (high strategic importance, high difficulty): critical, complex, few sources. Strategy: strategic alliances, collaborative design, long-term contracts.

4 factors for determining strategic importance: strategic importance, complexity, number of available suppliers, supply uncertainty.

Supplier Segmentation & Relationship Types

Customer Desirability Matrix — how much does the supplier need you?

  • Alignment with strategy: are your purchases what they want to sell?
  • % of supplier’s business: core customer = >~20% of supplier’s business — supplier will invest in the relationship.
Exhibit 3-8: Customer Desirability Matrix — 4 quadrants: core, development, nuisance, exploitable

5 types of supplier relationships (spectrum from arm’s length to merger):

  • Buy on the market: transactional, no ongoing relationship, choose freely from all suppliers. Lowest commitment.
  • Ongoing relationship: repeated transactions, medium-term agreements, some predictability. Arm’s length but continuous.
  • Partnership: longer-term contracts; ASCM def: “establishment of a working relationship with a supplier organization whereby two organizations act as one.”
  • Collaboration / Strategic Alliance: ASCM def: “relationship formed by two or more organizations that share proprietary information, participate in joint investments, and develop linked and aligned strategies.” Operates under blanket agreements; partners know each other’s goals.
  • Merger & Acquisition: supplier is folded into the purchasing entity. Full shared business goals and integration.

5 variables that differentiate relationship types: proximity (duration/scope), visibility (information sharing), supplier’s interaction with competitors, communication (touch points/trust), culture (shared values).

What is portfolio analysis?
ANSWER
A sourcing tool that segments purchased goods/services into 4 categories (commodity, bottleneck, leveragable, direct/core competency) based on strategic importance and supply chain difficulty.
TERM / CONCEPT
4 quadrants of portfolio analysis
ANSWER
1. Commodity: low importance + low difficulty → price competition. 2. Bottleneck: low importance + high difficulty → ensure supply continuity. 3. Leveragable: high importance + low difficulty → use volume leverage. 4. Direct/Core: high importance + high difficulty → strategic alliance.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the strategy for bottleneck materials?
ANSWER
Ensure supply continuity — build safety stock, qualify alternative suppliers, and avoid dependency on a single supplier. Risk of shortage is the primary concern even though the item isn’t highly strategic.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the strategy for direct/core competency materials?
ANSWER
Strategic alliances, collaborative design, long-term contracts. These are critical AND difficult to source — relationships must be deep and collaborative.
TERM / CONCEPT
What 4 factors determine strategic importance in portfolio analysis?
ANSWER
1. Strategic importance (can’t afford mistakes). 2. Complexity (process steps, specialized skills). 3. Number of available suppliers. 4. Supply uncertainty (price/availability fluctuation risk).
TERM / CONCEPT
ASCM def: Strategic Alliance
ANSWER
A relationship formed by two or more organizations that share proprietary information, participate in joint investments, and develop linked and aligned strategies. Long-term; often blanket agreements.
TERM / CONCEPT
ASCM def: Supplier Partnership
ANSWER
The establishment of a working relationship with a supplier organization whereby two organizations act as one — with longer-term contracts and aligned goals.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Customer Desirability Matrix?
ANSWER
A tool that classifies your company as a customer from the supplier’s perspective: core (aligned strategy + large %) vs. development vs. nuisance vs. exploitable. Determines which relationship type is achievable.
TERM / CONCEPT
What makes you a ‘core customer’ to a supplier?
ANSWER
1. Your purchases align with what they most want to sell (strategic fit). 2. You represent a large % of their business (~20%+). Core customers get priority, investment, and best terms.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 5 types of supplier relationships (spectrum)?
ANSWER
1. Buy on market (transactional, no ongoing). 2. Ongoing relationship (arm’s length, repeated). 3. Partnership (longer-term, acting as one). 4. Strategic alliance (proprietary info sharing, joint investment). 5. Merger & acquisition (full integration).
TERM / CONCEPT
What 5 variables differentiate supplier relationship types?
ANSWER
1. Proximity (duration/scope). 2. Visibility (information sharing). 3. Supplier’s interaction with your competitors (exclusivity). 4. Communication (touch points/trust). 5. Culture (shared values).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a category in sourcing?
ANSWER
A grouping of purchased goods or services and the suppliers that provide them. Categories are organized by type of item, total spend, and number of suppliers to enable category-specific strategies.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the strategy for leveragable materials?
ANSWER
Use purchasing volume leverage to get best TCO through competitive bidding. Multiple suppliers available so no single-source risk — focus is on extracting maximum value through negotiation.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the strategy for commodity materials?
ANSWER
Price-driven purchasing; buy on the open market from the cheapest qualified supplier. Low relationship investment needed. Use reverse auctions or spot purchasing.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is sole-source vs. single-source vs. multi-source?
ANSWER
Sole source: only one supplier exists (no choice). Single source: one supplier chosen even though others exist (deliberate choice). Multi-source: multiple suppliers intentionally used for risk mitigation and competition.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the supply plan flexibility test?
ANSWER
Test the plan under different scenarios: (1) Increase safety stock — higher cost but flexible. (2) Add warehouses — high fixed cost, less flexible. Results shape contingency plans.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a segmented sourcing strategy?
ANSWER
Creating supplier segments (transactional, preferred, strategic) aligned to portfolio analysis. Different segments get different contract types, information sharing, and relationship investment levels.
TERM / CONCEPT
When should you choose ‘buy on the market’?
ANSWER
For commodity items where many interchangeable suppliers exist, price is the primary criterion, and no relationship investment is needed. Relationship is transactional and non-exclusive.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is an M&A in a supplier relationship context?
ANSWER
When the purchasing organization acquires or merges with the supplier — the highest level of integration. Business goals and areas fully participate together. No longer arm’s length.

Question 1
What does MRP stand for and what is its primary function?
A.Manufacturing Resource Planning — a system for managing factory workers
B.Material Requirements Planning — a system that calculates what materials are needed, in what quantities, and when, based on the production plan (MPS) and bill of materials
C.Master Resource Procurement — a procurement optimization tool
D.Maximum Replenishment Protocol — a safety stock calculation method
MRP (Material Requirements Planning) uses the MPS, BOM, and inventory records to calculate dependent demand for components — determining exactly what to order/produce, in what quantity, and by when.
Question 2
What are the three key inputs to an MRP calculation?
A.Supplier contracts, production targets, and customer orders
B.Master Production Schedule (MPS), Bill of Materials (BOM), and Inventory Records (on-hand + on-order quantities)
C.Sales forecast, safety stock level, and reorder point
D.Demand forecast, cycle time, and supplier lead time
MRP requires: 1) MPS (what finished goods to produce and when), 2) BOM (what components go into each finished good), 3) Inventory records (what's on hand and already on order). These three inputs drive all MRP calculations.
Question 3
What is a 'planned order release' in MRP?
A.A production order that has already been released to the factory
B.An MRP-generated recommendation to issue a purchase order or production order at a specific date, calculated by offsetting the planned order receipt by the item's lead time
C.An automatic purchase order sent to a supplier
D.A customer order that has been confirmed for delivery
MRP works backward from required dates: it calculates when a planned order receipt is needed, then offsets by lead time to determine when the order should be released (placed with the supplier or production).
Question 4
What is 'net requirements' in MRP?
A.Gross requirements from the production plan, before considering inventory
B.Gross requirements minus on-hand inventory and scheduled receipts — the actual quantity that must be ordered or produced to meet demand after available supply is considered
C.The minimum order quantity from a supplier
D.The safety stock quantity for an item
Net requirements = Gross requirements - On-hand inventory - Scheduled receipts. MRP only triggers orders for the net requirement — the actual shortfall after considering what is already available.
Question 5
What is the purpose of a 'time fence' in MRP/MPS planning?
A.A physical fence around the production area
B.Boundaries in the planning horizon that define how easily changes can be made — inside the demand time fence, changes may disrupt commitments; inside the planning time fence, changes disrupt production but not supplier commitments
C.A limit on the maximum forecast period
D.A safety stock buffer for long-lead-time items
Time fences protect the execution environment: within the demand time fence, customer changes are restricted; within the planning time fence, schedule changes require management approval — beyond these fences, planning can change freely.
Question 6
What is 'lot sizing' in MRP and why does it matter?
A.The physical size of containers used in the warehouse
B.The decision of how much to order at a time — fixed order quantity, lot-for-lot, economic order quantity, or minimum order quantity — affecting inventory levels, order frequency, and total cost
C.The number of items in a production batch
D.The safety stock quantity for an item
Lot sizing determines order quantities: lot-for-lot minimizes inventory but maximizes orders; EOQ balances ordering and holding costs; fixed quantities may be imposed by suppliers. The choice significantly affects total cost.
Question 7
What is 'MRP nervousness' and how is it managed?
A.Overloaded MRP computer systems that slow down
B.Excessive rescheduling recommendations generated by MRP in response to small demand changes — managed by time fences, firmed orders, and filters that suppress changes below a minimum threshold
C.Errors in the bill of materials
D.Suppliers delivering orders late
MRP nervousness occurs when small changes in demand cascade into many rescheduling messages throughout the planning horizon. Time fences, order firming, and exception filters help planners focus on significant changes rather than constant micro-adjustments.
Question 8
What is the relationship between MPS and MRP?
A.MRP generates the MPS by aggregating component requirements
B.The MPS defines what finished goods to produce and when; MRP then explodes the MPS through the BOM to calculate component requirements — the MPS drives MRP
C.MPS and MRP are the same process
D.MRP creates demand; MPS fulfills that demand
The MPS (Master Production Schedule) is the input to MRP — it tells MRP what finished products are planned. MRP explodes these requirements through the BOM to calculate all component needs.
Question 9
What is 'safety stock' in MRP context?
A.Inventory kept in a secure location
B.Buffer inventory held above the expected requirement to protect against forecast error and supply uncertainty — MRP considers safety stock as a floor for inventory rather than usable supply
C.Inventory that has passed quality inspection
D.Stock reserved for the most important customers
Safety stock protects against demand variability and supply uncertainty. MRP treats safety stock as a minimum inventory floor — when on-hand falls below safety stock, MRP generates an order to replenish it.
Question 10
What does 'available to promise' (ATP) mean in an MPS/MRP context?
A.The maximum that production can produce in a month
B.The quantity of planned production that has not yet been committed to customer orders — can be promised to new customers without impacting existing commitments
C.All inventory on hand in the warehouse
D.The minimum order quantity from a supplier
ATP is calculated by comparing cumulative planned production to cumulative customer orders. The uncommitted balance (ATP) represents what can be promised to new customers without disrupting the existing production plan.
Question 11
What is 'DRP' (Distribution Requirements Planning) and how does it relate to MRP?
A.A quality management system for distribution centers
B.A planning system that extends MRP logic to the distribution network — using demand forecasts to calculate replenishment requirements at each DC level, generating orders that become demands on the factory or central warehouse
C.A transportation routing optimization system
D.A method for managing distribution costs
DRP applies MRP's time-phased netting logic to the distribution network — calculating replenishment needs at each distribution point based on forecast demand, current inventory, and target safety stock, then aggregating these as supply requirements at the source.
Question 12
When is a 'push' replenishment system appropriate vs. a 'pull' system in supply planning?
A.Push is always better because it enables economies of scale; pull is always wasteful
B.Push (forecast-driven) works best for products with predictable demand; pull (demand-driven) works best for products with variable demand and short lead times — many companies use a hybrid approach
C.Pull is always better because it eliminates forecasting
D.Push is used for raw materials; pull is used only for finished goods
Push works well when demand is predictable and lead times are long (must produce to forecast). Pull works when demand is variable and lead times are short enough to respond to actual orders. Hybrid (push-pull) uses the forecast for planning but actual demand to trigger replenishment.
