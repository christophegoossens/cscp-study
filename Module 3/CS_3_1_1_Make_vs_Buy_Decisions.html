Sourcing & Procurement Process

Sourcing covers strategic decisions (make vs. buy, category strategy, supplier relationships). Procurement is the operational execution (purchase orders, tracking, payment).

7 steps of the sourcing process:

  • 1. Develop/validate the supply plan
  • 2. Research the supply market
  • 3. Create/refine categories and category strategy
  • 4. Develop RFQ/ITT and evaluate responses
  • 5. Select suppliers and negotiate contracts
  • 6. Conduct procurement processes
  • 7. Continuously improve
Exhibit 3-1: Sourcing Process

Strategic purchasing tasks: identify strategic partners, manage sourcing risk portfolio, develop certification standards and supplier improvement programs, conduct make-vs-buy analyses, determine outsourcing/offshoring, negotiate long-term contracts, serve on cross-functional teams, enforce sourcing compliance.

Tactical purchasing tasks: managing supply (planning/procuring based on MPS), issuing POs, tracking orders, resolving discrepancies, monitoring supplier performance.

Make-Versus-Buy Analysis
Make-or-Buy Cost Analysis: a comparison of all the costs associated with making an item versus the cost of buying the item.
Make-or-Buy Decision: the act of deciding whether to produce an item internally or buy it from an outside supplier. Factors: relative expertise, volume, capacity, cost.

3 key questions in make-vs-buy:

  • Is it a core competency? — Activities that are core should generally be kept in-house. Process: (1) Do we have this as core competency? (2) Is there market need? (3) If need exists but not a core competency — develop it or outsource? (4) If outsourcing, shortlist and select partners.
  • What are the consequences of lost skills/knowledge? — Outsourcing may divest valuable expertise. Toyota example: designs transmissions in-house, outsources some production; both designs and manufactures engines (core).
  • What is the landed cost (TCO)? — Foreign suppliers may be cheaper per unit but total landed cost may be higher after transportation, duties, inventory, and risk.
Outsourcing & Offshoring
Outsourcing: the process of having suppliers provide goods and services that were previously provided internally. Involves substituting supplier production for internal production.

Insourcing = using the firm’s internal resources instead of an external supplier (opposite of outsourcing).

Offshoring = outsourcing to another country. First consideration: organizational needs, not lowest labor cost.

Smart contracts use blockchain (distributed ledger) to enable automatic execution of contract terms when conditions are met.

Benefits of contracting out: economies of scale, risk transfer, freed capital for investment, clearer strategic focus, access to new technologies, access to regional markets, faster development cycle times.

Risks of offshoring: failure to achieve cost reduction, supply disruptions, compliance/control cost increase, loss of IP, difficulty enforcing labor/environmental standards, longer lead times, currency risk. Key rule: the organization remains responsible in the eyes of the law and customers even when outsourcing.

What is SRM?
ANSWER
Supplier Relationship Management — the strategic process for managing relationships with key suppliers. May be outsourced to SRM specialists who take responsibility for a region’s supplier relationships.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why can’t you just choose cheapest labor for offshoring?
ANSWER
Lower labor cost countries may also have: less mature organizations, less TQM emphasis, onerous government regulations/bureaucracy, higher compliance costs, and IP protection risks. Total landed cost must be considered.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is an exit strategy in outsourcing?
ANSWER
A formal backup plan prepared in advance for when a new outsourced supplier is needed or the current one fails — to ensure continuity and avoid lock-in.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is landed cost and why does it matter in make-vs-buy?
ANSWER
The total cost to get a product to the buyer’s facility, including purchase price, transportation, customs/tariffs, and inventory costs. A lower purchase price may still result in higher total landed cost.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the “organizations remain responsible” principle?
ANSWER
Even after outsourcing, the brand owner is still accountable for product quality, safety, labor practices, and environmental standards of its supply chain — not just its own operations.

Question 1
What is the primary question in a make-vs-buy decision?
A.Which supplier has the lowest price?
B.Should the company produce a product or perform a service in-house, or outsource it to an external supplier?
C.Which production process is most efficient?
D.How many units should be ordered at a time?
Make-vs-buy (or insource vs. outsource) decisions weigh the relative benefits of internal production (control, IP protection, integration) vs. external sourcing (specialization, lower cost, flexibility).
Question 2
Which factor would most strongly support a 'make' (insource) decision?
A.The activity is a non-core commodity available from many suppliers
B.The activity involves proprietary technology or core competency that provides competitive advantage
C.External suppliers are significantly cheaper
D.The activity requires very low capital investment
If an activity is a core competency or involves proprietary technology, keeping it in-house protects competitive advantage. Outsourcing core activities risks loss of control, IP leakage, and competitive differentiation.
Question 3
Total cost of ownership (TCO) in a make-vs-buy analysis includes:
A.Only the purchase price or manufacturing cost
B.All direct and indirect costs: purchase price/manufacturing cost, quality costs, supply risk, transaction costs, tooling, IP risk, and switching costs
C.Only the variable costs of production
D.Only the labor costs
TCO analysis captures the full cost of each option — hidden costs (quality, supply risk, coordination, tooling) often reverse what appears to be a clear cost advantage on price alone.
Question 4
What is 'core competency' and how does it affect make-vs-buy decisions?
A.Any activity the company performs in-house
B.The activities in which the company has distinctive capabilities that provide competitive advantage — these should typically be kept in-house; non-core activities are candidates for outsourcing
C.The most expensive process in the value chain
D.Activities that can be easily automated
Core competencies are the organization's key differentiating capabilities. Make-vs-buy analysis should protect these (keep in-house) while outsourcing non-core, commodity activities where external specialists can provide better cost or service.
Question 5
What is the primary risk of outsourcing a critical activity?
A.The supplier will always be cheaper
B.Loss of in-house capability and expertise over time, creating dependency on the supplier and vulnerability to supply disruptions or supplier opportunism
C.Suppliers will always deliver on time
D.Outsourcing always reduces product quality
Outsourcing critical activities risks capability erosion — once internal skills are lost, they are very difficult to rebuild. This creates strategic dependency and vulnerability if the supplier fails or raises prices.
Question 6
Which model is used to evaluate the strategic importance and supply risk of outsourcing decisions?
A.Economic Order Quantity (EOQ)
B.Kraljic Portfolio Matrix — categorizing spend/activities by strategic importance and supply market complexity to guide make-vs-buy and sourcing strategies
C.ABC analysis
D.Total Quality Management framework
The Kraljic Matrix categorizes purchases by strategic importance (profit impact) and supply risk, guiding whether to manage as leverage, strategic, bottleneck, or non-critical items — directly informing make-vs-buy decisions.
Question 7
What is 'vertical integration' vs. 'horizontal integration'?
A.Vertical = adding products; horizontal = adding locations
B.Vertical integration = owning upstream suppliers or downstream distributors; horizontal integration = acquiring competitors at the same supply chain tier
C.Vertical = international expansion; horizontal = domestic expansion
D.They are the same concept in different industries
Vertical integration extends the company along the supply chain (backward toward suppliers or forward toward customers). Horizontal integration acquires competitors at the same level — growing market share vs. supply chain control.
Question 8
When does outsourcing to a low-cost country (LCC) sometimes result in HIGHER total cost?
A.When the quality of LCC production is always better
B.When the savings in labor cost are outweighed by longer lead times requiring more inventory, higher freight costs, quality problems, and intellectual property risks
C.When LCC suppliers have better technology
D.When LCC wages rise above domestic wages
LCC sourcing often underestimates total cost: longer lead times require more safety stock (inventory cost), ocean freight adds landed cost, quality issues add rework cost, and IP risks add business risk — sometimes eliminating the apparent labor cost advantage.
Question 9
What is a 'contract manufacturer'?
A.A company that designs and owns its own products
B.A company that manufactures products on behalf of another company (the brand owner) — the brand owner retains design, marketing, and sales; the contract manufacturer provides production capacity
C.A supplier that provides only raw materials
D.A distributor that packages and labels products
Contract manufacturers (e.g., Foxconn for Apple) produce goods to another company's design and specifications. The brand owner outsources manufacturing while retaining product design, marketing, and customer relationships.
Question 10
What is the 'backsourcing' strategy?
A.Outsourcing a new activity to a supplier
B.Bringing a previously outsourced activity back in-house — typically because the outsourcing relationship underperformed, costs increased, or the activity became strategically important
C.Sourcing from a backup supplier
D.Sourcing the same item from multiple suppliers simultaneously
Backsourcing reverses an outsourcing decision. It occurs when the expected benefits were not realized, the relationship deteriorated, costs increased, or the activity became strategically critical — but it is often expensive due to capability erosion.
Question 11
Which of the following best describes 'business process outsourcing' (BPO)?
A.Manufacturing outsourcing to a contract manufacturer
B.Outsourcing entire business processes (e.g., accounts payable, HR, customer service) to a third-party service provider — not just activities but end-to-end process management
C.Outsourcing transportation to a carrier
D.Outsourcing product design to an engineering firm
BPO outsources entire business processes to a specialist provider, not just individual tasks. Common BPO examples in supply chain: accounts payable processing, order management, customer service, and logistics management.
Question 12
What does 'right-shoring' mean in make-vs-buy and sourcing strategy?
A.Locating all operations offshore for cost savings
B.Choosing the optimal location for each supply chain activity based on the best combination of cost, quality, lead time, risk, and strategic fit — rather than defaulting to all onshore or all offshore
C.Only sourcing from countries with trade agreements
D.Moving all operations back onshore
Right-shoring (or best-shoring) is a nuanced approach that evaluates the optimal location for each specific activity — some may benefit from nearshoring, some from LCC offshoring, some from onshoring — based on total value, not just labor cost.
