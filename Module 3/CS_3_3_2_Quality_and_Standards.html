Quality Design Methods
Quality: conformance to requirements or fitness for use. 5 approaches: (1) Transcendent quality — ideal/excellence. (2) Product-based — based on product attribute. (3) User-based — fitness for use. (4) Manufacturing-based — conformance to requirements. (5) Value-based — excellence at an acceptable price.

Quality has 2 major components: quality of conformance (absence of defects) and quality of design (product designed to meet customer needs).

Design for Quality: a product design approach that uses quality measures to capture the extent to which the design meets the needs of the target market.
Design for Six Sigma: an approach to designing products and processes that attempts to ensure the firm can provide products or services that meet six sigma quality levels (3.4 defects per million opportunities).
Quality Function Deployment (QFD): a methodology to ensure all major customer requirements are identified and subsequently met or exceeded through the final design of a product or service.

QFD extends beyond design to operations and support. QFD benefits: shows relative levels of interactions between desired characteristics so they can be prioritized when in conflict.

Customization: Postponement, Mass Customization & Glocalization
Postponement (Delayed Differentiation): a product design or supply chain strategy that deliberately delays final differentiation (assembly, production, packaging, tagging) until the latest possible time, thereby reducing forecasting errors and inventory costs.
Product Differentiation: a strategy of making a product distinct from competition on a nonprice basis such as availability, durability, quality, or reliability.

Postponement is a push-pull strategy: product/components are produced in undifferentiated form (push) until customer demand triggers final differentiation (pull). Reduces bullwhip effect — fewer varieties of safety stock needed.

Mass Customization: the use of mass production techniques to create large volumes of products in a wide variety. Keeps production costs low while enabling product differentiation.

Mass customization example: HP printers — generic base product shipped globally; power supplies and manuals added at local distribution centers for each market. Requires modular design + real-time communication across supply chain.

Glocalization: in a supply chain context, a form of postponement in which a product or service is developed for distribution globally but modified to meet the needs of a local market.

Examples: McDonald’s local menus, GE handheld ECG for rural India (later sold globally = reverse innovation), MTV local music content, Unilever Lipton tea.

Reverse Innovation: product developed for emerging markets (cheaper, simpler) that is then sold successfully in developed markets. Challenges GE’s assumption that developing countries follow the same evolution as wealthy ones.

Sustainable Design
Design for the Environment (DFE): considering health, safety, and environmental aspects of a product during the design and development phases to reduce environmental impact throughout its life cycle.

DFE considerations:

  • Provision for reuse or recycling (e.g., motor oil recycling at service stations)
  • Reduced energy consumption (hybrid engines, energy-efficient appliances)
  • Avoidance of hazardous materials (lead-free paint and gasoline)
  • Use of lighter components and less material (lighter car = better fuel economy)
Design for Remanufacture: products developed in a manner that allows components to be used in other products. Associated with green manufacturing. Enables lower-cost remanufactured products vs. new — lower customer cost, lower environmental impact.

Design for Reverse Logistics: packaging designed to reduce consumer frustration on returns; simple return process; easy disassembly for repair/replacement. Requires a replacement parts supply chain.

Question 1
What is the ASCM definition of quality?
A.Producing products with no defects whatsoever
B.Conformance to requirements — delivering exactly what the customer expects, at the right time and cost
C.The grade or level of excellence of a product
D.The result of a quality audit
Quality (Juran/Crosby) = conformance to requirements. It means meeting the customer's specifications — not necessarily achieving the highest possible performance, but delivering exactly what was promised.
Question 2
What is 'Total Quality Management' (TQM)?
A.A quality inspection program at the end of the production line
B.A management philosophy that makes quality the responsibility of every person in the organization — focused on continuous improvement, customer focus, and process-based thinking
C.A government-mandated quality certification
D.A statistical method for measuring defect rates
TQM is a management philosophy (not just a quality department activity) that makes quality the responsibility of everyone — through customer focus, continuous improvement, fact-based decision-making, and strong leadership commitment.
Question 3
What is 'Six Sigma' and what is its goal?
A.A quality level of 6 defects per million
B.A rigorous methodology for process improvement using data and statistical analysis (DMAIC: Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control) aimed at achieving near-perfect quality (3.4 defects per million opportunities)
C.A Japanese quality management system
D.A certification program for quality managers
Six Sigma uses DMAIC to eliminate variation in processes, aiming for 3.4 DPMO (defects per million opportunities) — statistically 6 standard deviations from the mean. It is both a quality standard and a process improvement methodology.
Question 4
What does 'ISO 9001' certification mean?
A.The product meets the highest quality standard in its category
B.The organization's quality management system has been independently audited and found to conform to the ISO 9001 standard — demonstrating systematic quality management
C.The product has zero defects
D.The company has been certified by ISO to sell its products internationally
ISO 9001 certifies that a company has implemented a quality management system (QMS) meeting the ISO 9001 standard — covering customer focus, process approach, leadership, continual improvement, and evidence-based decision-making.
Question 5
What is the 'cost of quality' and what are its components?
A.The cost of the quality department
B.All costs incurred in preventing poor quality and dealing with its consequences: Prevention costs (training, process design), Appraisal costs (inspection, testing), Internal failure costs (rework, scrap), External failure costs (returns, warranty, recalls)
C.The cost of ISO certification
D.The cost of customer complaints
The cost of quality has four components: Prevention (reducing defect risk upfront), Appraisal (catching defects before delivery), Internal failure (fixing defects before shipping), and External failure (dealing with defects after delivery — the most expensive category).
Question 6
What is a 'supplier quality agreement' (SQA)?
A.A contract specifying the price and quantity to be purchased
B.A formal agreement defining quality expectations, inspection protocols, corrective action requirements, and audit rights for a supply relationship — ensuring suppliers maintain agreed quality standards
C.A certification that a supplier has passed a quality audit
D.An agreement to share quality defect costs
SQAs formalize quality requirements in the supply relationship — defining specifications, acceptable quality levels (AQLs), testing methods, corrective action response times, and the buying company's right to audit supplier facilities.
Question 7
What is 'acceptable quality level' (AQL) in incoming inspection?
A.The maximum defect rate the company can produce
B.The maximum percentage of defects considered acceptable in a batch of goods — the threshold above which a lot is rejected; used in sampling-based incoming inspection
C.The minimum quality required by the customer
D.The quality level achieved by a Six Sigma process
AQL defines the threshold for acceptance in statistical sampling inspection. If the sample defect rate is below the AQL, the lot is accepted; above the AQL (with associated LTPD), it is rejected.
Question 8
What is 'corrective action' in quality management?
A.A financial penalty for poor supplier performance
B.The process of identifying the root cause of a quality problem and implementing changes to prevent its recurrence — documented in a corrective action report (CAR) or 8D report
C.A product recall process
D.Increasing inspection frequency after a defect is found
Corrective action is the systematic response to a quality non-conformance: contain the problem, find the root cause (using 5-Why, Fishbone, etc.), implement a fix, and verify it works. It prevents recurrence rather than just fixing the immediate problem.
Question 9
What does the '8D methodology' stand for?
A.Eight Defect elimination steps
B.Eight Disciplines — a structured team-based problem-solving methodology for finding and correcting the root cause of quality problems, widely used in automotive and other industries
C.Eight Dimensions of quality management
D.Eight Days to correct a quality problem
8D (Eight Disciplines) is a structured problem-solving framework: D1 (Form team), D2 (Define problem), D3 (Interim containment), D4 (Root cause), D5 (Permanent corrective action), D6 (Implement), D7 (Prevent recurrence), D8 (Congratulate team).
Question 10
What is 'statistical process control' (SPC)?
A.A method for setting supplier contract prices
B.Using statistical methods (control charts) to monitor process variability and detect when a process is going out of control — enabling correction before defects are produced
C.A system for tracking customer quality complaints
D.A method for calculating defect rates in finished goods
SPC uses control charts to monitor process output in real time. When variation exceeds control limits (indicating a non-random cause), the process can be stopped and corrected before producing defects — shifting from detection to prevention.
Question 11
What is 'poka-yoke' (error-proofing)?
A.A Japanese term for quality inspection
B.A design or process feature that prevents errors from occurring or makes errors immediately obvious — so defects cannot be produced even if a mistake is made
C.A type of statistical process control chart
D.A quality certification standard
Poka-yoke (mistake-proofing) designs out errors — for example, a USB connector that can only be inserted one way, or a machine that won't operate unless safety guards are in place. It prevents defects at the source rather than detecting them downstream.
Question 12
What is 'first-time quality' (FTQ) or 'first pass yield' (FPY)?
A.The quality rating given to a product when it first enters the market
B.The percentage of units that complete the production process without any defects or rework — a high FTQ means less rework cost, shorter cycle times, and more predictable output
C.The quality score given by the first customer to receive a product
D.The defect rate for incoming materials from a first-time supplier
FTQ/FPY measures how many units go through the process correctly the first time, without rework or scrap. Low FTQ means hidden costs (rework labor, materials, cycle time), reduced actual capacity, and quality variability reaching customers.
