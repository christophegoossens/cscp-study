Contract Types
Contract: an agreement between two or more competent persons or companies to perform or not to perform specific acts or services or to deliver merchandise.

Related ASCM-defined contract types:

  • Annualized contract: negotiated agreement with a supplier for one year — sets pricing, ensures continuous supply of materials, and usually includes volume commitments.
  • Bilateral contract: each party makes a promise to the other party.
  • CISG (Contracts for the International Sale of Goods): contracts governing sale of goods in the international environment — specifies the rights and obligations of both parties.
  • Trading partner agreement: contract describing all facets of the business relationship; a legal document covering product, price, intellectual property, and other terms.

Contract types by risk allocation:

  • Cost-based contract: price tied to costs of key inputs or outputs. Supplier risk minimized (cost overruns charged to customer). Used when costs are unpredictable.
  • Cost-plus contract: buyer pays all allowable costs + a fixed fee or percentage markup.
  • Cost-plus-fixed-fee contract: buyer pays costs + a stipulated fixed fee (not percentage).
  • Cost-plus-incentive-fee contract: buyer pays costs + a performance-linked profit incentive.
  • Firm fixed-price contract: seller is paid a set price regardless of costs. Customer risk minimized (supplier absorbs overruns). Used for well-defined, low-risk items.
  • Incentive contract: buyer and seller agree on target cost and maximum price. Cost savings below target are shared between buyer and seller.
  • Fixed-price incentive fee contract: set price + additional profit if certain performance criteria are met.
  • Incentive arrangements: sharing of cost responsibility between buyer and seller — organizations agree on desired cost and profit margin.
💡 Key Point: Cost-based contracts = supplier bears no cost risk. Firm fixed-price = customer bears no cost risk. Incentive contracts = risk shared — aligned incentives.
Contract Terms & Conditions (11 Areas)
  1. Pricing: unit price, spare parts, accessories, packaging, price escalation clauses.
  2. Delivery requirements: dates, locations, conditions, how orders are placed, penalties for late delivery.
  3. Transfer of ownership (Incoterms®): when risk and responsibility pass from seller to buyer.
  4. Payment terms: timing, form of payment, currency, early payment discounts.
  5. Performance criteria: product/service specifications, ISO standards, documentation.
  6. Quality assurance: audits of manufacturing facility, quality management processes, standards.
  7. Order requirements: standard delivery quantities, dates, acceptable early/late windows.
  8. Associated incentives and penalties: bonuses for performance, liquidated damages for failures.
  9. Status reporting: portal communication, frequency of updates, who receives reports.
  10. Channels for resolving problems: alternate dispute resolution (ADR) process, escalation protocols.
  11. Security, language, legal authority, termination: confidentiality, governing law (jurisdiction), contract term and notice requirements.

Critical rule: Never accept “boilerplate” assurances for undesirable clauses. Every contract must be reviewed by legal counsel with international expertise before signing.

Payment Terms, Letters of Credit & Currency Risk
Payment Terms: the conditions under which a seller will complete a sale. Typically specifies the period allowed for payment and may include a discount for paying early.

Trade credit: sale of goods with deferred payment. Encourages sales. Common early payment discount notation: 2/10 net 30 = 2% discount if paid within 10 days; full amount due in 30 days.

Letter of Credit (L/C): used in international trade when open account or trade credit doesn’t offer sufficient risk protection.

Exhibit 3-17: Letter of Credit — 7-step process

L/C 7-step process:

  1. Buyer/importer gets L/C from its bank (demonstrates ability to pay).
  2. Buyer’s bank notifies seller’s bank that L/C is issued.
  3. Seller ships cargo (confident of payment once conditions met).
  4. Seller sends draft for purchase price to its bank.
  5. Seller’s bank forwards documents to buyer’s bank; B/L triggers cargo release.
  6. Buyer’s bank waits for cargo (may match documents).
  7. All outstanding drafts are honored when buyer approves/accepts delivery.

Operating Exposure: the risk introduced by flexible exchange rates when operating in the global environment, including their effect on production, storage, purchasing, and selling.

Currency hedging: using financial instruments (forward contracts, options) to lock in exchange rates and reduce currency risk. Available for major world currencies. Eliminates exchange rate uncertainty in cross-border trade.

Question 1
What is the primary purpose of a supply contract?
A.To transfer risk from the supplier to the buying company
B.To establish a legally binding framework defining the rights, obligations, and expectations of both parties — providing clarity and legal recourse if either party fails to perform
C.To lock in the lowest possible price
D.To eliminate the need for purchase orders
Contracts provide legal clarity and protection: they define what each party must do (specifications, quantities, prices, delivery), what happens if they don't (remedies, penalties), and how disputes are resolved.
Question 2
What is the difference between a 'spot purchase' and a 'blanket order'?
A.Spot purchases are always cheaper; blanket orders are always more expensive
B.Spot purchase: a one-time buy at current market price; blanket order: a longer-term commitment to buy specified quantities at agreed prices over a defined period — providing price stability and administrative efficiency
C.Spot purchases require contracts; blanket orders do not
D.Blanket orders are for services; spot purchases are for goods
Spot purchases are one-time, typically market-priced transactions. Blanket orders (or frame agreements) establish terms for a period (e.g., 1 year), allowing call-offs against the agreed price without renegotiating each purchase — reducing administrative burden.
Question 3
What is a 'service level agreement' (SLA) in a supply contract?
A.A document defining employee service standards
B.The contractual definition of required performance levels (delivery lead time, fill rate, quality, responsiveness) and the consequences (penalties, remedies) if those levels are not met
C.A statement of the supplier's commitment to customer service
D.A document defining the payment terms
SLAs define measurable performance targets (e.g., on-time delivery ≥98%, fill rate ≥99.5%) and specify remedies for non-performance — making performance expectations explicit and legally enforceable.
Question 4
What is a 'penalty clause' in a supply contract?
A.A clause that prevents the supplier from selling to competitors
B.A contractual provision specifying financial or other consequences if the supplier fails to meet agreed performance standards — incentivizing compliance and compensating the buyer for supplier failures
C.A clause allowing the buyer to cancel orders without notice
D.A clause requiring the supplier to provide insurance
Penalty clauses (liquidated damages) create financial consequences for non-performance — for example, a daily penalty for late delivery. They incentivize the supplier to meet commitments and compensate the buyer for disruption costs.
Question 5
What is intellectual property (IP) protection in supply contracts?
A.Protecting the supplier's brand from copying
B.Contractual provisions that clarify ownership of designs, tools, technology, and processes developed during the relationship — and restricting the supplier from using the buyer's IP for other customers
C.Protecting the buyer's patents from suppliers
D.Licensing agreements for software used by the supplier
IP protection clauses define ownership of designs, tooling, and technology (especially important when the buyer funds development) and prevent the supplier from using buyer-specific IP for other customers or competitors.
Question 6
What does 'force majeure' mean in a supply contract?
A.A French term for 'mandatory force' used in logistics
B.A clause that excuses a party from non-performance caused by events beyond their control — natural disasters, pandemics, wars — that make performance impossible or impractical
C.A clause requiring the supplier to use maximum effort
D.A performance standard for extraordinary circumstances
Force majeure clauses identify circumstances beyond a party's control (acts of God, wars, pandemics) that excuse non-performance without penalty — providing legal relief for genuinely unforeseeable events while not excusing normal business difficulties.
Question 7
What is 'price escalation' provision in a multi-year supply contract?
A.A clause allowing prices to be increased without limitation
B.A mechanism linking price adjustments to an index (e.g., commodity price index, labor index) — allowing predetermined, formula-based price changes that fairly share the impact of cost changes between buyer and supplier
C.A clause requiring the supplier to reduce prices annually
D.A provision for emergency price increases during supply shortages
Price escalation (or de-escalation) clauses link price changes to objective indices — commodity prices (copper, oil), labor cost indices — providing a fair, pre-agreed formula for price adjustments without requiring renegotiation.
Question 8
What is a 'termination for convenience' clause?
A.A clause allowing suppliers to terminate if they find a better customer
B.A clause giving the buying company the right to terminate the contract at any time for any reason, with specified notice and compensation to the supplier — providing flexibility to exit the relationship
C.A clause specifying the conditions under which either party can exit
D.A clause that automatically renews the contract indefinitely
Termination for convenience allows the buyer to exit the contract without cause — usually requiring notice (e.g., 90 days) and sometimes a payment for the supplier's transition costs. It provides strategic flexibility.
Question 9
What does 'incoterms' govern in international supply contracts?
A.The language in which the contract must be written
B.The allocation of costs, risks, and responsibilities between buyer and seller for international shipments — defining exactly who pays for freight, insurance, and duties, and where risk transfers
C.The currency in which payment must be made
D.The customs classification of goods
Incoterms (International Commercial Terms, published by ICC) are standardized trade terms (EXW, FOB, CIF, DDP, etc.) that define who bears freight cost and risk at each point in the international journey — critical for avoiding disputes.
Question 10
What is 'warranty' in a supply contract?
A.A promise that the product will never fail
B.A contractual commitment about the condition, quality, or performance of goods — typically covering defects in materials or workmanship for a specified period, and defining remedies (repair, replace, refund) if the warranty is breached
C.A financial guarantee of price stability
D.An insurance policy for product liability
Warranties define the seller's commitments about product quality and the remedies available if goods are defective. Express warranties are explicitly stated in the contract; implied warranties may exist by law even without explicit contractual language.
Question 11
What is 'governing law' and 'dispute resolution' provisions in international contracts?
A.Laws governing environmental compliance at supplier facilities
B.Clauses specifying which country's laws govern the contract interpretation and what mechanism (negotiation, mediation, arbitration, litigation) will be used to resolve disputes — critical in international relationships
C.The laws governing import and export of the contracted goods
D.Rules for managing quality disputes
Governing law determines which country's legal system interprets the contract. Dispute resolution specifies the process (arbitration is often preferred in international contracts for neutrality and enforceability) — both are critical in cross-border relationships.
Question 12
What is the key advantage of a long-term supply agreement vs. short-term spot purchasing for a strategic item?
A.Short-term purchasing always delivers lower prices
B.Long-term agreements provide price stability, supply assurance, priority access to capacity, and a framework for supply chain investment and collaboration — enabling both parties to plan and invest with confidence
C.Long-term agreements always result in lower total cost
D.Short-term spot purchasing provides better supply security
Long-term agreements (especially for strategic, high-value items) provide predictability for both parties: buyers get price stability and supply assurance; suppliers get volume certainty enabling investment. This enables the deeper collaboration needed for joint supply chain improvement.
