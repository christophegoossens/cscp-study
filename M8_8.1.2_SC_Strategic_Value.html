Supply Chain as a Strategic Asset
Supply chain as competitive advantage: When a supply chain's capabilities — speed, cost, flexibility, reliability — are superior to competitors' and difficult to replicate, the supply chain itself becomes a source of sustained competitive advantage.
Examples of supply chain as competitive advantage: Amazon (logistics speed + network creates delivery expectation competitors struggle to match); Zara (fast fashion supply chain enables 2-week design-to-store cycle); Apple (supply chain integration with TSMC and Foxconn creates manufacturing scale and quality competitors can't easily match).
Supply chain optimization: The use of data, analytics, and technology to find the best possible configuration and operation of the supply chain — minimizing cost while meeting service requirements, or maximizing service within a cost constraint.
Supply Chain Optimization Methods
Network optimization: Using mathematical models to determine the optimal number, location, and capacity of supply chain nodes (plants, warehouses, distribution centers) — minimizing total network cost (production + transportation + inventory) while meeting service level constraints.
Linear programming (LP): A mathematical optimization method finding the best value of an objective (minimize cost, maximize output) subject to constraints (capacity, demand, service level). Used for transportation planning, production scheduling, and network design.
Total landed cost: The complete cost of a product delivered to the customer — including production, transportation, duties, insurance, carrying costs, and compliance costs. Used for global sourcing decisions to compare true cost of different sourcing options.
Total Landed Cost = Production Cost + Transportation + Duties/Tariffs + Insurance + Carrying Cost + Compliance
Supply chain analytics progression: Descriptive (what happened?) → Diagnostic (why did it happen?) → Predictive (what will happen?) → Prescriptive (what should we do?). Prescriptive analytics (using optimization models) represents the highest maturity level.
Value Creation Metrics
Economic Value Added (EVA): A measure of financial performance: Net Operating Profit After Tax (NOPAT) minus the cost of capital employed. Supply chains add economic value when they generate returns exceeding the cost of the assets (inventory, equipment, warehouses) they employ.
EVA = NOPAT − (Capital Employed × Cost of Capital)
Supply chain ROI: The return generated by supply chain investments — quantifying how much value supply chain improvements (inventory reduction, cost savings, service improvement) contribute to the overall business.
Supply chain improvements directly affect EVA: reducing inventory → less capital employed → lower cost of capital charge. Improving margins through efficiency → higher NOPAT. Both pathways increase EVA.

TERM / CONCEPT
How supply chain creates competitive advantage
ANSWER
Supply chain becomes a competitive advantage when it: 1. Delivers value customers can't get from competitors (Amazon: 2-day delivery norm; Zara: trend-to-shelf in 2 weeks) 2. Is difficult for competitors to replicate (years of investment, proprietary technology, supplier relationships) 3. Enables better economics than competitors (cost leadership through scale, efficiency) Key: the supply chain is not just a cost to minimize — it's an asset to develop for competitive differentiation.
TERM / CONCEPT
Total Landed Cost formula and components
ANSWER
Total Landed Cost = Production Cost + Transportation + Duties/Tariffs + Insurance + Inventory Carrying Cost + Compliance Costs Why it matters: comparing a 'cheap' China supplier to a more expensive Mexico supplier. China: lower production cost BUT higher transport cost (ocean + inland), longer lead time (more inventory carrying cost), duty exposure, compliance complexity. Mexico: higher production cost BUT lower transport, shorter lead time, USMCA zero duty. Total landed cost may favor Mexico even though production cost favors China.
TERM / CONCEPT
Supply chain analytics maturity levels
ANSWER
Descriptive: What happened? (Historical reporting, dashboards) Diagnostic: Why did it happen? (Root cause analysis, drill-down) Predictive: What will happen? (Forecasting, ML demand prediction) Prescriptive: What should we do? (Optimization models, scenario analysis, AI recommendations) Most supply chains operate at descriptive/diagnostic level. Prescriptive analytics (network optimization, dynamic routing, AI demand response) represents the frontier — highest value but requires data infrastructure and capability.
TERM / CONCEPT
Economic Value Added (EVA) — formula and SC connection
ANSWER
EVA = NOPAT − (Capital Employed × Cost of Capital) NOPAT = Net Operating Profit After Tax Capital Employed = all assets used to generate that profit (including inventory!) Cost of Capital = required return on that capital (typically 8–12%) SC contribution to EVA: • Reduce inventory → lower Capital Employed → lower capital charge → higher EVA • Improve efficiency → higher NOPAT • Improve service → higher revenue → higher NOPAT Makes supply chain improvements directly linkable to shareholder value.
TERM / CONCEPT
Network optimization — what decisions it supports
ANSWER
Mathematical modeling to find optimal supply chain network configuration. Decisions: how many distribution centers? Where located? What size? Which suppliers serve which DCs? Which DCs serve which customers? Objective: minimize total network cost (production + transportation + inventory + facility) while meeting service constraints (delivery time, minimum fill rate). Tool: linear programming and simulation models. Inputs: demand by location, transport costs, facility costs, inventory policies. Output: optimal network design recommendation.
TERM / CONCEPT
Prescriptive analytics — what it is and examples
ANSWER
Highest maturity analytics level: not just 'what will happen' (predictive) but 'what should we do' (prescriptive). Examples: • Network optimization: what's the optimal DC network configuration? • Dynamic pricing: what price should we set given current demand and inventory? • Transportation optimization: which carrier/route/mode minimizes cost for these shipments? • Inventory optimization: exactly how much safety stock at each node given service targets? • Demand sensing: respond to real-time signals to adjust replenishment in near real-time Requires: clean data + optimization algorithms + business rules + human judgment.

Q1 of 5
Total Landed Cost is used in global sourcing decisions because:
Total Landed Cost: the complete cost of a product delivered to the point of use. A supplier with lower production cost may have higher total landed cost due to: longer ocean transit (more inventory carrying cost + lead time risk), import duties, inland transportation, and compliance costs. TLC enables an apples-to-apples comparison across global sourcing options. Without it, companies make sourcing decisions based on purchase price alone — often arriving at a higher actual cost than a seemingly more expensive alternative.
Q2 of 5
Economic Value Added (EVA) is reduced when:
EVA = NOPAT − (Capital Employed × Cost of Capital). Inventory is part of Capital Employed. Excess inventory increases Capital Employed → increases the capital charge (Capital Employed × Cost of Capital) → reduces EVA even if NOPAT stays constant. This is why supply chain professionals say 'inventory is capital in disguise' — every dollar of unnecessary inventory has a real cost: the company's required return on that capital, typically 8–12% annually.
Q3 of 5
Prescriptive analytics in supply chain is characterized by:
Analytics maturity: Descriptive (what happened) → Diagnostic (why) → Predictive (what will happen) → Prescriptive (what should we do). Prescriptive analytics goes beyond prediction to recommendation: optimization models, AI-driven decision support, automated replenishment signals. Examples: network optimization recommending DC closures, dynamic routing optimization selecting lowest-cost carrier in real time, inventory optimization calculating exact safety stock by SKU. This is the frontier of supply chain analytics.
Q4 of 5
Amazon's supply chain is an example of competitive advantage because:
Amazon's supply chain advantage: massive DC network (within 30 miles of ~90% of US population), proprietary delivery infrastructure (Amazon Logistics), sophisticated robotics (Kiva robots), AI-driven inventory positioning (predictive shipping), and Prime membership lock-in. These capabilities took years and billions to build — competitors can't replicate quickly. The result: Amazon set a delivery speed expectation (free 2-day delivery) that has become a market standard, forcing all e-commerce competitors to invest heavily to keep up.
Q5 of 5
Network optimization models determine the optimal supply chain network by:
Network optimization: mathematical model minimizes total supply chain cost (facility lease + labor + transportation + inventory) subject to constraints (customer delivery time requirements, minimum service levels, capacity limits). The optimal network balances trade-offs: fewer DCs = less facility cost but more transport cost and slower delivery; more DCs = more facility cost but lower transport cost and faster delivery. The model finds where the sum of all these costs is minimized while meeting service requirements — impossible to determine through intuition alone.
