Supply Planning
Supply plan: The strategic design of a supply network based on total cost of ownership, make-versus-buy decisions, and sourcing strategies. Answers key structural questions before tactical buying begins.

Key supply planning questions:

  • Centralized vs. decentralized sourcing — global leverage or local flexibility?
  • Local vs. lowest-cost sourcing — resilience vs. cost efficiency?
  • Contingency plans for supply risks
  • Sole-source vs. single-source vs. multi-source strategy
  • Different strategies for strategic vs. non-strategic materials
Sole source: Only one supplier exists in the market for this item — no choice. High risk.
Single source: Multiple suppliers exist but the company chooses to use only one — a strategic decision for volume leverage or partnership. Lower risk than sole source (can switch).
Multiple source: Two or more suppliers provide the same item — maximizes competition and resilience but reduces volume leverage per supplier.
Category Management and Strategy
Category: A grouping of similar products or services that share similar supply market characteristics and can be managed together strategically.
Category strategy: A plan for how the organization will source a specific category — covering supplier selection approach, contract type, relationship depth, and performance expectations.
The Kraljic Matrix is the most widely used tool for category segmentation in supply chain:
  • Strategic items (high spend, high risk): Deep supplier partnerships, long-term contracts, joint planning — e.g., key raw materials
  • Leverage items (high spend, low risk): Maximize purchasing power, competitive bidding, multiple suppliers — e.g., standard components
  • Bottleneck items (low spend, high risk): Ensure supply continuity, safety stock, qualify alternates — e.g., single-source specialty parts
  • Non-critical / routine items (low spend, low risk): Simplify and automate — blanket POs, e-procurement, vendor-managed inventory
Different categories require completely different sourcing strategies. Applying the same approach to all categories wastes resources and misses risk.
Supply Plan Validation

Before finalizing the supply plan, validate it against: corporate strategy alignment, financial feasibility, risk assessment, and supplier market realities.

The supply plan must be aligned with S&OP — what the supply plan promises must be supportable within the company's overall demand and operational plan.

Supply plan refinement often follows a spend analysis — using data on what the organization actually buys to validate that the planned sourcing approach matches spend realities.

Sole source: only ONE supplier exists in market — no choice, high risk. Single source: multiple suppliers exist, company chooses one — strategic decision for leverage/partnership. Multiple source: 2+ suppliers for same item — maximizes competition and resilience.

The Kraljic Matrix — 4 quadrants
ANSWER
Strategic (high spend, high risk): deep partnerships, long-term contracts. Leverage (high spend, low risk): competitive bidding, maximize power. Bottleneck (low spend, high risk): ensure supply continuity, safety stock. Non-critical (low spend, low risk): simplify and automate.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a category strategy?
ANSWER
A plan for sourcing a specific category — covering: supplier selection approach, contract type, relationship depth, performance targets, and risk mitigation. Different categories need fundamentally different strategies (Kraljic Matrix).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a supply plan?
ANSWER
The strategic design of a supply network — based on TCO, make-vs-buy decisions, and sourcing strategy. Answers structural questions: centralized vs. decentralized, local vs. lowest-cost, sole/single/multi-source, strategic vs. non-strategic materials.
TERM / CONCEPT
How should non-critical/routine items be sourced?
ANSWER
Simplify and automate — blanket purchase orders, e-procurement, vendor-managed inventory (VMI), or purchasing cards. Minimize management time on low-value, low-risk items to free resources for strategic categories.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why must the supply plan align with S&OP?
ANSWER
The supply plan defines supply capacity and sourcing strategy. If it doesn't align with the demand plan (S&OP output), the company risks being unable to source what S&OP plans to produce and sell. Supply planning and demand planning must be synchronized.

Q1 of 5
A company's key raw material is available from only one global supplier. How should this be categorized in the Kraljic Matrix?
Single global supplier = high supply risk regardless of spend level. If spend is LOW but risk is HIGH → Bottleneck item. Strategy: secure supply through safety stock, qualify alternates, and long-term contracts to lock in availability.
Q2 of 5
A purchasing team is buying standard office supplies that are widely available from many suppliers. Using the Kraljic Matrix, what is the recommended sourcing approach?
Office supplies = non-critical (low spend, low risk). The strategy is to minimize transaction cost through automation — blanket orders, e-procurement, or VMI. Using a full RFP process or strategic partnership wastes resources on low-value items.
Q3 of 5
A company sources a high-value component from 3 competing suppliers. This is called:
Multiple sourcing = using 2+ suppliers for the same item. Benefits: price competition, supply resilience (if one fails, others can cover). Tradeoff: lower volume leverage per supplier and more relationship management complexity.
Q4 of 5
The Kraljic Matrix segments purchases based on:
Kraljic Matrix axes: (1) Spend importance — low to high; (2) Supply risk — low to high. These two dimensions create four quadrants (Non-critical, Leverage, Bottleneck, Strategic) each requiring a different sourcing strategy.
Q5 of 5
Which supply planning decision directly affects supply chain resilience versus cost efficiency?
Single source → lower cost (volume leverage, simpler management) but higher risk (one supplier failure = stockout). Multiple source → higher cost per supplier but better resilience. This is the fundamental cost-resilience tradeoff in supply planning.
