Customs & Global Trade Management

Customs: A country’s regulation of import and export trade at its international ports and borders. Purposes: provide revenue and protect domestic industries.

Global trade management: The management and optimization of shipments across international borders to improve operating efficiencies and cash flows. Includes compliance with trade regulations and effective use of intermediaries.

Customs inspects shipments to: verify declared value, check markings and safety labels, filter forbidden items, enforce quotas, verify invoice accuracy, discourage dumping.

Best practices for customs clearance: Use a licensed customs house broker, start the process before arrival, use electronic documentation, check intermediary backgrounds, maintain long-term trusted relationships.

Incoterms® 2020
Terms of trade: The portion of a contract of sale that specifies the responsibilities of the seller/exporter and the responsibilities of the buyer/importer, including the point of delivery, risk transfer, and insurance obligations.
Incoterms®: A series of pre-defined commercial terms published by the International Chamber of Commerce relating to international commercial law. They indicate roles and responsibilities of seller and buyer, including point of delivery, risk transfer, and who pays insurance/freight.

Incoterms® 2020 updates: clarification that an owned fleet may be used instead of a third party. Not legally binding but globally accepted as standard terms.

Two groups:

  • Rules for any mode of transport (EXW, FCA, CPT, CIP, DAP, DPU, DDP)
  • Rules for sea and inland waterway only (FAS, FOB, CFR, CIF) — FOB only applies to sea/waterway, not air

Institute Cargo Clauses (ICC) A, B, or C specify insurance coverage levels — A is broadest (all risks), C is most limited. Do not confuse ICC (Institute Cargo Clauses) with ICC (International Chamber of Commerce) or Incoterms® abbreviations.
Export-Import Intermediaries
Freight forwarder: The middleman between the carrier and the organization shipping the product. A freight forwarder often combines smaller shipments into large lots. Does not own carrier space or provide labor.
Foreign freight forwarder: An entity that picks up goods at the production site and coordinates transport to the foreign customer’s location.
Non-Vessel Operating Common Carrier (NVOCC): Buys space on inland carriers and resells it to shippers at a marked-up price. Performs physical consolidation, loading, and unloading. Can handle inland freight. Issues its own bills of lading.

Differences between freight forwarders and NVOCCs:

  • NVOCCs actually buy and resell carrier space; forwarders do not
  • NVOCCs perform physical consolidation/loading labor; forwarders do not
  • NVOCCs can handle inland freight in many cases
  • Consolidators: Combine small shipments into larger ones for full-vehicle discounts. May work under NVOCCs.
  • Customs house brokers: Move shipments through customs; pay duties under power of attorney. Only licensed brokers can transact with customs.
  • Export Management Company (EMC): Represents companies in foreign markets with special market knowledge and connections.
  • Export Trading Company (ETC): Buys and resells goods in foreign markets. General trading companies may comprise banks, steamship lines, warehouses, and more.
  • Ship brokers: Independent contractors arranging ocean transport. Ship agents: Represent specific carriers.

Export Documents
  • Export declaration: U.S. Census Bureau form (AESDirect) capturing export data — commodity description, weight, marks, license numbers, destination, parties.
  • Export license: General (most goods) or Validated (strategically sensitive items requiring specific approval).
  • Commercial invoice: States value of goods, specifies payment terms — the seller’s bill. Consular invoice adds information required by the importing country. Pro forma invoice is a quoted/preliminary version.
  • ATA Carnet: Allows containers to cross borders duty- and tax-free without customs inspection (ATA = Admission Temporaire/Temporary Admission). Good for trade shows, samples.
  • Certificate of origin: Provides country where goods were produced — used to assign tariff rates.
  • Bill of lading (B/L): Issued by the shipping company; demonstrates ownership of goods. Order B/Ls are negotiable (can transfer title). Ship’s manifest summarizes vessel cargo based on processed B/Ls.
  • Air waybill (AWB): Standardized form for all air shipments. Non-negotiable. Facilitates faster customs clearance.
  • Dock receipt: Issued by ship agent; confirms steamship company received cargo from domestic carrier.
  • Certificate of insurance: Attests that cargo is insured per the terms of the sale.
  • TIR Carnet: Enables sealed road trailers to avoid customs inspections until final destination (covers multiple border crossings).
  • CMR Waybill: International road carriage document (1956 UN treaty).

Import Documentation: Harmonized Tariff Schedule & Duties
Harmonized Tariff Schedule (HTS): The U.S. International Trade Commission’s mechanism by which international tariffs are standardized. Importers and exporters classify goods under a common set of commodity codes (6-digit base + up to 4 more digits; total 10 digits in U.S.). Used by 200+ countries.
Harmonized system classification codes: An internationally standardized description of goods using a system of numbers to provide increasingly detailed classification of traded goods.

Duty drawback: A refund of all or part of duty paid on goods that were first imported and then re-exported.

Import cost calculation: Import duty (% of CIF or FOB value) + value-added tax (on CIF/FOB + duties) + excise taxes. CIF = Cost, Insurance, Freight; FOB = Free on Board.

What does FOB mean in Incoterms® 2020 and when is it applicable?
ANSWER
Free on Board: risk/cost transfers when goods are on board the vessel. Only applicable to sea and inland waterway transport — NOT air, even though it is sometimes misused for air shipments.

What are Institute Cargo Clauses (ICC) A, B, and C?
ANSWER
Insurance coverage levels for cargo: A = broadest (all risks), B = intermediate coverage, C = most limited. Can be specified within an Incoterm or as additional coverage.

TERM / CONCEPT
What is an NVOCC and how does it differ from a freight forwarder?
ANSWER
NVOCC (Non-Vessel Operating Common Carrier) buys and resells carrier space, performs physical consolidation/loading labor, and can handle inland freight. Freight forwarders do none of these — they coordinate and arrange but don’t own space or provide labor.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a customs house broker?
ANSWER
Moves shipments through customs; pays import duties under power of attorney from the importer. Only licensed brokers can transact business with customs. Liability for unpaid duties lies with the importer.
TERM / CONCEPT
Difference between EMC and ETC?
ANSWER
EMC (Export Management Company): represents a company in a foreign market, using special knowledge and connections. ETC (Export Trading Company): buys goods and resells them in foreign markets — takes title and acts as a buyer/reseller.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is an ATA Carnet?
ANSWER
Allows containers to cross multiple borders duty- and tax-free without customs inspection. Stands for Admission Temporaire/Temporary Admission. Used for trade show samples and temporary imports.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a TIR Carnet?
ANSWER
Enables sealed road transport trailers and containers to avoid customs inspections at each border crossing until they reach their final destination. Based on a 1975 UN convention.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Harmonized Tariff Schedule (HTS)?
ANSWER
The U.S. ITC’s mechanism for standardizing international tariffs using common commodity codes. Base = 6 digits (international); up to 4 additional digits per country (10 digits total in U.S.). Used by 200+ countries.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a duty drawback?
ANSWER
A refund of all or part of import duties paid on goods that were first imported and then re-exported — avoids double taxation on goods passing through a country.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is dumping and how do customs address it?
ANSWER
Dumping = exporting a product at a price lower than the price in the domestic market. Customs can impose a high-percentage duty (anti-dumping duty) to discourage this practice.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is an ATR Certificate?
ANSWER
A document required for EU–Turkey trade that grants zero import duty to goods that are “free circulating” in the EU — meaning all customs duties have already been paid.

Question 1
What is a 'commercial invoice' in international trade and what information does it contain?
A.An invoice for commercial advertising services
B.The primary trade document used for customs valuation — listing the seller, buyer, goods description, HS code, quantity, unit value, total value, and country of origin. Required for import customs entry and duty calculation
C.A bank document for letter of credit
D.A transport document issued by the carrier
The commercial invoice is the primary customs document: customs use it to verify the declared value of imported goods for duty calculation. It must accurately reflect the actual transaction price — undervaluation is customs fraud.
Question 2
What is a 'packing list' and why is it needed in international trade?
A.A list of safety packaging regulations
B.A document that details the physical contents of each package in a shipment — item descriptions, quantities, weights, and dimensions for each box or pallet — used by customs, the receiver, and insurers to verify the shipment contents
C.A list of items prohibited in packaging
D.The bill of lading for a small shipment
The packing list enables customs inspection (they can verify that package contents match the commercial invoice), receiving (checking quantities against the purchase order), and insurance claims (documenting what was in each package if items are damaged or missing).
Question 3
What is a 'certificate of origin' (COO) and when is it required?
A.A document certifying where a product was designed
B.A document certifying the country in which goods were manufactured — required to claim preferential tariff rates under free trade agreements, and sometimes to comply with country-specific import regulations
C.A document certifying product quality
D.A document certifying the route of transportation
COO documents are required to claim FTA preferential tariff rates (the product must meet the FTA's rules of origin to qualify). Non-preferential COOs are also required by some countries for all imports or specific goods categories.
Question 4
What is an 'export license' and when is one required?
A.A driver's license for truck drivers crossing borders
B.A government authorization required before certain goods, technology, or software can be exported — required for dual-use items (commercial goods with military applications), military hardware, encryption technology, and exports to restricted destinations/parties
C.A quality certification for exported goods
D.A customs authorization to expedite export clearance
Export licenses (EAR licenses from BIS; ITAR authorizations from DDTC) are required for items controlled under the EAR (dual-use goods) or ITAR (military/defense articles). Exporting without a required license results in severe criminal and civil penalties.
Question 5
What is an 'advance shipping notice' (ASN) and who issues it?
A.A notice from the airline that a shipment will be delayed
B.An electronic document sent by the supplier/shipper before or at the time of shipment, notifying the receiver of the shipment contents, quantities, and expected delivery time — enabling advance preparation for receiving
C.A notice from customs about goods arriving
D.A weather alert for shipping delays
The ASN (EDI 856) is sent by the shipper to the receiver when goods are shipped — detailing exactly what's in each box or pallet. It enables the receiver to pre-create receiving records, schedule dock resources, and update inventory in advance of arrival.
Question 6
What is a 'letter of credit' (L/C) and how does it mitigate payment risk in international trade?
A.A reference letter from a bank for credit applications
B.A bank-issued document guaranteeing payment to the seller upon presentation of specified documents — the bank assumes the payment risk instead of the seller trusting the buyer's ability/willingness to pay
C.A credit card used specifically for international purchases
D.A letter authorizing a freight forwarder to act on behalf of the importer
An L/C provides payment assurance to the seller: if the seller presents complying documents (B/L, commercial invoice, packing list, inspection certificate) to their bank, payment is guaranteed by the issuing bank — eliminating the risk that the buyer defaults.
Question 7
What is a 'bill of lading' (B/L) and what three functions does it serve?
A.A government-issued shipping permit
B.A carrier-issued document that serves as: (1) receipt for goods received, (2) evidence of the contract of carriage, and (3) document of title — the holder of a negotiable B/L has the right to the goods
C.A supplier's invoice for transportation services
D.A packing list for a specific container
The B/L's three functions are critical in international trade: it proves the carrier received the goods (receipt), documents the shipping contract terms (carriage contract), and (when negotiable) can transfer title — enabling banks and buyers to control goods in transit.
Question 8
What is 'AES' (Automated Export System) filing and when is it required?
A.An automated system for tracking export orders
B.The U.S. electronic export information filing system — required for exports with a single shipment value exceeding $2,500 per HS code, or for all exports requiring an export license, to comply with EAR/ITAR regulations
C.An accounting system for export revenues
D.A European trade reporting system
AES (now part of ACE — Automated Commercial Environment) is the mandatory electronic system for filing Electronic Export Information (EEI) with U.S. Census Bureau and CBP before export. Required for most exports above $2,500 per schedule B classification.
Question 9
What is a 'phytosanitary certificate' and why is it required?
A.A quality certificate for pharmaceutical products
B.A government-issued certificate confirming that agricultural products (plants, fruits, vegetables, wood) have been inspected and are free from pests and diseases — required for import of agricultural goods to prevent introduction of invasive species
C.An environmental compliance certificate
D.A safety certificate for food processing plants
Phytosanitary certificates (issued by national plant protection organizations) certify that plant-based goods meet the importing country's plant health requirements — preventing introduction of pests, diseases, and invasive species that could devastate agriculture.
Question 10
What is 'customs valuation' and why does it matter?
A.The value of the customs department's budget
B.The method for determining the value of imported goods on which ad valorem duties are calculated. The primary method (WTO Valuation Agreement) uses the 'transaction value' — the price actually paid for the goods
C.The market value of goods at the destination
D.The value of goods certified by the exporter
Customs valuation determines the duty base: ad valorem duties (% of value) are multiplied by the declared customs value. The WTO transaction value method requires declaring the actual price paid — undervaluation is fraud; overvaluation triggers scrutiny.
Question 11
What is a 'dangerous goods declaration' (DGD) and when is it required?
A.A notice to customers that goods contain hazardous materials
B.A shipper-completed form declaring that a shipment contains dangerous goods (hazardous materials) — required by IATA (air), IMDG (ocean), and DOT (domestic road) regulations for all shipments of classified hazardous materials
C.A government inspection report on dangerous goods
D.A carrier's internal safety checklist
The DGD (or Shipper's Declaration for Dangerous Goods) is a legally required document certifying that hazardous goods have been properly classified, packed, labeled, and marked per applicable regulations. The shipper (not carrier) is responsible for its accuracy.
Question 12
What is 'import licensing' and for which goods is it typically required?
A.A license required for all imported goods
B.Government authorization required before certain goods can be imported — typically applies to controlled goods (firearms, nuclear materials), agricultural commodities subject to quotas, pharmaceuticals, and items covered by international conventions (CITES)
C.A license required for all import brokers
D.A quality certification for imported goods
Import licenses control the import of specific goods for regulatory reasons: quota administration (agricultural products, textiles), health and safety (pharmaceuticals, medical devices), environmental protection (CITES-listed species), and security (firearms, encryption).
