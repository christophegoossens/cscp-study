Warehouse Types
  • Private warehouse: Owned or leased by the company storing its own goods. High control; high fixed cost. Best when volume is stable and high.
  • Public warehouse: Operated by a third party for multiple clients. Variable cost; no long-term commitment. Best for seasonal or variable demand.
  • Contract warehouse: Dedicated third-party facility for one client under a long-term contract. Balance of flexibility and control.

Warehouse Number and Location Decisions

Adding warehouses increases warehousing costs but decreases transportation costs. The optimal number minimizes total cost while meeting service requirements.

  • Throughput volume: Higher throughput favors private or contract warehouses.
  • Geographic coverage: More warehouses = shorter delivery distances = higher customer service, but higher fixed costs.
  • Market proximity: Locate near customers to reduce lead times and last-mile costs.
  • Infrastructure: Access to road, rail, port, or air links affects location choice.

Key warehouse zones: receiving, put-away/storage, order picking, packing/staging, shipping.

Stock Location Methods
  • Random (floating) storage: Any available slot is used. Maximizes space utilization but requires good inventory tracking (WMS).
  • Fixed location: Each SKU always stored in the same location. Easy to find manually; wastes space when inventory is low.
  • ABC (Pareto) slotting: Fast-moving A items stored near shipping dock; slow-moving C items stored farther away. Reduces travel time for pickers.
  • Functional grouping: Items stored by type (e.g., hazardous together, refrigerated together) for handling efficiency or safety compliance.
Materials Handling
Materials handling: The movement and storage of goods inside the distribution center. Represents a capital cost balanced against labor and throughput efficiency.

Key materials handling equipment types:

  • Forklifts: Most common; lift and move palletized loads. Various types (counterbalanced, reach, order-picker).
  • Conveyors: Move goods into/out of storage or vehicles. Fixed path; efficient for high-volume, repetitive flows.
  • Towlines (towline conveyors): Four-wheeled containers towed by a drive mechanism embedded in the floor. Good for horizontal movement in large DCs.
  • Cranes (bridge cranes): Horizontal girder spanning an aisle, moves heavy loads overhead. Good for very heavy or awkward items.
  • Carousels: Rotating shelving units (horizontal or vertical) that bring items to the picker. Reduces worker travel time.
  • AGVS (Automated Guided Vehicles): Driverless vehicles guided by wire, laser, or vision systems. Automated material movement.
  • AS/RS (Automated Storage and Retrieval Systems): Computer-controlled cranes in narrow aisles. Maximum storage density, tight inventory control, fast throughput, minimal labor.
  • Live racks (flow racks): Gravity-fed roller shelves. FIFO flow; good for perishables or date-sensitive items.
  • Robotics: Picking robots and collaborative robots (cobots) increasingly used for order fulfillment.
AS/RS provides maximum storage density, precise inventory control, and fast throughput with minimal labor — but has high capital cost and limited flexibility.
Poor selection of equipment or automation may add expense without increasing value if it cannot be effectively utilized.

What are the three types of warehouses?

Private (owned/leased by company, high control, high fixed cost), Public (third-party, variable cost, no commitment), Contract (dedicated third-party facility under long-term contract, balance of control and flexibility).

When is a public warehouse preferred over a private one?
ANSWER
When demand is seasonal or variable — public warehouses have variable costs with no long-term commitment, reducing risk when throughput fluctuates.

What is the tradeoff when adding more warehouses?
ANSWER
Warehousing costs increase (more fixed assets) but transportation costs decrease (shorter distances). The optimal number minimizes total cost while meeting service requirements.

1) Random/floating — any available slot; maximizes space but needs WMS. 2) Fixed — each SKU has a permanent location; easy but wastes space. 3) ABC slotting — fast movers near dock; slow movers farther. 4) Functional grouping — items grouped by type (hazardous, refrigerated, etc.).

What is ABC slotting?

Storing fast-moving (A) items near the shipping dock and slow-moving (C) items farther away — reduces picker travel time and improves throughput efficiency.

The movement and storage of goods inside the distribution center. Represents a capital cost balanced against labor and throughput efficiency.

What is an AS/RS?

What is an AGVS?
ANSWER
Automated Guided Vehicle System: driverless vehicles guided by wire, laser, or vision systems for automated material movement inside warehouses.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are live (flow) racks?
ANSWER
Gravity-fed roller shelving that enables FIFO product flow. Items loaded from one end roll forward to the pick face. Ideal for perishables or date-sensitive items.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are carousels in a warehouse?
ANSWER
Rotating shelving units (horizontal or vertical) that bring products to the picker — eliminating picker travel and improving picking speed.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between a forklift and a towline?
ANSWER
Forklifts are mobile vehicles that lift palletized loads. Towlines are fixed-path systems with four-wheeled containers towed by a drive mechanism in the floor — good for high-volume horizontal movement.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a bridge crane?
ANSWER
A horizontal girder (or pair of girders) spanning an aisle that moves heavy or awkward loads overhead — used when floor-level equipment cannot handle the load.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why might automation add costs without benefit?
ANSWER
Poor equipment selection or systems that cannot be effectively utilized may increase capital expense without improving throughput, accuracy, or labor efficiency.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is functional grouping in warehousing?
ANSWER
Storing items together by type — e.g., hazardous materials in one zone, refrigerated goods in another — for handling efficiency, safety compliance, or regulatory requirements.

Question 1
What are the primary functions of a warehouse?
A.Primarily storing goods for as long as possible
B.Receiving, storage, order picking, packing, shipping, and in some cases value-added services — collectively enabling products to flow from suppliers to customers efficiently and accurately
C.Manufacturing and assembly of products
D.Managing supplier relationships
Warehouses enable supply chain flow: receiving (unload, verify, put-away), storage (organized by product type, velocity, special requirements), order fulfillment (pick, pack, ship), and increasingly value-added services (kitting, labeling, returns processing).
Question 2
What is 'slotting' in warehouse management?
A.Cutting slots in warehouse shelving
B.The strategic placement of SKUs within the warehouse based on velocity, size, weight, and pick frequency — locating fast-moving items close to the shipping area to minimize travel time and improve picking efficiency
C.A method for scheduling receiving dock appointments
D.The process of assigning storage slots to incoming shipments
Slotting optimization places high-velocity items (A-items) near the shipping dock to minimize picker travel, groups items often ordered together, and considers ergonomics (heavy items at waist height). Good slotting reduces labor cost by 10-30%.
Question 3
What is the difference between 'pick-to-light' and 'voice picking' systems?
A.Pick-to-light is faster; voice is slower
B.Pick-to-light: LED lights on storage locations indicate what to pick and how many — hands-free, accurate for dense picking. Voice picking: spoken system instructions direct pickers while keeping hands and eyes free — flexible, accurate for variable environments
C.They are the same technology
D.Pick-to-light is used for large items; voice is used for small items
Pick-to-light works well in high-density picking areas (put walls, carton flow racks). Voice picking is flexible and portable — ideal for full-case picking or wide aisles where light systems are impractical. Both improve accuracy and productivity vs. paper.
Question 4
What is a 'warehouse management system' (WMS) and what are its core functions?
A.An enterprise planning system for the supply chain
B.Software that manages and optimizes warehouse operations — directing receiving, put-away, picking, packing, shipping, and inventory location tracking in real time using directed task management and system-assigned storage locations
C.A transportation scheduling system
D.A financial system for warehouse cost accounting
A WMS directs all warehouse activities: it assigns optimal storage locations, directs pickers to the right location via the most efficient route, tracks every inventory movement, manages labor, and interfaces with ERP for inventory and order management.
Question 5
What is the difference between 'FIFO' and 'FEFO' in warehouse management?
A.FIFO is more accurate; FEFO is an approximation
B.FIFO: First In, First Out — consume oldest received inventory first; FEFO: First Expired, First Out — consume inventory with the earliest expiry date first. FEFO is required for perishable goods where expiry date matters more than receipt date
C.They produce identical results in all cases
D.FIFO is for raw materials; FEFO is for finished goods
FIFO picks the oldest received inventory first (correct for most goods). FEFO picks the inventory expiring soonest first (essential for food, pharmaceuticals) — because a later-received shipment might have an earlier expiry date than an older receipt.
Question 6
What is the 'golden zone' in warehouse picking?
A.The most valuable storage location in a bonded warehouse
B.The storage area between knee and shoulder height — the most ergonomic and efficient zone for picking, where the fastest-moving items should be placed to minimize bending, reaching, and picker fatigue
C.A high-security storage area for valuable goods
D.The area closest to the warehouse dock
The golden zone is the ergonomic sweet spot for picking — knee to shoulder height. Placing high-velocity A-items in the golden zone reduces picking time, minimizes ergonomic strain, and reduces pick errors from awkward positions.
Question 7
What is 'cycle counting' and why is it preferred over annual physical inventory?
A.Counting inventory at the end of each production cycle
B.A continuous auditing approach that counts a small subset of inventory items daily or weekly on a rotating schedule — maintaining high accuracy without halting operations, unlike an annual full physical count that shuts down the warehouse
C.Counting items as they complete the production cycle
D.Counting inventory every quarter
Cycle counting maintains accuracy continuously: A-items counted most frequently, C-items less often. Problems are found and corrected daily — preventing error accumulation. Annual physical counts shut down the warehouse and may miss recurrence of systematic errors.
Question 8
What is 'pick accuracy' and how is it measured?
A.The speed at which orders are picked
B.The percentage of order lines picked correctly (right item, right quantity, right location) — typically 99.5%+ is the target. Errors cause costly returns, customer dissatisfaction, and reshipment costs
C.The number of items picked per hour by each picker
D.The accuracy of the warehouse inventory system
Pick accuracy = correctly filled lines / total lines × 100%. Even 99% accuracy means 1 in 100 lines is wrong — for a company shipping 10,000 lines/day, that's 100 errors/day. Technologies like pick-to-light, voice, and barcode scan-to-verify drive accuracy above 99.9%.
Question 9
What is a 'distribution center' (DC) vs. a 'warehouse'?
A.They are identical — 'distribution center' is just a modern name for 'warehouse'
B.A warehouse primarily stores goods for extended periods; a distribution center focuses on rapid throughput — receiving, cross-docking, and shipping with minimal storage time. DCs support distribution; warehouses support storage
C.A DC is owned by a 3PL; a warehouse is always company-owned
D.A DC is temperature-controlled; a warehouse is not
Though often used interchangeably, DCs emphasize throughput and distribution: goods move through quickly (same day or next day). Traditional warehouses hold inventory for longer periods. Modern DCs are highly automated for fast order processing and shipping.
Question 10
What is 'zone picking' in warehouse operations?
A.Picking only from designated safety zones
B.A picking method where the warehouse is divided into zones, each staffed by dedicated pickers who pick only items in their zone. Picked items are consolidated (by zone-skip or conveyor) into complete orders
C.A method for organizing storage by product category
D.Picking only during specified time zones (shifts)
Zone picking assigns each picker to a specific warehouse zone — they pick only items in their zone and pass the container to the next zone. This reduces travel time per picker, enables specialization, and supports parallel picking of large orders.
Question 11
What is 'labor management' in warehouse operations?
A.Human resources management for warehouse employees
B.Planning, measuring, and improving warehouse labor productivity — using engineered labor standards to set performance expectations, actual vs. standard measurement, coaching, incentive programs, and workforce scheduling to match labor to workload
C.Managing the warehouse manager's performance
D.Scheduling breaks for warehouse workers
Warehouse labor (typically 50-70% of operating cost) is the primary opportunity for improvement. Labor management systems (LMS) set engineered standards, measure actual performance vs. standard, identify training needs, and optimize labor allocation to maximize productivity.
Question 12
What is 'dock management' and why is it important?
A.Managing the physical structure of warehouse docks
B.Scheduling and coordinating inbound and outbound truck appointments, dock door assignments, and unloading/loading resources — to maximize dock throughput, minimize truck wait times, and balance inbound and outbound labor
C.Managing the dock employees' schedules
D.A safety protocol for dock equipment
Dock management (yard management) prevents bottlenecks: uncoordinated truck arrivals create peaks of waiting trucks and idle dock doors. Appointment scheduling and dock door assignment optimization maximize throughput and reduce carrier dwell time (often a contract penalty).
