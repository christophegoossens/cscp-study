Mode Selection: Value Density vs. Packaging Density

The best transport mode for a product is found by comparing its value density (value per unit of weight) vs. its packaging density (weight per unit of volume).

  • High value density — faster, more expensive mode (air, express)
  • Low value density — slower, cheaper mode (water, rail)
  • High packaging density — cheaper per pound to ship
  • Low packaging density — fills cube before weight limit; more expensive per pound

Rail Transport
  • Best for: Heavy loads, low-value goods, long distances, bulk commodities
  • Advantages: Very fuel-efficient (1 ton of freight ~470 miles per gallon in U.S.), low cost per ton-mile, environmentally friendly, carries virtually any product
  • Disadvantages: Slow, limited accessibility (can’t reach most final destinations), more damage to fragile goods, few carriers (U.S. oligopoly), expensive infrastructure
  • Intermodal: 25% of U.S. railroad revenue; TOFC/COFC (piggyback) combinations with trucks
Motor Carriers (Trucking)
  • Best for: Small-to-medium shipments, high-value goods, short-to-medium distances, door-to-door service
  • Advantages: Highest accessibility, faster than rail/water, low fixed costs, easy market entry, flexible
  • Three segments:
    • TL (Truckload): >15,000 lbs (6,818 kg); single shipper fills the trailer
    • LTL (Less-than-Truckload): <10,000 lbs (4,536 kg); consolidates multiple shippers
    • Specialty carriers: UPS, FedEx, specialized equipment
  • Disadvantages: Variable fuel costs, highway congestion, driver shortages, high variable costs per ton-mile vs. rail
Water Transport
  • Best for: Bulk, low-value, high-density cargo; long international routes
  • Advantages: Lowest cost per ton-mile (<1 cent per ton-mile), huge load capacity, sustainable, relieves highway congestion
  • Disadvantages: Slowest mode, very limited accessibility (ports/waterways only), weather delays, port strike risk, security concerns
  • Containers: Standard 8’ x 8’ wide; lengths 20’–53’. Containerization has revolutionized global trade.
Pipeline Transport
  • Best for: Liquids, gases (petroleum, natural gas, water, chemicals, slurry)
  • Advantages: Available 24/7/365, no weather disruptions, no labor required to operate once built, high safety record, natural monopoly, no return trip needed
  • Disadvantages: Only liquids/liquefiable products, very high construction cost, highly inflexible (fixed route), vulnerable to political disputes in international routes, limited use internationally
Air Transport
  • Best for: High-value, time-sensitive, low-weight/volume cargo; perishables
  • Advantages: Fastest mode, low loss/damage rates, low fixed costs (airports government-provided)
  • Disadvantages: Most expensive per pound, limited accessibility (few airports), weather delays, limited size/weight per shipment
  • Strong competitor with ocean freight for transoceanic carriage of high-value goods
Intermodal Transport
  • Piggyback (TOFC/COFC): Truck trailer or container placed on a flatcar (TOFC = trailer on flatcar; COFC = container on flatcar). Also RoadRailers (trailers with both rubber and steel wheels).
  • Trainship/Containership: Mixed mode including water carriage with rail or truck segments.
  • Truck-plane (birdyback): Air transport with surface legs before/after flight.
  • Benefits: Flexibility, efficiency, reduced costs. Transportation management software helps select optimal combinations.
Types of Carriers
Common (public) carrier: Transportation available to the public that does not provide special treatment to any one party and is regulated in regard to rates and liability. Regulated for public service, assumption of liability, no discrimination, and reasonable rates.
  • Private carrier: A company that owns or leases a fleet to transport its own products. Has control and scheduling flexibility but bears all capital costs. Can haul other goods (since 1980 U.S. deregulation).
  • Contract carrier: Not available to the general public; serves a limited number of shippers under specific contracts. Rates generally lower than common carriers. Independent truckers are a type of contract carrier.
  • Exempt carrier: Not subject to economic regulation of rates. Exemptions for specific commodities (fresh produce, some agricultural goods) or airport zones.
Common carrier = publicly available, regulated. Private = own goods only. Contract = selected clients under contract. Exempt = no rate regulation for specific commodities.

TERM / CONCEPT
What is value density and how does it affect mode selection?
ANSWER
Value density = value per unit weight. High value density → faster, more expensive mode (air, express). Low value density → slower, cheaper mode (water, rail, truck).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is packaging density and why does it matter?
ANSWER
Packaging density = weight per unit of volume. Low-density goods fill a vehicle’s cubic capacity before the weight limit (cube-out) — increasing cost per pound. High-density goods are more economical to ship.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are rail transport’s strengths and weaknesses?
ANSWER
Strengths: very fuel-efficient, low cost for bulk/long-distance, environmentally friendly. Weaknesses: slow, limited accessibility, higher damage risk for fragile goods, few carriers, expensive infrastructure.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the three trucking segments?
ANSWER
TL (Truckload): >15,000 lbs, single shipper fills trailer. LTL (Less-than-Truckload): <10,000 lbs, multiple shippers consolidated. Specialty: express carriers like UPS, FedEx.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is trucking dominant for last-mile delivery?
ANSWER
Highest accessibility (door-to-door), faster than rail/water, low fixed costs, easy market entry, flexible routing — trucks can reach destinations that other modes cannot.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is water transport best suited for?
ANSWER
Bulk, low-value, high-density cargo on long international routes. Lowest cost per ton-mile (<1 cent). Huge load capacity. Main drawbacks: slowest mode and very limited accessibility.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are pipeline transport’s unique characteristics?
ANSWER
Only stationary carrier (doesn’t move the vehicle). Available 24/7/365, no weather disruption, no return trip, no driver needed. Only transports liquids/liquefiable/gas products. Very high construction cost. Natural monopoly.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is air transport best suited for?
ANSWER
High-value, time-sensitive, low-weight cargo. Fastest mode with low damage rates. Most expensive per pound. Limited to airports; impractical for heavy/bulk freight.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is TOFC and COFC?
ANSWER
TOFC = Trailer On Flat Car (piggyback: truck trailer on a rail flatcar). COFC = Container On Flat Car. Both are intermodal combinations combining rail and truck advantages.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a RoadRailer?
ANSWER
A trailer with both rubber tires (for road) and steel wheels (for rail). A simple wheel change allows it to ride on either road or rail track — eliminating the need for a separate flatcar.
TERM / CONCEPT
ASCM definition: Common carrier
ANSWER
Transportation available to the public that does not provide special treatment to any one party and is regulated in regard to rates and liability.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the four regulations on common carriers?
ANSWER
1) Public service (must serve all within license scope), 2) Assumption of liability (carrier bears transport risk), 3) No discrimination (must take all legitimate business), 4) Reasonable rates (rates must be published and fair).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a private carrier?
ANSWER
A company that owns or leases vehicles to transport its own products. Must own/lease vehicles, manage their operation, and not be primarily in the transport business. Since 1980 deregulation, can also carry others’ goods.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a contract carrier?
ANSWER
Not required to serve the general public; makes contracts with a limited number of shippers. Rates generally lower than common carriers. The independent trucker is a special type of contract carrier.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is an exempt carrier?
ANSWER
A carrier not subject to economic rate regulation, with exemptions granted for specific commodities (e.g., fresh produce) or areas (e.g., airport zones). Still responsible for compliance with safety rules.

Question 1
Which transportation mode offers the fastest transit time for international shipments?
A.Ocean freight
B.Rail freight
C.Air freight — though at significantly higher cost, typically 4-6× ocean freight cost per unit weight
D.Truck freight
Air freight is the fastest mode for long-distance international shipments (1-3 days vs. 20-35 days for ocean). It is justified for high-value, time-sensitive, or emergency goods where holding cost or service failure cost exceeds the premium freight cost.
Question 2
What is the primary advantage of ocean freight vs. air freight?
A.Faster transit time
B.Dramatically lower cost per unit (typically 4-8× less than air) and very high capacity — ideal for large volumes of goods where time is not critical
C.Higher reliability and fewer delays
D.Better tracking and visibility
Ocean freight's cost advantage (4-8× less per unit than air) makes it the dominant mode for international trade in consumer goods, commodities, and non-perishables. The disadvantage: 20-35 day transit times require longer planning horizons and more pipeline inventory.
Question 3
What is a 'full container load' (FCL) vs. 'less than container load' (LCL) shipment?
A.FCL is only for liquid cargo; LCL is for dry cargo
B.FCL: shipper uses an entire ocean container exclusively (more economical for large shipments, direct loading, lower risk of damage); LCL: multiple shippers share a container (economical for small shipments but requires consolidation/deconsolidation, longer transit)
C.FCL is faster than LCL for the same route
D.LCL is only used for domestic ocean shipping
FCL gives shippers exclusive use of the container — lower cost per unit for large volumes, simpler handling, lower damage risk. LCL consolidates multiple shippers' cargo — economical for smaller volumes but adds consolidation time, cost, and handling.
Question 4
What is a 'freight forwarder' and what services does it provide?
A.A company that builds freight trucks
B.An intermediary that arranges international transportation on behalf of shippers — booking ocean/air space, managing documentation, arranging customs clearance, and coordinating the complete movement from origin to destination
C.A customs official who inspects freight
D.A company that finances freight invoices
Freight forwarders are international logistics intermediaries: they book transportation, prepare shipping documents (B/L, export declarations), arrange customs brokerage, and coordinate the full international shipment. They are essential for exporters/importers without in-house international expertise.
Question 5
What is a 'bill of lading' (B/L) in international shipping?
A.A government invoice for shipping duties
B.A legal document issued by the carrier to the shipper that serves as: (1) a receipt for goods, (2) evidence of the contract of carriage, and (3) a document of title (for negotiable B/Ls) — essential for international trade
C.A document listing the contents of a container
D.A customs declaration form
The bill of lading is one of the most important documents in international trade: it acknowledges receipt of goods by the carrier, evidences the contract terms (origin, destination, freight), and (when negotiable) can transfer title to goods in transit.
Question 6
What are 'incoterms' and why are they important in transportation?
A.Internal terms used by freight forwarders
B.Standardized international trade terms (published by ICC) that define the responsibilities of buyers and sellers for costs, risks, and insurance in international transactions — from EXW (seller's responsibility ends at factory) to DDP (seller delivers duty paid to destination)
C.Internet-based order terms for e-commerce
D.Terms specific to a particular country's import regulations
Incoterms (e.g., EXW, FOB, CIF, DDP) eliminate ambiguity in international contracts by precisely defining who pays for each leg of transportation, who bears risk during transit, and who arranges insurance — critical for avoiding costly disputes.
Question 7
What is a 'multimodal' shipment?
A.A shipment using multiple trucks
B.A shipment that uses two or more modes of transportation under a single contract and bill of lading — for example, truck from factory to port, ocean across the sea, and truck to the final destination
C.A shipment with multiple contents
D.A shipment requiring multiple customs declarations
Multimodal (or intermodal) transportation uses a single transport contract covering multiple modes — simplifying documentation and accountability. The carrier takes end-to-end responsibility even when physically handing off between modes.
Question 8
What is 'class' in LTL (less-than-truckload) pricing?
A.The quality grade of the cargo
B.A freight classification system (NMFC — National Motor Freight Classification) that assigns cargo to 18 classes (50-500) based on density, stowability, handling, and liability. Higher class = higher rate per unit weight
C.The cargo's hazardous material classification
D.A shipment size category
NMFC freight classes reflect the relative ease of shipping goods: high-density, easy-to-handle goods (class 50, e.g., bricks) are cheapest; low-density, difficult, or fragile goods (class 500, e.g., ping pong balls) are most expensive per pound.
Question 9
What is a 'spot rate' vs. a 'contract rate' in transportation?
A.Spot = rate for one location; contract = rate for multiple locations
B.Spot rate: current market rate for a one-time shipment, subject to supply/demand fluctuations; contract rate: negotiated rate locked in for a period (6-12 months), providing price stability but less flexibility
C.Spot = fastest transit; contract = standard transit
D.Contract rates are always lower than spot rates
Contract rates provide price stability (agreed for a period) and typically guaranteed capacity. Spot rates fluctuate with market conditions — lower than contract in weak markets (2020), dramatically higher in tight markets (2021-2022). Most shippers use a mix of both.
Question 10
What is a 'load broker' (freight broker) and how does it work?
A.A company that brokers insurance for freight
B.An intermediary that connects shippers with available truck capacity without owning any trucks — matching freight to carriers for a commission. Provides flexibility and market access but less control over carrier selection
C.A banker who finances freight operations
D.A government agency that monitors freight rates
Freight brokers (e.g., Echo, Coyote) match shippers with available carrier capacity through digital or traditional platforms — useful for spot freight, overflow capacity, and lanes where contract carriers can't pick up. The broker earns a margin on the difference between shipper and carrier rates.
Question 11
What is 'drayage' in logistics?
A.The process of drawing maps for delivery routes
B.Short-distance truck transportation, especially moving containers from a port or rail terminal to a nearby warehouse or between terminals — the first or last leg connecting intermodal transportation to the final destination
C.A type of ocean freight service
D.The process of loading goods into a container
Drayage is the short haul between a port/rail terminal and the nearby warehouse — the bridge between intermodal and the final destination. Drayage capacity constraints (a driver shortage problem) became a major bottleneck during the 2021 supply chain crisis.
Question 12
What is 'cross-border transportation' and what makes it more complex than domestic shipping?
A.Transportation crossing the equator
B.International transportation across country borders, which requires additional documentation (customs declarations, commercial invoices), compliance with trade regulations (tariffs, import licenses), and potentially multiple carriers in different countries
C.Crossing from one state to another in the US
D.Transportation that crosses multiple time zones
Cross-border adds regulatory complexity: export declarations, import customs entries, tariff classification (HS codes), duties and taxes, import licenses, trade compliance programs, and often multiple handoffs between domestic and international carriers.
