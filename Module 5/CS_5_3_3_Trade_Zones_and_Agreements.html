Free Trade Zones (FTZ)

A free trade zone (FTZ), called a foreign trade zone in the U.S., is a geographic area where normal trade barriers (customs, duties) are suspended to promote commerce. FTZs are organized around major seaports, international airports, and national frontiers.

FTZs are part of a broader category of Special Economic Zones (SEZs). Types of SEZs:

  • FTZs — support domestic or re-export trade
  • Export Processing Zones (EPZs) — enable export manufacturing
  • Urban enterprise zones — urban revitalization
  • Freeports — multi-use, integrated development

Key rule: Retail trade is forbidden in a free trade zone.

Benefits of FTZs for importers and manufacturers:

  • Exemption from customs formalities, duties, or quotas while goods are in the FTZ
  • Exemption from duties/quotas on re-exports from the FTZ
  • Deferral of duties on imports until goods enter the domestic market
  • Avoidance of fines — goods can be processed, remarked, repackaged before going through customs
  • Reduction of import duties on some cargoes via break-bulk before customs
  • Inspection of merchandise before paying duties
  • Avoidance of quota problems by holding cargo in FTZ until quota opens
  • Indefinite, cost-effective storage without local/state inventory taxes
  • Manufacture and assembly without “inverted duties” — when duty on components is higher than on finished goods

Drawback: Host country receives reduced revenue from import duties. However, economic activity, jobs, and trade volume typically offset this.

Trading Blocs
Trade bloc (trading bloc): An agreement between or among countries intended to reduce or remove barriers to trade within member countries. Frequently, but not always, a geographic region.

Types of trading blocs (from least to most integrated):

  • Free trade areas/zones: Reduce/eliminate tariffs among members; each country keeps its own external tariff policy
  • Preferential trade agreements: Preferential (not full) access to certain products from member countries
  • Customs unions: Free trade area + common external tariff policy
  • Common markets: Customs union + free movement of labor and capital; reduced physical/technical/fiscal barriers
  • Economic unions: Common market + unified economic policies
  • Customs and monetary unions: Customs union + shared currency
  • Economic and monetary unions: Common market + customs/monetary union (most integrated)
USMCA (replaced NAFTA 2020)

The USMCA went into effect July 1, 2020, replacing NAFTA. Key changes:

  • Agricultural tariffs: Zero tariffs remain; U.S. can export more dairy to Canada
  • Rules of origin: Higher local-content requirements (especially automotive) to qualify for zero tariffs
  • Certificate of origin: Eliminated — importers can claim favorable tariffs by self-certifying knowledge of origin
  • De minimis levels: Raised to support small/medium enterprises
  • Environmental and labor chapters: U.S., Canada, Mexico must enforce their own environmental and labor laws
  • Sunset clause: USMCA has an expiration date (unlike NAFTA); must be reviewed/renewed every 16 years
Effects of Trading Blocs on Supply Chains

Within blocs — gravity model: Trade volume between two countries is proportional to their economic size and inversely proportional to the distance between them. Bloc membership further amplifies trade between members.

  • Easier negotiation with fewer, larger partners
  • Lesser-developed member countries gain from more stable agreements
  • Reduced complexity for supply chains crossing member borders

Outside blocs:

  • Nonmembers may see their prices and export demand decrease (trade diversion)
  • Nonmembers’ supply chains can be impacted; their ability to negotiate multilateral deals may be reduced
Trade blocs create trade (more trade within the bloc) and divert trade (less trade with nonmembers). The net global economic effect is debated.

What are the key benefits of a free trade zone?

TERM / CONCEPT
What are inverted duties and how do FTZs help?
ANSWER
Inverted duties occur when the duty on imported components is higher than on the finished product. In an FTZ, components can be imported, assembled, and only the (lower) duty on the finished product is paid when entering the domestic market.
TERM / CONCEPT
ASCM definition: Trade bloc
ANSWER
An agreement between or among countries intended to reduce or remove barriers to trade within member countries — frequently, but not always, a geographic region.
TERM / CONCEPT
Rank the 7 types of trading blocs from least to most integrated.
ANSWER
1) Preferential trade agreements, 2) Free trade areas, 3) Customs unions (FTA + common external tariff), 4) Common markets (+ free movement of labor/capital), 5) Economic unions (+ unified policies), 6) Customs and monetary unions (+ shared currency), 7) Economic and monetary unions (most integrated).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between a customs union and a free trade area?
ANSWER
A free trade area reduces/eliminates tariffs among members but each country keeps its own external tariff policy. A customs union adds a common external tariff — members treat non-members identically.
TERM / CONCEPT
When did USMCA replace NAFTA and what is the sunset clause?
ANSWER
USMCA took effect July 1, 2020. The sunset clause means it has an expiration date and must be reviewed/renewed every 16 years — unlike NAFTA, which was in force until removed by members.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the USMCA rules of origin changes?
ANSWER
Higher local content requirements (especially automotive) to qualify for zero tariffs. Products not meeting these requirements face standard tariffs.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the gravity model in trade?
ANSWER
Used to predict trade volume between two countries: proportional to their economic sizes and inversely proportional to the distance between them. Bloc membership amplifies trade between members.
TERM / CONCEPT
How do trading blocs affect nonmember supply chains?
ANSWER
Nonmembers may see export prices and demand decrease (trade diversion). Their ability to negotiate multilateral deals may be weakened as bloc members increasingly trade with each other.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a special economic zone (SEZ)?
ANSWER
A broader category that includes FTZs. Types: FTZs (trade support), Export Processing Zones (manufacturing for export), Urban enterprise zones (revitalization), Freeports (multi-use integrated development).

Question 1
What is a 'free trade agreement' (FTA) and what benefit does it provide?
A.An agreement allowing goods to be transported without documentation
B.A treaty between two or more countries that reduces or eliminates tariffs, quotas, and other trade barriers between them — providing preferential market access for goods that meet the agreement's rules of origin
C.An agreement allowing free movement of workers between countries
D.A global treaty eliminating all tariffs on all goods
FTAs reduce/eliminate tariffs for goods that qualify (meet rules of origin) — providing cost advantages over competitors from non-FTA countries who pay full most-favored-nation (MFN) tariff rates.
Question 2
What is 'USMCA' and what did it replace?
A.A European trade agreement
B.The United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement — the free trade agreement replacing NAFTA (North American Free Trade Agreement) in 2020. It governs trade between the US, Mexico, and Canada with updated rules for automotive, digital trade, and labor standards
C.A global trade agreement administered by the WTO
D.A bilateral agreement between the US and Canada only
USMCA (effective July 2020) replaced NAFTA, maintaining duty-free trade between the US, Mexico, and Canada for qualifying goods while adding stricter rules of origin for automotive goods, enhanced labor and environmental provisions, and new digital trade chapters.
Question 3
What is a 'free trade zone' (FTZ) or 'foreign trade zone' and how does it benefit supply chains?
A.A zone where goods can be traded without any regulation
B.A designated area authorized by the government where goods can be imported, stored, manufactured, or processed with customs duties deferred or reduced until goods enter the domestic market — improving cash flow and enabling manufacturing cost optimization
C.A tax-free zone for retail shopping
D.A logistics zone free from all government regulations
FTZs (US) / EPZs / free zones offer: duty deferral (pay duties only when goods enter domestic market), duty inversion (pay lower finished goods rate vs. component rate), reduced merchandise processing fees, and streamlined customs procedures — improving supply chain cost competitiveness.
Question 4
What is the WTO (World Trade Organization) and its role in international trade?
A.A trade finance bank for international transactions
B.A global intergovernmental organization that provides a framework for trade negotiations, monitors trade policies, and provides dispute resolution between member countries — administered by the GATT/WTO agreements
C.A United Nations agency for managing global supply chains
D.An organization that sets global product standards
The WTO administers trade agreements (GATT, GATS, TRIPS), provides a forum for trade negotiations, monitors member trade policies, and resolves trade disputes through its dispute settlement mechanism — maintaining a rules-based international trading system.
Question 5
What is the 'most favored nation' (MFN) principle in trade?
A.A policy of giving the most important country special advantages
B.A WTO principle requiring that trade advantages given to one member country must be extended to all WTO members equally — so a country cannot discriminate by giving better tariff rates to some countries but not others (FTA preferences are an exception)
C.A policy favoring the country with which a company does the most trade
D.A rating system for preferred trading partners
MFN (non-discrimination) is a core WTO principle: tariff concessions granted to any one member must be given to all members. FTAs are an exception — they allow preferential tariffs for FTA partners, provided the agreement substantially covers trade between the parties.
Question 6
What is 'duty drawback' and how does it help manufacturers?
A.A refund given to importers who find damaged goods
B.A refund of customs duties paid on imported materials that are subsequently incorporated into exported goods — encouraging manufacturing for export by eliminating the duty cost on inputs
C.A duty reduction for companies that use domestic materials
D.A customs penalty for incorrect duty payment
Duty drawback allows manufacturers to recover duties paid on imported inputs when the finished goods are exported. For example, a furniture maker who imports wood, pays import duties, manufactures furniture, and exports it can claim a refund of 99% of the duties paid.
Question 7
What is 'tariff classification' and why is accuracy critical?
A.Classifying suppliers based on the tariffs they charge
B.Assigning the correct Harmonized System (HS) code to goods for customs purposes — determining the applicable tariff rate, import restrictions, and FTA preference eligibility. Misclassification can result in underpaid duties, fines, and shipment delays
C.Classifying tariffs by their payment method
D.Rating tariffs by their impact on business profitability
The HS code determines duty rate, import licensing requirements, FTA eligibility, and statistical reporting. Misclassification (intentional or accidental) can result in duty underpayment (customs fraud) or overpayment (lost savings) — accurate classification is both a compliance and cost management issue.
Question 8
What are 'Section 301 tariffs' and what supply chain decisions have they driven?
A.Tariffs on goods from Section 301 of the US Constitution
B.Additional tariffs (up to 25%) imposed by the US on goods from China under Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974, in response to China's alleged unfair trade practices. They have driven companies to shift sourcing from China to Vietnam, Mexico, and other countries
C.Tariffs on goods worth less than $301
D.Tariffs applied to all countries equally
Section 301 tariffs on Chinese goods (2018-present) added 7.5-25% to the cost of many products. This triggered major supply chain restructuring: nearshoring, Vietnam+1, Mexico manufacturing investment — demonstrating how trade policy directly drives supply chain network design.
Question 9
What is an 'economic sanctions' program and how does it affect supply chains?
A.A financial bonus program for high-performing trade partners
B.A government program that prohibits or restricts trade, financial transactions, or other dealings with specific countries, entities, or individuals — requiring all supply chain parties to screen against sanctions lists and avoid prohibited transactions
C.A tax incentive program for importing countries
D.An economic development program for emerging market suppliers
Sanctions (OFAC, EU, UN) prohibit trade with designated countries (Iran, North Korea, Cuba, Russia), entities, and individuals. Supply chains must screen all parties — suppliers, customers, freight forwarders, banks — against sanctions lists before completing transactions.
Question 10
What is 'transfer pricing' in international supply chains and why is it regulated?
A.The price charged to transfer goods between distribution centers
B.The prices charged between subsidiaries of the same multinational company for goods, services, or intellectual property transferred between them. Tax authorities regulate this to prevent profit shifting to low-tax jurisdictions — requiring arm's length pricing
C.The price charged for transferring goods through a free trade zone
D.The price adjustment made when goods are damaged in transit
Transfer pricing determines how profits are allocated among a multinational's subsidiaries. Tax authorities require 'arm's length' prices (what unrelated parties would charge) to prevent multinational companies from artificially shifting profits to low-tax countries through intra-company pricing.
Question 11
What is the 'Authorized Economic Operator' (AEO) program?
A.An international supply chain employee certification
B.A customs compliance program (the EU equivalent of C-TPAT) where trusted businesses that meet security and compliance standards are granted benefits including faster customs clearance and reduced inspections — recognition as a reliable supply chain partner
C.A government authorization for specific export products
D.A certification for customs brokers
AEO (EU) and equivalent programs (C-TPAT in US, Trusted Trader in various countries) certify that companies meet high security and compliance standards. Benefits: expedited clearance, reduced examination frequency, and mutual recognition with other countries' trusted trader programs.
Question 12
What is 'bonded warehouse' storage and when is it used?
A.A warehouse secured with physical bonds to prevent theft
B.A licensed facility where imported goods can be stored under customs supervision without paying import duties — duties are deferred until goods are released into the domestic market or re-exported. Used for duty management, staging, and import cost management
C.A warehouse requiring a financial bond from tenants
D.A government warehouse for seized goods
Bonded warehouses allow importers to defer duty payment — goods are stored duty-free while awaiting sale or distribution decisions. If goods are re-exported, no duty is owed. This improves cash flow and is useful for seasonal goods or goods awaiting import licenses.
