Transportation Objectives

Transportation has two primary objectives: product movement and temporary storage.

  • Product movement — efficient use of time: Inventory in transit is not earning a return. JIT emphasizes reducing transit time to lower inventory costs.
  • Product movement — efficient use of money: Minimize line-haul costs while meeting service needs. Total cost approach is essential.
  • Product movement — minimal harm to environment: Fuel use, emissions, noise, and water pollution. Regulations include Clean Truck Programs, EPA Tier 4, EU Low Emission Zones (LEZs).
  • Temporary storage: Vehicles used as short-term storage in three situations: (1) short-term holding before unloading, (2) crowded facility overflow, (3) changed destination (goods rerouted in transit via GPS).
Line-Haul Costs

Line-haul costs = basic costs of carrier operation to move a container of freight. Components include:

  • Vehicle costs: Own fleet vs. hire vs. contract — different capital vs. variable cost tradeoffs.
  • Driver/operator costs: Labor to operate the vehicle (except pipelines).
  • Vehicle operating costs: Repairs, cleaning, parking, maintenance.
  • General and administrative costs: Management of the transport function.
  • Insurance and security costs: Damage, loss, theft coverage.
Capacity Constraints: Economies of Distance & Scale
  • Distance: Cost increases with distance (more fuel, labor, time). But cost per unit decreases on a per-mile basis for longer hauls (tapering principle).
  • Volume: Economies of scale — cost per pound decreases as payload increases.
  • Density: Low-density goods cost more per pound to ship than dense goods. A weight cap exists (over-the-road limits); cube-out may occur before weight-out for low-density goods.

Capacity Constraints: Stowability, Handling, Liability
  • Stowability: Odd-shaped items that cannot efficiently share cargo space cost more. Packaging design affects stowability.
  • Handling: Special equipment, physical dangers, packaging groupings. Good packaging reduces handling costs. Packaging objectives: protection, efficiency, communication (labeling), low environmental impact.
  • Liability: Factors raising carrier liability risk: susceptibility to damage, perishability, high value per pound, susceptibility to theft. More liability risk = higher transport cost.
Transportation Tradeoffs (Conflicts of Interest)
  • Lot size vs. inventory: Large lots improve manufacturing efficiency but increase inventory. Advanced manufacturing and IT can reduce required lot sizes.
  • Inventory vs. transportation costs: Full payloads reduce per-unit transport cost but increase inventory. Distribution control systems (forecasting + delivery scheduling) help balance this.
  • Lead time vs. transport costs: Faster transport is more expensive. Forecasting reduces reliance on expediting.
  • Product variety vs. inventory: More SKUs = more inventory needed. Delayed differentiation (ship generic; customize near customer) is one solution.
Information technology reduces all four conflicts by improving forecast accuracy, enabling JIT, and supporting collaborative planning.
Transportation Capacity Forecasting Benefits

Integrating demand, marketing, manufacturing, and product information into transport forecasting produces:

  • Less reliance on expensive spot market for transport
  • Weeks of advance notice to carrier procurement
  • Carrier procurement decoupled from order creation
  • Easier arrangement of intermodal shipments (rail → truck) for savings
  • Backhauls planned to reduce deadheading (empty return trips)
  • Better warehouse staffing planning
  • More responsive and less expensive transport overall
Deadheading: An empty vehicle return trip after delivery. Backhaul planning reduces deadheading and lowers carrier costs.

TERM / CONCEPT
What are the two primary objectives of transportation?
ANSWER
1) Product movement (efficient use of time, money, and minimal environmental harm), and 2) Temporary storage (short-term holding, overflow handling, or rerouting in transit).
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the three situations for using vehicles as temporary storage?
ANSWER
1) Short-term storage before unloading, 2) Crowded facility overflow, 3) Changed destination — goods rerouted mid-route via GPS tracking.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the components of line-haul costs?
ANSWER
Vehicle costs, driver/operator costs, vehicle operating costs (maintenance, repairs), general and administrative costs, insurance and security costs.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the density constraint in transportation pricing?
ANSWER
Low-density goods cost more per pound to ship because they fill a vehicle's cube capacity before its weight limit. High-density goods are more economical per pound.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the volume constraint in transportation?
ANSWER
Economies of scale apply: cost per pound decreases as payload weight increases, since fixed operating costs are spread over more units.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the distance constraint (tapering principle)?
ANSWER
Shipping cost increases with distance but at a decreasing rate per mile — longer hauls have lower cost per mile because terminal/handling costs are fixed.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does liability affect transport cost?
ANSWER
Higher liability risk (damage, perishability, high value, theft risk) raises carrier costs. Carriers charge more to cover these risks.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the lot size vs. inventory tradeoff in transportation?
ANSWER
Manufacturers prefer large lots (lower per-unit cost), but large lots increase inventory holding costs. Advanced manufacturing and IT can enable smaller economic lot sizes.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is delayed differentiation in logistics?
ANSWER
Shipping generic products as far as possible and customizing/differentiating them closer to the customer — reduces inventory variety while maintaining responsiveness.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is deadheading and how is it reduced?
ANSWER
Deadheading = empty vehicle return trips after delivery. It is reduced by planning backhauls — coordinating return loads to fill the vehicle on the way back.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the benefits of transportation capacity forecasting?
ANSWER
Less spot market reliance, advance carrier procurement notice, decoupled procurement from orders, easier intermodal planning, reduced deadheading, better warehouse staffing, more responsive and cheaper transport.
TERM / CONCEPT
What packaging objectives should logistics consider?
ANSWER
1) Protection against damage, 2) Efficiency of handling/loading/unloading, 3) Communication (labeling for ID, tracking, customs, handling), 4) Low environmental impact.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the inventory vs. transport cost tradeoff?
ANSWER
Full payloads lower per-unit transport cost but require holding more inventory. Distribution control systems that combine forecasting and delivery scheduling can minimize this conflict.

Question 1
What are the five major modes of transportation?
A.Air, Sea, Rail, Bus, Bicycle
B.Truck (road), Rail, Air, Water (ocean/inland), and Pipeline — each with different cost, speed, capacity, and service characteristics making them suitable for different cargo and lane requirements
C.Truck, Rail, Air, Sea, and Drone
D.Express, Standard, Economy, Freight, and Parcel
The five modes: Road/truck (flexible, door-to-door, moderate cost), Rail (high capacity, low cost for bulk/heavy, but inflexible), Air (fastest, highest cost, for high-value/urgent), Water (highest capacity, lowest cost/unit, slowest), Pipeline (bulk liquids/gas only).
Question 2
What is a 'transportation management system' (TMS) and what does it optimize?
A.A system for managing transportation employees
B.Software that plans, executes, and optimizes freight movement — including carrier selection, load planning, route optimization, carrier tendering, freight audit, and shipment tracking. It typically reduces transportation cost by 5-15%
C.A system for tracking driver hours
D.A GPS system for fleet management
A TMS optimizes transportation across multiple dimensions: mode and carrier selection, load consolidation, route optimization, carrier tendering, freight bill audit, and performance tracking — saving 5-15% of freight cost while improving service.
Question 3
What is 'mode selection' in transportation strategy?
A.Selecting the truck driver for each shipment
B.Choosing the optimal transportation mode (truck, rail, air, ocean, intermodal) for each shipment based on cost, speed, reliability, and cargo characteristics — balancing service requirements with cost
C.Selecting the route for a specific truck
D.Choosing between full-truckload and less-than-truckload
Mode selection determines how goods move: high-value, time-sensitive goods may justify air; bulk commodities suit rail or ocean; regional fast-moving consumer goods need truck. The optimal mode balances transit time, cost, reliability, and special handling requirements.
Question 4
What is the difference between 'FTL' (full truckload) and 'LTL' (less-than-truckload) shipping?
A.FTL is faster; LTL is slower for the same distance
B.FTL: the shipper books an entire truck for their exclusive use — lower cost per unit, direct point-to-point. LTL: multiple shippers share a truck, their freight consolidated and deconsolidated at terminals — higher cost per unit but suitable for smaller shipments
C.FTL is for domestic; LTL is for international shipments
D.FTL is used only for hazardous materials
FTL: shipper pays for the truck regardless of how full it is — good for large shipments (≥15,000 lbs). LTL: pay only for the space used — efficient for smaller shipments, but involves multiple handling touches, longer transit, and higher per-pound cost.
Question 5
What is 'carrier selection' criteria in transportation management?
A.Choosing the carrier with the lowest quote
B.Evaluating carriers on total value: transit time, reliability (on-time delivery %), service area coverage, special handling capability, technology integration (EDI, tracking), safety record, and financial stability — not just price
C.Only selecting based on the carrier's brand reputation
D.Always selecting the carrier with the fastest transit time
Carrier selection should evaluate total value: a low-priced carrier with poor on-time delivery creates hidden costs (stock-outs, customer service failures, expediting). The optimal carrier provides the best combination of service, reliability, and cost for each lane.
Question 6
What is 'intermodal transportation' and what is its advantage?
A.Using two different trucks for one shipment
B.Combining two or more modes of transportation for a single shipment — most commonly rail for the long haul and truck for origin and destination pickup/delivery. Advantages: lower cost than all-truck, lower emissions, high capacity
C.A shipping method using special intermodal containers only
D.Using air and ocean freight in combination
Intermodal (primarily truck-rail, also ocean-rail-truck) captures rail's cost and environmental advantages for the long-haul while maintaining truck's door-to-door flexibility. It is typically 10-30% cheaper than all-truck for distances over 500 miles.
Question 7
What is a 'freight audit' and why is it important?
A.A government audit of freight company accounts
B.Systematic review and verification of carrier freight bills against contracted rates and actual shipment details — identifying overcharges, billing errors, and compliance with contract terms. Typically recovers 1-3% of freight spend
C.A quality audit of freight packaging
D.A safety inspection of freight vehicles
Freight audit verifies that carrier invoices match contracted rates and actual shipment details. Industry estimates suggest 3-5% of freight bills have errors — some in the carrier's favor. Automated audit and payment (AP) systems recover overcharges systematically.
Question 8
What is a 'routing guide' in transportation management?
A.A map of truck routes for drivers
B.A document or system that specifies preferred carriers and modes for each shipping lane (origin-destination pair) — ensuring shippers use contracted carriers at agreed rates, maintaining compliance and cost control
C.A GPS navigation system for warehouse vehicles
D.A guide for customs clearance routing
A routing guide specifies: for each lane (e.g., Chicago to Dallas, by weight break), use Carrier A as primary, Carrier B as backup, and mode X. It ensures compliance with contracted carriers and rates, and provides a systematic fallback when primary carriers can't pick up.
Question 9
What is 'freight cost optimization' and what levers are available?
A.Always choosing the cheapest carrier for every shipment
B.Minimizing total transportation cost through multiple levers: load optimization (fill trucks), mode shift (intermodal vs. all-truck), carrier consolidation (volume leverage), route optimization, delivery window management, and network design
C.Eliminating air freight from the transportation mix
D.Reducing the number of shipments by only delivering weekly
Transportation cost optimization uses multiple levers simultaneously: fill rates (consolidation reduces empty space), mode shift (rail/intermodal vs. truck), carrier consolidation (volume leverage for better rates), route optimization, and network design (fewer, better-located DCs).
Question 10
What is 'fuel surcharge' in freight pricing?
A.A government tax on fuel for transportation companies
B.A variable charge added to base freight rates that fluctuates with fuel prices — allowing carriers to recover fuel cost increases without renegotiating base tariffs. Typically calculated as a percentage of base freight
C.A discount for energy-efficient transportation choices
D.A fixed annual charge for fuel management
Fuel surcharges are typically indexed to the DOE diesel price index — as fuel prices rise, the surcharge % increases. Shippers should negotiate fuel surcharge indices and caps to limit exposure to fuel price volatility.
Question 11
What is 'private fleet' vs. 'for-hire carrier' and what drives the choice?
A.Private fleet is always cheaper; for-hire is always more flexible
B.Private fleet: company owns and operates its own trucks — provides control, service quality, and backhaul flexibility but requires capital and fleet management. For-hire: using external carriers — avoids capital but depends on carrier availability and pricing
C.Private fleet is only used for international shipping
D.For-hire carriers are always used for the last mile
Private fleet advantages: control of service, driver branding, backhaul flexibility. Disadvantages: capital investment, fixed cost regardless of volume, regulatory compliance burden. Most companies use a hybrid — private fleet for strategic lanes, for-hire for flexibility.
Question 12
What is 'collaborative transportation' and how does it reduce cost?
A.Competition between carriers to reduce rates
B.Multiple shippers sharing transportation capacity — for example, sharing truck loads, coordinating pickup/delivery routes, or jointly procuring transportation — to improve load factors and reduce total freight cost for all parties
C.A partnership between a carrier and their drivers
D.A government program to coordinate transportation infrastructure
Collaborative transportation (shippers co-loading, co-routing, or jointly procuring) reduces empty miles, improves load factors, and gives all parties leverage to negotiate better rates — reducing total transportation cost and environmental impact.
