Short-Term Demand Patterns
5 demand patterns relevant to short-term demand planning:
  • Trend: A long-run increase or decrease in demand over time (e.g., smartphones trend upward)
  • Seasonality: Predictable, recurring variation within a year (e.g., toy sales spike at Christmas)
  • Cycles: Longer-term oscillation tied to economic cycles — harder to predict (expansion/recession)
  • Random variation: Unexplainable, unpredictable fluctuations — cannot be modeled away
  • Promotion impact: Short-term demand spike from a sales promotion or marketing event
Trend: The long-run, underlying growth or decline in demand — separate from seasonal or cyclical movements.
Seasonality: A predictable, recurring demand variation within a year at the same time annually. Different from cycles (multi-year patterns) and trends (directional movement).
Random variation (noise): Demand fluctuations with no identifiable cause. Cannot be forecasted — only managed with safety stock.
Macroeconomic Demand Patterns
Macroeconomics: The analysis of the behavior of the overall economy (aggregate supply and demand) in response to various market forces.
Microeconomics: The study of economic behavior of individual market participants — firms, consumers, specific markets.
GDP (Gross Domestic Product): The total value of all final goods and services produced within a country's national boundaries in a given period. A key macroeconomic indicator.
Real GDP: GDP adjusted for inflation, so it reflects actual volume of production rather than just price changes.
The economy moves through cycles: expansion (rising GDP, low unemployment, high output) → peak → recession (falling GDP, rising unemployment) → trough → recovery. These cycles affect industry demand.
Economic cycle stages affect SC strategy: During expansion — invest in capacity, build inventory. During recession — reduce costs, manage excess inventory, protect cash flow.
Demand Curve Concepts
Demand curve: A graph showing the relationship between product price and quantity demanded. Normally downward sloping — higher price = lower demand.
Price elasticity of demand: Measures how much demand changes when price changes. Elastic demand = large change in demand for a price change. Inelastic demand = small change in demand regardless of price.
Supply chain implications of elasticity: For inelastic products (e.g., pharmaceuticals, utilities), price increases have little demand impact. For elastic products (e.g., luxury goods), price promotions significantly drive demand.
Demand volatility: The degree of variation in demand over time. High volatility requires more safety stock and more agile supply chains.
Analyzing Demand for Planning

Demand data should be cleansed before use — remove outliers from unusual events (one-time bulk orders, promotions) that won't repeat.

Causal analysis: Identifying which external factors drive demand changes — then tracking those factors as leading indicators.
Demand can be influenced at the tactical level (pricing, promotions, distribution changes) and at the operational level (managing orders and priorities).

Understanding demand patterns allows supply chain planners to choose the right forecasting method: trend models for trending data, seasonal models for seasonal data, etc.

How does seasonality differ from a cycle?
ANSWER
Seasonality: predictable, recurring variation WITHIN a year (e.g., Christmas toy spike). Cycles: longer-term oscillations spanning multiple years, tied to economic expansion/recession patterns — harder to predict.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is random variation and how should it be managed?
ANSWER
Random variation = unexplainable, unpredictable demand fluctuations (noise). It CANNOT be forecasted away. Managed through safety stock — buffer inventory held to absorb unexpected demand spikes.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is GDP and why does it matter for supply chain?
ANSWER
Gross Domestic Product = total value of all final goods and services produced within a country. GDP trends indicate economic expansion or recession — which affects industry demand levels and SC strategy.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is price elasticity of demand?
ANSWER
How much demand changes when price changes. Elastic = large demand change for a small price change (e.g., luxury goods). Inelastic = small demand change despite price changes (e.g., pharmaceuticals, utilities).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is demand volatility?
ANSWER
The degree of variation in demand over time. High volatility = harder to forecast accurately, requires more safety stock, needs more agile supply chain capabilities.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between macroeconomics and microeconomics?
ANSWER
Macroeconomics: studies behavior of the OVERALL economy (aggregate supply/demand, GDP, inflation, employment). Microeconomics: studies behavior of INDIVIDUAL markets, firms, and consumers.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why must demand data be cleansed before forecasting?
ANSWER
Raw historical data may contain outliers from one-time events (bulk orders, promotions, disasters) that won't repeat. Using uncleansed data corrupts the forecast. Outliers must be removed or adjusted.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a demand curve?
ANSWER
A graph showing the relationship between price and quantity demanded. Normally downward sloping — as price rises, demand falls. The slope (elasticity) determines how sensitive demand is to price changes.

Q1 of 6
A toy manufacturer sees sales spike every November-December, then drop sharply in January. This demand pattern is best described as:
Seasonality = predictable, recurring variation WITHIN a year at the same time annually. The November-December toy spike is a classic seasonal pattern — it recurs annually and is predictable, so it can be incorporated into forecasts.
Q2 of 6
After a major one-time bulk order inflates historical demand data, a supply chain planner should:
Demand data must be CLEANSED before forecasting. One-time events (bulk orders, disasters, promotions) that won't repeat must be removed or adjusted — otherwise they inflate the forecast with phantom demand.
Q3 of 6
For a pharmaceutical company's essential medicines, demand is likely:
Essential medicines are typically INELASTIC — patients must continue treatment regardless of price changes. This has SC implications: price promotions have little effect on demand, and supply chain design focuses on availability rather than price.
Q4 of 6
An economic recession would most directly affect a supply chain by:
During recessions, consumer and business spending falls — reducing demand for most goods. SC managers must respond by adjusting production, managing inventory down, protecting cash flow, and potentially renegotiating supplier contracts.
Q5 of 6
Random variation in demand is best handled by:
Random variation is, by definition, unpredictable — it cannot be forecasted away. The correct management approach is SAFETY STOCK — buffer inventory held to absorb unexpected demand spikes without causing stockouts.
Q6 of 6
A demand trend is best described as:
A trend is the long-run, underlying directional movement in demand — upward or downward over an extended period. It's separate from seasonal variation (annual recurrence) and cyclical fluctuation (multi-year economic swings).
