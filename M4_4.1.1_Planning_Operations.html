The Manufacturing Planning and Control (MPC) Hierarchy
Operations planning follows a hierarchical cascade — each level refines the previous into greater detail:
  • 1. Business plan: Financial goals, revenue targets — the highest-level plan
  • 2. Sales & Operations Plan (S&OP): Aggregate production plan at product family level (months)
  • 3. Master Production Schedule (MPS): Item-level plan for finished goods (weeks)
  • 4. Material Requirements Planning (MRP): Component and raw material requirements derived from MPS
  • 5. Production Activity Control (PAC): Day-by-day scheduling and execution of work orders on the shop floor
Master Production Schedule (MPS): A statement of what the company plans to produce, expressed in specific configurations, quantities, and dates. It is the primary driver of MRP.
Production plan: The output of S&OP — an aggregate plan specifying production rates, workforce levels, and inventory targets at the product family level.
Each planning level includes a capacity check: Resource planning (S&OP) → Rough-cut capacity planning (MPS) → Capacity Requirements Planning (MRP) → Capacity Control (execution).
Master Scheduling
Master scheduling: The process of creating and maintaining the MPS — committing the company to produce specific items in specific quantities at specific times.

The MPS is driven by: customer orders (firm demand) + forecast (anticipated demand) + safety stock requirements.

Available-to-Promise (ATP): The uncommitted portion of planned production — used by sales to promise specific delivery dates to customers without over-committing supply.
Planning horizon: How far forward the MPS extends. Must cover the cumulative lead time for the longest-lead-time item in the product.
Time fence: A boundary in the MPS planning horizon. Inside the demand time fence = firm (no automatic changes). Between demand and planning fence = limited changes. Beyond planning fence = flexible.
The MPS is the critical link between demand (S&OP/forecast) and supply (MRP/production). Getting the MPS right is foundational to the entire manufacturing planning process.

TERM / CONCEPT
ASCM definition: Master Production Schedule (MPS)
ANSWER
A statement of what the company plans to produce — in specific configurations, quantities, and dates. The primary driver of MRP. Bridges the aggregate production plan and the detailed component/material plan.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Available-to-Promise (ATP)?
ANSWER
The uncommitted portion of current inventory and planned production. Used by sales to promise delivery dates without over-committing supply. ATP = planned production − already committed customer orders.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a time fence in master scheduling?
ANSWER
A boundary in the MPS horizon: - Demand time fence (inner): frozen zone — no automatic changes, only manual by master scheduler - Planning time fence (outer): moderate flexibility - Beyond planning fence: fully flexible, system can auto-adjust
TERM / CONCEPT
4 capacity checks in the MPC hierarchy
ANSWER
1) Resource planning — checks S&OP against long-term capacity 2) Rough-cut capacity planning (RCCP) — checks MPS feasibility 3) Capacity Requirements Planning (CRP) — detailed check at MRP level 4) Capacity control — real-time check at execution (PAC)
TERM / CONCEPT
What drives the Master Production Schedule?
ANSWER
Customer orders (firm demand) + demand forecast (anticipated demand) + safety stock replenishment requirements. The MPS commits the company to produce specific items in specific quantities at specific dates.

Q1 of 5
The Master Production Schedule (MPS) is the primary input to:
MRP uses the MPS as its primary input — the MPS says what finished goods to make and when, and MRP calculates all the raw material and component requirements needed to support that plan.
Q2 of 5
Inside the demand time fence, the MPS is:
The demand time fence is the frozen zone. Inside it, the master scheduler manually controls any changes — to avoid costly disruption to production already in progress. Automatic system changes are blocked in this zone.
Q3 of 5
Rough-Cut Capacity Planning (RCCP) checks the feasibility of:
RCCP validates that the MPS is feasible given key resource capacities (critical machines, labor, key suppliers) — before investing in the full MRP calculation. It's a high-level feasibility check, not a detailed resource analysis.
Q4 of 5
The planning horizon of the MPS must be at least as long as:
The MPS horizon must extend far enough to allow time to procure all materials and complete all production. The minimum is the cumulative lead time (purchasing + manufacturing + assembly) for the product with the longest total lead time.
Q5 of 5
A sales rep wants to promise a customer a delivery in 3 weeks. The master scheduler should check:
ATP = the uncommitted portion of planned production. The sales rep checks ATP to confirm that enough planned production exists in week 3 that hasn't already been promised to other customers — before making the commitment.
