8.2.2 · Sustainability Guidelines & Standards

Sustainability Reporting Frameworks
GRI (Global Reporting Initiative): The most widely used framework for sustainability reporting — companies disclose their environmental, social, and governance impacts. GRI standards cover supply chain management, labor practices, emissions, and anti-corruption.
ISO 14001: International standard for Environmental Management Systems (EMS) — provides a framework for organizations to manage environmental responsibilities systematically, including supply chain impacts. Certification demonstrates credible environmental commitment.
ISO 26000: International guidance standard for social responsibility — covering human rights, labor practices, the environment, fair operating practices, consumer issues, and community involvement. Not certifiable (guidance only).
UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs): 17 global goals adopted by all UN member states — providing a shared blueprint for prosperity while protecting the planet. SDGs 8 (decent work), 12 (responsible consumption/production), and 13 (climate action) are most relevant to supply chain.
Industry-Specific Standards
SMETA (Sedex Members Ethical Trade Audit): A widely used social audit methodology for supply chains — covers labor, health & safety, environment, and business ethics. One audit can be shared across multiple buyers, reducing supplier audit fatigue.
Responsible Business Alliance (RBA) / EICC: Industry coalition (electronics, tech) setting standards for social, environmental, and ethical conduct in supply chains. Members include Apple, Samsung, Intel. RBA-VAP (Validated Audit Program) is the audit framework.
FSC (Forest Stewardship Council): Certification for wood and paper products sourced from responsibly managed forests. Chain of custody certification tracks certified materials through the supply chain.
Third-party audits and certifications provide independent verification — more credible than self-assessment because an accredited body assesses compliance against defined standards. Customers, investors, and regulators increasingly require certified supply chain standards.
Regulatory Requirements
Growing mandatory supply chain sustainability regulation:
  • EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD): Requires large companies to disclose sustainability information including supply chain impacts — effective 2024+
  • EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD): Requires companies to identify and address human rights and environmental impacts throughout their supply chains
  • US Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act (UFLPA): Prohibits imports from Xinjiang region absent proof goods are not made with forced labor
  • UK Modern Slavery Act: Requires large companies to report on steps taken to eliminate modern slavery in supply chains
The trend is clear: supply chain sustainability is moving from voluntary best practice to legal requirement — especially in EU markets. Companies supplying EU customers must comply regardless of where they're headquartered.

TERM / CONCEPT
GRI vs. ISO 14001 vs. ISO 26000
ANSWER
GRI (Global Reporting Initiative): REPORTING framework — companies disclose ESG impacts in a standardized way. Widely used for sustainability reports. ISO 14001: MANAGEMENT SYSTEM standard — structured EMS (Environmental Management System). Certifiable by third parties. Demonstrates credible environmental management. ISO 26000: GUIDANCE standard for social responsibility — not certifiable. Covers human rights, labor, environment, community. Use for guidance, not certification.
TERM / CONCEPT
Key EU supply chain sustainability regulations
ANSWER
CSRD (Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive): large companies must DISCLOSE sustainability information including supply chain impacts. Effective 2024+. CSDDD (Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive): companies must IDENTIFY AND ADDRESS human rights and environmental impacts throughout their supply chains. Legal liability if non-compliant. Both apply to EU companies AND non-EU companies that sell in the EU above certain revenue thresholds. Will reshape global supply chain standards.
TERM / CONCEPT
SMETA audit — what it covers and why it matters
ANSWER
Sedex Members Ethical Trade Audit: standardized social audit methodology. Covers 4 pillars: 1. Labor standards (working hours, wages, freedom of association) 2. Health & safety (facilities, emergency procedures, PPE) 3. Environment (waste, emissions, hazardous substances) 4. Business ethics (bribery, corruption, sub-contracting) Key feature: SHARED. One supplier's SMETA audit can be accessed by multiple buyers → reduces audit fatigue (supplier doesn't need multiple buyer-specific audits). Platform: Sedex (Supplier Ethical Data Exchange).
TERM / CONCEPT
UN SDGs most relevant to supply chain
ANSWER
SDG 8: Decent Work and Economic Growth — fair wages, safe conditions, no child/forced labor in supply chains SDG 12: Responsible Consumption and Production — sustainable sourcing, circular economy, waste reduction SDG 13: Climate Action — carbon reduction, emissions management across supply chains Also relevant: SDG 14/15 (Life below water / on land) for sustainable sourcing from natural ecosystems Supply chains touch virtually all 17 SDGs.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why third-party audits are more credible than self-assessment
ANSWER
Self-assessment: supplier fills in questionnaire themselves → conflict of interest, may misrepresent or not know actual conditions. Third-party audit: independent accredited auditor visits facility, checks records, interviews workers → no conflict of interest → more reliable. Limitations of audits: point-in-time snapshot; can be 'gamed' by suppliers who prepare for announced audits; don't address structural causes (pricing pressure forcing subcontracting). Best practice: combine audits with supplier development, capacity building, and addressing root causes of non-compliance.
TERM / CONCEPT
RBA (Responsible Business Alliance) — what it is
ANSWER
Industry coalition setting standards for social, environmental, and ethical conduct in electronics/tech supply chains. Members: Apple, Samsung, Intel, HP, Dell, and many others. RBA Code: covers labor, health & safety, environmental management, ethics, and management systems. RBA-VAP (Validated Audit Program): standardized audit protocol. One audit shared across multiple RBA members. Why it matters: Apple requires all its suppliers to comply with RBA Code — cascades through the entire electronics supply chain globally.

Q1 of 5
ISO 14001 is relevant to supply chain management because:
ISO 14001: international standard for Environmental Management Systems (EMS). A company with ISO 14001 certification has: defined its environmental aspects and impacts (including supply chain), set objectives and targets, established controls and procedures, trained employees, and undergone third-party audit verification. For supply chain: many buyers require ISO 14001 certification from key suppliers as evidence of credible environmental management — particularly for manufacturing suppliers with significant environmental footprints.
Q2 of 5
The SMETA audit methodology addresses audit fatigue by:
Audit fatigue: large suppliers may receive dozens of separate customer-specific audits annually — wasting time and money. SMETA solution: standardized audit methodology on the Sedex platform. Supplier undergoes ONE SMETA audit → the report is uploaded to Sedex → all buyers who are Sedex members can access it. Supplier audited once; many buyers' requirements satisfied. Same principle as RBA-VAP in the electronics industry.
Q3 of 5
The EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD) requires companies to:
CSDDD (also known as CS3D): goes beyond REPORTING (CSRD) to DUTY OF CARE. Companies must conduct due diligence to identify human rights and environmental violations in their supply chains, take action to prevent and address them, and can face civil liability if they fail. It applies to large EU companies and large non-EU companies selling in the EU. This fundamentally changes the legal landscape: supply chain violations are no longer just reputational risk — they create legal liability for the buyer.
Q4 of 5
The US Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act (UFLPA) impacts supply chains by:
UFLPA (effective June 2022): any goods produced in Xinjiang, China are PRESUMED to be made with forced labor and are banned from US import — unless the importer can demonstrate (with clear and convincing evidence) that the goods are NOT made with forced labor. The burden of proof is on the importer, not the government. Impact: companies must map their supply chains to identify any Xinjiang origin materials (cotton, polysilicon, tomatoes, etc.) and either eliminate those sources or be prepared to provide evidence of audit and traceability.
Q5 of 5
The UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) most directly relevant to supply chain management include:
SDG 8 (Decent Work): labor standards, living wages, safe working conditions, no child/forced labor — all core supply chain social responsibility topics. SDG 12 (Responsible Consumption/Production): sustainable sourcing, circular economy, waste reduction, product eco-design — all core supply chain environmental topics. SDG 13 (Climate Action): carbon reduction — supply chain Scope 3 emissions are the dominant corporate carbon source. These three SDGs collectively capture the major sustainability dimensions of supply chain management.
