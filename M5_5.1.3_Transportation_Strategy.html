Transportation's Role in the Supply Chain
Transportation: The physical movement of goods from one location to another — creating place utility. It is typically the largest single cost component of logistics (averaging 40–60% of total logistics cost).
Transportation strategy must balance three dimensions: cost (rate per unit shipped), service (transit time, reliability, damage rate), and flexibility (ability to handle exceptions). Optimizing all three simultaneously is the strategic challenge.
Freight terms (Incoterms): Internationally agreed terms defining who bears responsibility and cost for shipment at each stage. Common terms: EXW (Ex Works — buyer pays all), FOB (Free on Board — seller pays to named port/location), DDP (Delivered Duty Paid — seller pays everything to destination).
Transit time vs. transit time variability: Transit time = how long shipment takes. Variability = how unpredictable that time is. Variability is often more damaging than slow transit — uncertainty forces higher safety stock.
Transportation Modes
Five primary transportation modes — each with distinct cost/service trade-offs:
  • Truck (road): Most flexible, door-to-door delivery, high cost per ton-mile, ideal for short-to-medium distances
  • Rail: Low cost for bulk/heavy freight, slow, limited to rail network, long-haul advantage
  • Air: Fastest, highest cost, ideal for high-value, time-sensitive, low-weight goods
  • Ocean (water): Lowest cost per ton-mile, slow, ideal for international bulk/container shipping
  • Pipeline: Very low cost for liquids/gases, high fixed cost, no flexibility (fixed route)
Intermodal transportation: Using two or more modes in a single shipment — typically truck + rail (TOFC: trailer on flatcar) or truck + ocean (containerization). Combines the advantages of each mode: rail/ocean cost efficiency + truck door-to-door flexibility.
Transportation Management
Transportation Management System (TMS): Software that plans, executes, and optimizes freight movements — carrier selection, load planning, route optimization, freight payment, and performance tracking.
Freight consolidation: Combining multiple small shipments into a larger load — reducing cost per unit by filling carrier capacity. LTL (Less-than-Truckload) shipments are consolidated by carriers; shippers can consolidate internally before tendering.
LTL vs. FTL: Less-than-Truckload (LTL) = shipment doesn't fill a truck — multiple shippers share space, slower, more handling, lower cost for small volumes. Full Truckload (FTL) = one shipper fills the truck — faster, fewer touches, better for large volumes.
Key transportation cost drivers: distance, weight, density, stowability, handling requirements, liability (value of goods), and market factors (carrier capacity and fuel prices).

TERM / CONCEPT
What is intermodal transportation?
ANSWER
Using two or more transportation modes in a single shipment, typically in a standardized container. Examples: • Truck to port → ocean container → truck from port (international) • TOFC: Trailer On FlatCar (truck trailer carried by rail for long haul) Advantage: combines ocean/rail cost efficiency with truck door-to-door flexibility. Standardized containers eliminate reloading.
TERM / CONCEPT
LTL vs. FTL
ANSWER
LTL (Less-than-Truckload): shipment < full truck. Multiple shippers share space. Carrier consolidates loads at hub. Slower, more handling touches, risk of damage. Lower cost for small volumes. FTL (Full Truckload): one shipper fills truck. Direct delivery, faster, fewer touches. Lower cost per unit at scale. Best for large, regular shipments.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a TMS?
ANSWER
Transportation Management System — software for planning, executing, and optimizing freight. Capabilities: carrier selection, rate comparison, load/route optimization, shipment tracking, freight audit & payment, KPI reporting. Benefit: reduces freight cost 5–15% through better mode/carrier selection and load optimization; improves visibility and on-time performance.
TERM / CONCEPT
Transit time variability — why does it matter more than transit time?
ANSWER
Transit time = average delivery time (e.g., 3 days). Variability = range around that average (e.g., 2–5 days). Variability forces HIGHER safety stock: if you can't predict when product arrives, you must buffer against late deliveries. Low variability (reliable, consistent) = lower safety stock needed, even if average transit time is longer. High variability = must carry more safety stock regardless of average transit time.
TERM / CONCEPT
Key transportation cost drivers (DWSHLM)
ANSWER
Distance: farther = more cost Weight: heavier = more cost Stowability (density): low-density goods cost more (pay for space not weight) Handling: special requirements (hazmat, refrigeration) = premium Liability: high-value goods = higher insurance/care Market: carrier capacity and fuel prices Shippers can reduce costs by: shipping denser loads, consolidating, choosing optimal mode, using a TMS.

Q1 of 5
A company needs to ship electronics from China to the US. The least expensive mode would typically be:
Ocean freight has the lowest cost per ton-mile of any mode — ideal for international shipments where transit time (typically 2–4 weeks from Asia to US) is acceptable. Electronics are high-value but can tolerate ocean transit time with appropriate lead time planning. Air freight would be dramatically more expensive (8–10× ocean cost per kg) and is reserved for urgent/very high-value shipments where time cost justifies the premium.
Q2 of 5
Transit time VARIABILITY forces companies to maintain higher safety stock because:
Safety stock exists primarily to cover uncertainty — in demand AND in supply lead time. If a supplier consistently delivers in 5 days, you plan for 5 days. If it varies 3–8 days, you must plan for the worst case (8 days) to avoid stockouts — carrying extra safety stock for those extra 3 days. Reducing lead time VARIABILITY (not necessarily average lead time) is one of the highest-leverage ways to reduce safety stock.
Q3 of 5
Intermodal transportation is used to:
Intermodal = multiple modes, single shipment, standardized container. Example: truck picks up at factory (flexibility), container loaded on train for 1,000-mile haul (cheap bulk), truck delivers to customer (flexibility). The container doesn't change — eliminating reloading cost and time. Benefits: lower cost than all-truck, faster than all-rail, more flexible than all-rail.
Q4 of 5
A shipper with a small, time-sensitive shipment that doesn't fill a truck would use:
LTL = Less-than-Truckload. The carrier consolidates multiple small shippers' loads. The shipper pays only for the space their freight occupies. Trade-offs: slower (consolidation/deconsolidation adds time), more handling (damage risk), but cost-effective for small shipments. FTL would mean paying for an entire truck the shipper can't fill — wasteful for small volumes.
Q5 of 5
Freight consolidation reduces transportation cost by:
Carriers price freight based partly on filling their capacity. A full truck costs roughly the same as a half-empty truck — so cost per unit drops as load increases. Consolidation: combine multiple small shipments going to the same region into one full load. Ship the full load at FTL rates instead of multiple LTL shipments. Lower cost per unit, fewer carrier pickups to manage.
