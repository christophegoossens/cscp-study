What is Production Activity Control?
Production Activity Control (PAC): The function of routing and dispatching work through the production process according to the production schedule — and then tracking and reporting progress.

PAC is the execution layer of manufacturing — it translates the MRP plan into actual shop floor work.

PAC activities: release work orders → schedule → dispatch → monitor progress → report completions → manage exceptions (delays, quality holds, machine breakdowns).
Work order (shop order): An authorization to produce a specific quantity of an item by a specific date. Contains: item number, quantity, due date, routing, and material requirements.
Routing: A list of operations required to produce an item — in sequence — with the work center, setup time, and run time for each operation.
Scheduling and Dispatching
Scheduling: Assigning jobs or operations to specific time slots and resources (machines, workers) to meet production due dates.

Scheduling approaches:

  • Forward scheduling: Schedule operations starting from today forward — determines the earliest possible completion date
  • Backward scheduling: Schedule starting from the due date backward — determines the latest start date to still meet the due date
  • Finite scheduling: Considers actual capacity limits — no operation can exceed available capacity
  • Infinite scheduling: Ignores capacity constraints — shows overloads for manual resolution
Dispatching: Selecting and sequencing work orders at each work center based on priority rules.
Common dispatching priority rules:
  • FCFS (First Come, First Served): Process in order received
  • EDD (Earliest Due Date): Process by soonest due date first — minimizes late jobs
  • SPT (Shortest Processing Time): Process quickest jobs first — minimizes average flow time
  • CR (Critical Ratio): (Time remaining / Work remaining) — ratio < 1 = behind schedule, prioritize urgently
Critical Ratio (CR) = (Due Date − Today's Date) / (Remaining Work Days) CR < 1 = behind schedule (urgent) CR = 1 = on schedule CR > 1 = ahead of schedule

ASCM definition: Production Activity Control (PAC)
ANSWER
The function of routing and dispatching work through the production process per the production schedule — and tracking/reporting progress. The execution layer that translates MRP plans into shop floor action.
TERM / CONCEPT
Forward vs. backward scheduling
ANSWER
Forward: start from today, schedule forward → find EARLIEST possible completion. Backward: start from due date, schedule backward → find LATEST start to still meet due date. Most production uses backward scheduling to determine when to start.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a routing in manufacturing?
ANSWER
A list of operations required to produce an item — in sequence — specifying: work center, setup time, and run time for each step. Used by PAC/scheduling to plan and track production flow.
TERM / CONCEPT
Critical Ratio (CR) formula and interpretation
ANSWER
CR = (Due Date − Today) / Remaining Work Days CR < 1: behind schedule → highest priority CR = 1: exactly on schedule CR > 1: ahead of schedule → lower priority CR is a dynamic dispatching rule — recalculated as conditions change.
TERM / CONCEPT
4 dispatching priority rules
ANSWER
FCFS: First Come, First Served (order received) EDD: Earliest Due Date (minimize late jobs) SPT: Shortest Processing Time (minimize average flow time) CR: Critical Ratio (urgency = time remaining vs. work remaining)
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a work order (shop order)?
ANSWER
Authorization to produce a specific quantity of an item by a specific date. Contains: item number, quantity, due date, routing (operations), and material requirements. The primary document driving shop floor execution.

Q1 of 5
A work order has a due date 5 days from now, and 3 days of work remaining. The Critical Ratio is:
CR = (Due Date − Today) / Remaining Work = 5 / 3 = 1.67. CR > 1 means AHEAD of schedule — there is more time available than work remaining. This order can be deprioritized in favor of orders with CR < 1.
Q2 of 5
A factory wants to minimize the number of late orders in a busy period. Which dispatching rule should they use?
EDD (Earliest Due Date) minimizes the number of late jobs by always processing the job with the soonest due date first. SPT minimizes average flow time but can cause jobs with longer processing times to be very late. EDD is the standard rule for minimizing lateness.
Q3 of 5
Production Activity Control (PAC) is best described as:
PAC = execution. It translates the MRP plan into actual shop floor activity — releasing work orders, scheduling operations, dispatching jobs to work centers, and tracking/reporting completions. It is real-time operational management, not planning.
Q4 of 5
Backward scheduling from a due date is used to determine:
Backward scheduling starts from the DUE DATE and works backward through each operation's lead time to determine WHEN to start. This gives the latest allowable start date while still meeting the customer delivery commitment.
Q5 of 5
Finite scheduling differs from infinite scheduling in that it:
Finite scheduling respects actual capacity limits — if a work center is full, additional jobs are queued or moved to other periods. Infinite scheduling ignores capacity and creates overloads that planners must manually resolve. Finite is more realistic but computationally more complex.
