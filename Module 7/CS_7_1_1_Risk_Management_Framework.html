Risk Definitions
Risk: An uncertainty that could positively or negatively affect business objectives (includes threats and opportunities).
Supply chain risk: The variety of possible events and their outcomes that could have a negative effect on the flow of goods, services, funds, or information resulting in some level of quantitative or qualitative loss.
Risk management: The identification, assessment, and prioritization of risks followed by coordinated and economical application of resources to minimize, monitor, and control the probability and/or impact of unfortunate events or to maximize the realization of opportunities.
Supply chain risk management (SCRM): The systematic identification, assessment, and mitigation of potential supply chain disruptions with the objective of reducing their negative impacts on the supply chain’s performance.
  • Threat = negative risk; Opportunity = positive uncertainty
  • Redundancies, visibility, responsiveness, and adaptability promote steady operations
Risk Management Process (4 Steps)
  • Prerequisites: Develop risk strategy (assess tolerance & maturity) + risk management plan
  • 1. Identify risks: Brainstorm all threats/opportunities → assign attributes → enter in risk register
  • 2. Assess & classify: Qualitative analysis (Exhibit 7-1) → optional quantitative analysis (Exhibit 7-2)
  • 3. Develop response plan: Select best response per risk → assign owners → get approval & funding → add to register
  • 4. Execute response plan: Implement responses → analyze trends → review register regularly → retire irrelevant risks

Risk Managers & Maturity

Risk manager duties:

  • Map the entire supply chain and understand interdependencies
  • Identify potential failure points
  • Create risk awareness throughout the chain
  • Devise methods to lessen risks before they become costly
  • Implement prevention plans; prepare and practice contingency plans
  • Chair regular risk review meetings
Risk management maturity level: How well developed the organization’s risk management processes are relative to benchmarked competition.

Maturity by risk type (Schlegel & Trent):

  • Supply risk: Most mature — many tools (SRM, spend management, supplier assessment)
  • Demand risk: Moderately mature — CPFR and S&OP include risk; forecasting models don’t
  • Process risk: Middle-low — tools exist but most don’t incorporate risk
  • Environmental risk: Low — newest area; regulations change faster than processes can adapt
Risk Management Attitude
Risk appetite: Amount and type of risk that an organization is willing to pursue or retain.
Risk tolerance: An organization’s or stakeholder’s readiness to accept a threat or potential negative outcome in order to achieve its objectives.
Risk threshold: A cutoff point below which a risk will be accepted and above which some type of proactive response is required.
  • Risk-seeking: Takes measured risks if return justifies it (e.g., innovative IT products)
  • Risk-averse: Minimizes uncertainty (e.g., pharmaceutical safety)
  • Risk tolerance level influences which response is chosen for each risk
Risk Management Plan
  • Delineates how risks will be identified, grouped, and assessed
  • Specifies standardized tools for consistent probability/impact definitions
  • Ensures responses are cost-effective (benefit > cost)
  • Contains budget + reserve fund for unknown risks
  • Specifies who is responsible, accountable, and authorized to release reserve funds
  • Identifies risk thresholds, tolerances, and required response types by risk level
Known risks can be planned for; unknown risks are addressed via a reserve fund.
Risk Management Frameworks

COSO ERM (8 Components)

  • Internal environment — risk philosophy, appetite, ethics
  • Objective setting — aligned with mission and risk appetite
  • Event identification — threats and opportunities identified
  • Risk assessment — likelihood and impact determine response
  • Risk response — selected per risk appetite
  • Control activities — policies and procedures reinforce responses
  • Information and communication — timely sharing down, up, and across
  • Monitoring — management and audit continuously improve ERM

GRC (Governance, Risk, Compliance)

  • Governance: Board-level oversight of ethics, controls, and decision-making hierarchy
  • Risk management: Methodology to identify, analyze, and respond appropriately (commercial, IT, legal risks)
  • Compliance: Conformance to laws, regulations, contracts, and internal policies
GRC benefit: Organizations can take more calculated risks because they know their appetite and can quantify costs/benefits.
ISO Risk Standards
ISO 31000: A standard developed by ISO that outlines principles and guidelines to manage risk in any endeavor. Includes framework and processes for systematic risk management development. Not a certification standard.

ISO 31000 Principles (paraphrased):

  • Adds and protects value; integral to operations and decision-making
  • Addresses uncertainty in an orderly, structured, comprehensive, and timely manner
  • Uses best available information; customized to the organization
  • Inclusive, auditable, transparent; cyclical framework for continual improvement
ISO 73 (Guide 73:2009): An international standard providing definitions of generic terms related to risk management. Aims to encourage mutual and consistent understanding of risk concepts.
  • ISO 31010: Supporting standard for ISO 31000; helps organizations select systematic risk assessment techniques (controls assessment, consequence analysis, likelihood analysis, PERT, etc.)

Benefits of ISO 31000 family: Increases risk awareness, improves organizational resilience, reduces probability of identified risks, supports SWOT, aids compliance, enhances governance, reduces uncertainty.

Question 1
What is 'supply chain risk management' (SCRM) and why has its importance grown?
A.A financial insurance program for supply chain losses
B.The systematic process of identifying, assessing, and mitigating risks that could disrupt supply chain operations — including disruptions, demand volatility, supplier failures, geopolitical events, and natural disasters. Its importance has grown due to globalization, just-in-time practices, and increasing supply chain complexity
C.A government regulatory compliance program
D.A quality management system for supply chains
SCRM has become critical as supply chains grew longer and leaner: globalization created geographic concentration risks; JIT eliminated buffer inventory that absorbed disruptions; single-sourcing created single points of failure — events like COVID-19, the Suez Canal blockage, and the Japan earthquake revealed costly vulnerabilities.
Question 2
What is the 'risk management process' and what are its key steps?
A.The process of purchasing risk insurance
B.A cyclical process: (1) Risk identification — finding potential disruptions; (2) Risk assessment — evaluating probability and impact; (3) Risk response — developing mitigation strategies; (4) Risk monitoring — tracking risk indicators; (5) Risk review — updating the risk profile as conditions change
C.A process for managing employee safety risks only
D.A financial process for managing market price risk
The risk management process is continuous, not a one-time exercise: risks evolve (new suppliers, new geographies, new regulations), mitigation effectiveness must be monitored, and emerging risks require ongoing identification — making the process cyclical rather than linear.
Question 3
What is a 'risk register' and what information does it contain?
A.A register of insurance policies held by the company
B.A structured document or database that catalogs identified supply chain risks — including risk description, source/trigger, affected supply chain elements, probability (likelihood), impact (consequence), current controls, and risk owner — used to track and manage the complete risk portfolio
C.A list of suppliers who have previously caused disruptions
D.A legal register of supply chain contracts
The risk register centralizes risk management: by maintaining a comprehensive inventory of risks with their attributes, organizations can prioritize mitigation investment (high probability × high impact first), track mitigation actions, and demonstrate risk management governance to stakeholders.
Question 4
What is 'risk appetite' and 'risk tolerance' in supply chain risk management?
A.The amount of food safety risk acceptable in the supply chain
B.Risk appetite: the overall level of risk an organization is willing to accept in pursuit of its objectives. Risk tolerance: the acceptable variation around specific risk metrics. Together they define how much supply chain risk is acceptable before a response is required
C.A measurement of how quickly risk can be identified
D.The financial capacity to absorb supply chain losses
Risk appetite sets strategy: a company with a low risk appetite invests heavily in redundancy (dual sourcing, safety stock, alternate logistics routes) even at higher cost; a higher risk appetite accepts more vulnerability in exchange for lower cost. Risk tolerance sets operational thresholds (e.g., 'we will not accept a supplier with >40% single-site concentration').
Question 5
What are the main categories of supply chain risk?
A.Only financial and operational risks
B.Supply-side risks (supplier failure, quality, lead time), demand-side risks (forecast error, demand volatility), operational risks (facility disruption, IT failure), geopolitical risks (trade policy, sanctions, conflict), natural disaster risks, and financial risks (currency, commodity price, supplier financial health)
C.Only natural disasters and supplier failures
D.Only risks that are covered by insurance
A comprehensive risk taxonomy covers all risk sources: supply (upstream), demand (downstream), operational (internal), and external (geopolitical, environmental, regulatory) — ensuring the risk identification process doesn't miss major categories of potential disruption.
Question 6
What is 'business continuity planning' (BCP) in supply chain context?
A.Planning for new business opportunities in the supply chain
B.The process of developing documented plans and capabilities to ensure critical supply chain functions continue — or recover rapidly — when disruptions occur. BCP includes: identifying critical processes, establishing backup options, pre-positioning resources, and conducting regular recovery exercises
C.Annual financial budgeting for supply chain operations
D.A plan for growing the supply chain business
BCP for supply chains documents: which supply chain capabilities are most critical (ranked by impact of loss), what recovery options exist (alternate suppliers, alternate facilities, emergency inventory), what triggers activating the plan, who owns each recovery action, and how quickly each must be restored.
Question 7
What is 'supply chain resilience' and how does it differ from 'supply chain efficiency'?
A.A supply chain that is flexible in its pricing
B.The ability of a supply chain to anticipate, adapt to, and recover from disruptions — returning to normal operations (or better) after adverse events. Resilience often requires investment in redundancy that reduces efficiency, creating a deliberate tension between the two objectives
C.A supply chain with very fast delivery times
D.A supply chain that uses the least possible inventory
Efficiency (lean, JIT, single sourcing) reduces cost but increases fragility: every redundancy eliminated is a buffer removed. Resilience accepts some efficiency cost (dual sourcing, safety stock, geographic diversification) as insurance against disruption. COVID-19 revealed that many supply chains had become too efficient and not resilient enough.
Question 8
What is 'near-shoring' or 'reshoring' as a risk mitigation strategy?
A.Moving warehouses closer to customers
B.Moving manufacturing or sourcing from distant offshore locations to closer, domestic or near-home-country locations — accepting potentially higher unit costs to gain: shorter supply chains (faster response, lower lead time), reduced geopolitical risk, and less exposure to global logistics disruptions
C.A strategy for reducing transportation costs
D.A policy of using only domestic suppliers
Near-shoring/reshoring trades cost efficiency for resilience: shorter supply chains mean faster response to demand changes, less inventory in transit, and less exposure to global shipping disruptions (container shortages, port congestion) and geopolitical risk (tariffs, sanctions, trade disputes).
Question 9
What is 'scenario planning' in supply chain risk management?
A.Planning multiple supply chain operating scenarios for efficiency
B.Developing and analyzing specific plausible future disruption scenarios — 'what if our primary port closes?', 'what if our largest supplier fails?' — and pre-developing response plans for each. Allows rapid activation of pre-designed responses rather than improvising during a crisis
C.A financial planning method for multiple economic outcomes
D.A process for planning different product mix scenarios
Scenario planning converts reactive crisis management into proactive risk management: by pre-thinking 'what would we do if X happens?' and developing response protocols, organizations can activate pre-designed plans during actual disruptions — far faster and better than improvising under pressure.
Question 10
What is a 'supply chain risk management team' and what organizational structure supports it?
A.A team of insurance professionals managing supply chain risk
B.A cross-functional team — typically including procurement, logistics, operations, finance, and IT — responsible for identifying, assessing, and mitigating supply chain risks. Effective structure includes a risk owner for each major risk area and executive sponsorship
C.A separate department staffed entirely by risk professionals
D.A team of suppliers who manage their own risks
SCRM requires cross-functional governance: supply-side risks are owned by procurement; demand-side risks by sales/marketing; operational risks by operations; financial risks by finance — with a coordinating function (often supply chain or operations VP) ensuring consistent risk assessment methodology and enterprise-wide visibility.
Question 11
What is 'risk culture' and how does it affect supply chain risk management effectiveness?
A.The cultural practices of risk-taking companies
B.The degree to which risk awareness, risk identification, and risk escalation are embedded in daily supply chain decision-making — a strong risk culture means employees at all levels identify and report emerging risks proactively rather than waiting for leaders to ask
C.The attitude of senior leaders toward taking financial risks
D.The cultural differences between domestic and international supply chain partners
Risk culture determines whether risk management is proactive or reactive: in a strong risk culture, a procurement manager who sees a supplier showing financial stress immediately escalates rather than hoping it resolves; a logistics manager who sees port congestion building begins contingency planning before it becomes a crisis.
Question 12
What is 'supply chain mapping' and why is it foundational to risk management?
A.Creating a physical map showing the locations of supply chain facilities
B.The process of documenting the complete multi-tier supply network — identifying all Tier 1, Tier 2, and Tier 3 suppliers, their locations, what they supply, and how critical each is — revealing concentration risks, geographic risks, and single points of failure invisible when looking only at Tier 1 suppliers
C.A software tool for routing supply chain deliveries
D.Creating a diagram of the internal supply chain process flow
Supply chain mapping reveals hidden risks: a company may have 50 Tier 1 suppliers but discover that 80% of them depend on the same Tier 2 chemical supplier in a single-country location — a risk invisible without mapping. The COVID-19 pandemic exposed how poorly most companies understood their extended supply networks.
