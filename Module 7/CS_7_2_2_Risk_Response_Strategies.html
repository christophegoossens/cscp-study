4 Basic Risk Responses
Risk acceptance: A decision to take no action to deal with a risk, or an inability to formulate a plan to address a risk.
Risk avoidance: Changing a plan to eliminate a risk or to protect plan objectives from its impact.
Risk transfer: Moving the resource or financial effects of a risk to a third-party organization such as an insurance company or supplier.
Risk mitigation: Applying preventive measures to reduce the probability and/or impact of identified risks.
For opportunities (positive risks): Accept → exploit; Transfer → share; Mitigate → enhance.
Risk Response Planning
Risk response plan: A document defining known risks including description, cause, likelihood, costs, and proposed responses. Also identifies current status and the person accountable for each response.
Risk response planning: The process of developing a plan to avoid risks and to mitigate the effect of those that cannot be avoided.
  • Must be fully integrated into regular business processes to be effective
  • A living document — revisited weekly or monthly; actions tracked and closed out
  • Can be combined with the risk register into one large spreadsheet

Two Types of Risk Response Plans

  • Preventive action: Proactive response that occurs before a harmful event. Aims to reduce probability or impact.
  • Contingent/corrective action: Reactive response that occurs during or after a harmful event. Aims to minimize impact once the event has occurred.

Responses by Risk Category

Supply Risks

  • Supplier availability: Backup supplier contracts, capacity resilience audits, right-size supply base
  • Supplier quality: Penalty clauses, due diligence, corrective action plans
  • Supplier lead time: Safety stock, large orders for priority, diversify suppliers
  • Transportation: Penalty clauses, volume commitments, outsource to 3PLs
  • Customs/import delays: Translate paperwork, freight forwarders, government contacts

Demand Risks

  • Forecasting error: Statistical forecasting, aggregate forecasts, S&OP, demand-driven techniques
  • Interorganizational communications: Supply chain visibility, data sharing, shared systems
  • Unplanned promotions: Regular conference calls; key source of demand spikes / bullwhip effect
  • Quality/warranty/recall: Audit quality processes; liability insurance; limit warranty period

Process Risks

  • Capacity: Configurable equipment, backup supplier contracts
  • Inventory: Rationalize SKUs, product life cycle management, improve visibility
  • IT/telecom: Backup systems, team drills, practice response plans
  • IP: International contracts, split trade secrets among vendors, courts and penalties

Environmental Risks

  • Regulation changes: Change management projects, legal review, hire local experts
  • Conflict minerals: Due diligence, find alternate sources
  • Interest groups: Discover motivations, develop brand marketing plan, control media messaging
Hazard & Financial Risk Responses

Hazard Risks — Types of Insurance

Exhibit 7-19: Types of Insurance for Supply Chain
  • Two key approaches: contingency planning for business continuity + purchasing appropriate insurance
  • Business continuity insurance (not just asset insurance) is essential

Financial Risks

  • Insolvency monitoring: Track financial red flags (late payments, declining R&D, executive turnover, plant closures)
  • Market volatility: Build cash cushions, maintain unused credit facilities
  • Commodity price risk: Buy larger quantities when prices are favorable; purchase futures/options (hedging)
Hedging: A risk transfer tool for commodities, currency exchange, and financial instruments used to lock in a price now for future delivery, reducing uncertainty. Three types: forwards (private, custom contracts), futures (exchange-traded, standardized), and options (right but not obligation to buy/sell — pay a premium for flexibility).
Hedging reduces risk (uncertainty), not increases profits. The opposite — speculation — seeks profit from the same price uncertainty the hedger is trying to avoid.
Malfeasance & Litigation Risk Responses

Theft, Damage, Vandalism

  • Information gathering and sharing; GPS tracking; ISO/PAS 17712-approved seals on containers
  • Reroute shipments away from problem areas; select safer transport modes (rail > trucks for hijacking risk)
  • Reduce product complexity; redesign packaging; reduce perishable lead times

Fraud & Corruption (5 Best Practices)

  • Due diligence (legal, financial, government relations)
  • Contractual rights (compliance clauses, termination penalties, right to audit)
  • Monitor and audit (review high-risk payments, re-certify suppliers, conduct criminal checks)
  • Set and enforce policies (anticorruption education, internal/third-party reporting)
  • Governance (board-level committee reviews internal audits, legal, finance, compliance)

Counterfeiting & IP Infringement

  • Register trademarks and patents in all key markets quickly; especially in foreign countries
  • Create an enforcement team; pursue distributors and retailers, not just manufacturers
  • Track counterfeits in a database; encourage distributors to report counterfeits
  • Trade dress protection (U.S. Digital Millennium Copyright Act)
  • Consider leaving sensitive data/devices at home when traveling to high-risk countries

Reducing Lawsuit Risk

  • View actions from a jury’s perspective; adhere to truth and ethics
  • Invest in the details; document everything; enforce all policies
  • Stay professional and unemotional; don’t pretend problems don’t exist
Business Continuity
Business continuity management system (BCMS): Part of the overall management system that establishes, implements, operates, monitors, reviews, maintains, and improves an organization’s capability to deliver products/services at acceptable predefined levels following a disruptive incident.
Supply chain continuity: An organization’s strategic and tactical capability to plan for and respond to conditions, situations, and events as necessary in order to continue supply chain operations at an acceptable level of predefined output.
  • ISO 22301: International standard for business continuity management systems
  • Designate specific individuals for specific emergency roles
  • Develop step-by-step restoration priorities (communications first)
  • Designate a spokesperson for consistent press messaging

Business continuity planning tactics:

  • Get top-level governance support; run business impact analysis
  • Don’t rely only on inventory — it can never fully cover all problems and is itself vulnerable
  • Track shipments with RFID, GPS, IoT, and blockchain
  • Purchase business continuity insurance (not just asset loss insurance)
  • Test plans, train employees, and run drills
  • Develop sourcing alternatives for every key supplier
Coordinating Supply Chain Risk Management

Risk responses must be coordinated across the entire supply chain — not just internally. Best practices (from Supply Chain Council study):

Coordinated Risk Management

  • Coordinate risk management programs with partners (cooperation across departments and companies)
  • Develop sourcing risk mitigation strategies (multiple sources, strategic supplier agreements)
  • Create joint contingency plans; crisis communication planning

Supply Chain Designed to Manage Risk

  • Business rules: Set rules (customer/supplier/routing priorities) to minimize supply chain risk
  • Information: Manage information networks to minimize risk; share with partners
  • Network design: Node locations, transport routes, capacity size, number of suppliers — all designed to mitigate risk
Failure to implement preventive or contingency plans is itself a risk. Plans must be assigned to an individual responsible for turning them into projects.

What financial red flags indicate a supplier may be in financial distress?
ANSWER
Changing early payment incentives, paying its suppliers late, declining quality, unusual early shipments, longer lead times, falling R&D/IT investment, unusual executive turnover/stock sales, restating financial statements, laying off workers, closing plants.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is hedging considered risk transfer, not speculation?
ANSWER
Hedging aims to reduce uncertainty (price risk) by locking in a future price, not to profit from price movements. Speculation is the opposite — the speculator takes on the price risk the hedger is trying to avoid, hoping to profit from favorable price changes.

Question 1
What are the four main risk response strategies?
A.Accept, avoid, share, exploit
B.Avoid (eliminate the risk by changing plans/operations), Reduce/Mitigate (lower probability or impact through controls), Transfer (shift risk to another party through insurance or contracts), and Accept (acknowledge and monitor without active mitigation, appropriate for low-priority risks)
C.Prevent, detect, correct, recover
D.Ignore, monitor, act, review
The four response strategies match different risk levels: high-probability/high-impact → Avoid or Mitigate aggressively; medium risks → Mitigate to reduce to acceptable level; low-impact risks that can't be eliminated → Transfer (insurance) or Accept and monitor.
Question 2
What is 'dual sourcing' as a risk mitigation strategy and what are its tradeoffs?
A.Sourcing the same item from two departments within the company
B.Qualifying and using two suppliers for the same component — providing supply continuity (if one supplier fails, the other continues) and competitive tension (each supplier has incentive to maintain competitive pricing). Tradeoff: split volume reduces leverage with each supplier and increases management complexity
C.Sourcing from two countries simultaneously
D.A strategy of using two different logistics providers
Dual sourcing is a fundamental supply risk mitigation: typically split 70/30 or 80/20 — primary supplier gets most volume (maintaining their investment in the relationship); secondary supplier stays qualified and gets enough volume to remain capable. The secondary supplier can ramp up if the primary fails.
Question 3
What is 'safety stock' as a risk mitigation strategy for supply chain risk?
A.Inventory held to protect against warehouse safety incidents
B.Inventory held above cycle stock to buffer against uncertainty — supply uncertainty (variable lead times, supplier disruption risk) and demand uncertainty (forecast error, demand variability). Strategic safety stock for risk management holds additional inventory specifically for disruption scenarios
C.Inventory held in a locked facility for security
D.A minimum inventory level required by regulatory compliance
Safety stock as risk mitigation: beyond statistical safety stock (for forecast error), strategic inventory holds additional buffer for disruption scenarios — 'keep 60 days of this critical component because we have a sole-source supplier.' The cost (carrying cost) is the premium paid for supply continuity insurance.
Question 4
What is 'supply chain insurance' and what types of risk does it cover?
A.Insurance purchased by logistics providers for vehicles
B.Commercial insurance products that protect against financial losses from supply chain disruptions — types include: cargo insurance (goods in transit), business interruption insurance (lost revenue from supply disruption), product liability insurance (customer harm from product failure), and trade credit insurance (buyer default)
C.Government programs that insure supply chain investments
D.Insurance purchased by suppliers to protect their equipment
Supply chain insurance is risk transfer: rather than bearing the full financial cost of disruption internally, companies pay premiums to transfer the financial risk to insurers. Business interruption insurance (covering lost profit during a production shutdown) has become a critical coverage for supply chain-dependent manufacturers.
Question 5
What is 'supply chain diversification' as a risk mitigation strategy?
A.Diversifying the supply chain into new business areas
B.Deliberately spreading supply chain operations across multiple suppliers, geographies, logistics routes, and facilities — ensuring that no single disruption event can knock out a critical portion of supply chain capability. Includes: geographic diversification, supplier diversification, and modal diversification
C.A financial diversification strategy for supply chain investments
D.Diversifying the product range offered through the supply chain
Diversification reduces concentration risk: geographic diversification (suppliers in multiple countries) reduces geopolitical and disaster exposure; supplier diversification reduces single-supplier dependency; logistics diversification (multiple modes, routes, carriers) reduces transportation disruption exposure. Each adds resilience at a cost.
Question 6
What is 'risk transfer through contracts' and how is it implemented?
A.Transferring all supply chain activities to third parties
B.Shifting financial responsibility for specific risks to supply chain partners through contractual provisions — for example: supplier quality warranties (supplier pays for defect costs), delivery penalty clauses (supplier pays for late delivery costs), force majeure clauses (defining when neither party is responsible), and indemnification provisions
C.A legal process for transferring supply chain assets
D.Transferring supply chain management responsibility to a 3PL
Contract risk transfer provisions: liquidated damages (pre-agreed penalty for late delivery), product liability indemnification (supplier covers claims arising from their component), quality warranty (supplier replaces/refunds defective goods), and supply commitment (supplier commits to holding capacity) — each shifts a defined risk to the party best positioned to manage it.
Question 7
What is a 'business continuity plan' (BCP) and how is it tested?
A.A plan for growing the supply chain business
B.A documented plan specifying how critical supply chain operations will continue or recover after a disruption — identifying alternative suppliers, logistics routes, facilities, and resources, with assigned roles, communication plans, and recovery time objectives. Tested through: tabletop exercises, simulation drills, and actual alternate supplier/facility qualification
C.A financial plan for supply chain budget continuity
D.A regulatory compliance document for continuous operations
BCP testing validates that plans actually work: tabletop exercises (walk through the scenario verbally — are roles clear? are contacts correct?) identify plan gaps without operational risk; supply continuity tests (actually order from the backup supplier) verify the alternate is truly ready to deliver when needed.
Question 8
What is 'supply chain agility' as a risk response capability?
A.The ability to move goods quickly through the supply chain
B.The ability to rapidly reconfigure supply chain operations in response to unexpected changes — switching suppliers, changing logistics routes, adjusting production mix, redirecting inventory — enabled by flexible supplier contracts, multi-skilled workforce, and modular processes
C.A measurement of supply chain speed
D.The ability to quickly respond to customer pricing requests
Agility reduces recovery time when disruptions occur: an agile supply chain can switch to an alternate supplier in days rather than months; a rigid supply chain is locked into its existing configuration. Agility requires advance investment: qualifying alternates, maintaining flexible contracts, and building cross-trained teams.
Question 9
What is 'inventory hedging' as a commodity risk mitigation strategy?
A.Using inventory levels to predict stock prices
B.Building strategic inventory positions in commodities (raw materials, energy, components) to protect against price spikes or supply shortages — typically used for materials with significant price volatility or supply risk. Involves buying ahead of expected price increases or supply tightness
C.Using financial derivatives to hedge inventory costs
D.Reducing inventory to eliminate commodity exposure
Inventory hedging uses physical inventory to reduce risk: buying copper when prices are low protects against price increases; building component inventory ahead of anticipated semiconductor shortages secures supply. The cost is carrying cost and obsolescence risk — the premium for supply security.
Question 10
What is 'risk mitigation roadmap' and how is it prioritized?
A.A map of physical routes used for supply chain deliveries
B.A structured plan for implementing risk mitigation actions over time — prioritized by risk priority number (probability × impact), sequenced by feasibility (quick wins first, complex initiatives over 12-18 months), with owners, resources, and success metrics assigned for each mitigation action
C.A regulatory submission documenting risk mitigation activities
D.A financial plan for risk insurance premiums
The risk mitigation roadmap converts risk assessment into action: prioritizing by P×I ensures highest-priority risks are addressed first; sequencing considers feasibility (dual-source a critical component takes 6 months; build safety stock takes weeks); assigning owners ensures accountability for each mitigation action.
Question 11
What is 'supply chain risk monitoring' and what does it involve?
A.A security system for monitoring supply chain facilities
B.The ongoing process of tracking risk indicators, monitoring supplier and logistics partner performance, scanning for new risks, and updating the risk register — using: KRI dashboards, supplier financial monitoring tools, geopolitical news feeds, and regular risk reviews
C.A process for monitoring customer demand risks
D.An IT security monitoring system for supply chain networks
Risk monitoring closes the loop: risks identified and mitigated must be tracked to verify mitigation effectiveness; new risks must be identified before they materialize into disruptions; risk levels change (a supplier's financial position improves or deteriorates), requiring regular risk register updates.
Question 12
What is 'post-disruption review' and why is it a critical risk management step?
A.A financial review of costs incurred during a disruption
B.A structured assessment conducted after a supply chain disruption to understand: what happened, what the root causes were, how effective the response was, and what changes to processes, plans, or risk mitigation are needed to prevent recurrence or improve recovery — converting disruption experience into organizational learning
C.A legal review of contracts following a disruption
D.A customer satisfaction survey after a service failure
Post-disruption reviews generate organizational learning: without them, the same vulnerabilities that caused one disruption remain in place to cause the next. Reviews that identify root causes (not just symptoms) and lead to process changes, updated BCPs, and improved risk registers convert painful experiences into valuable resilience improvements.
