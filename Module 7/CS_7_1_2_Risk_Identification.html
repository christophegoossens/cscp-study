Risk Identification Tools

Risk identification must be comprehensive but focused on critical risks. It is an iterative process — new rounds of identification are needed as circumstances change.

  • Documentation & assumptions reviews: Pre-configured checklists, dashboards, and industry-standard risk software
  • Brainstorming: Group meeting with diverse experts; facilitator collects ideas without judgment
  • Delphi technique: Anonymous questionnaires collected and compiled in multiple rounds until consensus is reached
  • Interviews: Formal or informal discussions with experts and stakeholders
  • Root cause analysis: Searches for underlying causes rather than symptoms
  • Risk checklists: Lists of risks from past analyses at the organization or industry peers
  • Risk diagramming: Flowcharts/relationship diagrams reveal process weaknesses
  • SWOT analysis: Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats — maps internal capabilities to external environment
Risk Identification & Documentation

Each risk should be documented with:

  • Cause(s): Root causes, if known
  • Event(s): Triggering events, if known
  • Impact: Threats or opportunities for each critical asset or process; includes criticality assessment
  • Effect: Areas impacted within the organization or up/down the supply chain
Combine all four into a definitive risk statement. Example: “Derailment of multiple cars due to sabotage or disrepair of train tracks resulting in destruction of products onboard, possible injuries, and delays.”

The scope and time frame of each risk must be well understood before selecting an appropriate response.

Risk Register
Risk register: A useful summary report on qualitative risk analysis, quantitative risk analysis, and risk response planning. Contains all identified risks and associated details such as status, owners, priorities, and responses.
  • Typically a spreadsheet: one row per risk, one column per attribute
  • Attributes can be added as needed over time
  • Updated regularly at risk review meetings

Supply Chain Risks: Tangible & Intangible

Supply chain risks include loss of tangible assets (property, inventory, money) and intangible assets (intellectual property, reputation).

Loss of Tangible Assets

  • Loss of goods: Theft, damage, vandalism during shipment; Incoterms 2020 (FOB) determine who bears risk of loss during transfer
  • Malfeasance — Fraud & corruption: Attempts to make unfair profit (e.g., avoiding inspection fees, falsifying quality tests)
  • Malfeasance — Bribery: Gift/money given to influence a decision. Three types: commercial bribery, bribery of public officials, bribery of foreign representatives. U.S. Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (1977) addresses the latter.
  • Malfeasance — Abduction: ~$5.1B in ransoms collected annually; primarily targets citizens of the country where it occurs
  • Lawsuits: Organizations must protect against potential lawsuit losses

Loss of Intangible Assets

  • Intellectual property (IP): Brands, engineering designs, software code, trade secrets
  • Working with global suppliers creates IP risk — if they already perform the manufacturing, they can set up as a competitor
  • Countries with developed IP enforcement: File patents quickly; report violations to DHS’s National IP Rights Coordination Center (IPR Center)
  • Countries with higher IP risk: China, India (AMR Research) — more permissive cultures; China has demanded technology transfers
  • Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT): allows international patent filing in multiple countries simultaneously
  • Get in-country legal representation; review patent, trademark, copyright, and product liability laws per country

TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Delphi technique for risk identification?
ANSWER
Anonymous questionnaires collected and compiled in multiple rounds until the group reaches consensus. Reduces groupthink and bias from dominant personalities.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is root cause analysis in risk identification?
ANSWER
Searching for the underlying causes of a risk rather than focusing on symptoms, allowing more targeted and effective risk responses.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 4 components of a risk description/statement?
ANSWER
1. Cause(s) — root causes, 2. Event(s) — triggering events, 3. Impact — threats or opportunities for each critical asset/process, 4. Effect — areas impacted within the org or up/down the supply chain.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a risk register (ASCM definition)?
ANSWER
A useful summary report on qualitative risk analysis, quantitative risk analysis, and risk response planning. Contains all identified risks and associated details such as status, owners, priorities, and responses.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the two broad categories of supply chain risk?
ANSWER
Tangible assets: property, inventory, money. Intangible assets: intellectual property, reputation. Both can be lost through various supply chain risk events.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the three types of criminal bribery?
ANSWER
1. Commercial bribery (covering up inferior product or obtaining business/info), 2. Bribery of public officials (any attempt to influence a public official for private gain), 3. Bribery of foreign representatives (addressed by the U.S. Foreign Corrupt Practices Act, 1977).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act?
ANSWER
A 1977 U.S. law implemented to discourage bribing of foreign officials in order to secure favorable business contracts. One of three types of criminal bribery addressed in risk management.
TERM / CONCEPT
What IP risks arise from working with global suppliers?
ANSWER
Suppliers with access to proprietary designs and manufacturing capability can set up as competitors. Countries like China may demand technology transfers. Cultures in some countries are more permissive of IP infringement.
TERM / CONCEPT
How can organizations protect IP internationally?
ANSWER
File patents quickly (Patent Cooperation Treaty for multi-country filing); get in-country legal representation; review patent/trademark/copyright/product liability laws per country; report U.S. violations to the DHS National IP Rights Coordination Center (IPR Center).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is SWOT analysis used for in risk identification?
ANSWER
Starts by examining the organization’s internal capabilities (strengths, weaknesses) and external environment (opportunities, threats) to systematically identify supply chain risks.
TERM / CONCEPT
What does Incoterms 2020 (FOB) determine in the context of tangible asset risk?
ANSWER
Who bears the risk of loss during the transfer of goods. In North America, FOB Origin means risk transfers to the buyer when goods are loaded; FOB Destination means risk stays with the seller until delivery.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is risk identification described as ‘iterative’?
ANSWER
Because new rounds of identification are needed as circumstances change — new risks emerge, existing ones evolve, and some become irrelevant. The risk register must be updated continuously.

Question 1
What are the main methods for identifying supply chain risks?
A.Only reviewing past supply chain disruptions
B.Methods include: structured brainstorming (cross-functional teams), historical data analysis (past disruptions, near-misses), supplier assessments and audits, supply chain mapping (finding concentration risks), environmental scanning (monitoring geopolitical, regulatory, market trends), and scenario analysis
C.Only supplier quality audits
D.Only monitoring news for disruption events
Risk identification requires multiple lenses: past disruptions reveal patterns; supply chain mapping reveals structural vulnerabilities; environmental scanning identifies emerging threats; cross-functional brainstorming surfaces risks that individual functions don't see — no single method captures all risk types.
Question 2
What is 'PESTLE analysis' and how is it applied to supply chain risk identification?
A.A supply chain performance measurement framework
B.A framework for scanning the external environment across six categories: Political (trade policy, sanctions), Economic (recession, exchange rates), Social (labor availability, consumer trends), Technological (disruption, cybersecurity), Legal (regulation, compliance), and Environmental (climate, natural disasters) — identifying external risks in each category
C.A supplier quality scoring tool
D.A framework for analyzing product demand patterns
PESTLE applied to supply chain: Political → tariff/sanction risk; Economic → supplier financial stress, currency risk; Social → labor unrest at supplier sites; Technological → obsolescence, cyber attack; Legal → new compliance requirements; Environmental → climate event exposure. Structured scanning prevents missing risk categories.
Question 3
What is 'failure mode and effects analysis' (FMEA) in supply chain risk?
A.A quality tool for analyzing product defects only
B.A systematic methodology for identifying potential failure modes in supply chain processes, their effects on operations, and their causes — each failure mode is scored for Severity × Occurrence × Detectability (Risk Priority Number) to prioritize risk mitigation investment
C.A financial analysis of supply chain failures
D.A method for analyzing the effects of supply chain disruptions on financial results
FMEA for supply chains applies a rigorous failure analysis: 'what could go wrong at each supply chain step?' (failure mode), 'what's the impact?' (severity), 'how often?' (occurrence), 'would we detect it before customer impact?' (detectability). RPN = S×O×D prioritizes which risks to address first.
Question 4
What is 'supply concentration risk' and what metrics reveal it?
A.The risk of storing inventory in too few locations
B.The risk of over-dependency on a single supplier, single region, or single transportation route — measured by: % of spend with single supplier, % of a commodity from single country, % of revenue through single customer, % of production from single site
C.The risk of having too many products in the supply chain
D.The risk of price concentration in commodity markets
Concentration risk metrics: >50% of a critical component from one supplier = dangerous; >70% of a commodity from one country = geographic concentration; >30% of revenue through one customer = demand concentration. These metrics reveal vulnerabilities before they become disruptions.
Question 5
What is 'demand risk' in supply chain management?
A.The risk that demand will increase faster than supply
B.Uncertainty about future customer demand — including demand volatility (high variability), demand shifts (product substitution, channel changes), and demand collapse (economic downturn, product obsolescence) — leading to over- or under-stocking and supply chain misalignment
C.The risk of not having enough demand for products
D.The risk that customers will return products
Demand risk is bidirectional: demand higher than forecast → stockouts and lost sales; demand lower than forecast → excess inventory and markdowns. Demand risk is amplified by long lead times (committed supply months before actual demand is known) and by the bullwhip effect (demand signal distortion).
Question 6
What is 'supplier financial risk' and how is it monitored?
A.The risk that suppliers will increase their prices
B.The risk that a supplier will become financially distressed or insolvent — monitored through: credit ratings, financial statement analysis (debt ratios, cash flow), payment behavior (are they paying their own suppliers?), and third-party financial risk tools (Dun & Bradstreet, CreditRiskMonitor)
C.The risk that suppliers will demand faster payment
D.The risk that a supplier's costs will increase
Supplier financial risk is a leading indicator: a supplier with deteriorating financials will cut quality investment, defer maintenance, and eventually be unable to fulfill orders. Monitoring financial health enables early warning and proactive action — qualifying a backup supplier before the primary fails.
Question 7
What is 'cybersecurity risk' in supply chain context?
A.The risk of computer viruses in supply chain software
B.The risk that supply chain IT systems (ERP, WMS, TMS, EDI connections), supplier systems, or connected IoT devices are compromised — causing data breaches, operational disruption, or attackers gaining access to multiple companies through one compromised supply chain partner
C.The risk of losing customer data in a warehouse
D.The risk of outdated computer systems in supply chain operations
Supply chain cyber risk is increasingly significant: SolarWinds (2020) demonstrated that compromising one supplier's software can give attackers access to thousands of client organizations. Third-party cyber risk management — assessing supplier cybersecurity practices — is now a core element of supply chain risk management.
Question 8
What is 'geopolitical risk' and how does it affect supply chain decisions?
A.The risk of physical conflicts at supply chain facilities
B.The risk that political decisions, trade policy changes, sanctions, or civil unrest in key supply regions will disrupt supply chain operations — affecting suppliers, logistics routes, currency values, import/export rules, and market access
C.The risk of geographic concentration in logistics networks
D.The risk of natural disasters in supplier locations
Geopolitical risks include: new tariffs (Section 301, Brexit duties), sanctions (restricting suppliers in sanctioned countries), export controls (technology transfer restrictions), and civil unrest (supplier facility shutdowns, port disruptions). Supply chain network design must consider geopolitical stability of key sourcing regions.
Question 9
What is 'quality risk' in supply chain management?
A.The risk of poor customer service quality
B.The risk that supplied goods or services will not meet specifications, causing production failures, product recalls, customer complaints, and brand damage — sources include: supplier capability gaps, process variability, raw material quality, and insufficient testing/inspection
C.The risk of incorrect quality reports being generated
D.The risk of quality management system certification being lost
Quality risk in supply chains: a single defective component can stop a production line or cause a product recall. Quality risk management includes: supplier qualification (verify capability before sourcing), incoming inspection (detect defects on arrival), statistical process control (detect process drift), and traceability (isolate defective lots quickly).
Question 10
What is 'environmental and climate risk' in supply chain management?
A.The risk of environmental regulation compliance failures only
B.The risk that climate-related events (hurricanes, floods, droughts, wildfires, extreme heat) will disrupt supply chain operations — affecting supplier production, crop yields, logistics routes, and warehouse operations — and that transition risks (carbon pricing, energy costs) will affect operating costs
C.The risk of environmental damage caused by supply chain operations
D.The risk of failing environmental audits
Climate risk is both physical (disruption events: flooding a supplier's facility, drought reducing agricultural yields) and transitional (regulatory: carbon taxes, energy requirements increasing supplier costs). Supply chain risk management must assess suppliers' and logistics providers' climate event exposure and adaptation capability.
Question 11
What is 'regulatory and compliance risk' in supply chain management?
A.The risk of failing to comply with internal company policies
B.The risk that changes in regulations — import/export regulations, product safety standards, environmental regulations, labor laws, data privacy laws — will require supply chain modifications, increase costs, or result in penalties for non-compliance
C.The risk of regulatory audits discovering quality failures
D.The risk of customs delays at international borders
Regulatory risk is dynamic: new regulations can require rapid supply chain changes (e.g., REACH chemical regulations, conflict minerals reporting, forced labor import bans). Companies must monitor regulatory developments in their operating jurisdictions and maintain supply chain compliance programs that adapt to regulatory change.
Question 12
What is 'capacity risk' and how does it manifest in supply chains?
A.The risk of running out of physical storage capacity
B.The risk that supply capacity (manufacturing, logistics, supplier capacity) will be insufficient to meet demand — occurring during demand surges (seasonal peaks, promotional events), following supply disruptions (lost capacity), or when industry-wide capacity is constrained (semiconductor shortages)
C.The risk of having excess capacity and excess costs
D.The risk that a supplier will reduce their production capacity
Capacity risk creates supply crunches: the 2021-2022 semiconductor shortage showed how industry-wide capacity constraints cascade through supply chains, forcing production cuts across automotive and electronics. Capacity risk management includes: advance capacity reservations, demand flexibility mechanisms, and multi-sourcing across suppliers with different capacity bases.
