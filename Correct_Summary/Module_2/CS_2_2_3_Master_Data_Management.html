Overview

Master data is the foundational, non-transactional data that all supply chain processes depend on — items, suppliers, customers, bills of materials, locations. Master Data Management (MDM) ensures this data is accurate, complete, consistent, and available across systems.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Master data: The core data entities used across all business processes — products/items, customers, suppliers, locations, Bills of Materials (BOMs), routings, and pricing. Master data is shared and reused across transactions and systems.
📖 Definition:
Master Data Management (MDM): The technology, processes, and governance required to create and maintain a single, authoritative, accurate source of master data across the organization and its systems.
📖 Definition:
Item master: The file containing descriptive data for each item (SKU) — item number, description, unit of measure, weight/dimensions, lead time, planning parameters, cost. The foundational record for all supply chain transactions.

Key Master Data Types

💡 Key Point:
The five primary master data domains in supply chain: 1) Product/item master, 2) Customer master, 3) Supplier/vendor master, 4) Location master (plants, DCs, stores), 5) Bill of Materials (BOM) and routings.
  • Item master: description, UOM, weight, dimensions, shelf life, lead time, safety stock, planning method
  • Customer master: shipping addresses, payment terms, order entry defaults, segmentation data
  • Supplier master: contact info, payment terms, lead times, certifications, approved materials
  • BOM: parent-child relationships, quantities, effective dates, alternate components
  • Location master: addresses, capacities, operational hours, transport connections

MDM Challenges and Best Practices

💡 Key Point:
The 'golden record' concept: MDM aims to create one authoritative master record for each entity — the 'single source of truth' — eliminating duplicate, conflicting records across systems.
  • Poor master data quality is a leading cause of supply chain planning failures — 'garbage in, garbage out'
  • Master data governance: policies and processes defining who can create, modify, and approve master data changes
  • Data stewardship: designated individuals responsible for maintaining the accuracy of specific master data domains
  • Data cleansing: the process of identifying and correcting errors, duplicates, and inconsistencies in existing data
  • Global Trade Item Numbers (GTIN/EAN/UPC): standard product identification codes enabling interoperability

DEFINITION
What is master data in supply chain?
ANSWER
The core, non-transactional data entities shared across all business processes — products/items, customers, suppliers, locations, BOMs, and routings. Master data is reused across many transactions and must be accurate and consistent.
DEFINITION
What is Master Data Management (MDM)?
ANSWER
The technology, processes, and governance required to create and maintain a single, authoritative, accurate source of master data across all organizational systems. Eliminates data inconsistencies that cause supply chain errors.
DEFINITION
What is an item master?
ANSWER
The file containing all descriptive and planning data for each item/SKU — including item number, description, unit of measure, weight, dimensions, lead time, safety stock, planning method, and cost.
CONCEPT
What are the five primary master data domains in supply chain?
ANSWER
1) Product/item master (SKU data), 2) Customer master (buyer info, addresses, terms), 3) Supplier/vendor master (supplier info, certifications), 4) Location master (facilities), 5) Bill of Materials (BOM) and routings.
CONCEPT
What is the 'golden record' concept in MDM?
ANSWER
The idea of creating one authoritative master record for each data entity — the 'single source of truth' — that all systems refer to. Eliminates the problem of duplicate, conflicting records causing different departments to work with different data.
KEY PRINCIPLE
Why is poor master data quality a supply chain problem?
ANSWER
Supply chain planning systems are entirely dependent on master data accuracy. Wrong lead times, incorrect BOMs, or missing item data produce bad plans — regardless of how sophisticated the planning system is. 'Garbage in, garbage out.'
DEFINITION
What is master data governance?
ANSWER
Policies and processes defining who can create, modify, and approve master data changes — including approval workflows, data standards, and audit trails. Prevents unauthorized changes that corrupt the shared data foundation.
DEFINITION
What is a data steward in MDM?
ANSWER
A designated individual responsible for maintaining the accuracy and completeness of a specific master data domain (e.g., the item master steward ensures all new SKUs are correctly set up with complete attributes).
DEFINITION
What is data cleansing?
ANSWER
The process of identifying and correcting errors, duplicates, and inconsistencies in existing master data records. Typically required before implementing a new ERP or planning system — critical to a successful implementation.
DEFINITION
What is a GTIN (Global Trade Item Number)?
ANSWER
A standardized product identification number (including EAN and UPC codes) used to uniquely identify products across trading partners and systems. Enables interoperability between retailers, distributors, and manufacturers.
DEFINITION
What is a Bill of Materials (BOM) and what master data does it contain?
ANSWER
A structured list of all components, subassemblies, and raw materials needed to produce one unit of a parent item — including parent-child relationships, quantities per assembly, effective dates, and alternate component options.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between master data and transactional data?
ANSWER
Master data is stable reference data (item descriptions, supplier addresses, BOMs) shared across processes. Transactional data is operational event data (a specific purchase order, an inventory movement, a customer shipment) that references master data.
CONCEPT
What planning parameters are stored in the item master?
ANSWER
Lead time, safety stock quantity, reorder point, lot size (EOQ or fixed order quantity), planning method (MRP vs. reorder point), demand pattern code, ABC classification, and planner assignment.
DEFINITION
What is the supplier/vendor master and what does it contain?
ANSWER
The master record for each supplier — including contact information, payment terms, lead times, approved materials/services, quality certifications, performance history, and banking details for payment processing.
CONCEPT
What are the consequences of inconsistent master data across systems?
ANSWER
Different systems produce conflicting outputs — e.g., ERP calculates a lead time of 5 days while WMS shows 10 days. Planners lose trust in system outputs, resort to manual overrides, and supply chain execution becomes error-prone and expensive to manage.
