2.3.2 · Financial and Operational Metrics

Overview

Supply chain performance is measured through both financial metrics (cost, capital efficiency) and operational metrics (service, speed, quality). The best supply chain organizations use balanced scorecards linking both dimensions.

Financial Metrics

📖 Definition:
Supply chain cost as a percentage of revenue: Total supply chain costs (procurement, production, logistics, inventory) divided by total revenue. A key benchmark metric across industries.
📖 Definition:
Inventory turnover: Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) ÷ Average Inventory Value. Measures how many times inventory is 'turned over' per year. Higher turns = more efficient inventory use. Formula: Inventory Turns = COGS / Avg Inventory.
📖 Definition:
Return on Assets (ROA): Net income ÷ Total Assets. For supply chain, optimizing working capital (inventory, receivables, payables) directly improves ROA by reducing the asset base.
📖 Definition:
Working capital: Current Assets − Current Liabilities. In supply chain, the key working capital components are Accounts Receivable (DSO), Inventory (DIO), and Accounts Payable (DPO). Reducing working capital frees cash.

Operational Metrics

📖 Definition:
Fill rate: The fraction of customer demand immediately filled from available inventory. Order fill rate = orders filled complete / total orders. Line fill rate = order lines filled / total lines.
📖 Definition:
On-Time Delivery (OTD): The percentage of customer orders delivered on or before the promised delivery date.
📖 Definition:
Order-to-cash cycle time: The elapsed time from customer order placement to receipt of payment. Combines order fulfillment cycle time and payment collection time.

Balanced Scorecard Approach

💡 Key Point:
Supply chain metrics should be balanced across four dimensions: 1) Customer (service level, OTD, Perfect Order Fulfillment), 2) Financial (cost, ROA, working capital), 3) Process (cycle time, forecast accuracy, capacity utilization), 4) Learning and Growth (supplier development, employee capability).
  • Leading vs. lagging indicators: forecast accuracy is a leading indicator of future service performance; stockouts are a lagging indicator of demand/supply misalignment
  • Capacity utilization = Actual Output / Theoretical Capacity — high utilization is efficient but reduces flexibility
  • Throughput = rate at which goods flow through the supply chain (units per time period)
  • Return processing rate: percentage of returns processed within target time window

DEFINITION
What is inventory turnover and how is it calculated?
ANSWER
Inventory Turns = Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) ÷ Average Inventory Value. Measures how many times inventory is cycled per year. Higher turns = more efficient inventory management and less capital tied up.
DEFINITION
What is fill rate?
ANSWER
The fraction of customer demand immediately filled from available inventory. Order fill rate = complete orders filled ÷ total orders. Line fill rate = lines filled ÷ total order lines. A key customer service metric.
DEFINITION
What is On-Time Delivery (OTD)?
ANSWER
The percentage of customer orders delivered on or before the promised delivery date. A primary customer satisfaction and supply chain reliability metric.
CONCEPT
What is Return on Assets (ROA) and how does supply chain affect it?
ANSWER
ROA = Net Income ÷ Total Assets. Supply chain optimization reduces assets (inventory, warehouses, equipment) while maintaining revenue — directly improving ROA without changing the income statement.
DEFINITION
What is working capital in supply chain context?
ANSWER
Current Assets − Current Liabilities. Key supply chain components: Accounts Receivable (reduce via shorter DSO), Inventory (reduce via better turns), Accounts Payable (increase via longer DPO). Reducing working capital frees cash.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between order fill rate and line fill rate?
ANSWER
Order fill rate: percentage of complete orders shipped without any shortages. Line fill rate: percentage of individual order lines (specific items) filled from stock. A single shortage on one line can cause an order to fail the order fill rate.
CONCEPT
What is a balanced scorecard approach to supply chain metrics?
ANSWER
Measuring supply chain performance across four balanced dimensions: 1) Customer (service, quality), 2) Financial (cost, ROA), 3) Process (efficiency, cycle time), 4) Learning & Growth (capability, innovation). Prevents optimizing one dimension at the expense of others.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between leading and lagging indicators?
ANSWER
Leading indicators predict future performance (forecast accuracy predicts service level). Lagging indicators measure past outcomes (stockout rate confirms past demand-supply mismatch). Good scorecard uses both.
DEFINITION
What is capacity utilization?
ANSWER
Actual Output ÷ Theoretical Maximum Capacity × 100%. High utilization (90%+) is cost-efficient but reduces flexibility to handle demand spikes. Running at ~80% provides a buffer for variability.
DEFINITION
What is throughput in supply chain?
ANSWER
The rate at which goods flow through the supply chain from input to output — measured in units per time period. Throughput is the primary output measure in Theory of Constraints (TOC) thinking.
DEFINITION
What is supply chain cost as a percentage of revenue and why is it used?
ANSWER
Total supply chain costs ÷ Total Revenue × 100%. Enables comparison across companies of different sizes. Benchmarking this metric against industry peers identifies cost reduction opportunities.
DEFINITION
What is the order-to-cash cycle time?
ANSWER
Elapsed time from customer order placement to receipt of payment — combines order fulfillment cycle time and accounts receivable collection time. Reducing it improves both customer experience and cash flow.
CONCEPT
What is OTIF (On Time In Full) and how does it relate to Perfect Order Fulfillment?
ANSWER
OTIF measures delivery performance: was the order on time AND complete? It is a component of Perfect Order Fulfillment (POF) — POF is more comprehensive, also requiring damage-free, correct documentation, and correct location.
FORMULA
What is Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) and how is it related to inventory turns?
ANSWER
DIO = 365 ÷ Inventory Turns = Average Inventory ÷ Daily COGS. Measures how many days of supply is held on average. Fewer days = faster inventory turnover. DIO is the inverse of inventory turns expressed in days.
CONCEPT
How does supply chain performance affect a company's stock price?
ANSWER
Better supply chain performance → lower costs (higher margins), lower inventory (better ROA/ROE), shorter cash-to-cash cycle (better cash flow), and higher customer satisfaction (revenue growth). All of these drive shareholder value.

Question 1
Cash-to-Cash Cycle Time equals:
A.Days Payable Outstanding only
B.Days Sales Outstanding plus Days Inventory Outstanding minus Days Payable Outstanding
C.Inventory days times turnover
D.Revenue divided by COGS
C2C = DSO + DIO - DPO. It measures days between paying for inputs and collecting from customers.
Question 2
Reducing inventory days from 45 to 30 changes C2C by:
A.Increase of 15 days
B.Decrease of 15 days
C.No change
D.Increase of 30 days
C2C = DSO + DIO - DPO. Reducing DIO by 15 days reduces C2C by 15 days — improving working capital efficiency.
Question 3
ROA improves in supply chain when:
A.Overhead increases
B.Asset utilization rises or total assets decrease
C.Inventory levels increase
D.Headcount grows
ROA = Net Income / Total Assets. Supply chain improvements that reduce inventory or eliminate excess capacity increase asset utilization and ROA.
Question 4
Gross margin measures:
A.Overhead efficiency
B.How efficiently a company converts revenue to profit after deducting cost of goods sold
C.EBITDA performance
D.Net profit after all expenses
Gross margin = (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue. Supply chain efficiency directly improves gross margin.
Question 5
A supply chain achieving high perfect order fulfillment AND low total cost demonstrates:
A.An impossible trade-off
B.Efficient frontier performance
C.Poor asset utilization
D.Suboptimal inventory management
The efficient frontier describes supply chains that achieve best-in-class service at lowest cost.
