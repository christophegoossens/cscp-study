Cost Accounting & Standard Costing
Cost Accounting: the branch of accounting concerned with recording and reporting business operating costs. Used to calculate costs for supply chain and production management.
Standard Costs: the target costs of an operation, process, or product, including direct material, direct labor, and overhead charges.
Standard Cost Accounting System: a cost accounting system that uses cost units determined before production for estimating the cost of an order or product. Variances between standard and actual are tracked and investigated.

Key terms:

  • COGS (Cost of Goods Sold): direct materials + direct labor + manufacturing overhead allocated to goods sold.
  • Current price: the price currently being paid, as opposed to the standard cost.
  • Usage variance: deviation of actual consumption of materials vs. standard.
  • Cost variance: difference between budgeted and actual cost for an activity = rate variance + volume/usage variance.
  • Cost driver: a measurable aspect of an operation used to allocate overhead costs (e.g., direct labor hours, machine hours).

Standard Costing — Rate and Volume Standards:

  • Every cost has two components: rate (cost per unit of resource) and volume (units of resource used).
  • Variance = rate variance + usage/volume variance.

Overhead Rate Example:

  • Operation: 30,000 units, expected to use 1,500 direct labor hours.
  • Plant total: 15,000 expected DLH; total overhead = $300,000.
  • Overhead rate = $300,000 ÷ 15,000 = $20/DLH.
  • Standard overhead for operation = 1,500 × $20 = $30,000.
  • Actual: 1,300 DLH × $21/hour = $27,300.
  • Variance = $30,000 − $27,300 = $2,700 favorable (large favorable usage variance partly offset by unfavorable rate variance).

Efficiency Formula:

  • Efficiency = Standard hours produced ÷ Actual hours worked × 100%
  • Example: 110 standard hours produced in 100 actual hours = 110% efficiency (favorable).
Accounting Standards: IFRS vs. US GAAP
IFRS (International Financial Reporting Standards): a common global language for business affairs so that company accounts are understandable and comparable across international boundaries. Used in 144 of 166 jurisdictions as of 2018.

IFRS vs. US GAAP:

  • IFRS is principles-based: simple requirements, more judgment allowed.
  • US GAAP is rules-based: detailed, specific rules for every situation.
  • Both require the same 4 financial statements (+ notes).
  • Key difference: IFRS bans LIFO inventory valuation; US GAAP allows LIFO.

IFRS 5 Elements:

  • Assets: resource controlled by the entity from which future economic benefits are expected.
  • Liabilities: present obligation arising from past events, requiring future outflow of resources.
  • Equity: residual interest in assets after deducting all liabilities (= Assets − Liabilities).
  • Income: increases in economic benefits resulting in increases in equity (other than owner contributions).
  • Expenses: decreases in economic benefits resulting in decreases in equity (other than distributions to owners).
Financial Statements
Balance Sheet: a financial statement showing assets, liabilities, and equity at a specific point in time. Equation: Assets = Liabilities + Owners’ Equity.

Inventory valuation methods (balance sheet):

  • FIFO (First In, First Out): oldest inventory cost used first.
  • LIFO (Last In, First Out): newest cost used first. Banned under IFRS.
  • Average cost: weighted average of all units.
  • Once chosen, method must be kept consistent for comparability.
  • Obsolescence: condition of being out of date — inventory must be written off and removed from financial statements.
Income Statement: a financial statement showing revenues minus expenses for a period. Also called Statement of Comprehensive Income (IFRS). Basic equation: Revenue − COGS = Gross Profit; Gross Profit − Expenses = Net Income (“bottom line”).

Key income statement concepts:

  • Profit margin: difference between sales price and cost; measures degree of financial success.
  • Gross margin: Revenue − COGS (does not include operating expenses).
  • Net profit: all expenses deducted, including operating expenses, interest, and taxes.
  • Matching principle: report related revenues and expenses in the same accounting period.
  • Operating expenses (sales bonuses, G&A) are NOT in COGS — they appear below gross profit.

Funds Flow Statement (Statement of Cash Flows): a financial statement showing the effect of changes in balance sheet accounts and income on cash and equivalents. Net income is the starting point — then adjustments are made for non-cash items (e.g., depreciation is added back).

Cash flow components:

  • Operating activities: cash from normal business operations.
  • Investing activities: cash for/from capital expenditures and investments.
  • Financing activities: cash from borrowing, repaying debt, equity issuances.
  • 3 factors determining cash flows: sales, after-tax operating profit margins, capital requirements.
  • Depreciation: predetermined increment of asset cost — added back to net income on the cash flow statement (non-cash charge).

SC Cost Metrics & Multi-Organization Metrics

SC cost areas to measure:

  • Total landed cost (total cost), cost per SC function (order processing, picking, loading), cost per unit, cost as % of net sales.
  • Inbound freight, warehouse order processing, inventory carrying cost, administrative costs, direct labor.
  • Outbound freight, damage cost, return cost, service failure cost, backorder cost.

Multi-organization metrics address suboptimization (one partner optimizes at the expense of the whole chain):

  • SC total cost: aggregated costs of all organizations in the chain (raw material through customer delivery).
  • Inventory days of supply: total inventory anywhere in the supply chain.
  • Dwell time: all inventory idle time in all parts of the chain — idle inventory is non-value-added cost.
  • Fill rate + order-to-receipt response time: overall delivery effectiveness.
  • On-shelf-in-stock %: measures replenishment at retail level (fill rate only measures warehouse/distributor level).

Supplier Financial Health: Ratio Analysis & Altman Z-Score

4 groups of financial ratios for assessing supplier health:

  • Liquidity ratios: how quickly assets can convert to cash. Example: Quick Ratio = (Current Assets − Inventories) ÷ Current Liabilities
  • Activity ratios (turnover): effective use of assets. Examples:
    • Inventory Turnover = Sales ÷ Average Inventory (e.g., $100 sales ÷ $50 inventory = 2.0 turns)
    • Inventory Days’ Outstanding = 365 ÷ Inventory Turnover (e.g., 365 ÷ 2 = 182.5 days)
  • Leverage ratios: how much debt finances the business. Example: Current Debt to Equity = Current Liabilities ÷ Equity. Interest coverage ratio should be high (earnings covering interest payments).
  • Profitability ratios: return on investment. Example: Net Profit Margin = Net Profit ÷ Net Sales (10% margin = $0.10 profit per $1 of sales).

Altman Z-Score — bankruptcy prediction model:

  • Combines 4–5 weighted financial ratios to predict bankruptcy risk.
  • Public company formula (5 ratios): Z = 1.2X₁ + 1.4X₂ + 3.3X₃ + 0.6X₄ + 1.0X₅
  • Private company formula (4 ratios): slightly different weighting; X₄ uses book value of equity instead of market value.
  • Scored red/yellow/green: suppliers below the red threshold may not be selected; above green = low risk.
  • SC managers use Z-score to identify suppliers at financial distress risk — key supply chain risk signal.
Strategic Profit Model (ROA Breakdown / DuPont)

Return on Assets (ROA):

  • ROA = Net Profit Margin × Asset Turnover
  • Net Profit Margin = Net Profit ÷ Net Sales
  • Asset Turnover = Net Sales ÷ Total Assets
  • ROA = Net Profit ÷ Total Assets

Return on Equity (ROE) — extending ROA:

  • ROE = ROA × Financial Leverage (Total Assets ÷ Equity)

How SC improvements increase ROA:

  • Lower cost → higher net profit margin → higher ROA.
  • Lower inventory → lower total assets → higher asset turnover → higher ROA.
  • When the Strategic Profit Model is in a spreadsheet, changes to inventory, carrying cost, or net sales automatically cascade through to show the ROA impact — powerful what-if tool.

What are the 5 IFRS elements?
ANSWER
Assets (resource providing future benefits). Liabilities (present obligation requiring future outflow). Equity (Assets − Liabilities). Income (increases in equity from non-owner sources). Expenses (decreases in equity from non-owner sources).

What is the balance sheet equation?
ANSWER
Assets = Liabilities + Owners’ Equity. It is always in balance because equity is the residual (difference between assets and liabilities). Snapshot at one point in time.

What are FIFO, LIFO, and average cost?
ANSWER
Inventory valuation methods. FIFO: oldest cost used first. LIFO: newest cost used first (banned under IFRS). Average cost: weighted average of all units. Must be applied consistently year-to-year.
TERM / CONCEPT
Income statement basic equation
ANSWER
Revenue − COGS = Gross Profit. Gross Profit − Operating Expenses = EBIT. EBIT − Interest − Taxes = Net Income (“bottom line”). Covers a period (month/quarter/year).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the matching principle?
ANSWER
Report related revenues and expenses in the same accounting period. Ensures that costs incurred to generate revenue are matched against that revenue for accurate profit measurement.
TERM / CONCEPT
What 3 sections does a Statement of Cash Flows have?
ANSWER
1. Operating activities (cash from normal operations). 2. Investing activities (capex, acquisitions). 3. Financing activities (borrowing, debt repayment, equity). Starts from net income, adds back non-cash items like depreciation.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is depreciation added back on the cash flow statement?
ANSWER
Depreciation is a non-cash charge — it reduces net income on the income statement but does not represent an actual cash outflow. Adding it back converts net income to operating cash flow.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 4 financial ratio groups for supplier health?
ANSWER
1. Liquidity (Quick Ratio = (Current Assets − Inventories) ÷ Current Liabilities). 2. Activity/Turnover (Inventory Turnover = Sales ÷ Avg Inventory; Days’ Outstanding = 365 ÷ Turnover). 3. Leverage (Debt to Equity). 4. Profitability (Net Profit Margin = Net Profit ÷ Net Sales).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Altman Z-Score?
ANSWER
A bankruptcy prediction model using 4–5 weighted financial ratios. Public formula: Z = 1.2X₁ + 1.4X₂ + 3.3X₃ + 0.6X₄ + 1.0X₅. Private formula uses book value of equity. Rated red/yellow/green. Low score = high bankruptcy risk = SC risk.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is ROA and its formula in the Strategic Profit Model?
ANSWER
ROA = Net Profit Margin × Asset Turnover. Net Profit Margin = Net Profit ÷ Net Sales. Asset Turnover = Net Sales ÷ Total Assets. Therefore ROA = Net Profit ÷ Total Assets.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does reducing inventory improve ROA?
ANSWER
Lower inventory → lower Total Assets → higher Asset Turnover → higher ROA (same net profit, fewer assets). The Strategic Profit Model shows this cascade quantitatively.
TERM / CONCEPT
ROE formula in the Strategic Profit Model
ANSWER
ROE = ROA × Financial Leverage. Financial Leverage = Total Assets ÷ Equity. So ROE = (Net Profit ÷ Total Assets) × (Total Assets ÷ Equity) = Net Profit ÷ Equity.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is suboptimization in SC metrics?
ANSWER
When one partner optimizes its own metrics at the expense of overall chain performance. Example: pushing excess inventory to customers improves fill rate but increases total chain inventory cost. Addressed by end-to-end SC total cost metrics.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is dwell time in SC?
ANSWER
All inventory idle time in all parts of the supply chain. Idle inventory is non-value-added cost. Measuring dwell time across the chain helps identify waste and bottlenecks.

What is 'return on assets' (ROA) and why is it relevant to supply chain management?
A.Total revenue divided by number of employees
B.Net income divided by total assets — supply chain decisions (inventory levels, facility investments, equipment) directly affect the asset base and thus ROA
C.Gross profit divided by cost of goods sold
D.Total inventory value divided by annual sales
ROA measures how efficiently assets generate profit. SC managers control major assets (inventory, equipment, facilities) — reducing inventory or improving asset utilization directly improves ROA.
Question 2
What does 'days inventory outstanding' (DIO) measure?
A.The number of days a customer takes to pay an invoice
B.The average number of days inventory is held before being sold — lower DIO means faster inventory turns and less cash tied up
C.The number of days between placing an order and receiving it
D.The number of days a production order takes to complete
DIO = (Average Inventory / COGS) × 365. It measures how many days of inventory the company holds on average. Lower DIO = less working capital tied up in inventory = better cash efficiency.
Question 3
What is the 'cash-to-cash cycle time' formula?
A.Days in transit + Days in customs
B.Days Inventory Outstanding + Days Sales Outstanding - Days Payable Outstanding — the net number of days between paying suppliers and collecting from customers
C.Revenue / Total Supply Chain Cost
D.On-time delivery rate + Fill rate / 2
Cash-to-cash = DIO + DSO - DPO. A shorter (or negative) cash-to-cash cycle means the company collects from customers before it must pay suppliers — a favorable working capital position (e.g., Amazon, Walmart).
Question 4
What is 'gross margin return on inventory investment' (GMROII)?
A.Gross profit divided by cost of goods sold
B.Gross profit generated per dollar of average inventory investment — measuring how productively inventory is deployed
C.Net profit divided by total assets
D.Revenue minus all supply chain costs
GMROII = Gross Margin / Average Inventory Cost. It measures how much gross profit is generated for each dollar invested in inventory — a retailer's key metric for evaluating product line profitability and inventory efficiency.
Question 5
What is 'on-time delivery' (OTD) rate and what does it measure?
A.Percentage of items that arrive undamaged
B.The percentage of customer orders (or lines) delivered on or before the promised delivery date — a key customer-facing service metric
C.The percentage of production orders completed on time
D.The average number of days late for deliveries
OTD = orders delivered on/before committed date / total orders. It measures supply chain reliability from the customer's perspective — one of the most important metrics for customer satisfaction and retention.
Question 6
What is the 'perfect order rate' and what does it measure?
A.The percentage of orders shipped within 24 hours
B.The percentage of orders that are delivered on time, in full, undamaged, with correct documentation and invoicing — all conditions must be met simultaneously
C.The percentage of orders with no quality defects in the product
D.The percentage of orders delivered by the primary carrier
Perfect order rate = % of orders meeting ALL criteria simultaneously (on time, complete, undamaged, correctly invoiced). Even one failure counts as an imperfect order — it is the most demanding measure of order fulfillment performance.
Question 7
What is 'order fulfillment cycle time' (OFCT)?
A.The time required to receive goods from a supplier
B.The average time from when a customer places an order to when the order is delivered — measuring supply chain responsiveness
C.The time required to plan the production schedule
D.The time between placing and receiving a purchase order
OFCT measures end-to-end supply chain speed — from order receipt to delivery. Shorter cycle times improve customer satisfaction, reduce the need for safety stock, and provide competitive advantage.
Question 8
What does 'supply chain management cost' typically include?
A.Only transportation and warehousing costs
B.All costs incurred to plan, source, make, and deliver products — including planning costs, procurement costs, manufacturing/conversion costs, order management, and logistics costs
C.Only manufacturing labor costs
D.Only inventory carrying costs
Total SCM cost encompasses the full cost of operating the supply chain: planning (forecasting, S&OP), procurement (purchasing, supplier management), manufacturing/conversion, inventory carrying, and logistics (transportation, warehousing).
Question 9
Why is 'inventory carrying cost' an important financial metric?
A.It measures how quickly inventory is received from suppliers
B.It represents the total cost of holding inventory (capital cost, storage, insurance, obsolescence, damage) — typically 15–30% of inventory value per year, making inventory reduction a high-value opportunity
C.It measures how many times inventory is counted per year
D.It is only relevant for perishable products
Inventory carrying cost (typically 15–30% of average inventory value per year) includes cost of capital, warehousing, insurance, taxes, handling, obsolescence, and shrinkage. Reducing inventory by $1M can save $150K–$300K/year.
Question 10
What does 'return on investment' (ROI) for a supply chain project measure?
A.The percentage of orders returned by customers
B.(Net benefit / Cost of investment) × 100% — the financial return generated by the supply chain investment relative to its cost
C.The ratio of supplier invoices to purchase orders
D.The percentage of production capacity used
ROI measures whether a supply chain investment (new system, process improvement, network redesign) generates sufficient financial return. Positive ROI means the investment creates value; negative ROI means it destroys value.
Question 11
How is 'customer profitability analysis' useful in supply chain management?
A.It measures customer satisfaction scores
B.It calculates the true profit generated by each customer after deducting all supply chain costs to serve them — revealing that some high-volume customers may actually be unprofitable due to high service costs
C.It determines the credit limit for each customer
D.It measures customer order frequency
Customer profitability analysis (activity-based costing to the customer level) reveals the real cost to serve each customer. Some large customers require expensive services (small orders, special handling, returns) that make them unprofitable.
Question 12
What is 'Economic Value Added' (EVA) in supply chain context?
A.The total value of inventory in the supply chain
B.Net operating profit after tax minus the cost of all capital employed — measuring whether supply chain investments generate returns above the cost of capital
C.The difference between the selling price and manufacturing cost
D.Revenue generated from new supply chain capabilities
EVA = NOPAT - (WACC × Capital). Supply chain investments must generate returns above the cost of capital to create shareholder value. EVA is used to evaluate supply chain strategies and network investments.
