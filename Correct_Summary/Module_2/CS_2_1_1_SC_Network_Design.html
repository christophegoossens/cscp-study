Overview

Supply chain network design determines the physical configuration of the supply chain — where to locate facilities, how many echelons to use, and how to route product flows. It is a long-term strategic decision with major cost implications.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Supply chain network design: The strategic process of determining the number, location, and capacity of supply chain facilities (plants, warehouses, Distribution Centers/DCs) and the flow of goods between them to minimize total cost while meeting service requirements.
📖 Definition:
Distribution Center (DC): A warehouse or storage facility where the emphasis is on processing and outbound movement of goods rather than long-term storage. The DC receives goods, processes orders, and ships to customers or other DCs.
📖 Definition:
Hub-and-spoke network: A distribution configuration where products flow from multiple origins to a central hub (DC or cross-dock) and then redistribute to multiple destinations. Enables consolidation and reduces transportation cost.

Network Design Trade-offs

💡 Key Point:
The fundamental network design trade-off: fewer, larger facilities reduce fixed costs and enable specialization but increase transportation costs and lead times. More, smaller facilities reduce transportation costs and lead times but increase fixed costs.
  • Transportation cost vs. inventory cost: more DCs = lower transportation cost, higher inventory cost
  • Service level (lead time): more DCs = shorter lead times to customers
  • Fixed facility cost: fewer DCs = lower total fixed costs
  • Flexibility vs. efficiency: consolidated networks are more efficient; distributed networks are more flexible
  • Total landed cost = production cost + transportation + inventory holding + facility fixed costs

Network Design Factors

📖 Definition:
Total landed cost: The total cost of a product at the point of delivery, including production/purchase cost, transportation, customs duties, insurance, and holding costs. Used to compare sourcing and routing options.
  • Geographic factors: customer locations, supplier locations, port access, infrastructure quality
  • Demand density: high-density markets justify more DCs; sparse markets may be served from fewer central DCs
  • Product characteristics: perishability, value density, hazardous material requirements
  • Regulatory requirements: customs, tariffs, trade zones, local content rules
  • Risk considerations: geopolitical risk, natural disaster exposure, single-source dependency
💡 Key Point:
Network design optimization uses mathematical modeling (linear programming, simulation) to find the configuration that minimizes total supply chain cost while meeting service level constraints.

DEFINITION
What is supply chain network design?
ANSWER
The strategic process of determining the number, location, and capacity of supply chain facilities (plants, DCs, warehouses) and the flows between them to minimize total cost while meeting service level requirements.
DEFINITION
What is a Distribution Center (DC)?
ANSWER
A warehouse where the emphasis is on processing and outbound movement of goods rather than long-term storage. Receives goods from suppliers or plants, processes orders, and ships to customers or downstream DCs.
DEFINITION
What is a hub-and-spoke distribution network?
ANSWER
A network where products flow from multiple origins to a central hub (DC or cross-dock), then redistribute to multiple destinations. Enables freight consolidation, reducing per-unit transportation costs.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the fundamental trade-off in network design between number of DCs?
ANSWER
Fewer DCs: lower fixed costs and inventory costs, but higher transportation costs and longer lead times. More DCs: lower transportation costs and shorter lead times, but higher fixed costs and total inventory.
DEFINITION
What is total landed cost?
ANSWER
The total cost of a product at the point of delivery — including production/purchase cost, transportation, customs duties, insurance, and inventory holding costs. Used to compare sourcing options and network configurations.
CONCEPT
What factors affect supply chain network design decisions?
ANSWER
Customer and supplier locations, demand density, product characteristics (perishability, value density), regulatory requirements (customs, trade zones), infrastructure quality, and geopolitical/disaster risk exposure.
CONCEPT
What mathematical methods are used for network design optimization?
ANSWER
Linear programming (LP), mixed-integer programming (MIP), gravity models, simulation, and scenario analysis. These find the minimum-cost network configuration while satisfying service level constraints.
CONCEPT
What is the relationship between number of DCs and safety stock?
ANSWER
More DCs = more total safety stock needed (the 'square root rule'): safety stock at N locations ≈ √N × safety stock at 1 location. Centralizing inventory reduces total safety stock required.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is a cross-docking facility and how does it differ from a DC?
ANSWER
A cross-dock transfers goods directly from inbound trucks to outbound trucks with minimal storage — product moves through within hours, not days. Lower inventory than a traditional DC but requires synchronized arrival schedules.
CONCEPT
What does value density of a product tell you about network design?
ANSWER
High value density (high value per kg, e.g., electronics) justifies centralized inventory with premium shipping — holding costs outweigh transportation savings. Low value density (e.g., bottled water) needs local production/DC to keep transport costs manageable.
CONCEPT
What is a postponement strategy in network design?
ANSWER
Delaying product differentiation (customization, labeling, kitting) to a point as close to the customer as possible — often at the DC. Reduces inventory by holding undifferentiated product centrally and customizing per order.
CONCEPT
How do trade zones affect network design decisions?
ANSWER
Free Trade Zones (FTZs) and trade agreements can allow deferred duty payment, reduced tariffs, or duty-free movement — making some locations more cost-effective for DCs, manufacturing, or distribution.
FORMULA
What is the 'square root rule' for inventory centralization?
ANSWER
When consolidating N dispersed warehouses into 1, total safety stock decreases by a factor of 1/√N. Example: consolidating 4 warehouses into 1 reduces safety stock by 50%. Enables significant inventory savings through network consolidation.
CONCEPT
What is a greenfield network analysis?
ANSWER
A network design analysis that starts with no assumptions about existing facilities — optimizes from scratch to find the theoretically best network configuration. Used when a company is evaluating a major redesign.
CONCEPT
What is a nearshoring strategy and why is it used in network design?
ANSWER
Moving production or sourcing closer to the customer market (vs. offshore). Reduces lead time, transportation cost, inventory in transit, and geopolitical risk — at the potential expense of higher labor costs.

Question 2
Consolidating from 10 regional warehouses to 3 DCs primarily reduces:
A.Delivery lead times to all customers
B.Facility and inventory costs through consolidation
C.Per-unit transportation costs
D.Headcount
Consolidating warehouses reduces fixed facility costs and inventory (pooling safety stock), though transportation costs may increase for distant customers.
Question 3
The centre of gravity method in network design determines:
A.Optimal pricing strategy
B.The geographic location that minimizes weighted transportation costs
C.Economic order quantity
D.Supplier financial risk
The centre of gravity model finds the location that minimizes total weighted transportation cost based on customer and supplier coordinates and volumes.
Question 4
A make-to-order supply chain typically uses which network configuration?
A.Many DCs close to customers
B.Few centralized facilities with direct shipping
C.Cross-docking at every node
D.Consignment stock at all retailers
MTO requires fewer stocking points since products are built to specific orders — centralized production with direct shipment is common.
Question 5

Network design must balance multiple trade-offs: service level vs. cost, responsiveness vs. efficiency, centralization vs. decentralization.
