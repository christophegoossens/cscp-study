Technology Audits
  • IT audits test for system availability, security, confidentiality, and integrity; critical for U.S. Sarbanes-Oxley Act compliance
  • Audience: upper management (not IT or a specific department); may investigate multiple companies in the SC
  • Types: Pre- and post-implementation IT reviews, SDLC reviews, database reviews
  • Post-implementation review: Focuses on measurable, manageable items; assesses actual ROI; can also be run as a project post-mortem
  • Audit findings can reveal: False vendor promises, failure to deliver promised integration, underexploited features due to resistance or inadequate training
  • External auditor recommendations should include cost of continuing existing technology vs. replacement
Mitigating IT Project Risk

Proper analysis before project initiation, benefit-cost analysis, and risk analysis are essential. Key IT risk mitigation approaches:

  • Make incremental improvements: Large all-in-one projects are hard to manage and prone to failure
  • Clearly define business requirements: Define requirements before starting vendor evaluations (thousands of vendors)
  • Perform due diligence on proposals: Interview other customers of the provider; don’t rely only on marketing presentations
  • Control scope creep: Fully define activities in writing; require change orders for scope additions
  • Control excessive customization: Decide whether to customize software or change business processes to fit packaged software
Technology Requirements & Gap Analysis

Technology Requirements (Strategic → Tactical → Operational)

  • Strategic: Support organizational strategy and goals; highest-level requirements
  • Tactical: Degree of automation, user-friendliness, maintainability, throughput; must fit production process type (job shop, batch, lean)
  • Operational: Detailed, specific requirements developed from high-level requirements (e.g., specific visibility system specs)

Technology Gap Analysis

Gap analysis (ASCM): A tool designed to assess the differences between a service that is offered and customer expectations. More of a process than a single tool; uses process mapping, benchmarking, and data analysis.
  • Process mapping shows current state → to-be state; identifies non-value-added activities
  • Gaps revealed may include:
  • Legacy system architecture that must be replaced/upgraded before new technology can be supported
  • Processes that the technology wouldn’t support (must change business processes to adopt)
  • HR skills gaps (hiring, contracting, or training needed)
  • HR policy gaps restricting empowerment or job expansion
  • External partner gaps (unwillingness to change processes, share information, or share investment costs)
Technology Implementation

Cutover Strategies

  • Go-live date (big bang): All migration, switching, and testing done on a tight schedule; users start on the new system from day one. Lower cost but higher risk
  • Parallel running: Old and new systems maintained simultaneously during a cutover period. Lower risk but more expensive (maintaining two systems)
  • Rolling cutover: Implementation occurs in phases (e.g., one site at a time). Balances risk and cost

Project Management Fundamentals

  • Projects: temporary endeavors with unique deliverables, definite beginning and end
  • Require a formal charter signed by authorized executives (appoints PM, approves resources, defines scope/timeline/cost)
  • One or more executives serve as project sponsor (maintains enthusiasm, champions the project)
  • RACI matrix: Responsible, Accountable, Consult, Inform — clarifies stakeholder roles

Tuckman’s Team Development Stages

Tuckman’s Team Development Stages
  • Forming: Team members trying to understand roles
  • Storming: Conflict and competition; PM works through disagreements
  • Norming: Confidence grows; PM facilitates collaboration
  • Performing: Peak efficiency; PM supports long-term skill development
  • Adjourning: Lessons learned captured, successes celebrated, teams disbanded
Traditional vs. Agile Project Management

Traditional (Predictive) PM

  • Best for projects where requirements can be frozen early
  • Planning phase is most critical; even though execution has the largest costs, planning decisions drive those costs
  • Three project baselines: Scope baseline (scope statement + WBS + WBS dictionary), Schedule baseline (Gantt chart, dependencies, critical path), Budget baseline (bottom-up cost estimates)
Work breakdown structure (WBS) (ASCM): A hierarchical decomposition of project work into smaller, more manageable components. Displays and defines the product/service to be developed and the activities to be performed.

Agile PM (e.g., Scrum)

  • Best for software or new technologies requiring prototypes where requirements may vary
  • Scrum: Visual backlog board; fixed-time sprints (iterations); daily standups; retrospective at sprint end
  • Scrum master (not traditional PM): ensures scrum methodology is followed; removes obstacles
Supply chain digital transformation is a broad umbrella of SC improvements using change management. Change must be planned and executed with executive commitment; leaders must inspire enthusiasm and manage resistance using change management principles.

What is a gap analysis (ASCM)?
ANSWER
A tool designed to assess the differences between a service that is offered and customer expectations. More of a process than a single tool; uses process mapping, benchmarking, and data analysis to identify what needs to change.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 5 key IT project risk mitigation approaches?
ANSWER
1. Make incremental improvements, 2. Clearly define business requirements upfront, 3. Perform due diligence on proposals (interview customers of vendor), 4. Control scope creep (define activities in writing, require change orders), 5. Control excessive customization.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a post-implementation IT review?
ANSWER
A review focused on measurable, manageable items that assesses whether the company received the expected ROI from a technology investment. Can reveal false vendor promises, integration failures, and underexploited features.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 3 technology cutover strategies?
ANSWER
1. Go-live/big bang: All migration done on one date (lower cost, higher risk). 2. Parallel running: Old and new systems run simultaneously (lower risk, more expensive). 3. Rolling cutover: Phased implementation, e.g., one site at a time (balances risk and cost).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a project charter and what does it contain?
ANSWER
A formal document signed by authorized executives that appoints a project manager, formally approves the use of resources, and defines the project’s scope, objectives, timeline, and budget. Required for all projects.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the RACI matrix?
ANSWER
A tool that clarifies stakeholder roles on a project: Responsible (does the work), Accountable (owns the outcome), Consult (provides input), Inform (kept updated). Ensures all stakeholders have clear roles.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are Tuckman’s 5 team development stages?
ANSWER
1. Forming (understand roles), 2. Storming (conflict and competition), 3. Norming (confidence and collaboration), 4. Performing (peak efficiency), 5. Adjourning (capture lessons learned, disband).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a work breakdown structure (WBS) (ASCM)?
ANSWER
A hierarchical decomposition of project work into smaller, more manageable components. Displays and defines the product/service to be developed and the work required to deliver it. Primary planning tool for organizing project work.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 3 project baselines in traditional PM?
ANSWER
1. Scope baseline: Scope statement + WBS + WBS dictionary. 2. Schedule baseline: Activity definitions, sequencing, dependencies, Gantt chart, critical path. 3. Budget baseline: Bottom-up cost estimates from activity level up.
TERM / CONCEPT
When is traditional PM vs. agile PM most appropriate?
ANSWER
Traditional/predictive: When requirements can be frozen early (hardware, infrastructure, process improvement). Agile (Scrum): When requirements may vary and prototyping is needed (software, new technologies). Most critical phase in traditional PM is planning.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Scrum and how does it work?
ANSWER
An agile project management approach. Uses a visual backlog board; team plans fixed-time sprints (iterations); holds daily standups (what did I do, what will I do, any blockers); holds retrospective at sprint end. A scrum master (not traditional PM) ensures methodology is followed.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 3 technology requirement levels?
ANSWER
Strategic: Highest-level; supports organizational strategy and goals. Tactical: Automation degree, usability, throughput, fit with production type (lean, job shop, batch). Operational: Detailed specifications derived from tactical requirements.
TERM / CONCEPT
What types of gaps can a technology gap analysis reveal?
ANSWER
Legacy system architecture needing replacement; processes the technology wouldn’t support (requiring process redesign); HR skills gaps (training, hiring); HR policy gaps (empowerment restrictions); external partner gaps (unwillingness to change or share costs/information).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a supply chain digital transformation?
ANSWER
An umbrella term for a wide variety of supply chain improvements designed to upgrade supply chain maturity using technology and digital tools. Requires change management: planned execution, executive commitment, leadership that inspires enthusiasm, and management of resistance.

Payback period measures:
A.NPV over the investment life
B.Time required to recover the initial investment from net benefits
C.IRR of the project
D.Total cost of ownership
Payback period = initial investment / annual net benefit. It tells management how long before the technology investment breaks even.
Question 2
Change management in technology implementation is critical because:
A.New systems install themselves
B.People and process changes typically cause more implementation failures than technology failures
C.Technology is the only success factor
D.Change management only matters for large companies
Most technology implementation failures stem from inadequate change management — poor adoption, resistance, training gaps — not the technology itself.

A phased approach reduces risk by delivering incrementally, allowing issues to be resolved before full rollout.
