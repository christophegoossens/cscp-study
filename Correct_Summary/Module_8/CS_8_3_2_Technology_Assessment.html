Overview

Technology investment requires systematic evaluation, proper selection, and disciplined project and change management to achieve expected ROI and sustainable adoption.

  • Technology audit: assesses current IT capabilities, gaps, security, compliance; reported to upper management
  • IT audit tests: system availability, security, confidentiality, integrity; required for SOX compliance (US) / Bill 198 (Canada)
  • SOX requirement: persons who approve POs cannot also receive goods — enforced through IT controls
  • Post-implementation review: measures actual vs. projected ROI; identifies lessons learned for future projects
  • IT risk mitigation: make incremental improvements, define requirements clearly, perform due diligence on vendors
  • 80/20 rule for software selection: seek packages meeting 80% of needs out-of-the-box; customize only the rest
  • Scope creep: uncontrolled expansion of project scope; major cause of IT project failure
  • Technology selection criteria: functionality fit, integration capability, vendor stability, TCO, scalability
  • ERP: integrates planning, finance, HR, SC in one system; powerful but complex and expensive to implement
  • Project management: defines scope, schedule, budget, risk for IT initiatives; sets go/no-go milestones
  • Change management: ensures new systems become standard practice; addresses culture, training, resistance
  • Balanced scorecard for IT: measures financial success + customer satisfaction + quality — not stock price alone
  • Ongoing maintenance: patches, upgrades, user training, performance monitoring required post-go-live
  • Audit can reveal: false vendor promises, poor integration, underutilized features due to training gaps

The ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary defines a gap analysis as “a tool designed to assess the differences between a service that is offered and customer expectations.” When used for a technology assessment, a gap analysis starts by determining the current state of capabilities related to technology and then gathers and analyzes the requirements of internal and external customers to envision a future or ideal state for those capabilities. The difference between the current and future state is the gap. A gap analysis will help further refine technology requirements.

Implementing major business changes, including but not limited to new technologies, requires project management experience and the use of proven project management methodologies such as those defined by the Project Management Institute’s Project Management Body of Knowledge, or PMBOK. Project management is defined in the ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary, as “the use of skills and knowledge in coordinating the organizing, planning, scheduling, directing, controlling, monitoring, and evaluating of prescribed activities to ensure that the stated objectives of a project, manufactured good, or service, are achieved.”

Work breakdown structure (WBS)—The primary planning tool for organizing project work, defined in the ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary as “a hierarchical description of a project in which each lower level is more detailed.” The WBS is critical, since it provides the basis for planning project material, resources, cost, and timing and for identifying project risks. The WBS should include 100 percent of what will be done on the project. If it is not on the WBS, it is out of scope. A WBS lists deliverables or results that should be achieved, not activities to do, so it is a bit like a bill of materials. Question 1
Payback period measures:
A.NPV over the investment life
B.Time required to recover the initial investment from net benefits
C.IRR of the project
D.Total cost of ownership
Payback period = initial investment / annual net benefit. It tells management how long before the technology investment breaks even.
Question 2
Change management in technology implementation is critical because:
A.New systems install themselves
B.People and process changes typically cause more implementation failures than technology failures
C.Technology is the only success factor
D.Change management only matters for large companies
Most technology implementation failures stem from inadequate change management — poor adoption, resistance, training gaps — not the technology itself.
Question 3
A phased technology implementation:
A.Goes live with all functionality on one date
B.Rolls out capabilities in stages, reducing risk and allowing learning between phases
C.Requires more total cost than big bang always
D.Avoids all implementation risk
A phased approach reduces risk by delivering incrementally, allowing issues to be resolved before full rollout.

Blockchain's decentralized immutable ledger enables supply chain partners to share verified provenance records without trusting a central intermediary.
