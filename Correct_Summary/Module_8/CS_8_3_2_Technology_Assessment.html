Technology Audits
  • IT audits test for system availability, security, confidentiality, and integrity; critical for U.S. Sarbanes-Oxley Act compliance
  • Audience: upper management (not IT or a specific department); may investigate multiple companies in the SC
  • Types: Pre- and post-implementation IT reviews, SDLC reviews, database reviews
  • Post-implementation review: Focuses on measurable, manageable items; assesses actual ROI; can also be run as a project post-mortem
  • Audit findings can reveal: False vendor promises, failure to deliver promised integration, underexploited features due to resistance or inadequate training
  • External auditor recommendations should include cost of continuing existing technology vs. replacement
Mitigating IT Project Risk

Proper analysis before project initiation, benefit-cost analysis, and risk analysis are essential. Key IT risk mitigation approaches:

  • Make incremental improvements: Large all-in-one projects are hard to manage and prone to failure
  • Clearly define business requirements: Define requirements before starting vendor evaluations (thousands of vendors)
  • Perform due diligence on proposals: Interview other customers of the provider; don’t rely only on marketing presentations
  • Control scope creep: Fully define activities in writing; require change orders for scope additions
  • Control excessive customization: Decide whether to customize software or change business processes to fit packaged software
Technology Requirements & Gap Analysis

Technology Requirements (Strategic → Tactical → Operational)

  • Strategic: Support organizational strategy and goals; highest-level requirements
  • Tactical: Degree of automation, user-friendliness, maintainability, throughput; must fit production process type (job shop, batch, lean)
  • Operational: Detailed, specific requirements developed from high-level requirements (e.g., specific visibility system specs)

Technology Gap Analysis

Gap analysis (ASCM): A tool designed to assess the differences between a service that is offered and customer expectations. More of a process than a single tool; uses process mapping, benchmarking, and data analysis.
  • Process mapping shows current state → to-be state; identifies non-value-added activities
  • Gaps revealed may include:
  • Legacy system architecture that must be replaced/upgraded before new technology can be supported
  • Processes that the technology wouldn’t support (must change business processes to adopt)
  • HR skills gaps (hiring, contracting, or training needed)
  • HR policy gaps restricting empowerment or job expansion
  • External partner gaps (unwillingness to change processes, share information, or share investment costs)
Technology Implementation

Cutover Strategies

  • Go-live date (big bang): All migration, switching, and testing done on a tight schedule; users start on the new system from day one. Lower cost but higher risk
  • Parallel running: Old and new systems maintained simultaneously during a cutover period. Lower risk but more expensive (maintaining two systems)
  • Rolling cutover: Implementation occurs in phases (e.g., one site at a time). Balances risk and cost

Project Management Fundamentals

  • Projects: temporary endeavors with unique deliverables, definite beginning and end
  • Require a formal charter signed by authorized executives (appoints PM, approves resources, defines scope/timeline/cost)
  • One or more executives serve as project sponsor (maintains enthusiasm, champions the project)
  • RACI matrix: Responsible, Accountable, Consult, Inform — clarifies stakeholder roles

Tuckman’s Team Development Stages

Tuckman’s Team Development Stages
  • Forming: Team members trying to understand roles
  • Storming: Conflict and competition; PM works through disagreements
  • Norming: Confidence grows; PM facilitates collaboration
  • Performing: Peak efficiency; PM supports long-term skill development
  • Adjourning: Lessons learned captured, successes celebrated, teams disbanded
Traditional vs. Agile Project Management

Traditional (Predictive) PM

  • Best for projects where requirements can be frozen early
  • Planning phase is most critical; even though execution has the largest costs, planning decisions drive those costs
  • Three project baselines: Scope baseline (scope statement + WBS + WBS dictionary), Schedule baseline (Gantt chart, dependencies, critical path), Budget baseline (bottom-up cost estimates)
Work breakdown structure (WBS) (ASCM): A hierarchical decomposition of project work into smaller, more manageable components. Displays and defines the product/service to be developed and the activities to be performed.

Agile PM (e.g., Scrum)

  • Best for software or new technologies requiring prototypes where requirements may vary
  • Scrum: Visual backlog board; fixed-time sprints (iterations); daily standups; retrospective at sprint end
  • Scrum master (not traditional PM): ensures scrum methodology is followed; removes obstacles
Supply chain digital transformation is a broad umbrella of SC improvements using change management. Change must be planned and executed with executive commitment; leaders must inspire enthusiasm and manage resistance using change management principles.

What is a gap analysis (ASCM)?
ANSWER
A tool designed to assess the differences between a service that is offered and customer expectations. More of a process than a single tool; uses process mapping, benchmarking, and data analysis to identify what needs to change.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 5 key IT project risk mitigation approaches?
ANSWER
1. Make incremental improvements, 2. Clearly define business requirements upfront, 3. Perform due diligence on proposals (interview customers of vendor), 4. Control scope creep (define activities in writing, require change orders), 5. Control excessive customization.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a post-implementation IT review?
ANSWER
A review focused on measurable, manageable items that assesses whether the company received the expected ROI from a technology investment. Can reveal false vendor promises, integration failures, and underexploited features.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 3 technology cutover strategies?
ANSWER
1. Go-live/big bang: All migration done on one date (lower cost, higher risk). 2. Parallel running: Old and new systems run simultaneously (lower risk, more expensive). 3. Rolling cutover: Phased implementation, e.g., one site at a time (balances risk and cost).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a project charter and what does it contain?
ANSWER
A formal document signed by authorized executives that appoints a project manager, formally approves the use of resources, and defines the project’s scope, objectives, timeline, and budget. Required for all projects.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the RACI matrix?
ANSWER
A tool that clarifies stakeholder roles on a project: Responsible (does the work), Accountable (owns the outcome), Consult (provides input), Inform (kept updated). Ensures all stakeholders have clear roles.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are Tuckman’s 5 team development stages?
ANSWER
1. Forming (understand roles), 2. Storming (conflict and competition), 3. Norming (confidence and collaboration), 4. Performing (peak efficiency), 5. Adjourning (capture lessons learned, disband).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a work breakdown structure (WBS) (ASCM)?
ANSWER
A hierarchical decomposition of project work into smaller, more manageable components. Displays and defines the product/service to be developed and the work required to deliver it. Primary planning tool for organizing project work.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 3 project baselines in traditional PM?
ANSWER
1. Scope baseline: Scope statement + WBS + WBS dictionary. 2. Schedule baseline: Activity definitions, sequencing, dependencies, Gantt chart, critical path. 3. Budget baseline: Bottom-up cost estimates from activity level up.
TERM / CONCEPT
When is traditional PM vs. agile PM most appropriate?
ANSWER
Traditional/predictive: When requirements can be frozen early (hardware, infrastructure, process improvement). Agile (Scrum): When requirements may vary and prototyping is needed (software, new technologies). Most critical phase in traditional PM is planning.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Scrum and how does it work?
ANSWER
An agile project management approach. Uses a visual backlog board; team plans fixed-time sprints (iterations); holds daily standups (what did I do, what will I do, any blockers); holds retrospective at sprint end. A scrum master (not traditional PM) ensures methodology is followed.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 3 technology requirement levels?
ANSWER
Strategic: Highest-level; supports organizational strategy and goals. Tactical: Automation degree, usability, throughput, fit with production type (lean, job shop, batch). Operational: Detailed specifications derived from tactical requirements.
TERM / CONCEPT
What types of gaps can a technology gap analysis reveal?
ANSWER
Legacy system architecture needing replacement; processes the technology wouldn’t support (requiring process redesign); HR skills gaps (training, hiring); HR policy gaps (empowerment restrictions); external partner gaps (unwillingness to change or share costs/information).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a supply chain digital transformation?
ANSWER
An umbrella term for a wide variety of supply chain improvements designed to upgrade supply chain maturity using technology and digital tools. Requires change management: planned execution, executive commitment, leadership that inspires enthusiasm, and management of resistance.

Question 1
What is 'technology assessment' in supply chain management and what does it evaluate?
A.An IT audit of existing supply chain systems
B.The systematic evaluation of emerging or new supply chain technologies — assessing capability to deliver value, organizational readiness to adopt, total cost of implementation, risks of failure, and strategic alignment — before committing to implementation investment
C.A technical review of supplier technology capabilities
D.An annual review of technology spending
Technology assessment prevents costly mistakes: many supply chain technology implementations fail not because the technology doesn't work, but because the organization isn't ready, the use case doesn't match the technology's strengths, or the implementation underestimates complexity. Rigorous assessment reduces implementation risk.
Question 2
What is 'total cost of ownership' (TCO) for supply chain technology?
A.The cost of the technology hardware and software licenses
B.The complete cost of a technology solution over its expected life — including: software licenses/subscription fees, implementation costs (consulting, configuration, training), integration costs (connecting to existing systems), ongoing support/maintenance, change management, and the opportunity cost of staff time
C.The cost of maintaining the technology department
D.The cost to replace a technology system
Technology TCO reveals the true investment: a $500K software license may require $2M in implementation services, $300K/year in maintenance, and significant ongoing IT and user support cost — making the 5-year TCO $4-5M. Understanding full TCO prevents decisions based on license cost alone.
Question 3
What is 'change management' in supply chain technology implementation?
A.Managing changes to the supply chain system's software code
B.The structured process of managing the people side of technology change — communicating why change is needed, training users, redesigning processes to leverage new capabilities, managing resistance, and measuring adoption — recognizing that technology value is only realized when people actually use it effectively
C.A process for managing version changes in software
D.A technical process for migrating data between systems
Change management determines implementation success: research consistently shows that people and process factors, not technology, cause most implementation failures. User adoption, process redesign to leverage new capabilities, and organizational alignment around new ways of working determine whether a technology investment delivers its promised ROI.
Question 4
What is 'proof of concept' (PoC) and 'pilot' testing in supply chain technology adoption?
A.Testing supply chain processes in a laboratory
B.PoC: a small-scale test to validate that a technology works as claimed — typically lab-scale or with limited real data. Pilot: a controlled real-world implementation in a limited scope (one facility, one product category) — validating that the technology works in the actual business environment before broader rollout
C.A test of a supplier's proof of delivery documents
D.A financial pilot program for technology leasing
PoC and pilots reduce technology investment risk: a PoC proves technical feasibility; a pilot proves operational viability (works in real conditions with real users and real data). Both de-risk full investment by validating assumptions before committing full resources — especially critical for unproven technologies.
Question 5
What is 'make vs. buy' in supply chain technology decisions?
A.A decision about manufacturing products internally or outsourcing
B.The decision whether to build custom technology in-house vs. buying commercial software — build: offers exact fit to unique processes, competitive differentiation if unique; buy (SaaS): faster implementation, lower development cost, vendor maintains/updates, but requires adapting processes to software
C.A decision about purchasing technology equipment
D.A financial decision about technology capital vs. operating expense
Build vs. buy in technology: build custom when the capability provides competitive differentiation and no commercial solution exists (Amazon built its own WMS and logistics optimization). Buy commercial when standard software meets needs — buying provides proven functionality, ongoing development, and best practices built in, at lower total cost and risk than custom development.
Question 6
What is 'API integration' and why is it important for supply chain technology ecosystems?
A.A quality standard for supply chain software
B.Application Programming Interface — a standard way for software systems to communicate and share data. In supply chains: APIs enable different systems (ERP, WMS, TMS, supplier portals) to share data in real time without manual re-entry — creating integrated data flows across the supply chain technology landscape
C.A security protocol for supply chain data protection
D.A document format for supply chain data exchange
APIs are the connective tissue of modern supply chain technology stacks: instead of batch data transfers (export from ERP at midnight, import to WMS at 2am), APIs enable real-time data sharing — inventory status, order updates, shipment tracking — creating a live, connected supply chain technology ecosystem that enables real-time decision-making.
Question 7
What is 'data governance' in supply chain technology and why is it critical?
A.Government regulations about supply chain data
B.The framework of policies, standards, and accountability for managing supply chain data quality, security, access, and usage — ensuring data is accurate, consistent, complete, and accessible to those who need it, while protected from unauthorized access
C.A government oversight body for supply chain data
D.A process for archiving old supply chain records
Data governance is the foundation of supply chain analytics and AI: garbage in, garbage out. Poor data quality (duplicate records, inconsistent formats, inaccurate inventory records) undermines planning, analytics, and AI applications. Governance defines: who owns which data, what quality standards apply, how conflicts are resolved, and who can access what.
Question 8
What is 'implementation risk management' for supply chain technology projects?
A.Managing IT security risks during implementation
B.Identifying, assessing, and mitigating risks specific to technology implementation projects — including: scope creep (project expanding beyond original objectives), integration failure (systems don't connect properly), data migration errors (corrupted or lost data), user adoption failure (people don't use the system), and going live at a wrong time (peak season)
C.A process for managing vendor risks during technology purchase
D.Risk management by the IT department
Technology implementation risks are predictable and manageable: scope creep → clear scope definition and change control; integration failure → technical proof of concept early; data migration → data quality assessment and cleansing before go-live; adoption failure → change management program; wrong timing → avoid go-live during peak season or critical business events.
Question 9
What is 'interoperability' in supply chain technology and why does it matter?
A.The ability of a technology system to handle multiple languages
B.The ability of different technology systems to exchange, interpret, and use data from each other without manual intervention — critical as supply chains use multiple systems (ERP, WMS, TMS, procurement, demand planning) that must share data seamlessly
C.The ability of a system to be used by multiple users simultaneously
D.A feature that allows technology to work in multiple countries
Interoperability prevents data islands: a WMS that doesn't integrate with the ERP requires manual data re-entry; a transportation system that doesn't share data with the order management system misses real-time shipment information. Supply chain technology stacks require designed interoperability — through APIs, EDI, or integration platforms.
Question 10
What is 'scalability' as a technology selection criterion in supply chain?
A.The ability to use the technology across multiple scales of measurement
B.The ability of a technology system to handle increasing volumes of data, transactions, or users without degrading performance — critical as supply chains grow, add SKUs, expand geographically, or experience seasonal peaks
C.The ability of technology to work at a global scale
D.A feature that allows supply chain software to scale pricing automatically
Scalability prevents technology investments from becoming bottlenecks: a WMS that works perfectly at 10,000 orders/day but degrades at 50,000 orders/day will limit growth. Cloud-based supply chain technology (elastic compute) has made scalability easier — systems can automatically scale up computing resources during peak periods.
Question 11
What is 'return on investment' (ROI) calculation for supply chain technology?
A.The percentage return on the technology vendor's investment
B.The financial benefit calculation comparing technology investment (TCO) to the value delivered (cost savings, revenue enablement, working capital improvement) — ROI = (Net Benefits / Investment Cost) × 100. Business cases for supply chain technology typically include: labor savings, inventory reduction, service improvement (revenue protection), and error reduction
C.The financial return earned by the technology company
D.An accounting metric for technology depreciation
Technology ROI calculation requires quantifying benefits: labor savings (fewer FTE needed for manual processes), inventory savings (better forecasting → less safety stock × carrying cost), service improvement (higher OTIF → fewer penalty deductions and fewer lost sales), error reduction (fewer invoice disputes → lower DSO). Benefits must exceed TCO for the investment to make financial sense.
Question 12
What is 'technology roadmap' in supply chain management and what does it align?
A.A physical map of technology locations in a supply chain network
B.A strategic plan defining the sequence, timing, and interdependencies of technology implementations over a 3-5 year horizon — aligned with business strategy, supply chain strategy, and organizational change capacity — ensuring technology investments are sequenced for maximum value delivery and manageable change
C.A vendor's plan for software product development
D.A project plan for a single technology implementation
A supply chain technology roadmap prevents random technology adoption: by sequencing investments (foundation first: data quality and ERP → then planning: APS and demand management → then advanced: AI, digital twin), each investment builds on the previous one. Alignment with business strategy ensures technology enables competitive priorities rather than technology for its own sake.
