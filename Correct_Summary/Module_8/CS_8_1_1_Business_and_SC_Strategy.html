Strategy Definitions
Key success factors: The product attributes, organizational strengths, and accomplishments with the greatest impact on future success in the marketplace.
Business strategy (ASCM): A plan for choosing how to compete. Business strategies can be classified into three general categories: (1) least cost, (2) differentiation, and (3) focus.
Business plan (ASCM): A statement of long-range strategy and revenue, cost, and profit objectives usually accompanied by budgets, a projected balance sheet, and a cash flow statement.
  • Organizational strategy (corporate) → Business strategy (how to compete) → Supply chain strategy (how the SC will support business goals)
  • Strategic planning: Produces the strategic plan (organizational strategy)
  • Business planning: Bridge from strategic to operational; produces the business plan
  • Deliberate strategy: Planned, proactive. May become emergent strategy when adapted to changing conditions
Alignment of Strategies

Organizational strategy → Business strategy → Supply chain strategy must all be aligned. If misaligned, the organization’s direction and fit will be askew.

Exhibit 8-1: Alignment of Strategies

Processes: Developing Supply Chain Strategy

  • Aligning with business strategy (review business plan, financial statements, competitive priorities)
  • Creating the supply chain strategy (define customer service objectives, select revenue model, map SC objectives to business objectives, align in-house vs. contracted operating models)
  • Comparing SC strategy to actual SC capacity, resilience, sustainability, and adaptability
  • Creating action plans to resolve gaps (usually involves supply chain (re)design)

Processes: Designing or Redesigning Supply Chain

  • Identify customer and business requirements
  • Identify current and future states (collect historical data, map process flows, model as-is state)
  • Perform gap analysis between current and future states
  • Develop action plan to close gaps (change management, project charters, approval and funding)
Generic Business Strategies
  • Low cost: No-frills basics with low prices; cost is the basis for competition
  • Best cost: Best value at relatively low price; both cost and quality are the bases
  • Broad differentiation: Product/service attributes appeal to many buyers; experience and/or quality are the bases
  • Focused differentiation: Unique strategies for niche markets; niche marketing and innovation are the bases
  • Focused low cost: Niche market buyer needs at low cost; responsiveness can be the basis
Target costing (ASCM): The process of designing a product to meet a specific cost objective. Involves setting the planned selling price and subtracting the desired profit to get the target cost.
Global strategy (ASCM): A strategy that focuses on improving worldwide performance through the sales and marketing of common goods and services with minimum product variation. Leverages economies of scale by selling globally with little variation.
Postponement (ASCM): A product design or supply chain strategy that deliberately delays final differentiation (assembly, production, packaging, or tagging) of a product as long as possible.
Organizational Strategy Types

Demand-Driven Enterprise

Bullwhip effect (ASCM): An extreme change in the supply position upstream in a supply chain generated by a small change in demand downstream in the supply chain. Inventory can escalate quickly upstream.
Exhibit 8-2: Bullwhip Effect
Demand-driven supply network (ASCM): A situation in which customer purchase initiates real-time information flow through the supply chain, causing replenishment of that product throughout the chain.
  • Solution to bullwhip: replace forecasts with actual demand information (pull signal)
  • Most organizations pursue a push-pull strategy; the key decision is where push transitions to pull
  • Key steps: provide access to real demand data, establish trust/collaboration, increase agility of trade partners

Product-Type-Driven Strategy (Fisher)

  • Functional products: Mature, low margin, predictable demand → emphasize efficiency (high utilization, low inventory, short lead time, cost-based supplier selection)
  • Innovative products: Short life cycle, unpredictable demand → emphasize responsiveness (excess capacity, safety stock, aggressive lead time reduction, agile suppliers, modular design + postponement)
  • Byrnes (MIT): staples, seasonal products, fashion products may each need their own supply chain
Business Plan & Three Vs

The business plan ties organizational and business strategies to operations. Key functions (finance, engineering, marketing, manufacturing, supply chain) contribute to and are aligned by it.

Exhibit 8-3: Impact of Business Plan on Key Functions

Porter’s Five Fundamental Elements: Customer service, Sales channels, Operations and logistics, Competition, and Cost structure. Used to assess an organization’s fitness for a competitive strategy.

Three Vs of Supply Chain Management

Exhibit 8-4: Three Vs of Supply Chain Management
Visibility (ASCM): The ability to view important information throughout a facility or supply chain no matter where in the facility or supply chain the information is located.
Velocity (ASCM): A term used to indicate the relative speed of all transactions, collectively, within a supply chain community. A maximum velocity is most desirable because it indicates a rapid response to customer needs.
  • Variability: The natural tendency for results to fluctuate above/below an average. SC management works to reduce variability in both supply and demand
  • Additional Vs: Vocalization (communication between partners), Variety (right product mix), Volume (expand/contract to meet demand changes)
Benefits of SC management: streamlined operations, improved risk management, increased sustainability. Better SC management provides increased visibility/velocity and reduced variability, leading to smoother day-to-day functioning.

What is postponement (ASCM)?
ANSWER
A product design or supply chain strategy that deliberately delays final differentiation (assembly, production, packaging, or tagging) of a product as long as possible. Reduces risk of obsolescence and enables mass customization.

ANSWER
An extreme change in the supply position upstream in a supply chain generated by a small change in demand downstream. Inventory can escalate quickly upstream due to serial independent forecasting at each stage.

ANSWER
Functional: Mature, low margin, predictable → efficiency-focused (high utilization, low inventory, cost-based suppliers). Innovative: Short life cycle, unpredictable demand → responsiveness-focused (excess capacity, safety stock, agile suppliers, postponement).
