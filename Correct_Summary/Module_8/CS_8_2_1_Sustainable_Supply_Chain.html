Overview

Sustainable supply chains apply the triple bottom line to balance economic, environmental, and social performance simultaneously rather than prioritizing short-term profit alone.

  • Triple Bottom Line (TBL): economic (profit), environmental (planet), social (people)
  • TBL coined by Bruntland Commission (1987), popularized by Elkington's Cannibals with Forks
  • Sustainability "sweet spot": business plan satisfying all three bottom lines simultaneously
  • Sustainability can be legally required or voluntary; either way, it drives competitive advantage
  • Economic performance: balance short-term targets vs. long-term sustainability
  • Common short-term traps: order pulling, misleading metrics, excess inventory, window dressing
  • Window dressing: legal manipulation of orders/inventory to hit targets; harms long-term sustainability
  • Environmental performance: reduce carbon footprint, manage waste, apply life cycle thinking
  • Scope 1 emissions: direct (own operations); Scope 2: purchased energy; Scope 3: full value chain
  • Life Cycle Assessment (LCA): measures environmental impact across a product's entire life
  • Reverse logistics: returns, recycling, refurbishing, remanufacturing, disposal
  • Closed-loop supply chain = forward supply chain + reverse logistics integrated
  • Social performance: worker safety, fair wages, human rights, community impact
  • Green procurement: selecting suppliers based on environmental and social criteria
According to the ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary, sustainability involves the “activities that provide present benefit without compromising the needs of future generations.”

Question 1
The Triple Bottom Line (TBL) framework evaluates performance across:
A.Revenue, cost, and profit
B.People, Planet, and Profit — social, environmental, and financial dimensions
C.Quality, cost, and delivery
D.Supplier, internal, and customer metrics
TBL expands performance measurement beyond financial profit to include social (People) and environmental (Planet) outcomes.
Question 2
Sustainable Supply Chain Management (SSCM) integrates:
A.Only carbon reduction
B.Environmental, social, and governance (ESG) criteria into supply chain decisions alongside economic criteria
C.Only financial performance
D.Only supplier audits
SSCM embeds ESG considerations — environmental impact, labour rights, ethical sourcing, governance — into supply chain strategy.
Question 3
The circular economy designs supply chain to:
A.Dispose of all waste efficiently
B.Continuously reuse materials and eliminate waste through reuse, repair, remanufacturing, and recycling
C.Minimize only carbon emissions
D.Only manage product returns
The circular economy keeps materials in use — reuse, repair, remanufacturing, recycling — rather than the traditional linear take-make-dispose model.
Question 4
Supply chain carbon footprint measures:
A.Physical size of warehouse facilities
B.Total GHG emissions in CO2 equivalent across all supply chain activities
C.Energy costs only
D.Number of truck miles driven
Supply chain carbon footprint measures total GHG emissions (Scope 1, 2, and 3) across sourcing, manufacturing, transportation, warehousing, and returns.
