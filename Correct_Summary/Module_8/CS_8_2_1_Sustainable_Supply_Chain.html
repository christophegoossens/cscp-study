Overview

Sustainable supply chain management integrates environmental, social, and economic considerations into supply chain design, planning, and execution — balancing the triple bottom line of profit, people, and planet.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Sustainable supply chain management (SSCM): The management of material, information, and capital flows, as well as cooperation among companies along the supply chain while taking goals from all three dimensions of sustainable development — economic, environmental, and social — into account.
📖 Definition:
Triple bottom line: A framework for measuring organizational performance across three pillars: Economic (Profit), Environmental (Planet), and Social (People). Sustainable organizations must succeed across all three.
📖 Definition:
Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR): A company's commitment to conducting business in ways that are ethical, respect human rights, protect the environment, and contribute positively to the communities in which it operates.

Environmental Sustainability

💡 Key Point:
Carbon reduction strategies: transportation mode optimization (ocean/rail vs. air), fleet electrification, route optimization, renewable energy in manufacturing and warehouses, sustainable packaging, and designing products for end-of-life recyclability.
  • Greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions: Scope 1 (direct), Scope 2 (purchased energy), Scope 3 (value chain). Scope 3 is typically 70-90% of total corporate footprint — supply chain is the primary lever
  • Carbon neutrality: offsetting all GHG emissions through carbon credits, renewable energy, or removal projects
  • Net zero: actually reducing GHG emissions to zero — not just offsetting them. More ambitious than carbon neutrality
  • Circular economy: keeping materials in use through reuse, repair, remanufacturing, and recycling

Social Sustainability

💡 Key Point:
Supplier social compliance: ensuring supply chain suppliers meet labor standards — no child labor, fair wages, safe working conditions, no forced labor. Required by legislation (e.g., UK Modern Slavery Act, US UFLPA) and customer expectations.
  • Modern slavery and forced labor: companies must audit supply chains for labor exploitation — significant regulatory and reputational risk
  • Fair trade: ensuring suppliers in developing countries receive fair prices and conditions
  • Community impact: supply chain investment in local communities, supplier diversity programs
  • Supply chain transparency: publicly reporting on supply chain practices and performance

DEFINITION
What is sustainable supply chain management (SSCM)?
ANSWER
Managing material, information, and capital flows along the supply chain while incorporating economic, environmental, and social sustainability goals — balancing profit, planet, and people in all supply chain decisions.
DEFINITION
What is the triple bottom line?
ANSWER
A framework measuring organizational performance across: Economic (Profit — financial sustainability), Environmental (Planet — ecological impact), and Social (People — labor, community, ethics). Sustainable organizations succeed across all three dimensions.
CONCEPT
What are Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions?
ANSWER
Scope 1: direct emissions from owned sources (own fleet, manufacturing). Scope 2: emissions from purchased electricity/heat. Scope 3: all other value chain emissions — supplier production, inbound/outbound logistics, customer product use. Scope 3 is typically 70-90% of total corporate footprint.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between carbon neutrality and net zero?
ANSWER
Carbon neutrality: offsetting all GHG emissions through carbon credits or renewable energy. Net zero: actually reducing emissions to zero without relying on offsets. Net zero is more ambitious and credible — preferred by serious sustainability leaders.
DEFINITION
What is Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)?
ANSWER
A company's commitment to conducting business ethically — respecting human rights, protecting the environment, and contributing positively to communities. Supply chain CSR includes supplier auditing, community investment, and ethical sourcing.
DEFINITION
What is the circular economy and how does it apply to supply chains?
ANSWER
An economic model where materials stay in use as long as possible through reuse, repair, remanufacturing, and recycling — eliminating the linear 'take-make-dispose' model. Supply chains must be redesigned to enable reverse flows and material recovery.
CONCEPT
What is modern slavery in supply chain context?
ANSWER
The use of forced, bonded, or trafficked labor in the supply chain — including in Tier 2 and Tier 3 suppliers in high-risk regions. Companies are required by law (UK Modern Slavery Act, US UFLPA) to audit supply chains and report on risk management.
DEFINITION
What is supplier social compliance?
ANSWER
Ensuring that supply chain suppliers meet defined labor standards — no child labor, fair wages, safe working conditions, no forced labor, freedom of association. Verified through audits, certifications (SA8000, SMETA), and corrective action programs.
DEFINITION
What is supply chain transparency?
ANSWER
Publicly disclosing supply chain practices, supplier lists, environmental performance, and social compliance — enabling stakeholders (customers, investors, regulators, NGOs) to assess supply chain sustainability performance. Increasingly required by regulation.
CONCEPT
What is the carbon footprint of transportation and how can it be reduced?
ANSWER
Transportation (especially air and trucking) is a major GHG source. Reduction strategies: shift from air to ocean/rail, optimize routes (fewer miles), consolidate shipments (fuller vehicles), use lower-carbon fuels, and transition to electric vehicles for last-mile.
DEFINITION
What is sustainable packaging?
ANSWER
Packaging designed to minimize environmental impact — reduced material use, recycled content, recyclable or compostable materials, right-sizing to reduce waste. Also includes reusable/returnable packaging systems.
DEFINITION
What is Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)?
ANSWER
Regulatory frameworks requiring manufacturers to fund the collection, recycling, and disposal of their products and packaging at end of life. Common in Europe for electronics (WEEE), batteries, and packaging. Incentivizes design for recyclability.
DEFINITION
What is fair trade in supply chain?
ANSWER
A trading partnership ensuring suppliers in developing countries receive fair prices, decent working conditions, and direct long-term trading relationships. Fair trade certification verifies compliance. Addresses power imbalances in global supply chains.
DEFINITION
What is supplier diversity in sustainable supply chain?
ANSWER
A procurement strategy that includes minority-owned, women-owned, veteran-owned, and small/disadvantaged business enterprises in the supply base. Promotes economic equity, fulfills regulatory requirements, and accesses diverse talent and innovation.
CONCEPT
Why is Scope 3 emissions reduction the most challenging but most impactful for supply chain sustainability?
ANSWER
Scope 3 includes supplier production emissions (upstream) and customer product use (downstream) — areas the company doesn't directly control. Reduction requires supplier engagement programs, product design changes, and collaborative efforts across the whole value chain.
