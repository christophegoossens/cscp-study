Overview

Sustainability standards and guidelines provide frameworks for measuring, improving, and reporting environmental, social, and governance (ESG) performance in global supply chains.

  • Guidelines = principle-based best practices, non-binding; Standards = expert-consensus, more authoritative
  • Certification requires third-party (accredited) audit; guidelines do not require certification
  • Second-party audit: customer audits supplier directly; first-party: internal self-audit
  • Accreditation: certifies a body's competence to perform certifications (e.g., Registrar Accreditation Board)
  • UN Global Compact (UNGC): 10 voluntary principles — human rights, labor, environment, anti-corruption
  • UNGC uses GRI Reporting Guidelines as recommended sustainability reporting framework
  • OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises: responsible business conduct across 12 areas for MNEs
  • GRI (Global Reporting Initiative): most widely used sustainability reporting framework globally
  • ISO 9001: quality management system certification — customer focus, process approach, continual improvement
  • ISO 14001: environmental management system (EMS) certification — identify, control, monitor environmental impacts
  • ISO 26000: social responsibility guidance — NOT certifiable; covers 7 core subjects
  • SA8000: social accountability standard — worker rights, child labor, forced labor, safe working conditions
  • ANSI Z.10: occupational health and safety management system standard

Sustainability reporting has emerged as an important tool within an overall supply chain management strategy. According to the ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary, the Global Reporting Initiative (GRI) is “a network-based organization that pioneered the world’s most widely used sustainability reporting framework.” The GRI is committed to the framework’s continuous improvement and application worldwide. In order to ensure the highest degree of technical quality, credibility, and relevance, the reporting framework was developed through a consensus-seeking process with participants drawn globally from business, civil society, labor, and professional institutions. The GRI also has strategic partnerships with the United Nations, the UNGC, ISO, and the Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development. In effect, the GRI is helping businesses develop sustainability and reverse logistics key performance indicators to assess and improve their environmental performance. This form of self-regulation may reduce the need for new laws and regulations.
