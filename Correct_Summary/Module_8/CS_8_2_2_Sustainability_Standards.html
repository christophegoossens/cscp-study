Overview

Sustainability standards and frameworks provide structured guidelines for measuring, reporting, and improving supply chain environmental and social performance. They enable comparability and stakeholder accountability.

Environmental Standards

📖 Definition:
ISO 14001: The international standard for Environmental Management Systems. Certification demonstrates an organization systematically identifies, monitors, and manages its environmental impacts through a continuous improvement cycle.
📖 Definition:
Science-Based Targets (SBTi): A framework for companies to set GHG reduction targets aligned with the Paris Agreement goal of limiting global warming to 1.5°C. Requires both Scope 1/2 and Scope 3 targets. Credible, third-party validated commitment.
📖 Definition:
ISO 50001: The international standard for Energy Management Systems. Helps organizations systematically improve energy performance, efficiency, and consumption.

Social/Labor Standards

📖 Definition:
SA8000: A global social accountability standard for decent work — covering child labor, forced labor, health and safety, freedom of association, discrimination, working hours, and remuneration.
📖 Definition:
SMETA (Sedex Members Ethical Trade Audit): A widely used social audit methodology for supply chain labor standards — used by many retailers to assess supplier social compliance.
📖 Definition:
UN Global Compact: A voluntary initiative for companies to commit to 10 principles in human rights, labor, environment, and anti-corruption — and to report annual progress.

Reporting Frameworks

📖 Definition:
GRI (Global Reporting Initiative): The most widely used framework for sustainability reporting — providing standardized disclosures on economic, environmental, and social topics.
📖 Definition:
TCFD (Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures): A framework for disclosing climate-related financial risks and opportunities — increasingly required by financial regulators globally.
💡 Key Point:
ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) reporting: a framework used by investors to evaluate company sustainability performance. E = environmental (climate, water, waste), S = social (labor practices, diversity, community), G = governance (board, transparency, ethics).
  • Conflict minerals: Dodd-Frank Section 1502 requires US public companies to report on 3TG minerals sourced from conflict zones
  • CSRD (Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive): EU regulation requiring detailed sustainability reporting from large companies from 2024
  • Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM): EU tariff on carbon-intensive imports from non-EU countries

DEFINITION
What is ISO 14001?
ANSWER
The international standard for Environmental Management Systems. Certification demonstrates an organization systematically manages its environmental impacts through policy, planning, implementation, monitoring, and continuous improvement.
DEFINITION
What are Science-Based Targets (SBTi)?
ANSWER
A framework for companies to set GHG reduction targets aligned with the Paris Agreement (1.5°C pathway). Requires targets for Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions. Independently validated — considered the gold standard for credible corporate climate commitments.
DEFINITION
What is SA8000?
ANSWER
A global social accountability standard for decent work in supply chains — covering child labor, forced labor, health and safety, freedom of association, non-discrimination, working hours, and fair remuneration. Used for supplier social compliance auditing.
DEFINITION
What is SMETA?
ANSWER
Sedex Members Ethical Trade Audit — a widely adopted social audit methodology assessing supplier labor standards, health and safety, environment, and business ethics. Used by major retailers to evaluate their supplier social compliance.
DEFINITION
What is GRI (Global Reporting Initiative)?
ANSWER
The most widely used framework for corporate sustainability reporting — providing standardized disclosures on economic, environmental, and social topics. Used by thousands of companies globally to report ESG performance in a comparable format.
DEFINITION
What is TCFD?
ANSWER
Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures — a framework for disclosing climate-related financial risks and opportunities (physical risks, transition risks). Increasingly mandated by financial regulators globally (EU, UK, Australia, US SEC).
CONCEPT
What is ESG and what does each letter represent?
ANSWER
Environmental (climate, water, waste, biodiversity), Social (labor practices, diversity, community, human rights), Governance (board composition, executive pay, ethics, transparency). Investors use ESG scores to assess sustainability risk and opportunity.
CONCEPT
What are conflict minerals and what law requires their reporting?
ANSWER
3TG minerals (tin, tungsten, tantalum, gold) that may be sourced from conflict zones (e.g., DRC). US Dodd-Frank Act Section 1502 requires public companies to report whether products contain 3TG and trace the supply chain of origin.
DEFINITION
What is the UN Global Compact?
ANSWER
A voluntary UN initiative where companies commit to 10 principles in human rights, labor standards, environmental responsibility, and anti-corruption — and publish annual progress reports. Over 15,000 companies participate globally.
DEFINITION
What is ISO 50001?
ANSWER
The international standard for Energy Management Systems — helping organizations systematically improve energy performance, efficiency, and reduce energy costs. Aligned with the structure of ISO 14001 for integrated management system adoption.
DEFINITION
What is the EU CSRD?
ANSWER
Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive — EU regulation (effective 2024) requiring detailed sustainability reporting from large EU companies and non-EU companies with significant EU operations. More comprehensive than voluntary frameworks.
DEFINITION
What is the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM)?
ANSWER
An EU tariff on carbon-intensive imports from non-EU countries that do not have equivalent carbon pricing. Prevents 'carbon leakage' where production shifts to countries with lax climate rules. Affects supply chain decisions on where to source from.
CONCEPT
What is the Paris Agreement and why does it matter for supply chains?
ANSWER
A 2015 international climate treaty committing signatory countries to limit global warming to well below 2°C (ideally 1.5°C). Companies setting Science-Based Targets align their supply chain emission reductions with the 1.5°C pathway.
DEFINITION
What is a supplier code of conduct?
ANSWER
A document specifying the ethical, environmental, and labor standards that a buying company requires all its suppliers to meet. Typically covers: labor practices, safety, environmental compliance, anti-corruption, and data privacy. Acceptance is a condition of doing business.
CONCEPT
What is third-party sustainability verification?
ANSWER
Independent auditing or certification of a company's sustainability claims by an accredited third party — e.g., ISO certification body, audit firm, or certification organization. Prevents 'greenwashing' and provides credibility to sustainability reporting.
