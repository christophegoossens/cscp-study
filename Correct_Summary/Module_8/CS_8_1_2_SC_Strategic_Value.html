Aligning Business and Supply Chain Strategies

Organizational market/environment analysis → Mission and goals → Organizational strategy → Business strategy → Functional strategies (including supply chain) → SC capabilities → continuous loop.

Exhibit 8-5: Aligning Organizational, Business, and Supply Chain Strategies

Supply chain strategies need the same elements as a business model: value proposition, core competencies, cost structure, and revenue model.

Value Chains and Value Stream Mapping
Value stream (ASCM): The processes of creating, producing, and delivering a good or service to the market. For a good, encompasses the raw material supplier, the manufacturer, and the distributor. For a service, encompasses all processes from concept to delivery.
Value stream mapping (ASCM): A lean production tool to visually understand the flow of materials from supplier to customer that includes the current process and flow as well as the value-added and non-value-added activities.

Types of Value & Stakeholders

Supply chains should create three types of value for both for-profit and non-profit organizations:

  • Financial value: Improved bottom line, stable cash flows, increased sales through customer-desired value
  • Customer value: Meeting customer needs for product/service quality, reliability, availability, and cost
  • Social value: Contributions to community, workers, environment, and broader society

Stakeholders and their value priorities:

  • Companies in SC: Profit margin, market share, revenues, expenses, image/reputation
  • End customers: Affordable and reliable goods/services
  • Investors/lenders: Capital growth, dividends, interest payments, return of capital
  • Communities/governments: Community well-being, environmental protection, jobs, tax base
Supply chain activities that benefit one stakeholder may harm another. Managing these tradeoffs is a core supply chain challenge.
Core Competencies & Excellence

A supply chain strategy builds off its value proposition and core competencies to add financial value. Core competencies are areas where the organization or its SC performs distinctively better than the competition.

Excellence in Customer Service

Customer service (ASCM): The ability of a company to address the needs, inquiries, and requests of customers. Includes availability, operational performance, and customer satisfaction.
  • Availability: Having the product where and when the customer wants it
  • Operational performance: Elapsed time from order to delivery
  • Customer satisfaction: Customer perceptions, expectations, and opinions based on their experience

Effectiveness vs. Efficiency

Efficiency (ASCM): A measurement (usually expressed as a percentage) of the actual output relative to the standard output expected. Inward-focused; measures how well something does what it is supposed to do.
  • Effectiveness: Doing the right things (achieving business objectives with the right resources)
  • Efficiency: Doing things right (maximizing output relative to inputs, increasing capacity)

Resolving Misalignments & Financial Modeling

Types of Misalignment

  • Dynamic alignment: Adapting the SC to expected future changes (pursue cost efficiency without sacrificing flexibility; watch consumer-end trends; design for modularity)
  • Complexity alignment: SC should be only as complex as needed (reduce SKUs, limit product lines to maximize profit, not just revenue)
  • Partner alignment: Use channel master’s influence to promote collaboration over control

Financial Modeling for Supply Chain Strategy

  • Determines financial feasibility and ROI of SC strategy or product/service strategy
  • Uses Net Present Value (NPV): Future cash inflows discounted to present values minus present values of all outflows
  • Uses Discounted payback period: How long to break even, discounting future cash flows to reflect the time value of money
  • Economic value added (EVA): Measures whether the organization is creating more value than the cost of all capital employed

Network Modeling and Operations Research

Operations research (OR) (ASCM): 1) The development and application of quantitative techniques to the solution of problems; 2) theory and methodology in mathematics, statistics, and computing applied to find optimal solutions. Used to find minimum cost or maximum profit/yield/performance across complex tradeoffs.
  • Network modeling (network design): Finds least-cost (efficient) or best-service (responsive) solution for the entire SC network
  • Models adjust for facility locations, supplier choices, inventory levels, transportation costs, and demand scenarios
  • A good model reveals risks and opportunities, enabling both resilience and cost efficiency
