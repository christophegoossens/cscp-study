4.3.1 · Inventory Types and Classification

Overview

Inventory is a buffer that decouples supply from demand. Understanding inventory types, classification methods, and cost components enables supply chain professionals to make better investment and replenishment decisions.

ASCM Definitions

📖 ASCM Definition — Inventory:

Those stocks or items used to support production (raw materials and work-in-process items), supporting activities (maintenance, repair, and operating supplies), and customer service (finished goods and spare parts).

📖 ASCM Definition — Cycle Stock (Lot-Size Inventory):

That portion of inventory that results from the replenishment of customer demand in planned lots. The average cycle stock = Q/2 where Q is the order quantity.

📖 ASCM Definition — Safety Stock (Buffer Stock):

A quantity of stock planned to be in inventory to protect against fluctuations in demand or supply. Safety stock is held above average demand and above average lead time to provide a cushion against variability.

📖 ASCM Definition — Anticipation Inventory:

Additional inventory above basic pipeline stock built up in advance of expected demand, such as a seasonal peak, promotion, plant shutdown, or price increase.

📖 ASCM Definition — Pipeline Stock (Transportation Inventory):

Inventory in transit — goods that have been shipped but not yet received. Also called in-transit inventory or work-in-process for goods moving between facilities.

Inventory Types by Purpose

  • Raw materials: Unprocessed inputs to manufacturing
  • Work-in-process (WIP): Items partially through the manufacturing process
  • Finished goods: Completed products awaiting sale/shipment
  • Maintenance, Repair, and Operating (MRO) supplies: Items used to maintain operations, not incorporated into the product
  • Spare parts: Components held to replace failed equipment parts

ABC Classification

💡 Key Point:

ABC analysis applies the Pareto principle (80/20 rule) to inventory: A items = ~20% of SKUs, ~80% of annual spend/volume — tightly controlled, frequent review. B items = ~30% of SKUs, ~15% of volume — moderate control. C items = ~50% of SKUs, ~5% of volume — minimal control, simpler replenishment rules.

Inventory Costs

  • Holding (carrying) cost: Cost of keeping inventory — includes capital (opportunity cost), storage, insurance, obsolescence, and handling. Typically 20–30% of item value per year.
  • Ordering (setup) cost: Cost to place an order or set up a production run — purchasing admin, transportation, receiving, inspection.
  • Shortage (stockout) cost: Lost sales, backorder costs, expediting, and customer goodwill loss when demand exceeds supply.

Key Inventory Performance Metrics

  • Inventory turns: Annual COGS / Average Inventory Value — higher is better (faster-moving inventory)
  • Days of Supply (DOS): Average Inventory / Average Daily Demand — how many days of demand are on hand
  • Fill rate: % of demand satisfied from stock on hand
  • Service level: % of replenishment cycles with no stockout

DEFINITION
What is the ASCM definition of inventory?
ANSWER
Stocks or items used to support production (raw materials and WIP), supporting activities (MRO supplies), and customer service (finished goods and spare parts).
DEFINITION
What is cycle stock (lot-size inventory)?
ANSWER
Inventory resulting from replenishing demand in planned lots. Average cycle stock = Q/2, where Q is the order quantity. Larger order quantities create more cycle stock.
DEFINITION
What is safety stock and why is it held?
ANSWER
Inventory above average demand held to protect against variability in demand and/or supply lead time. It provides a buffer so stockouts do not occur when demand exceeds forecast or supply is delayed.
DEFINITION
What is anticipation inventory?
ANSWER
Inventory built up in advance of an expected demand spike — such as a seasonal peak, promotional event, plant shutdown, or price increase. Also called seasonal inventory.
DEFINITION
What is pipeline (in-transit) inventory?
ANSWER
Inventory that has been shipped but not yet received. It is "in the pipeline" between supplier and customer. Also includes WIP moving between manufacturing stages.
CONCEPT
What are the five types of inventory by purpose?
ANSWER
1) Raw materials; 2) Work-in-process (WIP); 3) Finished goods; 4) MRO (maintenance, repair, operating) supplies; 5) Spare parts/service parts.
CONCEPT
What is ABC inventory classification?
ANSWER
A prioritization method applying the Pareto principle: A items = ~20% of SKUs, ~80% of value (tight control). B items = ~30% of SKUs, ~15% of value (moderate). C items = ~50% of SKUs, ~5% of value (minimal control).
KEY POINT
What are the three main categories of inventory cost?
ANSWER
1) Holding/carrying cost (capital, storage, insurance, obsolescence — ~20-30% of value/year); 2) Ordering/setup cost (admin, transport, receiving); 3) Shortage/stockout cost (lost sales, backorders, expediting).
DEFINITION
How is inventory turns calculated?
ANSWER
Inventory Turns = Annual Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) / Average Inventory Value. Higher turns indicate faster-moving inventory and better capital efficiency.
DEFINITION
What is Days of Supply (DOS)?
ANSWER
Days of Supply = Average Inventory / Average Daily Demand. Tells how many days of demand are currently covered by on-hand inventory.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between service level and fill rate?
ANSWER
Service level = % of replenishment cycles in which no stockout occurs (cycle-based). Fill rate = % of demand satisfied immediately from stock (demand-based). A high service level does not guarantee a high fill rate if stockouts are large when they occur.
CONCEPT
What is MRO inventory?
ANSWER
Maintenance, Repair, and Operating supplies — items consumed to keep operations running (lubricants, cleaning materials, office supplies) but not incorporated into finished products. Often managed separately from production inventory.
CONCEPT
Why is ABC classification important for inventory management?
ANSWER
It directs management attention and control effort to the items that matter most. A items get tight controls (frequent review, accurate forecasts, low safety stock). C items get simpler rules (two-bin, periodic review). Prevents over-managing low-value items.
CONCEPT
What factors determine the appropriate level of safety stock?
ANSWER
Demand variability, supply lead time variability, desired service level, and the cost of holding inventory vs. the cost of a stockout. Higher variability or higher desired service level requires more safety stock.
CONCEPT
What is the Pareto (80/20) principle in inventory?
ANSWER
Approximately 80% of inventory value (or activity) is generated by approximately 20% of the items. This drives ABC classification — focus control effort on the vital few A items rather than spreading effort uniformly.
CONCEPT
What is decoupling inventory?
ANSWER
Inventory held between operations or supply chain stages to decouple them from each other — allowing each stage to operate at its own pace without waiting for the upstream stage. Reduces dependencies but increases inventory investment.
CONCEPT
What is the trade-off in inventory management?
ANSWER
Higher inventory levels improve service (lower stockouts, faster response) but increase holding costs. Lower inventory reduces costs but increases stockout risk. Optimal inventory minimizes total relevant costs.
CONCEPT
What is a Stock Keeping Unit (SKU)?
ANSWER
A unique identifier for a specific product in a specific location. Used to track inventory at the individual item level. A single product in three locations = three SKUs.
CONCEPT
What is obsolescence in inventory management?
ANSWER
The risk that inventory will lose value or become unsellable due to product changes, technology advances, or expiry. Obsolescence is a holding cost component and is especially high for fashion, technology, and perishable items.
CONCEPT
What is the difference between raw materials, WIP, and finished goods?
ANSWER
Raw materials = inputs not yet entered production. WIP = partially processed items in the manufacturing process. Finished goods = completed items ready for sale. Each stage has different management priorities and cost components.

A.Demand is variable
B.We order in batches larger than immediate demand (lot sizing)

Cycle stock fluctuates between replenishment orders — it exists because we order in batches rather than one unit at a time.

Question 4
