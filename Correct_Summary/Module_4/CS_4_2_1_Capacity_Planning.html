Overview

Capacity planning ensures that an organization has sufficient production capacity to meet demand. It operates at multiple levels — from long-range resource planning through detailed capacity requirements planning — and is tightly integrated with the master scheduling and MRP processes.

ASCM Definitions

📖 ASCM Definition — Capacity:

The capability of a worker, machine, work center, plant, or organization to produce output per time period. Capacity required represents the system capability needed to produce a given output in a given time period.

📖 ASCM Definition — Rated Capacity:

The expected output capability of a resource or system. Rated capacity is calculated from the work center information and assumes a given level of efficiency and utilization. Also called calculated capacity, nominal capacity, or theoretical capacity.

📖 ASCM Definition — Demonstrated Capacity:

Proven capacity calculated from actual performance data, usually of the average of actual output over a recent period. Also called actual capacity.

📖 ASCM Definition — Design Capacity:

The maximum output rate a facility is designed to achieve under ideal conditions. It represents the upper limit of capacity without overtime or extraordinary measures.

📖 ASCM Definition — Efficiency:

The ratio of actual output to standard output, usually expressed as a percentage. Efficiency = Actual Output / Standard Output × 100.

📖 ASCM Definition — Utilization:

A measure of how much available capacity is being used. Utilization = Actual Hours Worked / Available Hours × 100.

Levels of Capacity Planning

Capacity planning occurs at three levels, each corresponding to a planning horizon:

  • Resource Planning (RP): Long-range (months to years); checks whether strategic capacity (facilities, major equipment) can support the production plan. Also called Rough-Cut Capacity Planning (RCCP) at the aggregate level.
  • Rough-Cut Capacity Planning (RCCP): Medium-range; validates that the Master Production Schedule (MPS) is feasible given key work center capacities. Uses simplified data (capacity bills or resource profiles).
  • Capacity Requirements Planning (CRP): Short-range; detailed check that MRP-planned orders can be executed given actual work center capacity. Works with routing data and work center capacities.

Key Formulas

💡 Key Point:

Capacity Available = No. of Machines × Shifts × Hours/Shift × Utilization × Efficiency
Rated Capacity = Available Time × Utilization × Efficiency

Capacity Strategies

  • Lead strategy: Build capacity ahead of demand — avoids lost sales but risks excess capacity.
  • Lag strategy: Add capacity only after demand materializes — lower risk but potential service level issues.
  • Match/track strategy: Add capacity in small increments to keep pace with demand.

Managing Capacity Constraints

💡 Key Point:

A bottleneck is any resource whose capacity is less than or equal to demand placed upon it. Bottleneck management is central to the Theory of Constraints (TOC). The bottleneck determines overall throughput — all non-bottleneck resources should be subordinated to the bottleneck's pace.

Capacity Balancing Options

  • Overtime and extra shifts (short-term increase)
  • Outsourcing / subcontracting to third parties
  • Cross-training workers to flex between stations
  • Machine purchase or lease (medium-term)
  • Process redesign and efficiency improvement
  • Demand management: price incentives to shift demand to off-peak periods

DEFINITION
What is the ASCM definition of "capacity"?
ANSWER
The capability of a worker, machine, work center, plant, or organization to produce output per time period. Capacity required represents the capability needed to produce a given output in a given time period.
DEFINITION
What is the difference between rated capacity and demonstrated capacity?
ANSWER
Rated capacity is the expected (theoretical) output calculated from work center data assuming set efficiency and utilization. Demonstrated capacity is proven capacity from actual historical output data.
DEFINITION
What is design capacity?
ANSWER
The maximum output rate a facility is designed to achieve under ideal conditions — the theoretical upper limit without overtime or special measures.
DEFINITION
How is efficiency calculated in capacity planning?
ANSWER
Efficiency = Actual Output / Standard Output × 100. A work center producing 90 units/hour against a standard of 100 has 90% efficiency.
DEFINITION
How is utilization calculated?
ANSWER
Utilization = Actual Hours Worked / Available Hours × 100. If a machine works 36 out of 40 available hours, utilization is 90%.
FORMULA
What is the formula for rated/available capacity?
ANSWER
Capacity Available = Number of Machines × Shifts × Hours per Shift × Utilization × Efficiency. This gives the net productive hours available.
PROCESS
What are the three levels of capacity planning?
ANSWER
1) Resource Planning (RP) — long-range, validates production plan against major resources; 2) Rough-Cut Capacity Planning (RCCP) — validates MPS against key work centers; 3) Capacity Requirements Planning (CRP) — detailed check of MRP orders against work center capacity.
CONCEPT
What is Rough-Cut Capacity Planning (RCCP)?
ANSWER
RCCP validates the Master Production Schedule (MPS) against key resource capacities using simplified data (capacity bills or resource profiles). It identifies major capacity problems early without full routing detail.
CONCEPT
What is Capacity Requirements Planning (CRP)?
ANSWER
A detailed check that all MRP-planned orders can be executed given actual work center capacity. Uses routing data (time standards, setup time) and current work center loads to identify over- or under-loaded periods.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between a bottleneck and a non-bottleneck?
ANSWER
A bottleneck has capacity ≤ demand — it limits overall throughput. A non-bottleneck has capacity > demand. TOC says to protect and maximize the bottleneck; non-bottleneck idle time is acceptable.
CONCEPT
What are the three capacity strategies?
ANSWER
1) Lead — build ahead of demand (avoids lost sales, risks excess capacity); 2) Lag — add capacity only after demand materializes (lower risk, potential service issues); 3) Match/track — add in small increments to track demand growth.
CONCEPT
What short-term options exist to increase capacity?
ANSWER
Overtime, extra shifts, subcontracting/outsourcing, cross-training workers, temporary staff, machine rental, reducing setup time, improving scheduling efficiency.
CONCEPT
What is the relationship between capacity planning levels and planning horizons?
ANSWER
Resource Planning = long-range (years); RCCP = medium-range (months); CRP = short-range (weeks). Each level feeds from the planning level above it: RP supports the production plan, RCCP supports the MPS, CRP supports MRP.
CONCEPT
What input does Capacity Requirements Planning (CRP) use?
ANSWER
CRP uses the MRP planned orders, routing files (work center, time standards, setup times), and current work center capacity data to calculate load vs. capacity for each period.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between capacity and load?
ANSWER
Capacity = what a work center CAN produce per period. Load = what is REQUIRED of the work center based on planned orders. When load > capacity, the work center is overloaded.
CONCEPT
What is an input/output control chart used for in capacity management?
ANSWER
To track actual inputs (work center load) and outputs (production) against planned values. Helps identify capacity imbalances and backlog build-up before they become service problems.
CONCEPT
What is a capacity bill?
ANSWER
A simplified tool used in RCCP that shows how much capacity (hours) is needed per unit of a product at key work centers. Multiplied by MPS quantities to project capacity needs without full routing detail.
CONCEPT
What is a resource profile in capacity planning?
ANSWER
A time-phased representation of the resource requirements for a product or family across key work centers. Used in RCCP to project capacity needs over multiple planning periods.
KEY POINT
Why is the bottleneck critical in capacity planning?
ANSWER
The bottleneck constrains total system throughput. All other work centers will eventually idle or queue behind the bottleneck. Improving non-bottleneck capacity has no impact on total output — only improving the bottleneck increases throughput.
CONCEPT
What is demand management in the context of capacity?
ANSWER
Using pricing, lead time quotes, promotions, or allocation policies to shift demand to periods with available capacity. This avoids costly physical capacity additions by smoothing the demand placed on the system.
