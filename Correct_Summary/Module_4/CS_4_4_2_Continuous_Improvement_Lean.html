Overview

Lean manufacturing is a production philosophy focused on eliminating waste (muda) from all supply chain processes to maximize customer value. Continuous improvement (kaizen) is an ongoing effort to incrementally improve processes.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Lean production: A methodology that focuses on eliminating waste (non-value-adding activities) from the production process while improving quality, lead time, and customer value.
📖 Definition:
Waste (muda): Any activity that consumes resources without adding value for the customer. Toyota identified eight types: Transportation, Inventory, Motion, Waiting, Overproduction, Overprocessing, Defects, and Skills (TIMWOODS).
📖 Definition:
Just-in-Time (JIT): A philosophy focused on producing only what is needed, when it is needed, and in the quantity that is needed. Minimizes waste through pull systems, small lot sizes, and tight supplier coordination.
📖 Definition:
Kaizen: A Japanese term meaning 'continuous improvement' — small, incremental improvements made continuously by all employees. Distinguished from innovation by its focus on small, frequent changes rather than large breakthrough improvements.
📖 Definition:
Value stream: All activities (value-adding and non-value-adding) required to bring a product from raw material to the customer. Value Stream Mapping (VSM) documents these activities to identify and eliminate waste.

The Eight Wastes (TIMWOODS)

💡 Key Point:
T — Transportation (unnecessary movement of materials), I — Inventory (excess stock), M — Motion (unnecessary movement of people), W — Waiting (idle time), O — Overproduction (making more than needed), O — Overprocessing (doing more than required), D — Defects (rework, scrap), S — Skills (underutilizing people's talents). The first seven are from Toyota; Skills was added later.

Lean Tools

📖 Definition:
5S: A workplace organization methodology: Sort (remove unneeded items), Set in order (organize for efficiency), Shine (clean), Standardize (maintain standards), Sustain (make it habitual).
📖 Definition:
Kanban: A visual pull-based replenishment signal (card or empty container) that triggers production or delivery of only what is actually consumed — preventing overproduction.
  • Poka-yoke (mistake-proofing): designing processes so errors are impossible or immediately obvious
  • SMED (Single Minute Exchange of Die): reducing setup/changeover time to less than 10 minutes — enables small lot sizes
  • Andon: a visual signaling system that alerts supervisors to problems as soon as they occur
  • Heijunka (production leveling): smoothing production volume and mix over time to reduce variability

DEFINITION
What is lean production?
ANSWER
A methodology focused on eliminating waste (non-value-adding activities) from all processes — improving quality, reducing lead time, and maximizing customer value. Originated at Toyota as the Toyota Production System (TPS).
DEFINITION
What is waste (muda) in lean thinking?
ANSWER
Any activity that consumes resources without adding value for the customer. Toyota identified eight types: Transportation, Inventory, Motion, Waiting, Overproduction, Overprocessing, Defects, and Skills — remembered as TIMWOODS.
CONCEPT
What are the eight lean wastes (TIMWOODS)?
ANSWER
Transportation (moving materials unnecessarily), Inventory (excess stock), Motion (unnecessary movement of people), Waiting (idle time), Overproduction (making more than needed), Overprocessing (doing more than required), Defects (rework/scrap), Skills (underutilizing talent).
DEFINITION
What is Just-in-Time (JIT)?
ANSWER
A philosophy of producing only what is needed, when it is needed, in the quantity needed. Minimizes inventory waste through pull systems, small lot sizes, frequent deliveries, and tight coordination with suppliers.
DEFINITION
What is kaizen?
ANSWER
Japanese for 'continuous improvement' — small, incremental process improvements made continuously by all employees at all levels. Contrasts with innovation (large, infrequent breakthroughs). Creates a culture of constant waste elimination.
DEFINITION
What is a value stream?
ANSWER
All activities — both value-adding and non-value-adding — required to bring a product from raw material to the customer. Value Stream Mapping (VSM) documents these activities to identify waste, bottlenecks, and improvement opportunities.
DEFINITION
What is Value Stream Mapping (VSM)?
ANSWER
A lean tool that maps all material and information flows for a product family — from customer demand back through production to supplier. Distinguishes value-adding steps from waste to design a leaner 'future state.'
DEFINITION
What is 5S?
ANSWER
Sort (remove unneeded items), Set in order (organize for efficiency), Shine (clean the workspace), Standardize (create and maintain standards), Sustain (make it a habit). A workplace organization methodology that reduces waste and reveals problems.
DEFINITION
What is kanban in lean production?
ANSWER
A visual pull-based signal (card or empty container) that triggers production or delivery of exactly what was consumed — no more. Prevents overproduction (the worst lean waste) by making production respond to actual demand.
DEFINITION
What is SMED (Single Minute Exchange of Die)?
ANSWER
A lean methodology for reducing equipment setup/changeover time to less than 10 minutes ('single minute'). Enables small lot sizes without excessive setup cost — key to achieving Just-in-Time production.
DEFINITION
What is poka-yoke (mistake-proofing)?
ANSWER
Designing processes, equipment, or fixtures so that errors are physically impossible or immediately detectable — preventing defects from being passed to the next step. Example: USB connectors that only insert one way.
CONCEPT
What is takt time and how does it relate to lean production?
ANSWER
Takt = Available Time ÷ Demand Rate. The required production pace to meet customer demand. Lean production aims to balance all operations to takt time — eliminating bottlenecks and idle stations by design.
DEFINITION
What is heijunka (production leveling)?
ANSWER
Smoothing production volume and product mix over time to reduce variability — producing a level mix of products in small quantities throughout each day rather than batching. Reduces WIP, lead time, and capacity demand spikes.
DEFINITION
What is an andon system?
ANSWER
A visual alarm system (lights, boards, or alerts) that signals to supervisors and support staff when a production problem occurs. Allows immediate intervention to prevent defects from propagating downstream — a key TPS element.
CONCEPT
What is the Toyota Production System (TPS) and how does it relate to lean?
ANSWER
TPS is the production system developed by Toyota — the origin of lean manufacturing. Its two pillars are Just-in-Time (JIT) and Jidoka (automation with a human touch — stopping when defects occur). Lean is the Western interpretation of TPS principles.
