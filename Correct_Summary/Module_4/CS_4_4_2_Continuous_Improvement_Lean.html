With the information from the master production schedule and bill of material and on inventory status and planning factors, it is possible to develop a complete schedule for ordering (and delivering) all the components and materials necessary to create the items in the master schedule. The MRP process results in planned orders for making or buying all the components required. It typically includes the following specifications as defined in the ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary:
  • Net requirements = gross requirements − on-hand − scheduled receipts
  • Planned order receipt = planned receipt (not yet released); planned order release = offset left by lead time

Distribution requirements planning (DRP) is “the function of determining the need to replenish inventory at branch warehouses” (ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary). DRP organizes inventory requirements so the organization has time to plan for when and how many goods will be required.
In terms of distribution, the ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary defines a push system as “a system for replenishing field warehouse inventories in which replenishment decision-making is centralized, usually at the manufacturing site or central supply facility.” This traditional approach to distribution replenishment pushes inventory through manufacturing to the company distribution centers. Determination of product requirements is done by a central planning organization and pushed out. Inventory is then in storage until a customer (distributor, wholesaler, retailer) pulls material to meet projected demand.
The ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary defines a pull system as “a system for replenishing field warehouse inventories in which replenishment decisions are made at the field warehouse itself, not at the central warehouse or plant.” The benefit of this decentralized inventory planning approach accrues mostly to the entity doing the ordering, because it is able to operate independently to balance its supply and demand requirements as it sees fit. This assumes that the company orders intelligently. At the retail end, for example, the company might be a hardware franchise with a history in the community and good forecasting instincts.
There are three particular drawbacks to the pull system:
  • Push: centralized; systemwide coordination; less responsive to local demand
  • Pull: decentralized; bullwhip-prone; ignores supply chain partner situations
  • DRP (hybrid): centrally planned push to DC level; then pull by customers

4.4.2 Lean continuously improves by eliminating waste throughout the value stream.

image170.png
  • Lean objectives: eliminate waste, meet customer demand, gain market share, develop workforce, produce perfect quality
  • House of Toyota: foundation (operational stability via leveling, standard work, kaizen) + two pillars (jidoka + JIT) + roof (best quality, lowest cost, shortest lead time)
  • 8 wastes (TPS): overproduction (most serious), waiting, transport, inventory, motion, over-processing, defects, unused skills
  • Takt time: production rhythm = available time ÷ customer demand rate
  • Muda = waste; mura = unevenness; muri = overburden; mura/muri are root causes of muda
  • 5S: Sort, Simplify, Scrub, Standardize, Sustain — creates organized, clean lean workspace
  • Value stream mapping: current-state + future-state maps showing material and information flows

The six-sigma process for conducting a continuous improvement initiative takes place in five phases, known by the initials of the phases as Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control (DMAIC) process (as described in the ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary).
  • DMAIC: Define → Measure → Analyze → Improve → Control (six sigma improvement framework)
  • TOC 5 steps: Identify constraint → Exploit constraint → Subordinate everything else → Elevate constraint → Repeat
