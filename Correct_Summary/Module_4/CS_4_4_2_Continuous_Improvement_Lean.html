Waste (Muda) and the 8 Deadly Wastes
Waste (muda): (1) Any activity that does not add value to the good or service in the eyes of the consumer. (2) A by-product of a process or task with unique characteristics requiring special management control.

Toyota’s Taiichi Ohno identified 7 deadly wastes; the 8th (unused talent) was added later:

  • Transportation — unnecessary movement of materials
  • Inventory — excess stock beyond immediate needs
  • Motion — unnecessary movement of people
  • Waiting — idle time waiting for materials, machines, or information
  • Overproduction — producing more or sooner than needed (the worst waste — causes other wastes)
  • Overprocessing — doing more work than required by the customer
  • Defects — scrap, rework, returns
  • Unused talent/skills — not leveraging employee knowledge and creativity (8th waste)
Mnemonic: TIM WOOD U. Overproduction is the worst waste because it causes all other wastes (excess transportation, inventory, motion, waiting).
Three Broad Types of Waste (Muda, Mura, Muri)
Muda: waste — any activity consuming resources without adding value. The 8 wastes described by Toyota are all forms of muda.
Mura: unevenness or irregularity in the production process — variation in workloads that causes some periods to be rushed and others to be idle.
Muri: overburden — pushing workers or machines beyond their natural limits; creates stress, fatigue, breakdowns, and safety issues.
Mura and muri are often the root causes of muda. Fixing muda symptoms without addressing mura/muri leads to recurring waste. Management must address all three.
Lean Philosophy and Definition
Lean (lean production): a philosophy of production that emphasizes the minimization of the amount of all the resources (including time) used in the various activities of the enterprise. It involves identifying and eliminating non-value-adding activities.

8 Lean Objectives:

  • Eliminate waste in business value streams
  • Meet customer demand (pull-based production)
  • Increase velocity (shorten lead times)
  • Reduce need for working capital
  • Increase inventory turns
  • Gain market share through quality and speed
  • Increase profitability by reducing non-value-added costs
  • Develop the workforce through empowerment and training

House of Toyota

The House of Toyota (House of Lean) is a framework explaining the scope of lean. It has four parts:

  • Foundation — operational stability; achieved through load leveling (heijunka), standardized work, and kaizen
  • Left pillar: Just-in-Time (JIT) — right items, right time, right place, right amount; takt time, one-piece flow, pull systems
  • Right pillar: Jidoka — quality at the source; automation with a human touch; stop production when defects occur
  • Roof — best quality, lowest cost, shortest lead time (the goals of the system)
Heijunka: leveling the type and quantity of production over a fixed time period. Enables use of standard work and smooth pull systems by smoothing demand variability on the shop floor.
Kaizen: continuing improvement involving everyone — managers and workers. In manufacturing, kaizen relates to finding and eliminating waste in machinery, labor, or production methods.
Jidoka: automation with a human touch; the ability of a production line to be stopped — by a machine or a worker — when a defect is detected. Prevents defects from flowing to the next process.

Lean Tools and Techniques

Value Stream Mapping (VSM): a paper-and-pencil tool that maps the flow of material and information for a product family from supplier to customer. Reveals where waste exists; used to design future-state lean flows.

Kaizen Event / Kaizen Blitz:

  • Kaizen event: a scheduled, time-boxed improvement event (typically 5 days) involving a cross-functional team; starts and completes a focused improvement
  • Kaizen blitz: a more intense version covering a limited process area; rapid improvement of a production cell; includes basic training, analysis (flowchart), and implementation

Five Ss (5S Workplace Organization):

  • Seiri — Sort: remove unnecessary items from the workplace
  • Seiton — Set in order: organize and label what remains for easy access
  • Seiso — Shine: clean the workplace and equipment
  • Seiketsu — Standardize: create standards for the first three Ss
  • Shitsuke — Sustain: maintain discipline to keep the system working

Setup Time Reduction: 5 steps to reduce changeover time:

  • 1. Classify internal vs. external tasks; 2. Convert internal to external; 3. Streamline internal setup; 4. Eliminate adjustments; 5. Minimize external prep time
Internal tasks must be done while the machine is stopped; external tasks can be done while running. Converting internal to external is the highest-leverage setup reduction step.
Total Productive Maintenance (TPM)
Total Productive Maintenance (TPM): preventive maintenance plus continuing efforts to adapt, modify, and refine equipment to increase flexibility, reduce material handling, and promote continuous flows. It is operator-centered maintenance with the goal of equipment reliability through worker ownership.

TPM aims to eliminate:

  • Equipment breakdowns (unplanned downtime)
  • Setup and adjustment losses
  • Idling and minor stops
  • Reduced speed
  • Quality defects and rework
  • Startup losses

TPM engages all levels and functions — operators perform routine maintenance; maintenance department handles complex repairs. Shifts from reactive to proactive maintenance culture.

Just-in-Time (JIT)
Just-in-Time (JIT): a philosophy of manufacturing based on planned elimination of all waste and on continuous improvement of productivity. It has as its principal objectives having only the required inventory when needed; to improve quality to zero defects; to reduce lead times by reducing setup times, queue lengths, and lot sizes; and to incrementally revise the operations themselves.

3 JIT basics:

  • Waste reduction — eliminate storage, inspections, queues, and defects
  • Variability reduction — reduce demand and supply variability through standardization and supplier development
  • Pull production — items pulled into production only when demanded; not pushed by a preset schedule
Kanban: a system of cards or signals used in JIT to authorize the movement or production of materials. When a work center is ready for more material, it sends a kanban signal upstream. Only the rate of customer demand drives the system.

Poka-yoke: a mistake-proofing device or procedure that prevents a defect from being produced. Supports jidoka by making errors impossible or immediately detectable. Comes from Japanese "poka" (inadvertent errors) + "yoke" (avoid).

What is Standardized Work?
ANSWER
A documented, repeatable set of tasks defining the most efficient sequence and method for completing work. Provides a stable baseline for improvement and is the foundation of the House of Toyota.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is Overproduction considered the worst of the 8 wastes?
ANSWER
Overproduction creates and hides all other wastes: it builds excess inventory (requiring transportation and storage), creates motion (handling excess), causes waiting (next process overwhelmed), and masks defects in large batches.
