Overview

Quality management tools provide structured methods for identifying, analyzing, and solving quality problems. The 'seven basic quality tools' and additional advanced tools are used across supply chains for process improvement.

The Seven Basic Quality Tools

📖 Definition:
Cause-and-effect diagram (Ishikawa/fishbone): A diagram showing the relationship between a quality problem (effect) and its potential causes, organized into categories. Categories often: Machine, Method, Material, Man, Measurement, Environment (6Ms).
📖 Definition:
Check sheet: A structured form for collecting and tallying data in real time at the point where the data is generated. Simple, manual data collection tool.
📖 Definition:
Control chart: A statistical chart showing process performance over time with control limits (typically ±3σ). Points outside limits or non-random patterns indicate out-of-control process.
📖 Definition:
Pareto chart: A bar chart showing defects or problems in descending frequency, with a cumulative percentage line. Based on the Pareto principle: 80% of problems come from 20% of causes.
📖 Definition:
Scatter diagram: A graph plotting two variables to investigate potential relationships or correlations. Used to test hypotheses about cause-effect relationships.
📖 Definition:
Histogram: A bar chart showing the distribution of a data set — frequency of values in each interval. Used to understand process variation and distribution shape.
📖 Definition:
Stratification (flow charts): Separating data into meaningful subgroups to reveal patterns. Stratifying defect data by shift, machine, or operator can reveal the root cause.

Advanced Quality Tools

📖 Definition:
Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA): A proactive tool identifying potential failure modes in a process or product, assessing their likelihood and severity, and prioritizing prevention actions. Risk Priority Number (RPN) = Severity × Occurrence × Detection.
💡 Key Point:
Pareto principle (80/20 rule): approximately 80% of quality problems are caused by 20% of root causes. Pareto charts help identify the 'vital few' causes to prioritize for improvement.
  • 5 Whys: repeatedly asking 'Why?' to drill down to the root cause of a problem — typically 5 iterations reaches the root cause
  • A3 report: a problem-solving methodology on a single A3-sized page — problem statement, current state, root cause, countermeasures, implementation plan, results
  • Poka-yoke (mistake-proofing): designing processes so errors cannot occur or are immediately detected

CONCEPT
What are the seven basic quality tools?
ANSWER
1) Cause-and-effect (fishbone) diagram, 2) Check sheet, 3) Control chart, 4) Pareto chart, 5) Scatter diagram, 6) Histogram, 7) Stratification (flowcharts). Together they cover data collection, analysis, and display for most quality problems.
DEFINITION
What is a cause-and-effect (fishbone/Ishikawa) diagram?
ANSWER
A structured diagram showing the relationship between a quality problem (effect) and its potential causes, organized into categories (6Ms: Machine, Method, Material, Man, Measurement, Environment). Used for root cause analysis.
DEFINITION
What is a Pareto chart and what principle does it illustrate?
ANSWER
A bar chart showing defects/problems in descending frequency with a cumulative % line. Illustrates the Pareto principle (80/20 rule): 80% of problems come from 20% of causes — focus improvement on the 'vital few.'
DEFINITION
What is a control chart?
ANSWER
A statistical chart displaying process measurements over time with upper and lower control limits (typically ±3σ). Points outside limits or non-random patterns indicate the process is 'out of statistical control' — requiring investigation.
DEFINITION
What is FMEA (Failure Mode and Effects Analysis)?
ANSWER
A proactive quality tool identifying potential failure modes in a process or product design, assessing their severity and likelihood, and prioritizing prevention. RPN = Severity × Occurrence × Detection — higher RPN = higher priority to address.
DEFINITION
What is the 5 Whys technique?
ANSWER
A root cause analysis method that repeatedly asks 'Why?' (typically 5 times) to drill through symptoms to the underlying root cause of a problem. Simple but powerful for moving beyond surface-level fixes.
DEFINITION
What is a histogram?
ANSWER
A bar chart showing the frequency distribution of a data set — how often values fall in each range. Used to understand the shape of process variation (normal, skewed, bimodal) and detect problems like centering errors or excessive spread.
DEFINITION
What is a scatter diagram?
ANSWER
A graph plotting two variables against each other to investigate potential correlations or relationships. Used to test hypotheses about cause-effect relationships — e.g., 'Does temperature affect defect rate?'
DEFINITION
What is a check sheet?
ANSWER
A structured form for collecting and tallying data at the point of generation — marking occurrences of specific events (defect types, machine failures, process steps). Simple manual data collection enabling quick pattern recognition.
CONCEPT
What is the Pareto principle (80/20 rule)?
ANSWER
Approximately 80% of quality problems are caused by 20% of root causes. Prioritizing the few vital causes that generate most of the defects/losses is far more effective than treating all causes equally.
DEFINITION
What is an A3 report in problem-solving?
ANSWER
A structured one-page problem-solving tool using an A3-sized sheet: defines the problem, shows current state, identifies root causes, documents countermeasures, sets implementation plan, and tracks results. Originated at Toyota as a communication and thinking tool.
DEFINITION
What is poka-yoke (mistake-proofing)?
ANSWER
Designing processes or equipment so mistakes are physically impossible or immediately obvious — preventing defects from being made or passed forward. Example: USB-C reversible connectors eliminating 'wrong way' insertion.
DEFINITION
What is stratification in quality analysis?
ANSWER
Separating a data set into meaningful subgroups (by machine, shift, operator, material lot) to reveal patterns not visible in aggregate data. A stratified defect analysis may show that 90% of defects come from one machine or shift.
CONCEPT
What are the 6Ms in cause-and-effect analysis?
ANSWER
Machine, Method, Material, Man (People), Measurement, and Environment — the six common cause categories in an Ishikawa (fishbone) diagram. Provides a structured approach to brainstorming potential root causes.
CONCEPT
What is Statistical Process Control (SPC) and which tool enables it?
ANSWER
SPC uses statistical methods — primarily control charts — to monitor process performance and detect when a process goes out of statistical control. Enables proactive intervention before defects reach customers.
