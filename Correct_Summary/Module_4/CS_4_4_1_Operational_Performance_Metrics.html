Overall Equipment Effectiveness (OEE)
Overall Equipment Effectiveness (OEE): a measure of how well a manufacturing operation is utilized relative to its full potential during planned production time. OEE = Availability × Performance × Quality.

OEE components:

  • Availability = Actual running time / Planned production time. Lost to unplanned downtime (breakdowns) and setup/changeover time.
  • Performance = Actual output rate / Theoretical output rate. Lost to speed losses and minor stops.
  • Quality (First Pass Yield) = Good units / Total units started. Lost to defects and rework.
World-class OEE is considered to be 85%+. An OEE of 100% means: running all the planned time (availability), at full speed (performance), with zero defects (quality). Each component identifies a different category of loss.

Six Big Losses (from TPM methodology):

  • Equipment failures (breakdowns) — hits Availability
  • Setup and changeover time — hits Availability
  • Idling and minor stoppages — hits Performance
  • Reduced speed — hits Performance
  • Process defects (during stable production) — hits Quality
  • Reduced yield (during startup) — hits Quality
Throughput and Capacity Metrics
Throughput: the rate at which a system generates money through sales — or in an operational context, the rate at which the system produces output. In TOC, it is revenue minus totally variable costs.
Takt time: the rate at which products must be produced to meet customer demand. Takt time = Available production time / Customer demand rate. Sets the drumbeat of a lean production system.

Takt time example: If available time = 480 min/day and customer demand = 240 units/day, takt time = 480/240 = 2 minutes/unit. Production must produce one unit every 2 minutes to meet demand.

  • Cycle time < takt time: production faster than demand — risk of overproduction (waste)
  • Cycle time > takt time: production slower than demand — bottleneck; need to increase capacity or reduce cycle time
Cycle time: the elapsed time from when a unit enters a process to when it exits. Includes all processing, waiting, and move time within the operation.
Schedule Adherence and Delivery Metrics
  • Schedule adherence — % of production orders completed on time per the plan. Measures execution reliability of the production schedule.
  • On-time delivery (OTD) — % of customer orders delivered on or before the promised date. External view of schedule adherence.
  • Fill rate — % of demand filled immediately from stock (Type II service level)
  • Order cycle time (OCT) — total time from customer order placement to delivery at customer
Perfect Order: an order that is delivered on time, complete, damage-free, and with correct documentation. The Perfect Order Rate measures the % of orders meeting ALL four criteria simultaneously.
Inventory Performance Metrics
  • Inventory turns (turnover) = Annual COGS / Average inventory value. Higher turns = more efficient inventory use; lower working capital needed per unit of revenue.
  • Days of Supply (DOS) = Average inventory / Average daily demand. How many days of demand current inventory can cover. Inverse of inventory turns.
  • Inventory accuracy = % of locations where physical count matches system record. Target: 95%+
  • Obsolescence rate — % of inventory written off as obsolete over a period
Inventory turns and Days of Supply are inversely related: higher turns = fewer days of supply. Lean organizations aim for high turns (low inventory); service-focused organizations trade turns for safety stock.
Quality Metrics
  • First Pass Yield (FPY) — % of units that complete a process without requiring rework or scrapping. Component of OEE (Quality factor).
  • Defects per Million Opportunities (DPMO) — the Six Sigma metric. World class = 3.4 DPMO (six sigma level).
  • Scrap rate — % of units scrapped; waste of both materials and labor
  • Rework rate — % of units requiring additional processing to meet spec
  • Customer returns rate — % of shipped units returned due to defects
Cost and Financial Metrics
  • Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) — all costs associated with acquiring, using, and maintaining an item or supplier relationship over its lifetime. Used for make vs. buy and supplier selection decisions.
  • Value-added ratio = Value-added time / Total lead time. Typically very low (1–5%); most lead time is non-value-added waiting.
  • Cost of Quality (COQ) — total cost of all quality-related activities: prevention + appraisal + internal failure + external failure costs
  • Labor productivity = Output / Labor input. Measured in units per hour or $ output per $ labor cost.
Cost of Quality (COQ): the total cost incurred in preventing defects, appraising quality, and dealing with internal and external failures. Lower failure costs = better ROI on prevention spending.

TERM / CONCEPT
What is Overall Equipment Effectiveness (OEE)?
ANSWER
A measure of manufacturing utilization vs. full potential. OEE = Availability × Performance × Quality. World-class OEE is 85%+. Identifies losses in availability (downtime), performance (speed), and quality (defects).
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 3 OEE components and what losses affect each?
ANSWER
Availability: unplanned downtime and changeovers. Performance: speed losses and minor stoppages. Quality: defects and startup scrap (First Pass Yield). OEE = Availability × Performance × Quality.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the Six Big Losses in TPM/OEE?
ANSWER
1) Equipment failures (Availability), 2) Setup/changeover (Availability), 3) Idling/minor stoppages (Performance), 4) Reduced speed (Performance), 5) Process defects (Quality), 6) Reduced yield at startup (Quality).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Takt Time?
ANSWER
The rate at which products must be produced to meet customer demand. Takt time = Available production time / Customer demand rate. Sets the drumbeat of a lean production system.
TERM / CONCEPT
What happens when Cycle Time > Takt Time?
ANSWER
Production is slower than customer demand — a bottleneck exists. Must either increase production rate at the constraint or reduce cycle time to meet demand without building backlogs.
TERM / CONCEPT
What happens when Cycle Time < Takt Time?
ANSWER
Production is faster than demand — risk of overproduction (a lean waste: muda). Excess output leads to inventory buildup. Lean systems deliberately slow production to match takt time.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Throughput?
ANSWER
The rate at which a system generates output. In TOC: revenue minus totally variable costs. In operations: units produced per time period. Maximizing throughput at the bottleneck is the primary focus of TOC.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Schedule Adherence?
ANSWER
The percentage of production orders completed on time per the production plan. Measures how reliably operations execute the schedule. A lagging indicator of planning effectiveness.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Perfect Order Rate?
ANSWER
The percentage of orders that are delivered on time, complete, damage-free, and with correct documentation. All four criteria must be met simultaneously — it is a composite measure of end-to-end supply chain performance.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Inventory Turns (Turnover)?
ANSWER
Annual COGS / Average inventory value. Measures how many times inventory is sold and replaced per year. Higher turns = more efficient use of working capital. Lean organizations target high turns.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Days of Supply (DOS)?
ANSWER
Average inventory / Average daily demand. Indicates how many days current inventory can cover demand. Inverse of inventory turns. Lower DOS = less cash tied up in inventory.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is First Pass Yield (FPY)?
ANSWER
The percentage of units completing a process without requiring rework or scrapping. FPY is the Quality component of OEE. High FPY means defects are low and no extra processing is needed.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is DPMO?
ANSWER
Defects per Million Opportunities — the Six Sigma quality metric. Six Sigma level = 3.4 DPMO. Represents near-perfect quality. Used to compare quality across processes of different complexities.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)?
ANSWER
All costs associated with acquiring, using, and maintaining an item or supplier relationship over its lifetime. Includes purchase price plus quality costs, logistics, warranty, maintenance, end-of-life. Used for make vs. buy and supplier selection.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Value-Added Ratio?
ANSWER
Value-added time / Total lead time. Typically only 1–5% in manufacturing — most lead time is non-value-added waiting, moving, or inspecting. Lean aims to increase this ratio by eliminating waste.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Cost of Quality (COQ)?
ANSWER
Total cost of all quality-related activities: Prevention (avoiding defects) + Appraisal (inspecting/testing) + Internal failure (scrap, rework before shipment) + External failure (returns, warranty, recalls). Investing in prevention reduces total COQ.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the On-Time Delivery (OTD) metric?
ANSWER
The percentage of customer orders delivered on or before the promised delivery date. An external view of schedule adherence — measures what the customer actually experiences.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Order Cycle Time (OCT)?
ANSWER
Total elapsed time from when a customer places an order to when it is received. Includes order processing, manufacturing (if MTO), picking, packing, and shipping. A key customer experience metric.
TERM / CONCEPT
How are Inventory Turns and Days of Supply related?
ANSWER
They are inversely related: Days of Supply = 365 / Inventory Turns. Higher turns = fewer days of supply held. Example: 12 turns/year = 30 days of supply; 4 turns/year = 91 days of supply.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Inventory Accuracy and why does it matter?
ANSWER
The % of locations where physical count matches the system record. Target: 95%+. Low accuracy undermines MRP (wrong net requirements) and causes customer service failures (stockouts not predicted by the system).
