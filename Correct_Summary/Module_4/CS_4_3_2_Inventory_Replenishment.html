Overview

Inventory replenishment strategies determine when and how much to order. The right strategy balances service levels against inventory investment by choosing appropriate review systems and order parameters.

ASCM Definitions

📖 ASCM Definition — Economic Order Quantity (EOQ):

The order quantity that minimizes total annual inventory holding and ordering costs. EOQ = √(2DS/H), where D = annual demand, S = ordering cost per order, H = annual holding cost per unit.

📖 ASCM Definition — Reorder Point (ROP):

A set inventory level where, if the total stock on hand plus on order falls to or below that point, action is taken to replenish the stock. ROP = Average Demand during Lead Time + Safety Stock.

📖 ASCM Definition — Safety Stock:

A quantity of stock planned to be in inventory to protect against fluctuations in demand or supply. Also called buffer stock or safety inventory.

📖 ASCM Definition — Vendor-Managed Inventory (VMI):

A means of optimizing supply chain performance in which the supplier has access to the customer's inventory data and is responsible for maintaining the inventory level required by the customer.

Replenishment System Types

💡 Key Point:

Continuous review (Q-system / fixed-order-quantity system): Order a fixed quantity Q whenever inventory drops to the reorder point. Requires continuous monitoring. Order quantity is fixed; timing varies.
Periodic review (P-system / fixed-order-period system): Review inventory at fixed time intervals and order up to a target level. Order timing is fixed; quantity varies. Better for items ordered from same supplier (can combine orders).

EOQ Key Concepts

  • EOQ minimizes total cost = holding cost + ordering cost
  • Holding cost rises with order quantity (more avg. inventory)
  • Ordering cost falls as order quantity rises (fewer orders)
  • EOQ is where these two costs are equal
  • Assumptions: constant demand, fixed costs, no stockouts, instantaneous replenishment

Min/Max Systems

Min/Max (two-bin) systems set a minimum inventory level (reorder point) and a maximum level (order-up-to point). When inventory falls below the minimum, order enough to reach the maximum.

Kanban Replenishment

Kanban is a visual pull system where a signal (card, bin, or electronic signal) triggers replenishment when inventory is consumed. Used in lean manufacturing; keeps inventory minimal by replenishing only what is consumed.

Advanced Replenishment Strategies

  • VMI: Supplier monitors customer inventory and triggers replenishment — reduces customer ordering burden
  • CPFR: Collaborative Planning, Forecasting, and Replenishment — supplier and customer jointly develop forecasts and replenishment plans
  • Consignment inventory: Supplier owns inventory at customer location until it is consumed/sold
  • Just-in-Time (JIT): Replenish exactly what is needed, when needed — minimal safety stock

DEFINITION
What is the ASCM definition of Economic Order Quantity (EOQ)?
ANSWER
The order quantity that minimizes total annual holding and ordering costs. EOQ = √(2DS/H), where D = annual demand, S = ordering cost, H = annual holding cost per unit.
DEFINITION
What is a Reorder Point (ROP)?
ANSWER
The inventory level at which a replenishment order is triggered. ROP = Average Demand during Lead Time + Safety Stock. Ensures inventory does not run out while awaiting the replenishment order.
DEFINITION
What is Vendor-Managed Inventory (VMI)?
ANSWER
A replenishment strategy where the supplier has access to the customer's inventory data and is responsible for maintaining the customer's required inventory level — reducing the customer's ordering burden.
DEFINITION
What is Collaborative Planning, Forecasting, and Replenishment (CPFR)?
ANSWER
A process where supplier and customer jointly develop demand forecasts and replenishment plans, sharing information to improve accuracy and reduce inventory across the supply chain.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between continuous (Q) and periodic (P) review systems?
ANSWER
Q-system: order a fixed quantity Q whenever inventory falls to the ROP. Timing varies, quantity is fixed. P-system: review at fixed intervals, order up to a target level. Timing is fixed, quantity varies.
FORMULA
How is Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) calculated?
ANSWER
EOQ = √(2DS/H), where D = annual demand (units), S = cost per order (ordering/setup cost), H = annual holding cost per unit. EOQ minimizes total cost = holding cost + ordering cost.
CONCEPT
What happens to the EOQ if ordering costs decrease significantly?
ANSWER
Lower ordering costs shift the optimal order quantity downward — smaller, more frequent orders become cost-effective. This is the rationale behind electronic ordering and JIT deliveries.
CONCEPT
What is a kanban replenishment system?
ANSWER
A visual pull signal that triggers replenishment when inventory is consumed. Cards, bins, or electronic signals authorize the upstream process to produce/deliver only what has been consumed. Keeps WIP minimal.
CONCEPT
What is a min/max replenishment system?
ANSWER
A system with a minimum level (reorder point) and a maximum level (order-up-to point). When inventory falls below the minimum, an order is placed to bring inventory up to the maximum.
CONCEPT
What is consignment inventory?
ANSWER
An arrangement where the supplier retains ownership of inventory held at the customer's location. The customer pays only when inventory is consumed/sold — transfers financial risk back to the supplier.
CONCEPT
What are the assumptions underlying EOQ?
ANSWER
Constant, known demand; fixed ordering cost per order; fixed holding cost per unit; no stockouts allowed; instantaneous replenishment (no lead time variation); no quantity discounts. Real conditions often violate these.
CONCEPT
How does safety stock relate to the reorder point?
ANSWER
Safety stock is added to average demand during lead time to set the reorder point. ROP = (Average Demand/Day × Lead Time in Days) + Safety Stock. Safety stock protects against demand/supply variability.
CONCEPT
What is Just-in-Time (JIT) inventory replenishment?
ANSWER
A philosophy of delivering materials exactly when needed in exactly the quantities needed — minimizing safety stock and WIP. Requires reliable suppliers, stable demand, and short, consistent lead times.
CONCEPT
When is the periodic review (P-system) preferred?
ANSWER
When items are ordered from the same supplier (can consolidate orders), when continuous monitoring is costly, or when management prefers regular review intervals. Requires higher safety stock than Q-system due to the review period adding to exposure.
CONCEPT
What is a planned order in MRP-driven replenishment?
ANSWER
An MRP-generated recommendation to release a purchase order or manufacturing order at a specific time for a specific quantity. The planner reviews and approves planned orders before they are released.
CONCEPT
What is the trade-off between order frequency and inventory level?
ANSWER
Ordering more frequently reduces average inventory (lower holding cost) but increases total ordering costs. Less frequent orders reduce ordering cost but increase average inventory and holding cost. EOQ finds the optimal balance.
CONCEPT
What is supplier-managed replenishment?
ANSWER
A broad term for strategies where the supplier (not the customer) initiates replenishment. Includes VMI (supplier decides timing and quantity) and CPFR (jointly planned). Reduces customer administrative burden.
CONCEPT
How does demand variability affect safety stock requirements?
ANSWER
Higher demand variability requires more safety stock to maintain a given service level. Safety stock = z × σ × √L, where z = service level factor, σ = demand standard deviation, L = lead time. Higher σ → more safety stock.
CONCEPT
What is a distribution requirements plan (DRP)?
ANSWER
DRP applies MRP logic to distribution networks — time-phased replenishment planning across distribution centers. It calculates when each DC should order from a central warehouse or factory to meet anticipated demand.
CONCEPT
What is the bullwhip effect and how do replenishment strategies affect it?
ANSWER
The bullwhip effect is amplification of demand variability upstream in the supply chain. Poor replenishment practices (order batching, safety stock stacking) worsen it. CPFR, VMI, and real-time data sharing help mitigate it.

EOQ minimizes the sum of:
A.Purchase price and quality costs
B.Annual ordering costs and annual holding costs
C.Transportation and warehouse costs
D.Stockout and overstock costs
EOQ = sqrt(2DS/H) minimizes total annual cost = ordering cost (D/Q x S) + holding cost (Q/2 x H).
Question 2
Reorder point (ROP) equals:
A.EOQ times lead time
B.Average demand during lead time plus safety stock
C.Maximum inventory minus safety stock
D.Order cycle time times daily demand
ROP = (Average daily demand x Lead time) + Safety stock. A new order is triggered when on-hand hits the ROP.
Question 3
In VMI, replenishment is initiated by:
A.The buyer issuing purchase orders
B.The supplier monitoring buyer inventory and replenishing based on agreed targets
C.The 3PL provider
D.The government
In VMI, the supplier monitors buyer stock and initiates replenishment — reducing buyer ordering workload and improving synchronization.
Question 4
A fixed-quantity (Q) replenishment system orders:
A.A variable quantity on a fixed schedule
B.A fixed quantity whenever inventory falls to the reorder point
C.Whatever quantity is needed to fill to maximum
D.EOQ whenever convenient
A Q system triggers a standard order quantity (often EOQ) whenever stock falls to the ROP — also called a continuous review or two-bin system.
