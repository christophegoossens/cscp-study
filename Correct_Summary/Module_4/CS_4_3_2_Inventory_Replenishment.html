Overview

Inventory replenishment policies determine when to order and how much to order. Selecting the right replenishment model balances ordering costs, holding costs, and service level requirements.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Reorder point (ROP): The inventory level at which a replenishment order should be placed. ROP = Average Demand during Lead Time + Safety Stock.
📖 Definition:
Economic Order Quantity (EOQ): The order quantity that minimizes the total annual cost of ordering and holding inventory. Formula: EOQ = √(2 × Annual Demand × Ordering Cost / Holding Cost per unit per year).
📖 Definition:
Replenishment lead time: The time from placing a replenishment order to receiving the goods in inventory. Includes supplier processing time, manufacturing time (if make-to-order), and transportation time.
📖 Definition:
Safety stock: Inventory held to protect against demand variability and supply lead time variability. Safety Stock = z × σ(demand) × √Lead Time (service level factor × demand standard deviation × square root of lead time).

Replenishment Systems

💡 Key Point:
Fixed order quantity (Q system / continuous review): monitor inventory continuously; when inventory falls to the reorder point, order a fixed quantity (EOQ). Responds faster to demand; more complex to administer.
💡 Key Point:
Fixed order interval (P system / periodic review): review inventory at fixed intervals (e.g., weekly); order enough to raise inventory to a target level. Simpler administration; requires more safety stock to cover review interval variability.
  • Min-max system: order when inventory falls to the minimum (reorder point); order up to the maximum. Practical implementation of the Q system
  • Two-bin system: physical implementation — when first bin empties, order replenishment; use second bin during lead time
  • Vendor-managed inventory (VMI): supplier monitors customer inventory and triggers replenishment automatically
  • Kanban: visual replenishment signal (card or empty container) triggering upstream production or delivery
💡 Key Point:
Cycle service level (CSL) vs. fill rate: CSL is the probability of no stockout during a replenishment cycle. Fill rate (Type 2) is the fraction of demand filled without backorder. Fill rate is typically higher than CSL for the same safety stock.

DEFINITION
What is the reorder point (ROP)?
ANSWER
The inventory level that triggers placing a replenishment order. Formula: ROP = (Average Demand Rate × Lead Time) + Safety Stock. Ensures inventory doesn't run out while waiting for the replenishment to arrive.
FORMULA
What is the Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) formula?
ANSWER
EOQ = √(2 × Annual Demand × Ordering Cost / Holding Cost per unit per year). The EOQ minimizes total annual inventory cost (ordering cost + holding cost). As order quantity increases, holding cost rises but ordering cost falls.
DEFINITION
What is replenishment lead time?
ANSWER
The time from placing a replenishment order to receiving goods in inventory — including supplier processing, manufacturing (if applicable), and transportation time. Longer lead time requires higher reorder point and more safety stock.
FORMULA
What is the safety stock formula?
ANSWER
Safety Stock = z × σ(demand) × √Lead Time. Where z = service level factor (e.g., 1.65 for 95%), σ(demand) = standard deviation of demand, and √Lead Time accounts for variability accumulating over the replenishment period.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between the Q (fixed order quantity) and P (fixed order interval) systems?
ANSWER
Q system: continuously monitor inventory; order a fixed quantity (EOQ) when inventory hits the reorder point. P system: review inventory at fixed intervals; order variable quantity to reach a target level. Q responds faster; P is simpler to administer.
DEFINITION
What is a min-max replenishment system?
ANSWER
Order when inventory falls to the minimum level (= reorder point); order up to the maximum level. The order quantity = max − current inventory. A practical implementation of the continuous review Q system with fixed upper and lower bounds.
DEFINITION
What is Vendor-Managed Inventory (VMI)?
ANSWER
An arrangement where the supplier monitors customer inventory levels (via data sharing) and automatically triggers replenishment when needed — without the customer placing a purchase order. Reduces buyer's ordering burden; gives supplier better demand visibility.
DEFINITION
What is a kanban replenishment system?
ANSWER
A visual pull-based replenishment system where an empty container or card (kanban) signals the upstream process or supplier to send more material. Ensures production is triggered only by actual consumption — a lean/JIT principle.
DEFINITION
What is cycle service level (CSL)?
ANSWER
The probability that inventory will not run out during a single replenishment cycle. CSL = 95% means in 95% of cycles, no stockout occurs. Setting the safety stock service factor z based on desired CSL: z = NORM.INV(CSL, 0, 1).
KEY DISTINCTION
What is fill rate and how does it differ from cycle service level?
ANSWER
Fill rate (Type 2 service level) is the fraction of total demand filled without backorder. Fill rate is typically higher than CSL for the same safety stock — because stockouts are partial, not full-cycle failures.
CONCEPT
What is the trade-off between ordering cost and holding cost in EOQ?
ANSWER
Higher order quantity → fewer orders (lower annual ordering cost) but higher average inventory (higher holding cost). Lower order quantity → more orders (higher ordering cost) but lower average inventory (lower holding cost). EOQ is where these costs are equal.
DEFINITION
What is a two-bin replenishment system?
ANSWER
A physical replenishment method using two containers of inventory. Use items from the first bin until empty — the empty bin triggers a replenishment order. Continue using the second bin during the lead time. Simple, visual, requires no system.
CONCEPT
How does demand variability affect safety stock requirements?
ANSWER
Higher demand variability (larger standard deviation) requires more safety stock to maintain the same service level. SS = z × σ(demand) × √Lead Time — doubling σ doubles safety stock needed.
CONCEPT
What is the effect of lead time reduction on inventory?
ANSWER
Shorter lead time reduces both the reorder point (less inventory needed to cover the lead time) and safety stock (less variability accumulates over shorter lead time) — reducing total inventory investment significantly.
CONCEPT
What is periodic review (P system) and when is it preferred?
ANSWER
Periodic review checks inventory at fixed intervals (weekly, monthly) and orders to a target level. Preferred when items are delivered together (reducing transport cost per visit) or when continuous monitoring is too expensive (many low-value items).

A.Purchase price and quality costs
B.Annual ordering costs and annual holding costs
C.Transportation and warehouse costs
D.Stockout and overstock costs
EOQ = sqrt(2DS/H) minimizes total annual cost = ordering cost (D/Q x S) + holding cost (Q/2 x H).
Question 2
Reorder point (ROP) equals:
A.EOQ times lead time
B.Average demand during lead time plus safety stock
C.Maximum inventory minus safety stock
D.Order cycle time times daily demand
ROP = (Average daily demand x Lead time) + Safety stock. A new order is triggered when on-hand hits the ROP.
Question 3
In VMI, replenishment is initiated by:
A.The buyer issuing purchase orders
B.The supplier monitoring buyer inventory and replenishing based on agreed targets
C.The 3PL provider
D.The government
In VMI, the supplier monitors buyer stock and initiates replenishment — reducing buyer ordering workload and improving synchronization.
Question 4
A fixed-quantity (Q) replenishment system orders:
A.A variable quantity on a fixed schedule
B.A fixed quantity whenever inventory falls to the reorder point
C.Whatever quantity is needed to fill to maximum
D.EOQ whenever convenient
A Q system triggers a standard order quantity (often EOQ) whenever stock falls to the ROP — also called a continuous review or two-bin system.
