Economic Order Quantity (EOQ)
Economic order quantity (EOQ): a type of fixed order quantity model that determines the amount of an item to be purchased or manufactured at one time. Minimizes the combined costs of acquiring and carrying inventory.

EOQ formula: Q* = √(2DS / H)

  • D = annual demand (units)
  • S = ordering/setup cost per order ($)
  • H = annual holding cost per unit ($ per unit per year)

EOQ assumptions (must be understood for the exam):

  • Demand is constant and known
  • Lead time is constant and known
  • Unit cost does not vary with order quantity (no quantity discounts)
  • Replenishment is instantaneous (orders arrive all at once)
  • No stockouts are permitted
EOQ is where the ordering cost curve and holding cost curve intersect — at this point, total cost is minimized. Increasing order size reduces per-order cost but increases average inventory (holding cost).
Reorder Point (ROP)
Reorder point (ROP): the inventory level at which a replenishment order should be placed. The point at which demand during the replenishment lead time (plus safety stock) equals the inventory on hand.

ROP formula: ROP = (Average demand per day × Lead time in days) + Safety stock

Key concepts:

  • ROP is triggered when on-hand inventory falls to the reorder level
  • During lead time, safety stock protects against stockouts
  • Higher variability in demand or lead time requires higher ROP (more safety stock)
Safety stock: inventory held to protect against fluctuations in demand or supply. Also called buffer stock. Calculated based on desired service level and variability of demand/lead time.

Safety stock formula (common approach): SS = Z × σd × √LT

Where Z = service level factor (e.g., 1.65 for 95%), σd = standard deviation of daily demand, LT = lead time in days.

Inventory Replenishment Systems

Two main replenishment systems for independent demand:

  • Fixed Order Quantity (Q-system, continuous review): order a fixed quantity Q whenever inventory falls to the reorder point. The timing of orders varies; the quantity is fixed. Requires continuous inventory monitoring.
  • Fixed Order Period (P-system, periodic review): review inventory at fixed time intervals (e.g., every week). Order quantity varies to bring inventory up to a target level. Easier to coordinate with suppliers.
Q-system: fixed quantity, variable timing — best for high-value/critical items (A items). P-system: fixed timing, variable quantity — better for C items and when coordinating multiple items from same supplier.
Lot Sizing Methods

Lot sizing determines how much to order each time. Key methods:

  • Lot-for-lot (L4L) — order exactly what is needed; zero surplus; best for JIT; highest ordering frequency
  • Fixed order quantity (FOQ) — order a constant amount each time (e.g., pallet size, container load)
  • Economic order quantity (EOQ) — mathematically optimal order size based on costs
  • Period order quantity (POQ) — EOQ logic converted to a number of periods; order covers a fixed number of weeks of demand
  • Min-max system — order when inventory falls below the minimum; order enough to reach the maximum level
  • Least unit cost (LUC) — iteratively adds periods to the order until unit cost starts rising
  • Part period balancing (PPB) — equates carrying costs to ordering costs over the order horizon
Vendor-Managed Inventory and Consignment
Vendor-managed inventory (VMI): a supply chain practice where the supplier manages replenishment decisions for the buyer’s inventory, using shared demand and inventory data. Reduces buyer’s ordering burden and can reduce bullwhip effect.
Consignment inventory: inventory at a customer location that remains the property of the supplier until it is actually used/sold. Reduces buyer’s financial risk while ensuring availability.

Benefits of VMI:

  • Supplier sees real demand data — reduces bullwhip effect
  • Buyer reduces inventory management workload
  • Supplier can optimize delivery routes and quantities
  • Requires high trust and data sharing
Service Level and Fill Rate
Service level: a measure of the ability to meet customer demand from available inventory. Often expressed as the probability of not stocking out during a replenishment cycle, or as a fill rate (% of units demanded filled immediately from stock).

Trade-off: service level vs. holding cost

  • Higher service level requires more safety stock — higher holding cost
  • 100% service level is rarely economical; 95–99% is common
  • Type I service level: probability of no stockout per order cycle
  • Type II service level (fill rate): fraction of demand filled without backorder

TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Economic Order Quantity (EOQ)?
ANSWER
A fixed order quantity model that minimizes total inventory cost (ordering + holding). Formula: Q* = √(2DS/H) where D = annual demand, S = ordering cost per order, H = annual holding cost per unit.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 5 assumptions of the EOQ model?
ANSWER
1) Constant known demand, 2) Constant known lead time, 3) No quantity discounts (constant unit cost), 4) Instantaneous replenishment (all units arrive at once), 5) No stockouts permitted.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Reorder Point (ROP)?
ANSWER
The inventory level at which a replenishment order should be placed. ROP = (Average daily demand × Lead time days) + Safety stock. Ensures inventory doesn’t run out during the replenishment period.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Safety Stock?
ANSWER
Buffer inventory held to protect against variability in demand or lead time. Also called buffer stock. Calculated as: SS = Z × σd × √LT, where Z = service level factor, σd = daily demand std dev, LT = lead time.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between a Q-system and a P-system?
ANSWER
Q-system (fixed order quantity): order a fixed amount Q whenever inventory hits the ROP; timing varies; requires continuous monitoring. P-system (fixed order period): review at fixed intervals; order quantity varies to reach target level.
TERM / CONCEPT
When would you use a Q-system vs. a P-system?
ANSWER
Q-system: high-value/A items needing tight control; items where continuous monitoring is feasible. P-system: C items; items ordered from the same supplier (batch orders); when supplier visits on a schedule.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Lot-for-Lot (L4L) lot sizing?
ANSWER
Order exactly the net requirement for each period — no surplus. Minimizes carrying costs but maximizes ordering frequency. Preferred in JIT environments where setup costs are very low.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Period Order Quantity (POQ)?
ANSWER
A lot sizing method that orders enough to cover a fixed number of periods of demand. Derived from EOQ logic but expressed as a number of weeks rather than a quantity. Balances ordering and holding costs over multiple periods.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Vendor-Managed Inventory (VMI)?
ANSWER
A supply chain practice where the supplier manages replenishment for the buyer’s inventory using shared demand and inventory data. Reduces buyer’s ordering workload and bullwhip effect.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Consignment Inventory?
ANSWER
Inventory located at the customer’s site that remains the property of the supplier until actually used or sold. Reduces buyer’s financial risk while ensuring product availability.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does safety stock relate to service level?
ANSWER
Higher service level (e.g., 99% vs. 95%) requires more safety stock. The Z factor in the safety stock formula corresponds to the desired service level (Z=1.65 for 95%, Z=2.33 for 99%). Higher SS = higher holding cost.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the trade-off in setting safety stock levels?
ANSWER
More safety stock = higher service level (fewer stockouts) but higher holding cost. Less safety stock = lower holding cost but higher risk of stockouts. Organizations balance these based on the cost of a stockout vs. cost of holding.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a Min-Max replenishment system?
ANSWER
An inventory system with a minimum (reorder point) and maximum (target) level. When on-hand inventory falls below the minimum, order enough to reach the maximum. Simple and intuitive for C items.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Fill Rate?
ANSWER
The percentage of customer demand filled immediately from stock (also called Type II service level). A fill rate of 98% means 98% of units demanded are immediately available from existing inventory.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Part Period Balancing (PPB)?
ANSWER
A lot sizing method that finds the order quantity where cumulative carrying costs equal the ordering cost over the order horizon. More dynamic than EOQ — adjusts to actual period-by-period demand.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does VMI reduce the Bullwhip Effect?
ANSWER
The supplier sees real downstream demand data directly — not filtered through the buyer’s ordering behavior. This visibility eliminates the order amplification that occurs when each party makes independent ordering decisions.
TERM / CONCEPT
What happens to EOQ if ordering cost decreases (e.g., from automation)?
ANSWER
EOQ decreases — smaller, more frequent orders become optimal. This is the logic behind JIT: reducing setup/ordering cost drives the ideal lot size toward one unit (L4L).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the relationship between holding cost percentage and EOQ?
ANSWER
Higher holding cost percentage (H) results in smaller EOQ — it becomes more expensive to hold inventory, so smaller orders are optimal. Lower holding cost results in larger EOQ.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Fixed Order Quantity (FOQ) method?
ANSWER
Always ordering a predetermined fixed quantity each time a replenishment is triggered, regardless of actual demand. The quantity may be set by container size, supplier minimum, or economic analysis.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between Type I and Type II service level?
ANSWER
Type I: probability of NOT stocking out during a replenishment cycle (cycle service level). Type II (fill rate): fraction of total demand filled without backorder. Both measure service but from different perspectives.

EOQ minimizes the sum of:
A.Purchase price and quality costs
B.Annual ordering costs and annual holding costs
C.Transportation and warehouse costs
D.Stockout and overstock costs
EOQ = sqrt(2DS/H) minimizes total annual cost = ordering cost (D/Q x S) + holding cost (Q/2 x H).
Question 2
Reorder point (ROP) equals:
A.EOQ times lead time
B.Average demand during lead time plus safety stock
C.Maximum inventory minus safety stock
D.Order cycle time times daily demand
ROP = (Average daily demand x Lead time) + Safety stock. A new order is triggered when on-hand hits the ROP.
Question 3
In VMI, replenishment is initiated by:
A.The buyer issuing purchase orders
B.The supplier monitoring buyer inventory and replenishing based on agreed targets
C.The 3PL provider
D.The government
In VMI, the supplier monitors buyer stock and initiates replenishment — reducing buyer ordering workload and improving synchronization.
Question 4

A.A variable quantity on a fixed schedule
