Overview

Several complementary CI philosophies are used together to systematically reduce waste, defects, and constraints.

In brief, here are some well-accepted continuous improvement approaches:
  • Lean: waste elimination using empowered teams; built on JIT; takt-time driven
  • JIT: reduce inventory to zero; deliver materials exactly when needed, not before
  • Six sigma: ≤3.4 defects per million opportunities; focus on high-quality (not just fast) processes
  • TOC: identify constraint → exploit → subordinate → elevate → repeat; inherently continuous improvement
  • Combining all three: TOC sets system speed; lean reduces waste; six sigma reduces variability
Waste is given a broad definition by the ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary:
  • 8 wastes (TPS by Taiichi Ohno + Womack/Jones): overproduction (most serious), waiting, transport, inventory, motion, over-processing, defects, unused people skills
  • Waste = any activity consuming resources without adding customer value
Lean improvement initiatives focus on reduction or elimination of waste in all areas. The ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary identifies “lean” and “lean manufacturing” as synonyms for lean production, which it defines as follows:

  • Lean objectives: eliminate waste, meet customer demand at customer rate, gain market share, increase profitability, develop workforce, achieve perfect quality
  • 5 lean principles (Womack & Jones): value → value stream → flow → pull → perfection
  • Long-term supplier relationships; synchronize supplier processes with lean delivery schedules
Increase velocity. Velocity refers to the time it takes to provide value to the customer. Velocity in supply chain management is defined in the ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary as “the relative speed of all transactions, collectively, within a supply chain community.” The definition goes on to state that “a maximum velocity is most desirable because it indicates higher asset turnover for stockholders and faster order-to-delivery response for customers.” Through the use of JIT and lean techniques, velocity produces products and services only as fast as customers want them.

In the Toyota Production System, heijunka is the Japanese term akin to leveling. The ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary defines it as part of the just-in-time philosophy and as “an approach to level production throughout the supply chain to match the planned rate of end product sales.”
Standardized work (or standard work) has been compared to the steps in a dance; it defines tasks (such as content, sequence, and timing) to achieve an optimum process in the time available.
The ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary defines kaizen as follows:
  • Jidoka: "automation with a human touch"; machines/operators stop on defect; prevents defective output propagation
  • Takt time = available production time ÷ customer demand; sets production heartbeat
  • Lean accounting vs. standard cost accounting: lean focuses on cycle time and inventory turns, not machine utilization
Lean accounting also necessitates different types of financial, behavioral, and core process performance metrics. A lean metric is one “that permits a balanced evaluation and response, such as improving quality without sacrificing quantity objectives” (ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary).
  • Value stream mapping: paper-and-pencil; current state → future state (eliminate waste); shows material + information flows
  • 5S: Sort, Simplify (Set in order), Scrub, Standardize, Sustain — lean workplace organization
  • Muda = waste; mura = unevenness; muri = overburden; mura/muri are root causes of muda
  • Work cells: U-shaped, cross-trained workers; minimize transport, enable one-piece flow

Kaizen is the Japanese word for continuous improvement, and it signifies a carefully designed, evolutionary process. A kaizen event is defined in the ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary as follows:
  • Kaizen event: time-boxed improvement (e.g., one week); for changes without major process disruption
  • Kaizen blitz: more intense, narrow focus; quick demo of reform potential; useful at start of lean/TQM initiative
A related term for a more intense version of the process that could make many improvements but keeps the focus area narrow is a kaizen blitz®. It is defined by the ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary as follows:
  • JIT elements for CI: pull scheduling (level + kanban), work cells, cross-training, JIT supplier relationships
  • JIT suppliers: on-time, zero-defect delivery; may move warehouse into plant for minimal transport
The ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary defines TPM as follows.
  • Six sigma: tools include DMAIC, process mapping, control charts, Pareto charts; data-driven
  • Belts: green belt (part-time), black belt (full-time leader), master black belt (primarily teaches)

Just in time (JIT) is a major component in lean's House of Toyota. The ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary defines just in time (JIT) as follows: A philosophy of manufacturing based on planned elimination of all waste and on continuous improvement of productivity. The primary elements of just in time are to have only the required inventory when needed; to improve quality to zero defects; to reduce lead times by reducing setup times, queue lengths, and lot sizes; to incrementally revise the operations themselves; and to accomplish these activities at minimum cost.
The ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary defines six sigma as follows: A methodology that furnishes tools for the improvement of business processes. The intent is to decrease process variation and improve product quality.
The six-sigma process for conducting a continuous improvement initiative takes place in five phases, known by the initials of the phases as Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control (DMAIC) process. Phase 1: Define the scope and goals of the improvement project; Phase 2: Measure existing performance and record data about underlying causes; Phase 3: Analyze data to identify root causes; Phase 4: Improve the process by eliminating root causes; Phase 5: Control the improved process to sustain gains.
  • TOC 5 steps: 1) Identify constraint 2) Exploit constraint (max output without major changes) 3) Subordinate all else to constraint 4) Elevate constraint (invest to increase capacity) 5) Repeat (new constraint emerges)
  • Drum/Buffer/Rope (DBR): drum = constraint pace; buffer = time/material buffer before constraint; rope = release timing to prevent WIP buildup

Question 1
TOC focuses improvement on:
A.All processes simultaneously
B.The constraint (bottleneck) limiting overall system throughput
C.Reducing defects to zero
D.Minimizing labour costs
TOC directs improvement to the system constraint. Improving non-constraints yields no throughput benefit until the constraint is resolved.

B.Speed, Safety, Service, Standard, Sustain

5S is a workplace organization methodology: Sort, Set in order (organize), Shine (clean), Standardize, Sustain — the foundation for other lean tools.

A.Maximum safety stock

JIT is the lean ideal of producing and delivering the right item, right quantity, at the right time — eliminating waste from overproduction and excess inventory.
