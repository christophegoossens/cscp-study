Overview

Continuous improvement methodologies provide structured frameworks for systematically improving supply chain processes. Theory of Constraints (TOC) offers a specific lens focused on identifying and exploiting the system's limiting factor.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Theory of Constraints (TOC): A management philosophy developed by Dr. Eliyahu Goldratt, asserting that every system has at least one constraint that limits its output. The only way to improve system performance is to identify and manage the constraint.
📖 Definition:
Constraint (bottleneck): Any resource whose capacity is less than the demand placed on it — limiting system throughput. In TOC: every organization has exactly one true constraint at any given time.
📖 Definition:
Throughput (TOC definition): The rate at which the system generates money through sales = Sales Revenue − Truly Variable Costs (materials). NOT the same as production output rate.
📖 Definition:
Continuous improvement (CI): An ongoing effort to incrementally improve products, services, and processes. CI is embedded in methodologies including Lean, Six Sigma, Kaizen, and PDCA.

The Five TOC Focusing Steps

💡 Key Point:
TOC five steps: 1) IDENTIFY the constraint, 2) EXPLOIT the constraint (maximize its output without major investment), 3) SUBORDINATE everything else to the constraint, 4) ELEVATE the constraint (invest to increase its capacity), 5) PREVENT inertia — if the constraint moves, go back to step 1.

CI Methodologies Comparison

💡 Key Point:
PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Act) / Deming cycle: Plan (define the problem and solution), Do (implement on small scale), Check (measure results), Act (standardize if successful, adjust if not). The foundational CI loop.
💡 Key Point:
Lean: eliminate waste. Six Sigma: reduce variation. TOC: identify and exploit the constraint. These are complementary — lean removes waste, Six Sigma improves quality, TOC focuses resources where they matter most.
  • DMAIC (Six Sigma): Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control
  • PDCA (Lean/Deming): Plan, Do, Check, Act
  • A3 thinking: single-page structured problem solving
  • Kaizen events (Rapid Improvement Events): focused 3-5 day events where a cross-functional team implements improvements to a specific process
📖 Definition:
Drum-Buffer-Rope (DBR): A TOC production scheduling method. Drum = bottleneck sets the pace. Buffer = time buffer before the bottleneck protects its utilization. Rope = work release is tied to the drum's rate, controlling WIP.

DEFINITION
What is Theory of Constraints (TOC)?
ANSWER
A management philosophy asserting every system has at least one constraint limiting output. Improving system performance requires identifying, exploiting, and eventually elevating the constraint. Developed by Dr. Eliyahu Goldratt.
CONCEPT
What are the five TOC focusing steps?
ANSWER
1) Identify the constraint, 2) Exploit the constraint (maximize its output without investment), 3) Subordinate everything else to the constraint, 4) Elevate the constraint (invest to increase capacity), 5) Prevent inertia — if constraint moves, return to step 1.
DEFINITION
What is throughput in TOC?
ANSWER
The rate at which the system generates money through sales = Sales Revenue − Truly Variable Costs (materials only). Not the same as production output rate. TOC maximizes throughput while minimizing inventory and operating expense.
DEFINITION
What is the TOC definition of a constraint?
ANSWER
Any resource whose capacity is less than the demand placed on it — limiting system throughput. TOC asserts that every organization has exactly one governing constraint at any given time. Improving non-constraints does not improve total system throughput.
DEFINITION
What is the PDCA cycle?
ANSWER
Plan (identify problem and define solution), Do (implement on small scale), Check (measure results vs. targets), Act (standardize if successful; adjust and repeat if not). The foundational continuous improvement loop — also called the Deming cycle.
DEFINITION
What is DMAIC and when is it used?
ANSWER
Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control — the Six Sigma improvement methodology used for solving existing problems with a defined defect. More rigorous and data-driven than PDCA; used for complex, statistically significant problems.
DEFINITION
What is Drum-Buffer-Rope (DBR) scheduling?
ANSWER
A TOC production scheduling method. Drum = the bottleneck sets the production rhythm. Buffer = time buffer before the bottleneck protects its utilization. Rope = work release is tied to the drum's pace, limiting WIP throughout the system.
CONCEPT
How do Lean, Six Sigma, and TOC complement each other?
ANSWER
Lean: eliminates waste and speeds flow. Six Sigma: reduces process variation and defects. TOC: identifies and focuses resources on the system's constraint. Together: eliminate waste (Lean), reduce variation (Six Sigma), optimize the bottleneck (TOC).
DEFINITION
What is a kaizen event (Rapid Improvement Event)?
ANSWER
A focused 3-5 day improvement event where a cross-functional team analyzes a specific process, identifies waste, implements improvements, and documents the new standard. More intensive and faster than incremental kaizen.
KEY INSIGHT
What is the key insight of TOC regarding non-bottleneck resources?
ANSWER
Improving the output of a non-bottleneck resource does NOT increase total system throughput — it only builds WIP queue in front of the bottleneck. Investments should be directed at the constraint, not everywhere equally.
CONCEPT
What is 'subordinate to the constraint' mean in TOC step 3?
ANSWER
Every non-bottleneck process should be scheduled and operated to support the constraint's efficiency — feeding it exactly what it needs, when it needs it. Non-bottleneck processes should not run at full speed if it would starve or overwhelm the constraint.
DEFINITION
What is A3 thinking?
ANSWER
A structured problem-solving process documented on a single A3 page: Define the problem, show the current state (with data), identify root causes, propose countermeasures, plan implementation, verify results, and standardize if successful. Developed as a communication tool at Toyota.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between kaizen and innovation in continuous improvement?
ANSWER
Kaizen: small, frequent, incremental improvements by all employees — continuous and sustainable. Innovation: large, infrequent breakthrough improvements driven by R&D — high potential but high risk and cost. Effective organizations need both.
CONCEPT
What does 'elevate the constraint' mean in the TOC five steps?
ANSWER
Step 4: after exploiting the current constraint to its maximum, invest to increase its capacity — add equipment, staff, shifts, or new methods. This breaks the constraint, but may cause a new constraint to emerge elsewhere (return to step 1).
CONCEPT
What is the role of buffer management in TOC?
ANSWER
Buffers (time or inventory) protect the constraint from starvation due to upstream variability. Buffer management monitors buffer consumption to identify which upstream problems are actually threatening constraint utilization — focusing corrective action.
