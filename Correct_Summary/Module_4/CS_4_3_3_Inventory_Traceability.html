Overview

Inventory traceability is the ability to track and trace inventory through the supply chain — where it came from, where it is now, and where it went. Traceability is essential for quality management, recalls, and regulatory compliance.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Traceability: The ability to track and trace a product through the supply chain — from raw material origin through production to the end customer. Includes both 'track' (current status) and 'trace' (history).
📖 Definition:
Lot control: The tracking of production lots or batches through the supply chain — maintaining records of which lot numbers were received, when, used in what production runs, and shipped to which customers.
📖 Definition:
Serial number tracking: Assigning and tracking unique serial numbers to individual units. Provides item-level traceability — more granular than lot-level tracking. Required for high-value or safety-critical products.
📖 Definition:
Product recall: The action of retrieving unsafe or defective products from customers or distribution channels. Traceability data determines which customers received products from a specific lot and where the products currently are.

Traceability Methods

💡 Key Point:
Lot-level traceability: tracks groups of items produced together under the same conditions. Required by food safety (FDA, FSMA), pharmaceutical (FDA 21 CFR), and automotive (IATF 16949) regulations.
💡 Key Point:
Serial number traceability: tracks individual units — essential for warranty management, regulated industries (medical devices, aerospace), and high-value products. More data to manage but enables precise recall scope.
  • RFID (Radio Frequency Identification): enables automated item tracking without manual scanning
  • Barcode scanning: the foundational traceability technology — GS1-128 barcodes encode lot numbers and expiry dates
  • GS1 standards: global standards for barcode structures (GTIN, GLN, SSCC) enabling supply chain-wide traceability
  • Chain of custody: the documented record of who handled a product at each step — important for perishables, pharmaceuticals, and luxury goods
  • Expiry date tracking: managing First-Expired-First-Out (FEFO) rotation for perishable or date-sensitive items

Regulatory Requirements

  • FSMA (Food Safety Modernization Act): US law requiring food supply chain traceability records
  • EU Food Safety Regulation (EC 178/2002): 'one step forward, one step back' traceability requirement
  • Pharmaceutical serialization: DSCSA (US) and FMD (EU) require unit-level serial numbers for prescription drugs
  • Conflict minerals reporting: Dodd-Frank Act requires traceability of tin, tungsten, tantalum, gold from conflict zones

DEFINITION
What is traceability in supply chain?
ANSWER
The ability to track and trace a product through the supply chain — from raw material origin through production to the end customer. Includes tracking (current status) and tracing (historical record of movements and handling).
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between 'track' and 'trace'?
ANSWER
Track: monitoring the current location and status of an item in real time (forward-looking). Trace: reconstructing the history of an item's movements, handling, and provenance (backward-looking). Both are required for full traceability.
DEFINITION
What is lot control?
ANSWER
Tracking production lots or batches through the supply chain — maintaining records of which lot numbers were received, when, used in which production runs, and shipped to which customers. Enables targeted product recalls.
DEFINITION
What is serial number tracking?
ANSWER
Assigning and tracking unique serial numbers to individual units (not just batches). Provides item-level traceability — more granular than lot tracking. Required for medical devices, aerospace components, and high-value products.
CONCEPT
What is a product recall and how does traceability support it?
ANSWER
Retrieving unsafe or defective products from customers or channels. Traceability enables rapid identification of which customers received products from the defective lot, the current location of those products, and scope of the affected population.
CONCEPT
What is the GS1 standard and why is it important for traceability?
ANSWER
GS1 is a global organization maintaining standards for supply chain identification (GTIN for products, GLN for locations, SSCC for shipping units). GS1-128 barcodes encode lot numbers and expiry dates. Enables interoperability across trading partners.
DEFINITION
What is First-Expired-First-Out (FEFO) inventory management?
ANSWER
A rotation policy for perishable or date-sensitive items: items with the earliest expiry date are picked and shipped first. Requires expiry date tracking in the WMS. Prevents expired goods from reaching customers.
CONCEPT
What is RFID (Radio Frequency Identification) and how does it enable traceability?
ANSWER
Technology using electromagnetic fields to automatically identify and track tagged items — works without line-of-sight, enables bulk scanning at dock doors. Enables automatic, accurate receiving and shipping records without manual scanning.
CONCEPT
What does the FSMA require in food supply chain traceability?
ANSWER
The Food Safety Modernization Act (US) requires food companies to maintain records that enable tracing food back to its source and forward to first recipients — enabling rapid recall when a contamination event occurs.
DEFINITION
What is a chain of custody document?
ANSWER
A formal record documenting who handled a product at each step of the supply chain — when, where, and in what condition. Critical for perishables (cold chain), pharmaceuticals, legal evidence, and luxury goods authenticity.
CONCEPT
What is pharmaceutical serialization and what laws require it?
ANSWER
Assigning unique serial numbers to each drug unit (bottle, blister pack). DSCSA (US Drug Supply Chain Security Act) and FMD (EU Falsified Medicines Directive) require serialization to prevent counterfeit drugs from entering the supply chain.
CONCEPT
What is the 'one step forward, one step back' traceability requirement?
ANSWER
EU Food Safety Regulation (EC 178/2002) requires food businesses to identify who they bought from (one step back) and who they sold to (one step forward) — minimum traceability without requiring full chain tracking. A baseline requirement.
CONCEPT
What are conflict minerals and what traceability is required?
ANSWER
Tin, tungsten, tantalum, and gold (3TG) mined in conflict zones (e.g., DRC). US Dodd-Frank Act Section 1502 requires publicly traded companies to report whether their products contain 3TG and trace the origin through the supply chain.
CONCEPT
What is a Quality Management System (QMS) traceability requirement?
ANSWER
ISO 9001 and industry standards (IATF 16949 for automotive, AS9100 for aerospace, ISO 13485 for medical devices) require documented traceability of materials, production records, and test results — enabling root cause analysis and targeted recalls.
CONCEPT
What technology enables automated traceability in a DC?
ANSWER
Barcode scanning (manual or fixed-mount conveyor scanners), RFID portals at dock doors, WMS integration for real-time lot/serial tracking, and vision systems for label verification. Together they create automated, accurate receiving and shipping records.

A.Most recently purchased inventory is sold first
B.Oldest inventory is sold first
C.All inventory has the same cost
D.Inventory is valued at market price
FIFO assumes the oldest stock is consumed first — important for perishable goods and accurate balance sheet valuation.

A.Tracking the financial cost of each batch
B.The ability to identify the origin, movement, and disposition of a specific production lot
C.Counting inventory by lot number
D.Scheduling production by lot size
Lot traceability records the history of each production batch from raw material through manufacturing to customer.
Question 3

A.Serial tracking is cheaper
B.Serial numbers identify individual units; lot numbers identify batches
C.Lot tracking is only for food
D.Serial tracking requires blockchain
Serial number tracking identifies each individual unit; lot/batch tracking identifies groups of units produced under the same conditions.
