Inventory Traceability Overview
Traceability: the ability to track a product or material back through the supply chain to its origin (backward traceability) or forward to its final destination (forward traceability). Critical for quality control and product recalls.

Two directions of traceability:

  • Backward traceability (track back) — from the finished product back to raw materials and suppliers. Answers: "Where did this come from?"
  • Forward traceability (track forward) — from raw materials forward to finished products and customers. Answers: "Where did this go?"
Full traceability requires both directions. Backward traceability alone does not locate products already in customer hands; forward traceability is needed to find and recall affected units.
Lot and Serial Number Tracking
Lot (batch) traceability: tracking inventory by production lot or batch number, enabling identification of all units produced under the same conditions. Essential for food, pharma, and regulated industries.
Serial number tracking: assigning a unique identifier to each individual unit. Provides the most granular traceability — can trace a specific unit from manufacture to specific customer. Used for high-value items (medical devices, electronics).

FIFO and FEFO:

  • FIFO (First In, First Out) — oldest inventory is issued first. Standard inventory rotation method; prevents old stock from being buried by new stock.
  • FEFO (First Expired, First Out) — inventory with the earliest expiration date is issued first, regardless of receipt date. Critical for perishables, pharmaceuticals, food products.
FEFO is the preferred rotation method for any product with a shelf life or expiry date. Using FIFO for expiry-sensitive products can result in expired product being held while newer product is issued first.
Barcodes and GS1 Standards
GS1: a global organization that develops and maintains supply chain standards, including barcodes, Electronic Product Codes (EPC), and data communication standards. GS1 standards enable interoperability across supply chain partners.

Key GS1 identifiers:

  • GTIN (Global Trade Item Number) — identifies a product (replaces UPC/EAN); used on barcodes
  • SSCC (Serial Shipping Container Code) — identifies a logistics unit (pallet, carton); used on labels
  • GLN (Global Location Number) — identifies a company location (warehouse, dock door, legal entity)
  • GRAI (Global Returnable Asset Identifier) — identifies returnable transport items (pallets, containers)

Barcode types:

  • 1D barcodes (UPC, EAN-13, Code 128) — encode GTIN and other linear data; require line-of-sight scanning
  • 2D barcodes (QR code, Data Matrix) — encode more data including lot number, expiry, serial number; GS1 application identifiers used in GS1-128 and GS1 DataMatrix
RFID (Radio Frequency Identification)
RFID (Radio Frequency Identification): a technology that uses electromagnetic fields to automatically identify and track tags attached to objects. Tags contain electronically stored information and can be read without line-of-sight.

RFID vs. Barcodes:

  • RFID does not require line-of-sight; barcodes do
  • RFID can read multiple tags simultaneously; barcodes require individual scanning
  • RFID tags can be read/write; barcodes are read-only
  • RFID is more expensive per tag; barcodes are nearly free to print
  • RFID enables real-time inventory tracking without human intervention

RFID tag types:

  • Passive — no battery; powered by reader signal; shorter range; lower cost
  • Active — has battery; longer range; more expensive; can broadcast continuously
  • Semi-passive — has battery for powering the chip but uses reader signal for communication
Electronic Product Code (EPC): a GS1 standard for RFID tags that uniquely identifies items, cases, pallets, and locations. Used with EPCglobal Network for real-time supply chain visibility.
Product Recalls and Reverse Traceability

Effective traceability is critical for executing product recalls quickly and accurately:

  • Speed matters: faster identification of affected lots = smaller scope of recall = lower cost and risk
  • Precision matters: lot-level traceability limits recall to affected batches; without it, entire product lines may be recalled

Recall effectiveness requires:

  • Accurate lot/serial number records throughout the supply chain
  • Forward traceability to identify which customers received affected units
  • Supplier data enabling backward traceability to identify source of defect
  • Clear recall communication protocols (FDA, USDA, or relevant authority notification)
Mock recall exercises are used to test traceability systems — companies simulate a recall to verify they can trace a product within a specified time window (often 2–4 hours in food industry).
Track and Trace Technologies

Modern supply chains use multiple technologies for inventory tracking:

  • WMS (Warehouse Management System) — manages physical inventory movements, lot/serial tracking, location assignments
  • EDI (Electronic Data Interchange) — standardized electronic document exchange; ASN (Advance Ship Notice) communicates lot data in advance of shipment
  • Blockchain — provides tamper-resistant distributed ledger for recording chain of custody across multiple supply chain partners
  • IoT sensors — monitor conditions (temperature, humidity) during storage and transit; critical for cold chain compliance

What is Traceability?
ANSWER
The ability to track a product or material back to its origin (backward) or forward to its destination (forward). Critical for quality control and product recalls.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between backward and forward traceability?
ANSWER
Backward: from finished product back to raw materials/suppliers — answers 'where did this come from?' Forward: from raw materials to customers — answers 'where did this go?' Both are needed for effective recalls.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is FIFO?
ANSWER
First In, First Out — oldest inventory is issued first. Standard inventory rotation method that prevents old stock from being buried by newer arrivals.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is FEFO?
ANSWER
First Expired, First Out — inventory with the earliest expiration date is issued first, regardless of receipt date. Used for perishables, food, and pharmaceuticals where shelf life matters.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is FEFO preferred over FIFO for perishable products?
ANSWER
FEFO ensures that products closest to expiration are used first, regardless of when they were received. FIFO could result in newer product (with later expiry) being used before older stock — leading to expired product.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is GS1?
ANSWER
A global organization that develops and maintains supply chain standards including GTINs, SSCCs, GLNs, barcodes, and RFID standards (EPC). Enables interoperability between supply chain partners worldwide.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a GTIN?
ANSWER
Global Trade Item Number — a GS1 identifier for a product (replaces UPC/EAN). Used on barcodes to uniquely identify a trade item at any point in the supply chain.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is an SSCC?
ANSWER
Serial Shipping Container Code — a GS1 identifier for a logistics unit (pallet, carton). Used on shipping labels to identify and track containers through the supply chain.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is RFID?
ANSWER
Radio Frequency Identification — a technology using electromagnetic fields to identify and track tags attached to objects without requiring line-of-sight. Tags can store item identity, lot, serial number, etc.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the key differences between RFID and barcodes?
ANSWER
RFID: no line-of-sight required, reads multiple tags simultaneously, can be read/write, higher cost. Barcodes: require line-of-sight, one at a time, read-only, nearly free to print.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 3 RFID tag types?
ANSWER
1) Passive — no battery, powered by reader (low cost, short range). 2) Active — has battery, longer range, higher cost, can broadcast continuously. 3) Semi-passive — battery for chip but uses reader for communication.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is an Electronic Product Code (EPC)?
ANSWER
A GS1 standard for RFID tags that uniquely identifies items, cases, pallets, and locations. Part of the EPCglobal Network for real-time supply chain visibility.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Lot (Batch) Traceability?
ANSWER
Tracking inventory by production lot or batch number, enabling identification of all units produced under the same conditions. Essential for regulated industries (food, pharma) and product recalls.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Serial Number Tracking?
ANSWER
Assigning a unique identifier to each individual unit — the most granular traceability level. Enables tracking of a specific unit from manufacture to a specific customer. Used for high-value items.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is forward traceability critical for product recalls?
ANSWER
Forward traceability identifies which specific customers received affected lots. Without it, companies cannot locate products already in the field and must issue blanket recalls — broader and more costly.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a Mock Recall Exercise?
ANSWER
A simulated recall used to test traceability systems — companies practice tracing a product to verify they can identify all affected lots and customers within a specified time (often 2–4 hours in food industry).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the role of EDI in traceability?
ANSWER
Electronic Data Interchange enables standardized electronic document exchange. ASNs (Advance Ship Notices) communicate lot/serial data in advance of shipment, maintaining the chain of custody without manual data entry.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the role of a WMS in inventory traceability?
ANSWER
A Warehouse Management System tracks physical inventory movements with lot/serial number assignment, location management, and rotation rules (FIFO/FEFO). Provides the operational layer of traceability.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a GLN (Global Location Number)?
ANSWER
A GS1 identifier for a specific business location — a warehouse, dock door, storage location, or legal entity. Enables precise identification of where inventory is located in the supply chain.
TERM / CONCEPT
What makes blockchain valuable for supply chain traceability?
ANSWER
Blockchain provides a tamper-resistant distributed ledger that records chain of custody across multiple, independent supply chain partners. Once recorded, data cannot be altered — creating trusted provenance records.

FIFO (First In, First Out) assumes:
A.Most recently purchased inventory is sold first
B.Oldest inventory is sold first
C.All inventory has the same cost
D.Inventory is valued at market price
FIFO assumes the oldest stock is consumed first — important for perishable goods and accurate balance sheet valuation.
Question 2
Lot traceability means:
A.Tracking the financial cost of each batch
B.The ability to identify the origin, movement, and disposition of a specific production lot
C.Counting inventory by lot number
D.Scheduling production by lot size
Lot traceability records the history of each production batch from raw material through manufacturing to customer.
Question 3
Serial number tracking differs from lot tracking in that:
A.Serial tracking is cheaper
B.Serial numbers identify individual units; lot numbers identify batches
C.Lot tracking is only for food
D.Serial tracking requires blockchain
Serial number tracking identifies each individual unit; lot/batch tracking identifies groups of units produced under the same conditions.
