Production Activity Control (PAC) Overview
Production activity control (PAC): the function of routing and dispatching the work to be accomplished through the production facility and of performing supplier control. Also the function of overseeing and adjusting the execution of the production schedule.

PAC is the execution layer of the MPC system. It translates MRP-released orders into specific shop floor actions:

  • Releases orders to the shop floor at the right time
  • Dispatches work to work centers according to priority rules
  • Monitors progress vs. schedule
  • Manages queues and WIP
  • Reports completed quantities and scrap back to MRP
PAC bridges planning (MRP) and execution. Without PAC, even a perfect MRP plan cannot be executed reliably.
Dispatching
Dispatching: the function of selecting and sequencing available jobs to be run at individual work centers and the assignment of those jobs to workers.

The dispatch list (shop floor schedule) tells each work center which jobs to run, in what sequence, updated frequently (often daily).

Key priority sequencing rules:

  • FCFS — First Come, First Served: jobs processed in the order they arrive; simple but ignores due dates
  • SPT — Shortest Processing Time: smallest job first; minimizes average flow time and WIP, but delays large jobs
  • EDD — Earliest Due Date: job with earliest due date runs first; minimizes maximum lateness
  • CR — Critical Ratio: CR = (Time remaining / Work remaining); CR <1 means job is behind schedule; dynamic rule that updates automatically as time passes
  • S/O — Slack per Operation: distributes remaining slack across remaining operations; similar to CR but accounts for number of operations
  • LCFS — Last Come, First Served: most recent job runs first; reflects how stacks of material naturally form
Critical Ratio (CR) is the most dynamic sequencing rule — it automatically re-prioritizes jobs as schedules change. CR < 1 = behind schedule; CR = 1 = on schedule; CR > 1 = ahead of schedule.
Order Release and Control

Order release — the process of authorizing a manufacturing order to be issued to the shop floor. Should only happen when materials and capacity are confirmed available (gatekeeping).

  • Planned order — not yet released; exists only in the system
  • Released order (open order) — authorized for production; physical materials committed

Gatekeeping: reviewing and approving planned orders before release to prevent releasing orders that lack materials or capacity, which would inflate WIP and queues without producing output.

Order splitting: dividing a manufacturing order into two or more smaller lots to allow simultaneous processing at multiple machines or work centers, reducing total lead time.
Operation overlapping: sending the first completed units of a lot to the next operation before the entire lot is completed at the current operation; reduces total lead time.
Queue Management and Lead Time

Queue time is typically the largest component of manufacturing lead time. Reducing queue time is the most effective way to compress lead time.

Components of manufacturing lead time:

  • Setup time — time to prepare the machine/work center
  • Run time — time to actually process the job
  • Queue time — time waiting for the work center to become available (often 70–90% of total lead time)
  • Move time — time to transport between work centers
  • Wait time — time waiting after processing for the next operation to start
In most manufacturing environments, queue time accounts for 70–90% of total lead time. Reducing WIP at bottlenecks is the fastest path to lead time compression.
Production Reporting and Performance

PAC generates feedback data that flows back to planning systems:

  • Quantities completed and scrapped per operation
  • Actual vs. standard setup and run times
  • Actual completion dates vs. planned dates
  • Labor hours by work center and job

Key PAC performance metrics:

  • Schedule adherence — % of jobs completed on time vs. planned schedule
  • Queue levels — average WIP at each work center
  • Actual vs. planned input/output — monitored via I/O control reports
  • Throughput — units completed per time period
Shop Floor Scheduling Techniques
  • Forward scheduling — schedule from the order release date forward; determines the earliest completion date; tends to create WIP and queues
  • Backward scheduling — schedule backward from the required delivery date; determines the latest acceptable start date; preferred in MRP environments
  • Finite scheduling — schedules based on actual available capacity; prevents overloading work centers; more realistic but computationally intensive
  • Infinite scheduling — plans without considering capacity constraints; identifies load but requires manual adjustment; used in MRP

What are the 5 components of manufacturing lead time?
ANSWER
1) Setup time, 2) Run time, 3) Queue time (usually 70–90% of total), 4) Move time, 5) Wait time.

Why is queue time so important in lead time reduction?
ANSWER
Queue time typically accounts for 70–90% of total manufacturing lead time. Reducing queues (by controlling WIP input) is the most effective lever for compressing lead times.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Gatekeeping in order release?
ANSWER
Reviewing and approving planned orders before releasing them to the shop floor to ensure materials and capacity are confirmed available. Prevents releasing orders that would only inflate WIP and queues.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between Forward and Backward Scheduling?
ANSWER
Forward: schedule from order release date forward to find earliest completion. Backward: schedule backward from due date to find latest acceptable start. MRP uses backward scheduling.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between Finite and Infinite Scheduling?
ANSWER
Finite: schedules respecting actual capacity limits — no work center can be overloaded. Infinite: plans without capacity constraints — identifies load but requires manual adjustment. MRP uses infinite scheduling.
TERM / CONCEPT
What does the Dispatch List tell shop floor workers?
ANSWER
Which jobs to run at their work center, in what sequence, usually updated daily. Based on priority rules (CR, EDD, SPT, etc.) applied to the current queue of open orders.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Schedule Adherence?
ANSWER
A PAC performance metric measuring the percentage of jobs completed on time vs. the planned schedule. Higher adherence = better execution against plan.
TERM / CONCEPT
What feedback data does PAC provide to planning systems?
ANSWER
Quantities completed/scrapped per operation, actual vs. standard setup and run times, actual vs. planned completion dates, labor hours by work center and job.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Input-Output (I/O) Control in PAC?
ANSWER
A tool that monitors actual vs. planned input and output at each work center. If input consistently exceeds output, queue grows, lead times lengthen, and corrective action is needed.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a Released Order (Open Order)?
ANSWER
A manufacturing order that has been authorized and issued to the shop floor. Physical materials have been committed. Differs from a planned order, which exists only in the planning system.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Slack per Operation (S/O) priority rule?
ANSWER
A sequencing rule that calculates remaining slack divided by the number of remaining operations. Distributes slack evenly across operations — similar to Critical Ratio but accounts for number of steps remaining.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the relationship between WIP reduction and lead time?
ANSWER
Per Little’s Law: Lead time = Queue / Throughput rate. Reducing WIP (queue) directly reduces lead time. Increasing throughput also reduces lead time. Reducing input when bottlenecks are overloaded is key.
