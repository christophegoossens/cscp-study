Overview

Production Activity Control (PAC) — also called shop floor control — manages the execution of manufacturing plans at the work center level. It translates MRP-planned orders into actual work assignments, tracks progress, and reports on performance.

ASCM Definitions

📖 ASCM Definition — Production Activity Control (PAC):

A subset of manufacturing execution systems (MES) that manages the release, tracking, and completion of manufacturing work orders and schedules. PAC ensures that work orders are released to the shop floor in a controlled manner and that actual performance is tracked against planned performance.

📖 ASCM Definition — Dispatching:

The selecting and sequencing of available jobs to be run at individual work stations and the assignment of those jobs to workers. Also called shop floor dispatching.

📖 ASCM Definition — Work Order (Manufacturing Order):

An authorization to manufacture a particular item in a specified quantity. It communicates what to produce, how much, by when, and which routing to follow.

📖 ASCM Definition — Routing:

A document describing the sequence of operations to be performed in producing a manufactured item. A routing includes the operations, work centers, tooling, and standards (setup and run time) for each operation.

PAC Functions

  • Order release: authorize and issue manufacturing orders to the floor
  • Scheduling: determine when and where each operation will be performed
  • Dispatching: assign specific jobs to specific workers/machines
  • Progress tracking: monitor actual vs. planned progress
  • Exception management: identify and resolve delays or quality issues
  • Reporting: feed back actual performance data to MRP and capacity systems

Priority Dispatching Rules

💡 Key Point:

Dispatching rules determine the sequence in which jobs are processed at a work center. Common rules:
FIFO — First In, First Out (jobs processed in order of arrival)
SPT — Shortest Processing Time (minimizes average flow time)
LPT — Longest Processing Time (maximizes machine utilization)
EDD — Earliest Due Date (minimizes maximum lateness)
CR — Critical Ratio: (Due Date — Today) / Lead Time Remaining; CR < 1.0 = behind schedule

Input/Output Control

Input/output (I/O) control monitors the flow of work into and out of work centers to manage queue lengths and throughput rates. It compares planned vs. actual input and output to detect and correct bottleneck conditions and backlog build-up.

Lead Time Components

  • Queue time: Time waiting to be processed (often 80–90% of total lead time)
  • Setup time: Time to prepare the machine for a specific job
  • Run time: Actual processing time per unit × quantity
  • Wait time: Time after processing, waiting to move
  • Move time: Transport time between work centers

💡 Key Point:

Queue time typically dominates manufacturing lead time. Reducing queue length — through better scheduling, smaller batches, or bottleneck management — is the most effective way to compress lead times.

PROCESS
What are the core functions of Production Activity Control?
ANSWER
1) Order release; 2) Scheduling; 3) Dispatching; 4) Progress tracking; 5) Exception management; 6) Reporting actual performance back to planning systems.
CONCEPT
What is the Critical Ratio (CR) dispatching rule?
ANSWER
CR = (Due Date — Today) / Lead Time Remaining. CR < 1.0 means the job is behind schedule. CR = 1.0 means on-track. CR > 1.0 means ahead of schedule. Jobs with lower CR get priority.
CONCEPT
What is the Shortest Processing Time (SPT) rule and its advantage?
ANSWER
SPT processes the job with the shortest remaining time first. It minimizes average flow time and average job lateness across all jobs — best for throughput and average wait time.
CONCEPT
What is the Earliest Due Date (EDD) rule and when is it used?
ANSWER
EDD sequences jobs by their due dates, earliest first. It minimizes maximum lateness — useful when avoiding any job being very late is more important than average lateness.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between SPT and EDD dispatching?
ANSWER
SPT minimizes average flow time and waiting time (good for throughput). EDD minimizes maximum lateness (good for preventing any single job from being very late). Neither is universally superior.
CONCEPT
What is input/output (I/O) control?
ANSWER
A tool that monitors planned vs. actual work flow into and out of work centers. Detects queue build-up (input > output) or starved work centers (input < output), enabling corrective scheduling actions.
CONCEPT
What are the five components of manufacturing lead time?
ANSWER
1) Queue time — waiting to be processed; 2) Setup time — machine preparation; 3) Run time — actual processing; 4) Wait time — after processing, before moving; 5) Move time — transport between work centers.
KEY POINT
Which lead time component typically dominates and why?
ANSWER
Queue time typically accounts for 80–90% of total lead time. Reducing queues through smaller batches, better dispatching, or bottleneck management is the most effective way to cut lead times.
CONCEPT
What is a dispatch list?
ANSWER
A prioritized list of jobs waiting to be processed at a work center, showing each job's due date, priority, estimated time, and status. Updated frequently to reflect current conditions.
CONCEPT
What is the purpose of order release in PAC?
ANSWER
Order release authorizes jobs to be placed on the shop floor with all necessary materials and documentation. Controlled release prevents work-in-process (WIP) from exploding and keeps queue lengths manageable.
CONCEPT
What is overlap in production scheduling?
ANSWER
Starting processing at a downstream operation before the upstream operation is completely finished — passing a partial batch forward. Reduces total lead time at the cost of more complex coordination.
CONCEPT
What is lot splitting in production?
ANSWER
Dividing a production lot into two or more sublots that can be run simultaneously on parallel machines. Reduces lead time but increases setup costs and scheduling complexity.
CONCEPT
What does a Manufacturing Execution System (MES) do?
ANSWER
MES links enterprise planning systems (ERP/MRP) to the shop floor by managing work order release, real-time tracking, quality data collection, equipment status, and reporting. PAC is a core MES function.
CONCEPT
What is backflushing in shop floor control?
ANSWER
Automatically issuing (deducting) component inventory when a finished goods transaction is recorded, rather than issuing components at the start of each operation. Reduces paperwork but requires accurate BOMs.
CONCEPT
What is the relationship between PAC and MRP?
ANSWER
MRP generates planned orders based on demand and lead times. PAC executes those orders on the shop floor and reports actual completion data back to MRP to update inventory records and scheduled receipts.
CONCEPT
What is expediting in production control?
ANSWER
Actively prioritizing and accelerating specific jobs to meet urgent deadlines — often involves rerouting work, authorizing overtime, or displacing lower-priority jobs. Frequent expediting signals systemic planning problems.
