Overview

Production planning translates the Sales and Operations Plan (S&OP) into executable schedules. The Master Production Schedule (MPS) is the central tool connecting aggregate planning to detailed material and capacity planning.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Master Production Schedule (MPS): A plan for production of specific end items. It drives Material Requirements Planning (MRP) and Rough-Cut Capacity Planning (RCCP). States what will be produced, in what quantities, and by when.
📖 Definition:
Production plan: A plan for the production of product families, developed in the S&OP process. It establishes production rates and inventory levels at an aggregate level. The MPS disaggregates this plan to specific products.
📖 Definition:
Available-to-Promise (ATP): The uncommitted portion of a company's inventory and planned production, maintained in the MPS for customer order promising. Answers: 'Can we promise this order?'
📖 Definition:
Planning horizon: The time period covered by the plan. S&OP: 18-24 months. MPS: 3-18 months. MRP: 1-3 months. Shorter horizons are more precise; longer horizons are more aggregate.
📖 Definition:
Time fence: A policy point in the planning horizon. Demand Time Fence (DTF): no MPS changes without management approval within this window. Planning Time Fence (PTF): changes allowed but may require expediting. Beyond PTF: free planning zone.

MPS Development

💡 Key Point:
MPS is developed by disaggregating the S&OP production plan from product families to individual end items, then time-phasing production to specific weeks. The MPS must be feasible (capacity-checked via RCCP) and stable (frozen period prevents constant rescheduling).
  • The MPS should be realistically achievable — not an optimistic wish list that supply chain cannot execute
  • MPS stability is critical: constant changes ('nervousness') cause disruption throughout MRP and purchasing
  • Consumption logic: customer orders consume the forecast in the MPS to prevent double-planning
  • ATP = previous ATP + MPS production - booked customer orders in each time bucket
  • MPS drives both MRP (materials) and RCCP (capacity) — it is the central planning document

Planning Hierarchy

💡 Key Point:
Planning hierarchy: Business Plan → Sales and Operations Plan (S&OP) → Master Production Schedule (MPS) → Material Requirements Planning (MRP) → Production Activity Control / Purchasing. Each level disaggregates the next.

DEFINITION
What is the Master Production Schedule (MPS)?
ANSWER
A time-phased plan for producing specific end items — states what will be produced, in what quantities, and by when. Drives both MRP (materials) and Rough-Cut Capacity Planning (RCCP). The central production planning document.
DEFINITION
What is Available-to-Promise (ATP)?
ANSWER
The uncommitted portion of inventory and planned production maintained in the MPS for customer order promising. ATP = Previous ATP + MPS quantity − Booked customer orders. Answers: 'Can we promise this order?'
DEFINITION
What is a time fence in MPS planning?
ANSWER
A policy point in the planning horizon. Demand Time Fence (DTF): MPS changes within this window require management approval — protects firm production plans. Planning Time Fence (PTF): changes allowed but may require expediting. Beyond PTF: free planning.
CONCEPT
What is the planning hierarchy from top to bottom?
ANSWER
Business Plan → S&OP (aggregate, families) → MPS (specific end items, weeks) → MRP (components, materials) → Production Activity Control / Shop Floor. Each level disaggregates the level above it.
CONCEPT
What is MPS nervousness?
ANSWER
Frequent, disruptive changes to the MPS that cascade down to MRP — causing constant rescheduling of purchase orders and production orders. Prevented by time fences that stabilize the near-term MPS from routine changes.
CONCEPT
How does the MPS consume the forecast?
ANSWER
When a customer order arrives, it 'consumes' (replaces) the forecast in the same time bucket by the same quantity. The MPS uses the larger of actual orders vs. remaining forecast — preventing double-planning of the same demand.
DEFINITION
What is a production plan?
ANSWER
An aggregate plan developed in S&OP for product families — establishes production rates, inventory levels, and workforce levels. The MPS disaggregates this into specific end-item schedules by week.
DEFINITION
What is Rough-Cut Capacity Planning (RCCP)?
ANSWER
A high-level capacity feasibility check that validates whether the MPS can be executed with available key resources (bottleneck work centers, critical equipment). Done before committing the MPS to prevent infeasible plans.
CONCEPT
What is a planning horizon and how does it vary by planning level?
ANSWER
The time period covered by the plan. S&OP: 18-24 months. MPS: 3-18 months. MRP: typically 1-3 months. Shorter horizons are more precise; longer horizons are more uncertain and aggregated.
CONCEPT
What is the frozen zone in MPS planning?
ANSWER
The period within the Demand Time Fence where the MPS is 'frozen' — no changes allowed without management approval. Typically equal to cumulative lead time (longest supply lead time). Prevents disruption to firm production commitments.
CONCEPT
What is cumulative lead time and why does it determine the planning horizon?
ANSWER
Cumulative lead time = the total time needed from ordering the first raw material to producing the finished product. The MPS must extend at least this far into the future so that material orders can be placed in time.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between the MPS and the production plan?
ANSWER
Production plan: aggregate plan at product family level from S&OP (e.g., 500 units of Family A per month). MPS: specific plan at end-item level by week (e.g., 150 units of Model A1, 200 of A2, 150 of A3 in Week 1).
CONCEPT
What drives the MPS — demand or forecast?
ANSWER
Both — in the near term (within DTF), firm customer orders drive the MPS. In the medium term (between DTF and PTF), a mix of orders and forecast drives it. Beyond PTF, the forecast (from S&OP) drives the MPS.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is make-to-stock MPS vs. make-to-order MPS?
ANSWER
Make-to-stock: MPS produces to forecast before orders arrive — finished goods inventory buffer. Make-to-order: MPS triggered by customer orders — no finished goods inventory, longer customer lead time.
CONCEPT
What is the relationship between MPS and ATP?
ANSWER
The MPS generates planned production quantities. ATP tracks how much of that production is still available (uncommitted). Each time a customer order is booked, it reduces ATP for that period. ATP prevents over-promising.
