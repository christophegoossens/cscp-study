Overview

MRP calculates time-phased component requirements using MPS, BOM, and inventory. It plans dependent demand — quantities are calculated, not forecast.

  • MRP time fences: each component has its own; planning fence = material ordered; demand fence = material received
Time fences in MRP help manage the end item’s cumulative lead time because the operation will have its requisite materials available when they are needed. Cumulative lead time is defined by the ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary as

MRP plans dependent demand items—the raw materials and components needed to produce finished goods for consumers. The ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary defines dependent demand as
The ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary defines independent demand as
  • Dependent demand: derived from BOM; calculated, never forecast
  • Independent demand: end-item/service part demand; subject to forecasting
  • Same item can have both (e.g., spare part sold independently AND used in production)
  • BOM = complete component list + quantities for one end item ("product tree")
  • Multilevel BOM: shows all sub-components down to raw materials
  • Modular (planning) BOM: used for mass customization and configure-to-order
  • Planned order: system-generated; auto-adjustable; explodes to component gross requirements at next BOM level
  • Firm planned order (FPO): frozen in quantity and time by planner; not auto-changed by system
  • Routing file: maps component journey work-center to work-center; lists each operation and time required
  • Lot-for-lot: exact quantity needed, no buffer; default for dependent demand
  • FOQ (fixed order quantity): required when batch sizes are fixed (e.g., brewing vat)
  • Offsetting: count backward from due date by lead time to calculate release dates for each component
With the information from the master production schedule and bill of material and on inventory status and planning factors, it is possible to develop a complete schedule for ordering (and delivering) all the components and materials necessary to create the items in the master schedule. The MRP process results in planned orders for making or buying all the components required. It typically includes the following specifications as defined in the ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary:
  • Net requirements = gross requirements − on-hand inventory − scheduled receipts
  • System nervousness: excessive frequent MRP changes; costly and demoralizing; mitigated by time fences
Another way to mitigate excessive nervousness is pegging each component to its parent in the bill of material. The ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary defines pegging as follows:
  • Pegging links each component requirement to its parent; enables rapid impact analysis on schedule changes
  • Reconciling MRP + JIT: small/bucketless buckets speed flow; balanced flow (MRP plans, JIT pulls via kanban)
  • Backflushing: automatically deducts components from inventory on unit completion using BOM
Setting aside that problem, MRP systems provide very important benefits:
  • Closed-loop MRP: adds capacity feedback; reroutes or reschedules orders when work center capacity exceeded
Manufacturing resource planning (MRP II) is defined by the ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary as
  • MRP II adds: financials, S&OP integration, capacity visibility, translates activities to financial statements
  • ERP: extends MRP II across all enterprise functions and supply chain partners
