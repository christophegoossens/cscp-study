Overview

Materials planning determines what components, subassemblies, and raw materials are needed to execute the Master Production Schedule (MPS). The Bill of Materials (BOM) is the foundational structure that enables this calculation.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Bill of Materials (BOM): A list of components, ingredients, and materials needed to produce an item as well as the quantities of each needed to make one unit of the item. Also called a product structure or product tree.
📖 Definition:
Material Requirements Planning (MRP): A set of techniques using the BOM, inventory data, and MPS to calculate material requirements. Generates planned orders to replenish components and materials.
📖 Definition:
Indented BOM: A BOM displayed in hierarchical format showing all levels of components under the parent item. Each subassembly is indented beneath its parent to show the parent-child relationship.
📖 Definition:
Phantom BOM (phantom item): A BOM code indicating that the item does not physically exist as a stocked item — it is assembled and consumed immediately in the next level of production. Common for subassemblies produced on the same work order as the parent.
📖 Definition:
Engineering Change Order (ECO): A formal document authorizing a change to the product design — updating drawings, BOM, routings, and specifications. ECOs must be managed carefully to avoid planning disruptions.

BOM Structure

💡 Key Point:
BOM levels: Level 0 = finished product (end item). Level 1 = major subassemblies or components. Level 2 = components within the Level 1 subassembly. Each level down is more detailed.
  • Single-level BOM: shows only the immediate components of the parent — does not show sub-components
  • Multi-level BOM: shows all components at all levels of the product structure
  • BOM accuracy is critical — a 1% BOM error compounds across all production orders using that component
  • Effective date: the date from which a BOM change is active — planned orders after this date use the new BOM
  • Parent-child relationship: the parent requires the child components to be produced/assembled

MRP Calculation

💡 Key Point:
MRP Net Requirement = Gross Requirement − Projected On-Hand. When net requirement > 0, a planned order is generated. Planned order release date = net requirement due date − component lead time.
  • Lot-for-lot: order exactly the net requirement — no excess, but may be costly if orders are frequent
  • Fixed order quantity: always order a set quantity regardless of net requirement
  • EOQ: order the Economic Order Quantity — balances ordering cost and holding cost
  • Period order quantity: consolidate requirements over a set number of periods into one order

DEFINITION
What is a Bill of Materials (BOM)?
ANSWER
A list of all components, subassemblies, and raw materials needed to produce one unit of a finished product — including the required quantities of each and the hierarchical parent-child relationships between items.
CONCEPT
What are the BOM levels and what does each represent?
ANSWER
Level 0: finished product (the end item). Level 1: major subassemblies or top-level components. Level 2: components within Level 1 subassemblies. Each lower level is more detailed — raw materials are at the lowest level.
DEFINITION
What is an indented BOM?
ANSWER
A BOM displayed in hierarchical, indented format showing all levels — parent items appear at the left margin, child components are indented beneath their parent, sub-components are further indented. Shows the complete product tree structure.
DEFINITION
What is a phantom BOM (phantom item)?
ANSWER
A BOM code for an item that does not physically exist as a stocked item — it is assembled and immediately consumed in the next production step on the same work order. Simplifies planning by not creating a separate inventory location or pick operation.
DEFINITION
What is an Engineering Change Order (ECO)?
ANSWER
A formal document authorizing a change to the product design — updating drawings, BOMs, routings, and specifications. Must be managed with effective dates to ensure correct BOM versions are used in production and planning.
FORMULA
What is MRP net requirements calculation?
ANSWER
Net Requirement = Gross Requirement − Projected On-Hand (on-hand inventory + scheduled receipts − previous allocations). When net requirement > 0, MRP generates a planned order for the deficient quantity.
DEFINITION
What is lot-for-lot (L4L) lot sizing in MRP?
ANSWER
An MRP lot sizing method that orders exactly the net requirement for each period — no more, no less. Minimizes carrying cost but may result in frequent, small orders with high ordering cost. Best for expensive or perishable items.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between a single-level BOM and a multi-level BOM?
ANSWER
Single-level: shows only the immediate children of the parent (one level down). Multi-level: shows all components at all levels of the product structure — the complete product tree from finished good to raw materials.
CONCEPT
What is BOM accuracy and why is it critical?
ANSWER
BOM accuracy = percentage of BOMs that correctly reflect actual product structure. Even 1% BOM errors cascade into planning errors — wrong quantities ordered, production shortages, quality escapes. Industry standard target: 99%+ accuracy.
DEFINITION
What is an effective date in BOM management?
ANSWER
The date from which a BOM change is active. Planned orders after the effective date use the new BOM version; prior orders use the old version. Critical for managing Engineering Change Orders (ECOs) without disrupting in-process work.
FORMULA
What is the Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) lot sizing method?
ANSWER
EOQ orders the quantity that minimizes total inventory cost (ordering cost + holding cost). Formula: EOQ = √(2 × Annual Demand × Ordering Cost / Holding Cost per unit). Balances order frequency against carrying cost.
CONCEPT
What does MRP do with a planned order?
ANSWER
MRP generates a planned order when a net requirement exists. The planner reviews and 'firms' or releases it: for purchased items → becomes a purchase order to supplier; for manufactured items → becomes a production/work order to the shop floor.
DEFINITION
What is a period order quantity (POQ) lot sizing method?
ANSWER
Consolidates net requirements from multiple MRP periods into a single order. Example: POQ = 4 weeks means order enough to cover the next 4 weeks of net requirements in one order. Reduces order frequency while covering near-term needs.
CONCEPT
What is a common part and why is it beneficial in BOM design?
ANSWER
A component used in multiple parent items (shared across products). Common parts allow inventory pooling — safety stock can be shared, volume is consolidated for pricing, and fewer unique items need to be managed.
CONCEPT
What is a where-used report in BOM analysis?
ANSWER
A report showing all parent items that use a specific component. Used when a component change occurs — allows planners to identify all products affected by the change and assess the impact on production and inventory.
