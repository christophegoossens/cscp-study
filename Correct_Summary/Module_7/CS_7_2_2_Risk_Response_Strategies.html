Overview

Risk response strategies determine how the organization deals with identified, prioritized risks. The four fundamental strategies are avoidance, mitigation, transfer, and acceptance — each appropriate for different risk profiles.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Risk avoidance: Eliminating a supply chain risk by changing strategy — choosing not to source from a high-risk region, not using a single-source supplier, or discontinuing a high-risk product line.
📖 Definition:
Risk mitigation: Taking actions to reduce the likelihood or impact of a risk — dual sourcing, safety stock, supplier development, geographic diversification. Does not eliminate risk but reduces its probability or consequences.
📖 Definition:
Risk transfer: Shifting the financial consequences of a risk to another party — through insurance, supply contracts with penalty clauses, or hedging instruments. The risk still occurs, but someone else absorbs the financial loss.
📖 Definition:
Risk acceptance: Consciously deciding to accept a risk without active mitigation — appropriate for low-likelihood/low-impact risks where mitigation cost exceeds expected value of protection. Must be a documented, explicit decision.

Mitigation Strategies by Risk Type

💡 Key Point:
Supply risk mitigation: dual/multiple sourcing, safety stock, long-term contracts, supplier financial monitoring, geographic diversification, inventory pre-building before expected disruptions.
💡 Key Point:
Demand risk mitigation: customer diversification, demand sensing improvement, flexible production capacity, price/promotion strategies to manage demand volatility.
💡 Key Point:
Process risk mitigation: equipment redundancy, cross-training, preventive maintenance, quality management systems, business continuity plans.
💡 Key Point:
Environmental/geopolitical risk mitigation: geographic supply diversification, nearshoring, multi-country sourcing strategies, scenario planning and BCP.

Resilience Building

📖 Definition:
Supply chain resilience: The ability to anticipate, prepare for, respond to, and adapt from known and unknown changes in the supply chain environment — returning to the desired state with minimal disruption.
  • Redundancy: duplicate sources, facilities, or inventory to ensure backup if primary fails
  • Flexibility: capability to switch suppliers, products, or routes quickly when conditions change
  • Visibility: real-time awareness of supply chain status enabling early detection and rapid response
  • Collaboration: sharing risk information with trading partners to enable coordinated response
  • Agility: speed of response to disruption — compressed recovery time reduces total impact

CONCEPT
What are the four supply chain risk response strategies?
ANSWER
1) Avoidance (eliminate the risk by changing strategy), 2) Mitigation (reduce likelihood or impact), 3) Transfer (shift financial consequences to others via insurance/contracts), 4) Acceptance (consciously accept risk where mitigation cost exceeds benefit).
DEFINITION
What is risk avoidance?
ANSWER
Eliminating a risk by changing strategy — not sourcing from a high-risk region, moving from single-source to dual-source, or discontinuing a high-risk product. Removes the risk entirely but may sacrifice other benefits (cost, performance).
DEFINITION
What is risk mitigation?
ANSWER
Taking actions to reduce the likelihood or impact of a risk without eliminating it — dual sourcing, safety stock, geographic diversification, supplier development, business continuity planning. Lowers but does not eliminate exposure.
DEFINITION
What is risk transfer?
ANSWER
Shifting the financial consequences of a risk event to another party — insurance (covers losses), contracts with penalty clauses (supplier pays for late delivery), or financial hedging (protect against commodity price or currency changes).
DEFINITION
What is risk acceptance?
ANSWER
A documented, deliberate decision to accept a risk without active mitigation — appropriate for low-likelihood/low-impact risks where mitigation cost exceeds expected value. Must be explicit and reviewed periodically.
CONCEPT
What are the primary strategies for mitigating supply risk?
ANSWER
Dual/multiple sourcing, strategic safety stock, long-term supplier contracts, supplier financial health monitoring, geographic supply diversification, and inventory pre-building before anticipated disruptions.
DEFINITION
What is supply chain resilience?
ANSWER
The ability to anticipate, prepare for, respond to, and recover from supply chain disruptions — returning to normal operations with minimal impact. Built through redundancy, flexibility, visibility, collaboration, and organizational agility.
DEFINITION
What is redundancy in risk management?
ANSWER
Maintaining duplicate resources, sources, or capacity that can be activated when the primary option fails — dual suppliers, backup production sites, safety stock, alternative transportation modes. Costs more normally but enables rapid recovery.
DEFINITION
What is supply chain flexibility in risk management?
ANSWER
The ability to rapidly shift to alternative suppliers, production locations, transportation modes, or product configurations when the normal plan is disrupted. Flexibility reduces the impact of disruptions by providing alternative paths.
DEFINITION
What is supply chain agility in risk management?
ANSWER
The speed at which a supply chain can respond to a disruption and restore normal operations. High agility = shorter time to detect, decide, and respond. Reduces the total duration and cost of supply chain disruptions.
CONCEPT
How does supply chain visibility contribute to risk management?
ANSWER
Real-time visibility enables early detection of developing problems (supplier delivery delays, inventory shortfalls) — allowing response before the problem reaches the customer. Shorter detection-to-response time reduces total disruption impact.
DEFINITION
What is a Business Continuity Plan (BCP) and when is it activated?
ANSWER
A documented plan for maintaining critical operations when normal operations are disrupted. Activated when a risk event (natural disaster, major supplier failure, cyberattack) exceeds normal operational recovery capability. Should be tested and updated regularly.
CONCEPT
What is nearshoring as a risk mitigation strategy?
ANSWER
Moving sourcing or production closer to the consuming market — reduces supply chain length, transit time, and geopolitical risk exposure. Typically trades lower offshore labor cost for shorter lead time, less inventory, and reduced disruption exposure.
CONCEPT
What is geographic diversification in supply risk mitigation?
ANSWER
Sourcing the same material or component from suppliers in different geographic regions — so a regional event (earthquake, flood, geopolitical disruption) affects only part of supply rather than all of it.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between a reactive and proactive supply chain risk management approach?
ANSWER
Reactive: responding to disruptions after they occur — firefighting mode. Proactive: identifying, assessing, and mitigating risks before they occur — investment in prevention and resilience. Proactive SCRM is less costly per disruption event on average.

B.Reduces single-source dependency by qualifying a second supplier, enabling continuity if one fails
