Overview

Supply chain risk management (SCRM) is the systematic process of identifying, assessing, mitigating, and monitoring risks that could disrupt supply chain operations. A structured framework enables proactive risk governance.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Supply chain risk: The potential for an event or condition to adversely affect a supply chain's ability to deliver products or services as planned — affecting cost, quality, delivery, or availability.
📖 Definition:
Supply chain risk management (SCRM): The systematic identification, assessment, and mitigation of supply chain risks to ensure supply continuity and protect business performance.
📖 Definition:
Risk appetite: The level of risk an organization is willing to accept in pursuit of its objectives. Organizations with low risk appetite invest more in mitigation; those with higher appetite accept more vulnerability in exchange for cost savings.
📖 Definition:
Business Continuity Plan (BCP): A documented plan for maintaining or restoring critical business operations when normal operations are disrupted — covering people, processes, technology, and facilities.

The SCRM Framework

💡 Key Point:
The four-step SCRM process: 1) IDENTIFY risks (all potential disruption sources), 2) ANALYZE risks (likelihood × impact = risk priority), 3) RESPOND to risks (mitigation strategies), 4) MONITOR risks (ongoing tracking and updating).

Risk Categories

💡 Key Point:
Supply chain risks span six categories: 1) Supply risks (supplier failure, quality issues, commodity shortages), 2) Demand risks (demand volatility, customer concentration), 3) Process risks (manufacturing failures, quality escapes), 4) Environmental risks (natural disasters, pandemics), 5) Geopolitical risks (trade wars, sanctions, political instability), 6) Information/cyber risks (system failures, cyberattacks).
  • Supply risk: supplier insolvency, single-source dependency, geographic concentration, raw material scarcity
  • Demand risk: demand volatility, customer concentration, product obsolescence
  • Environmental risk: natural disasters (earthquakes, floods, storms), pandemics, climate change
  • Geopolitical risk: trade restrictions, sanctions, tariff changes, political instability
  • Cybersecurity risk: supply chain cyberattacks, data breaches, ransomware affecting operations
📖 Definition:
Risk register: A documented inventory of identified risks, including description, likelihood, impact, risk owner, and mitigation actions. The primary tool for tracking and managing supply chain risks.

DEFINITION
What is supply chain risk management (SCRM)?
ANSWER
The systematic identification, assessment, and mitigation of risks that could disrupt supply chain operations — protecting supply continuity, cost, quality, and delivery performance.
CONCEPT
What are the four steps of the SCRM framework?
ANSWER
1) Identify risks (catalog all potential disruptions), 2) Analyze risks (assess likelihood and impact), 3) Respond to risks (develop mitigation strategies), 4) Monitor risks (ongoing tracking, updating, and early warning).
DEFINITION
What is supply chain risk?
ANSWER
The potential for an event or condition to adversely affect the supply chain's ability to deliver products or services as planned — impacting cost, quality, delivery timing, or availability.
DEFINITION
What is risk appetite?
ANSWER
The level of risk an organization is willing to accept in pursuit of its objectives. Low risk appetite → invest heavily in mitigation and redundancy. High risk appetite → accept more vulnerability for lower cost. Should be explicit and board-approved.
DEFINITION
What is a Business Continuity Plan (BCP)?
ANSWER
A documented plan for maintaining or restoring critical business operations when normal operations are disrupted — covering how to maintain people, processes, technology, and facilities through a disruption.
CONCEPT
What are the six supply chain risk categories?
ANSWER
1) Supply risks (supplier failures, shortages), 2) Demand risks (volatility, concentration), 3) Process risks (manufacturing failures), 4) Environmental risks (disasters, pandemics), 5) Geopolitical risks (trade wars, sanctions), 6) Information/cyber risks (attacks, system failures).
DEFINITION
What is a risk register?
ANSWER
A documented inventory of identified supply chain risks — including description, likelihood rating, impact rating, risk priority score, risk owner, and planned mitigation actions. The core tool for tracking and managing supply chain risks.
CONCEPT
What is supply risk from single-source dependency?
ANSWER
Relying on a single supplier for a critical material or component — if that supplier is disrupted (fire, insolvency, quality failure), there is no alternative and supply stops. Mitigated by dual sourcing or safety stock.
CONCEPT
What is geographic concentration risk?
ANSWER
Sourcing critical materials or components from a single geographic region — exposed to regional disruptions (natural disasters, geopolitical events, infrastructure failures). Mitigated by geographic diversification of the supply base.
CONCEPT
What is demand concentration risk?
ANSWER
When a high percentage of revenue comes from a small number of customers — if a key customer is lost or cuts orders significantly, it creates a major revenue and operational shock. Managed by customer diversification.
CONCEPT
What is a cyberattack risk to supply chains?
ANSWER
Ransomware, data breaches, or denial-of-service attacks on supply chain IT systems (ERP, WMS, TMS) can halt operations completely. Supply chain cyberattacks on software vendors (SolarWinds 2020) can compromise many companies simultaneously.
CONCEPT
What is a Risk Priority Number (RPN) and how is it used?
ANSWER
RPN = Likelihood × Impact (and sometimes Detectability). Used to prioritize risks — highest RPN risks receive the most mitigation attention. Similar to the FMEA methodology used in product quality management.
DEFINITION
What is resilience in supply chain risk management?
ANSWER
The ability of the supply chain to anticipate, absorb, adapt to, and recover from disruptions — returning to normal operations quickly. Resilience is built through redundancy, flexibility, visibility, and collaborative relationships.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between risk mitigation and risk avoidance?
ANSWER
Risk mitigation: taking actions to reduce the likelihood or impact of a risk while still accepting some exposure (e.g., dual sourcing reduces but doesn't eliminate supply risk). Risk avoidance: eliminating the risk entirely by changing strategy (e.g., not sourcing from a high-risk region).
CONCEPT
What is supply chain disruption and how does frequency/severity matter?
ANSWER
A disruption is any event that interrupts normal supply chain flow. Frequency and severity determine the management approach: low frequency/high severity (earthquakes) → resilience planning. High frequency/low severity (late deliveries) → process improvement.
