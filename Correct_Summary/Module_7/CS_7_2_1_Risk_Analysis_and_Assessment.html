Overview

Risk analysis evaluates the likelihood and potential impact of identified supply chain risks — enabling prioritization of mitigation efforts where they are most needed. Quantitative and qualitative methods both play important roles.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Risk assessment: The process of evaluating identified risks by analyzing their likelihood of occurrence and potential impact on supply chain performance — to prioritize mitigation actions.
📖 Definition:
Risk matrix: A grid plotting risks by likelihood (probability of occurrence) and impact (severity of consequences). Risks in the high-likelihood/high-impact quadrant are the highest priority.
📖 Definition:
Likelihood (probability): The estimated probability that a specific risk event will occur within a defined time period. Often rated on a 1-5 or 1-10 scale from 'rare' to 'almost certain.'
📖 Definition:
Impact (severity): The estimated magnitude of consequences if a risk event occurs — on supply chain cost, service, quality, or safety. Often rated on a 1-5 scale from 'negligible' to 'catastrophic.'

Risk Quantification

💡 Key Point:
Risk Priority Score = Likelihood × Impact. The higher the score, the higher the priority for mitigation investment. Some frameworks add 'Detectability' (how quickly you would know a disruption occurred) as a third factor — similar to FMEA's RPN approach.
  • Qualitative assessment: expert judgment and consensus scoring (1-5 scales) — fast and flexible, suitable for most supply chain risks
  • Quantitative assessment: statistical modeling, simulation, financial modeling — more rigorous, used for high-stakes decisions
  • Value at Risk (VaR): a statistical measure of the potential financial loss from supply chain disruptions
  • Monte Carlo simulation: modeling thousands of possible scenarios to estimate the distribution of supply chain outcomes

Financial Impact Analysis

💡 Key Point:
Risk financial impact includes: direct costs (lost revenue, emergency sourcing, premium freight, inventory write-offs) and indirect costs (customer service penalties, lost market share, brand damage, regulatory fines). Total impact is often 5-10x the direct cost.
  • Supply chain disruption cost modeling: quantifying the expected annual loss = likelihood × impact in dollars
  • Cost-benefit analysis: comparing the cost of mitigation to the expected value of risk reduction
  • Insurance: transferring financial risk — business interruption insurance, trade credit insurance, cargo insurance
  • Risk tolerance: defining thresholds — which risks are acceptable and which require mandatory mitigation

DEFINITION
What is risk assessment in supply chain?
ANSWER
The process of evaluating identified risks by analyzing their likelihood of occurrence and potential impact on supply chain performance — to prioritize mitigation investments where they are most needed.
DEFINITION
What is a risk matrix?
ANSWER
A grid plotting risks by likelihood (vertical axis) and impact (horizontal axis). Risks in the high-likelihood/high-impact quadrant require immediate mitigation priority. Visual tool for communicating and prioritizing the risk portfolio.
FORMULA
How is Risk Priority Score calculated?
ANSWER
Risk Priority Score = Likelihood × Impact. Both are rated on defined scales (e.g., 1-5). A risk rated Likelihood=4, Impact=5 has a score of 20 — higher priority than Likelihood=2, Impact=3 (score=6). Used to rank risks for mitigation attention.
DEFINITION
What is qualitative risk assessment?
ANSWER
Evaluating risks using expert judgment and consensus scoring — rating likelihood and impact on defined scales (1-5) without detailed statistical analysis. Fast, flexible, and practical for most supply chain risk assessments.
DEFINITION
What is quantitative risk assessment?
ANSWER
Using statistical models, simulation, or financial modeling to calculate probability distributions of risk outcomes. More rigorous and data-intensive than qualitative assessment — used for high-stakes financial or strategic decisions.
CONCEPT
What is Monte Carlo simulation in supply chain risk?
ANSWER
A computerized method that runs thousands of iterations of a supply chain model with random inputs (based on probability distributions) to estimate the range of possible outcomes — e.g., what's the 95th percentile annual supply chain disruption cost?
DEFINITION
What is Value at Risk (VaR) in supply chain context?
ANSWER
A statistical measure of the maximum potential financial loss from supply chain disruptions at a given confidence level (e.g., 95%). 'There is a 95% chance that our annual supply chain disruption cost will not exceed $X million.'
CONCEPT
What are direct vs. indirect costs of supply chain disruption?
ANSWER
Direct: lost revenue, emergency sourcing premium, premium freight, inventory write-offs. Indirect: customer service penalties, market share loss to competitors, brand damage, regulatory fines. Total impact is often 5-10x the direct cost.
DEFINITION
What is business interruption insurance?
ANSWER
Insurance that covers lost revenue and additional costs (extra expenses to maintain operations) when normal business is disrupted by a covered event (fire, flood, equipment breakdown). A risk transfer mechanism for high-impact, low-frequency events.
CONCEPT
What is a cost-benefit analysis of risk mitigation?
ANSWER
Comparing the expected cost of a mitigation action to the expected value of risk reduction it provides. If the mitigation cost exceeds the risk reduction value (likelihood × impact in dollars), the investment may not be justified.
DEFINITION
What is risk tolerance in supply chain?
ANSWER
The defined boundaries of acceptable risk exposure — which risks require mandatory mitigation action and which are acceptable within the organization's risk appetite. Should be explicitly defined and approved by leadership.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between risk likelihood and risk detectability?
ANSWER
Likelihood: probability the risk event occurs. Detectability: how quickly the organization would know if the risk materializes. Low detectability means a problem may grow large before being caught — justifies adding monitoring/early warning systems.
CONCEPT
What is supply chain financial risk modeling?
ANSWER
Quantifying the expected annual cost of supply chain disruptions: Expected Annual Loss = Probability of Event × Impact in dollars. Used to build a business case for risk mitigation investments — compare to cost of mitigation.
CONCEPT
What is scenario-based risk assessment?
ANSWER
Developing detailed descriptions of specific risk scenarios (e.g., 'key supplier fails suddenly') and assessing their timeline of impact, recovery options, and total cost. More realistic than abstract likelihood × impact scoring for complex, systemic risks.
CONCEPT
What is the criticality of a supply chain node?
ANSWER
How important a specific supply chain node (supplier, DC, manufacturing plant) is to the overall system. Criticality = uniqueness (no alternatives) × volume dependency. High-criticality nodes require the most intensive risk mitigation.
