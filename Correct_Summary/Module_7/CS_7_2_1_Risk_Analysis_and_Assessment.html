Overview

Risk analysis starts with qualitative classification and prioritization, then optional quantitative assessment for significant risks.

  • Qualitative = non-mathematical; factors: probability, impact, urgency, data quality, risk tolerance
  • Output = risk rating used to rank risks and trigger appropriate response levels
  • GARP operational categories: personnel, physical assets, technology, relationships, external/regulatory
  • SCRM categories: strategic, supply, demand, process, environmental, hazard, financial, malfeasance, litigation
  • Supply + demand + process + environmental = operational risks (core supply chain risks)
  • Demand risk: SKU-level forecast error = 40%+; aggregated product line error ≈ 10%
  • Financial distress signals: late payments, dropping quality, unusual executive turnover, restated financials
  • Probability × impact = risk rating; 5×5 matrix categorizes risks as low/medium/high
  • Risk urgency assessment: high urgency may elevate priority regardless of probability/impact score
  • Poor data quality = inaccurate risk ratings = poor decisions; flag low-quality data risks for later review
  • EMV = probability × monetary impact; sets the spending ceiling for each risk response
  • Decision tree: compares EMVs of alternatives (e.g., insure vs. not insure)
  • Sensitivity analysis: changes one variable at a time; simulation: changes multiple variables simultaneously
  • Only apply quantitative analysis to risks with sufficient data quality
  • Three-point estimate: (optimistic + most likely + pessimistic) / 3; PERT weights most likely 4×, divides by 6

A common type of simulation is a Monte Carlo simulation. The ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary defines a Monte Carlo simulation as “a subset of digital simulation models based on random or stochastic processes.” Monte Carlo simulations use specialized tools or spreadsheet add-ons to allow developers to enter a range for each input variable rather than just one value. Then the simulation is run thousands of times using different random values from each input range at each pass. The result is an averaged set of results along with statistics such as probability distributions.

Question 2
Monte Carlo simulation:
A.Is a card game
B.Runs thousands of simulations with random inputs to model probability distributions of outcomes
C.Is the same as a decision tree
D.Uses only deterministic inputs
Monte Carlo simulation randomly samples from probability distributions across many iterations, producing a distribution of possible outcomes.
Question 3
FMEA (Failure Mode and Effects Analysis) assesses:
A.Market demand probability
B.Potential failure modes of processes, their causes, and effects on the supply chain
C.Only equipment failures
D.Customer credit risk
FMEA identifies failure modes, rates each on Severity x Occurrence x Detection (Risk Priority Number), and prioritizes improvement actions.
Question 4
First priority in risk management should go to risks with:
A.Low probability and low impact
B.High probability and high impact
C.High probability and low impact
D.Low probability and high impact
High probability + high impact risks represent the greatest threat and must be addressed with immediate mitigation strategies.
