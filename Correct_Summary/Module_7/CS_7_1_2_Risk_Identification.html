Risk Identification Tools

Risk identification must be comprehensive but focused on critical risks. It is an iterative process — new rounds of identification are needed as circumstances change.

  • Documentation & assumptions reviews: Pre-configured checklists, dashboards, and industry-standard risk software
  • Brainstorming: Group meeting with diverse experts; facilitator collects ideas without judgment
  • Delphi technique: Anonymous questionnaires collected and compiled in multiple rounds until consensus is reached
  • Interviews: Formal or informal discussions with experts and stakeholders
  • Root cause analysis: Searches for underlying causes rather than symptoms
  • Risk checklists: Lists of risks from past analyses at the organization or industry peers
  • Risk diagramming: Flowcharts/relationship diagrams reveal process weaknesses
  • SWOT analysis: Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats — maps internal capabilities to external environment
Risk Identification & Documentation

Each risk should be documented with:

  • Cause(s): Root causes, if known
  • Event(s): Triggering events, if known
  • Impact: Threats or opportunities for each critical asset or process; includes criticality assessment
  • Effect: Areas impacted within the organization or up/down the supply chain
Combine all four into a definitive risk statement. Example: “Derailment of multiple cars due to sabotage or disrepair of train tracks resulting in destruction of products onboard, possible injuries, and delays.”

The scope and time frame of each risk must be well understood before selecting an appropriate response.

Risk Register
Risk register: A useful summary report on qualitative risk analysis, quantitative risk analysis, and risk response planning. Contains all identified risks and associated details such as status, owners, priorities, and responses.
  • Typically a spreadsheet: one row per risk, one column per attribute
  • Attributes can be added as needed over time
  • Updated regularly at risk review meetings

Supply Chain Risks: Tangible & Intangible

Supply chain risks include loss of tangible assets (property, inventory, money) and intangible assets (intellectual property, reputation).

Loss of Tangible Assets

  • Loss of goods: Theft, damage, vandalism during shipment; Incoterms 2020 (FOB) determine who bears risk of loss during transfer
  • Malfeasance — Fraud & corruption: Attempts to make unfair profit (e.g., avoiding inspection fees, falsifying quality tests)
  • Malfeasance — Bribery: Gift/money given to influence a decision. Three types: commercial bribery, bribery of public officials, bribery of foreign representatives. U.S. Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (1977) addresses the latter.
  • Malfeasance — Abduction: ~$5.1B in ransoms collected annually; primarily targets citizens of the country where it occurs
  • Lawsuits: Organizations must protect against potential lawsuit losses

Loss of Intangible Assets

  • Intellectual property (IP): Brands, engineering designs, software code, trade secrets
  • Working with global suppliers creates IP risk — if they already perform the manufacturing, they can set up as a competitor
  • Countries with developed IP enforcement: File patents quickly; report violations to DHS’s National IP Rights Coordination Center (IPR Center)
  • Countries with higher IP risk: China, India (AMR Research) — more permissive cultures; China has demanded technology transfers
  • Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT): allows international patent filing in multiple countries simultaneously
  • Get in-country legal representation; review patent, trademark, copyright, and product liability laws per country

TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Delphi technique for risk identification?
ANSWER
Anonymous questionnaires collected and compiled in multiple rounds until the group reaches consensus. Reduces groupthink and bias from dominant personalities.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is root cause analysis in risk identification?
ANSWER
Searching for the underlying causes of a risk rather than focusing on symptoms, allowing more targeted and effective risk responses.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 4 components of a risk description/statement?
ANSWER
1. Cause(s) — root causes, 2. Event(s) — triggering events, 3. Impact — threats or opportunities for each critical asset/process, 4. Effect — areas impacted within the org or up/down the supply chain.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a risk register (ASCM definition)?
ANSWER
A useful summary report on qualitative risk analysis, quantitative risk analysis, and risk response planning. Contains all identified risks and associated details such as status, owners, priorities, and responses.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the two broad categories of supply chain risk?
ANSWER
Tangible assets: property, inventory, money. Intangible assets: intellectual property, reputation. Both can be lost through various supply chain risk events.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the three types of criminal bribery?
ANSWER
1. Commercial bribery (covering up inferior product or obtaining business/info), 2. Bribery of public officials (any attempt to influence a public official for private gain), 3. Bribery of foreign representatives (addressed by the U.S. Foreign Corrupt Practices Act, 1977).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act?
ANSWER
A 1977 U.S. law implemented to discourage bribing of foreign officials in order to secure favorable business contracts. One of three types of criminal bribery addressed in risk management.
TERM / CONCEPT
What IP risks arise from working with global suppliers?
ANSWER
Suppliers with access to proprietary designs and manufacturing capability can set up as competitors. Countries like China may demand technology transfers. Cultures in some countries are more permissive of IP infringement.
TERM / CONCEPT
How can organizations protect IP internationally?
ANSWER
File patents quickly (Patent Cooperation Treaty for multi-country filing); get in-country legal representation; review patent/trademark/copyright/product liability laws per country; report U.S. violations to the DHS National IP Rights Coordination Center (IPR Center).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is SWOT analysis used for in risk identification?
ANSWER
Starts by examining the organization’s internal capabilities (strengths, weaknesses) and external environment (opportunities, threats) to systematically identify supply chain risks.
TERM / CONCEPT
What does Incoterms 2020 (FOB) determine in the context of tangible asset risk?
ANSWER
Who bears the risk of loss during the transfer of goods. In North America, FOB Origin means risk transfers to the buyer when goods are loaded; FOB Destination means risk stays with the seller until delivery.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is risk identification described as ‘iterative’?
ANSWER
Because new rounds of identification are needed as circumstances change — new risks emerge, existing ones evolve, and some become irrelevant. The risk register must be updated continuously.

Question 2

A.Demand forecasting errors
B.Trade wars, tariff changes, political instability, and export controls affecting cross-border supply flows
C.Machine breakdowns
D.Supplier quality failures
Geopolitical risk encompasses government actions and political instability that disrupt international supply chains.
Question 3
Which disruption type has the longest average recovery time?
A.Short-term supplier delivery delay
B.Natural disaster destroying a key facility
C.Demand spike during a promotion
D.IT system downtime of a few hours
Natural disasters that destroy facilities or infrastructure typically cause the longest recovery times — months to years.
Question 4

A.Physical theft of goods
B.Disruption from cyberattacks on supply chain systems — ransomware, data theft, or attacks on IoT devices

Cyber risk includes ransomware attacks on ERP/WMS systems and data breaches — increasingly a top supply chain risk.
