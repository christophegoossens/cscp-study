Overview

Risk identification is the first step in supply chain risk management — systematically discovering and documenting all potential sources of disruption before they occur. Incomplete identification leaves blind spots that can cause unexpected crises.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Risk identification: The process of discovering, recognizing, and describing all supply chain risks that could affect the organization's ability to meet its objectives.
📖 Definition:
Risk map (risk heat map): A visual tool plotting identified risks on a matrix of likelihood vs. impact — enabling prioritization of risks by their overall threat level.
📖 Definition:
Black swan event: A highly unlikely but extremely high-impact risk event that is difficult to predict based on historical data. Examples: global pandemics, major geopolitical disruptions, financial system collapse.

Risk Identification Methods

💡 Key Point:
Common risk identification methods: 1) Structured interviews with supply chain stakeholders, 2) Scenario analysis (what-if planning), 3) FMEA (Failure Mode and Effects Analysis), 4) Supply chain mapping (tier 1-2-3 supplier mapping), 5) Historical incident review, 6) Expert workshops, 7) External risk intelligence services.
  • Supply chain mapping: documenting all Tier 1, 2, and 3 suppliers and their geographic locations — reveals hidden concentration risks and single-point dependencies
  • Scenario planning: developing detailed 'what-if' scenarios for high-impact risks to test whether the supply chain could respond
  • FMEA: applying FMEA methodology to supply chain processes identifies failure modes and their downstream effects
  • External intelligence: using risk intelligence providers (Dun & Bradstreet, Resilinc, Everstream) for supplier financial health monitoring

Risk Categories to Identify

  • Upstream supply risks: supplier financial health, sole source dependency, geographic concentration, raw material scarcity
  • Internal process risks: production quality, capacity constraints, IT system failures, labor disruptions
  • Downstream demand risks: customer concentration, product obsolescence, channel partner stability
  • External macro risks: natural disasters, pandemics, regulatory changes, geopolitical disruptions, climate change
  • Financial risks: currency fluctuation, commodity price volatility, credit risk
💡 Key Point:
Supply chain mapping to Tier 2 and 3 is critical: the 2011 Tohoku earthquake/tsunami disrupted global auto and electronics supply chains primarily through Tier 2 and 3 supplier impacts that were unknown to most OEMs until it was too late.

DEFINITION
What is risk identification in supply chain?
ANSWER
The systematic process of discovering and documenting all potential sources of supply chain disruption — before they occur. The foundation of SCRM; incomplete identification leaves dangerous blind spots.
DEFINITION
What is a risk heat map?
ANSWER
A visual tool plotting identified risks on a likelihood × impact matrix. High likelihood + high impact = top priority risks. Used to communicate risk portfolio and prioritize mitigation resource allocation.
DEFINITION
What is a black swan event?
ANSWER
A highly unlikely but extremely high-impact risk event that is difficult to predict from historical data — e.g., global pandemic, major cyberattack, geopolitical shock. Requires scenario planning and resilience-building rather than probability-based mitigation.
CONCEPT
What is supply chain mapping and why is it important for risk identification?
ANSWER
Documenting all Tier 1, 2, and 3 suppliers with their geographic locations. Reveals hidden single-source dependencies, geographic concentration risks, and critical supply paths that aren't visible at Tier 1 alone.
CONCEPT
What risk was revealed by the 2011 Tohoku earthquake/tsunami?
ANSWER
Most automotive OEMs had unknown Tier 2 and 3 supplier dependencies in the affected region — suppliers of critical specialty materials (pigments, electronic components) that caused global production shutdowns. Demonstrated the critical need for sub-Tier 1 supply chain mapping.
CONCEPT
What are the seven main risk identification methods?
ANSWER
1) Structured stakeholder interviews, 2) Scenario analysis (what-if), 3) FMEA on supply chain processes, 4) Supply chain mapping (Tier 1-2-3), 5) Historical incident review, 6) Expert workshops/brainstorming, 7) External risk intelligence services.
CONCEPT
What is scenario planning in supply chain risk?
ANSWER
Developing detailed 'what-if' scenarios for high-impact risks — defining the event, its supply chain effects, and whether the current supply chain could survive and recover. Identifies gaps in resilience before a real event occurs.
CONCEPT
What is external risk intelligence in supply chain?
ANSWER
Using third-party services (Dun & Bradstreet, Resilinc, Everstream, Moody's Analytics) that continuously monitor supplier financial health, geopolitical events, and supply chain disruption signals — providing early warning before disruptions affect you.
CONCEPT
What is FMEA applied to supply chain risk identification?
ANSWER
Using FMEA methodology on supply chain processes — identifying potential failure modes at each step (supplier delivery failure, customs delay, production equipment failure), their effects on downstream operations, and likelihood/severity scores.
DEFINITION
What is single-point-of-failure in supply chain risk?
ANSWER
Any node in the supply chain where a disruption would immediately halt operations with no alternative path — sole-source supplier, single DC serving all customers, one critical piece of equipment with no backup. Must be identified and mitigated.
CONCEPT
What are the main categories of upstream supply risk to identify?
ANSWER
Supplier financial health/insolvency, sole-source dependency, geographic concentration, raw material scarcity, supplier quality failure, capacity constraints at critical suppliers, and Tier 2/3 dependency unknowns.
CONCEPT
What are the main categories of macro/external risks to identify?
ANSWER
Natural disasters (earthquakes, floods, hurricanes, wildfires), pandemics and public health crises, geopolitical disruptions (trade wars, sanctions, political instability), regulatory changes (new import restrictions, environmental regulations), and climate change impacts.
CONCEPT
What is currency risk in supply chain?
ANSWER
The risk that exchange rate changes make foreign-sourced goods more expensive or export products less competitive. Companies with significant international sourcing or sales use currency hedging to mitigate this risk.
CONCEPT
What is commodity price risk?
ANSWER
The risk that prices for raw materials (oil, steel, copper, agricultural commodities) change significantly — affecting supply chain cost unpredictably. Managed through price indexes in contracts, hedging, or inventory building ahead of price increases.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between known risks and unknown unknowns in supply chain?
ANSWER
Known risks: identified and documented in the risk register (supplier concentration, commodity price). Unknown unknowns: risks not yet conceived or identified — black swans, novel failure modes. SCRM addresses known risks; resilience and redundancy provide protection against unknown unknowns.

A.Demand risk
B.Supply concentration risk — if the sole supplier fails, production stops with no immediate alternative

Question 2
Geopolitical risk in supply chain includes:
A.Demand forecasting errors
B.Trade wars, tariff changes, political instability, and export controls affecting cross-border supply flows
C.Machine breakdowns
D.Supplier quality failures
Geopolitical risk encompasses government actions and political instability that disrupt international supply chains.
Question 3
Which disruption type has the longest average recovery time?
A.Short-term supplier delivery delay
B.Natural disaster destroying a key facility
C.Demand spike during a promotion
D.IT system downtime of a few hours
Natural disasters that destroy facilities or infrastructure typically cause the longest recovery times — months to years.
Question 4
Cyber risk in supply chain refers to:
A.Physical theft of goods
B.Disruption from cyberattacks on supply chain systems — ransomware, data theft, or attacks on IoT devices
C.Currency fluctuation risk
D.Labour strike risk
Cyber risk includes ransomware attacks on ERP/WMS systems and data breaches — increasingly a top supply chain risk.
