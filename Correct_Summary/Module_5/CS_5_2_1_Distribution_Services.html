Warehouse Activities (ASCM Definitions)
Receiving: Encompassing the physical receipt of material, the inspection of the shipment for conformance with the purchase order (quantity and damage), the identification and delivery to destination, and the completion of the records.
Put-away: Removing the material from the dock (or other location of receipt), transporting the material to a storage area, placing and recording the material in a storage area.
Order picking (order selection): Selecting or picking the required quantity of specific products for movement to a packaging area (usually in response to one or more customer orders).
Move: The physical transportation of inventory from one location to another within a facility.
Shipping: The function that performs tasks for the outgoing shipment of parts, components, and products. Includes packaging, marking, weighing, and loading.
Packaging: Materials surrounding an item to protect it from damage during transportation. The type of packaging influences the danger classification.
Packing and marking: All the activities of packing for safe shipping and unitizing one or more items of an order, placing them into an appropriate container, and marking and labeling the container.

Additional: Prepackaging — products received in bulk from a supplier and subsequently packaged into smaller quantities (e.g., consumer packs) at the warehouse. Storing — putting items under warehouse control in a storage point.

Value-Added Warehouse Services
  • Consolidation: Receives materials from multiple plants; combines into outgoing containers to reduce transport cost.
  • Break-bulk: Divides truckloads/railcars/containers of homogeneous items into smaller, more appropriate quantities. Can build new truckloads of mixed goods for a given location.
  • Cross-docking: Packing products on incoming shipments so they can be easily sorted and redirected at intermediate warehouses or at the final destination — minimal or no storage time.
  • Postponement: Goods enter as components; final assembly happens at the distribution center. Enables production efficiency + variety closer to demand.
  • Anticipation (stockpiling): Seasonal inventory stored in advance of demand peaks (clothing, lawn furniture, agricultural products).
  • Spot-stocking: Allocating inventory in advance of heavy demand in strategic markets — advance shipments placed close to seasonal customers.
  • Assortment warehousing: Stores goods close to the customer to ensure short lead times; reduces the number of suppliers the customer must deal with.
  • Mixing: Similar to break-bulk but from multiple manufacturers. Breaks down and reassembles the desired product mix for each customer or market.

Fulfillment Channels (Delivery Patterns)

The type of distribution network has a huge impact on facility numbers, location decisions, and service levels.

  • Manufacturer storage with direct delivery: Manufacturer ships directly to customer. Common in B2B; avoids intermediaries.
  • Manufacturer storage with drop ship: Distributor/retailer takes the order; manufacturer ships directly. No direct manufacturer-customer contact.
  • Manufacturer → DC → Retailer: Traditional supply chain. Channel intermediaries and retailers generally independent.
  • Independent distributor with omni-channel network: Distributor serves multiple channels (online, retail, direct) via owned/contracted carrier network.
  • Independent aggregator with e-business network: Similar to above but primarily online; aggregates offerings from multiple suppliers.

What is consolidation in warehousing?
ANSWER
Receiving materials from more than one plant and combining them into outgoing containers to qualify for full-vehicle shipping rates and reduce transport cost.
TERM / CONCEPT
Difference between break-bulk and cross-docking?
ANSWER
Break-bulk: divides large homogeneous shipments into smaller quantities and can rebuild mixed truckloads. Cross-docking: incoming products are sorted and redirected immediately with minimal or no storage time.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is postponement in distribution?
ANSWER
Goods enter a distribution center in component form; final assembly or customization occurs there. Enables production efficiency while adding variety closer to actual demand.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is spot-stocking?
ANSWER
Allocating inventory in advance of heavy seasonal demand in strategic markets — advance shipments placed close to customers to minimize shortage risk.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is assortment warehousing?
ANSWER
Storing goods close to the customer to ensure short lead times; reduces the number of suppliers the customer must deal with by offering a range of products from one location.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is mixing in warehousing?
ANSWER
Similar to break-bulk but involves shipments from multiple manufacturers. Breaks down and reassembles the desired product mix for each customer or market — avoids multiple separate shipments.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the drop-ship model?
ANSWER
A distributor or retailer takes the order; the manufacturer ships directly to the end customer. The retailer never takes physical possession of the goods.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the traditional distribution channel?
ANSWER
Manufacturer → Distribution Center → Retailer → Customer. Channel intermediaries and retailers generally independent; some DCs may be owned if economically justified.
TERM / CONCEPT
What trend is affecting last-mile delivery?
ANSWER
The shift from large retailer shipments to individual consumer orders (e-commerce / direct-to-consumer) — driving need for urban fulfillment centers and faster delivery capabilities.
