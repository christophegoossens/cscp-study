Overview

Reverse logistics manages the return of goods from customers back through the supply chain — for repair, refurbishment, remanufacturing, recycling, or disposal. It is a growing and strategic supply chain function.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Reverse logistics: A complete supply chain dedicated to the reverse flow of products and materials for the purpose of returns, repair, remanufacturing, and/or recycling. The 'Return' process in the SCOR model.
📖 Definition:
Return Merchandise Authorization (RMA): A return process where the customer must receive authorization before returning a product. The RMA number tracks the return and authorizes credit, replacement, or repair.
📖 Definition:
Refurbishment: Restoring a returned product to 'like new' condition — cleaning, repairing, testing, and repackaging. Product is then sold as 'refurbished' or 'certified pre-owned.'
📖 Definition:
Remanufacturing: Rebuilding a product to its original specifications — disassembling, cleaning, inspecting, replacing worn parts, reassembling, and testing. Results in a product that meets original performance standards.

Types of Returns

💡 Key Point:
Return categories: 1) Consumer returns (e-commerce/retail), 2) Commercial returns (B2B defective or excess), 3) End-of-life returns (recycling/disposal), 4) Recall returns (safety issue). Each category requires different processing and has different value recovery potential.
  • Consumer returns in e-commerce can exceed 30-40% of shipped volume — a major cost driver
  • Gate-keeping: the process of deciding whether to accept a return and on what terms — prevents fraudulent or unjustified returns
  • Triage: initial sorting of returned items to determine best disposition (resell, refurbish, recycle, dispose)
  • Disposition: the decision about what to do with returned items — return to stock, repair, liquidate, donate, recycle, dispose

Value Recovery

💡 Key Point:
Value recovery hierarchy (from most to least value): 1) Reuse (sell as-is), 2) Repair/Refurbishment, 3) Remanufacturing, 4) Recycling (recover materials), 5) Disposal (landfill/incineration). The goal is maximizing value recovery while minimizing environmental impact.
  • Liquidation channels: secondary market, auction, employee purchase program, donation
  • Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR): regulations requiring manufacturers to take responsibility for end-of-life recovery of their products
  • Circular economy principles: design products for reuse, repair, and recycling rather than single use

DEFINITION
What is reverse logistics?
ANSWER
A complete supply chain dedicated to the reverse flow of products and materials for returns, repair, remanufacturing, and/or recycling. The 'Return' process in the SCOR model. Increasingly important in e-commerce and sustainability-focused businesses.
DEFINITION
What is a Return Merchandise Authorization (RMA)?
ANSWER
A return process requiring the customer to receive authorization before returning a product. The RMA number tracks the return and authorizes the appropriate disposition — credit, replacement, or repair. Controls the return process and prevents unauthorized returns.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between refurbishment and remanufacturing?
ANSWER
Refurbishment: restoring a product to 'like new' condition — clean, repair, test, repackage. Remanufacturing: full disassembly, part-by-part inspection/replacement, and reassembly to original specifications — product meets original performance standards.
CONCEPT
What are the five reverse logistics value recovery options from most to least value?
ANSWER
1) Reuse (sell as-is), 2) Repair/Refurbishment (restore and resell), 3) Remanufacturing (rebuild to spec), 4) Recycling (recover raw materials), 5) Disposal (landfill/incineration). Maximize value recovery; minimize disposal.
DEFINITION
What is disposition in reverse logistics?
ANSWER
The decision about what to do with a returned item — return to stock for resale, repair, refurbish, liquidate to secondary market, recycle, donate, or dispose. Proper disposition maximizes value recovery from returns.
DEFINITION
What is gate-keeping in reverse logistics?
ANSWER
The process of deciding whether to accept a return and on what terms at the point of entry. Prevents fraudulent or unjustified returns (e.g., wardrobing, returning used items). Reduces reverse logistics cost by rejecting unqualified returns.
DEFINITION
What is triage in reverse logistics?
ANSWER
Initial sorting and inspection of returned items to determine their condition and best disposition. Faster than full inspection — quickly categorizes returns into: resell as-is, repair, refurbish, recycle, or dispose.
CONCEPT
Why have e-commerce return rates become a major supply chain challenge?
ANSWER
E-commerce return rates of 20-40% (vs. 8-10% in physical retail) create massive reverse logistics volume. Processing returns costs nearly as much as the original fulfillment — requiring dedicated reverse logistics operations to remain economically viable.
DEFINITION
What is Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)?
ANSWER
Regulations requiring manufacturers to take financial or physical responsibility for the end-of-life recovery (collection, recycling, disposal) of their products. Common in Europe for electronics (WEEE), packaging, and batteries.
DEFINITION
What is circular economy in supply chain context?
ANSWER
An economic model where products are designed for reuse, repair, remanufacturing, and recycling — keeping materials in use as long as possible and eliminating waste. Contrasts with the linear 'take-make-dispose' model.
CONCEPT
What is a secondary market in reverse logistics?
ANSWER
A channel for selling returned or excess goods below original retail price — through liquidators, discount retailers, online marketplaces (eBay, Amazon Renewed), or employee purchase programs. Generates value from returned inventory.
CONCEPT
What are the types of returns by category?
ANSWER
Consumer returns (e-commerce/retail), commercial returns (B2B defective or excess inventory), end-of-life returns (recycling/regulatory disposal), and recall returns (safety-driven). Each has different economics and required processing.
CONCEPT
What is the SCOR Return process?
ANSWER
The SCOR model includes 'Return' as one of its six core processes — covering both Source Return (returning goods to suppliers) and Deliver Return (receiving goods returned by customers). Each has associated metrics and best practices.
DEFINITION
What is a Closed-Loop Supply Chain?
ANSWER
A supply chain that incorporates both forward (production to customer) and reverse (customer to reuse/recycle) flows — creating a loop where materials flow continuously. Reduces virgin material needs and waste.
DEFINITION
What is cradle-to-cradle product design?
ANSWER
Designing products so that all materials can be fully recovered and reused at end of life — either as biological nutrients (compostable) or technical nutrients (recyclable). Goes beyond recyclability to full material circularity.
