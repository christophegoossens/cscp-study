Transportation Objectives

Transportation has two primary objectives: product movement and temporary storage.

  • Product movement — efficient use of time: Inventory in transit is not earning a return. JIT emphasizes reducing transit time to lower inventory costs.
  • Product movement — efficient use of money: Minimize line-haul costs while meeting service needs. Total cost approach is essential.
  • Product movement — minimal harm to environment: Fuel use, emissions, noise, and water pollution. Regulations include Clean Truck Programs, EPA Tier 4, EU Low Emission Zones (LEZs).
  • Temporary storage: Vehicles used as short-term storage in three situations: (1) short-term holding before unloading, (2) crowded facility overflow, (3) changed destination (goods rerouted in transit via GPS).
Line-Haul Costs

Line-haul costs = basic costs of carrier operation to move a container of freight. Components include:

  • Vehicle costs: Own fleet vs. hire vs. contract — different capital vs. variable cost tradeoffs.
  • Driver/operator costs: Labor to operate the vehicle (except pipelines).
  • Vehicle operating costs: Repairs, cleaning, parking, maintenance.
  • General and administrative costs: Management of the transport function.
  • Insurance and security costs: Damage, loss, theft coverage.
Capacity Constraints: Economies of Distance & Scale
  • Distance: Cost increases with distance (more fuel, labor, time). But cost per unit decreases on a per-mile basis for longer hauls (tapering principle).
  • Volume: Economies of scale — cost per pound decreases as payload increases.
  • Density: Low-density goods cost more per pound to ship than dense goods. A weight cap exists (over-the-road limits); cube-out may occur before weight-out for low-density goods.

Capacity Constraints: Stowability, Handling, Liability
  • Stowability: Odd-shaped items that cannot efficiently share cargo space cost more. Packaging design affects stowability.
  • Handling: Special equipment, physical dangers, packaging groupings. Good packaging reduces handling costs. Packaging objectives: protection, efficiency, communication (labeling), low environmental impact.
  • Liability: Factors raising carrier liability risk: susceptibility to damage, perishability, high value per pound, susceptibility to theft. More liability risk = higher transport cost.
Transportation Tradeoffs (Conflicts of Interest)
  • Lot size vs. inventory: Large lots improve manufacturing efficiency but increase inventory. Advanced manufacturing and IT can reduce required lot sizes.
  • Inventory vs. transportation costs: Full payloads reduce per-unit transport cost but increase inventory. Distribution control systems (forecasting + delivery scheduling) help balance this.
  • Lead time vs. transport costs: Faster transport is more expensive. Forecasting reduces reliance on expediting.
  • Product variety vs. inventory: More SKUs = more inventory needed. Delayed differentiation (ship generic; customize near customer) is one solution.
Information technology reduces all four conflicts by improving forecast accuracy, enabling JIT, and supporting collaborative planning.
Transportation Capacity Forecasting Benefits

Integrating demand, marketing, manufacturing, and product information into transport forecasting produces:

  • Less reliance on expensive spot market for transport
  • Weeks of advance notice to carrier procurement
  • Carrier procurement decoupled from order creation
  • Easier arrangement of intermodal shipments (rail → truck) for savings
  • Backhauls planned to reduce deadheading (empty return trips)
  • Better warehouse staffing planning
  • More responsive and less expensive transport overall
Deadheading: An empty vehicle return trip after delivery. Backhaul planning reduces deadheading and lowers carrier costs.

TERM / CONCEPT
What are the two primary objectives of transportation?
ANSWER
1) Product movement (efficient use of time, money, and minimal environmental harm), and 2) Temporary storage (short-term holding, overflow handling, or rerouting in transit).
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the three situations for using vehicles as temporary storage?
ANSWER
1) Short-term storage before unloading, 2) Crowded facility overflow, 3) Changed destination — goods rerouted mid-route via GPS tracking.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the components of line-haul costs?
ANSWER
Vehicle costs, driver/operator costs, vehicle operating costs (maintenance, repairs), general and administrative costs, insurance and security costs.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the density constraint in transportation pricing?
ANSWER
Low-density goods cost more per pound to ship because they fill a vehicle's cube capacity before its weight limit. High-density goods are more economical per pound.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the volume constraint in transportation?
ANSWER
Economies of scale apply: cost per pound decreases as payload weight increases, since fixed operating costs are spread over more units.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the distance constraint (tapering principle)?
ANSWER
Shipping cost increases with distance but at a decreasing rate per mile — longer hauls have lower cost per mile because terminal/handling costs are fixed.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does liability affect transport cost?
ANSWER
Higher liability risk (damage, perishability, high value, theft risk) raises carrier costs. Carriers charge more to cover these risks.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the lot size vs. inventory tradeoff in transportation?
ANSWER
Manufacturers prefer large lots (lower per-unit cost), but large lots increase inventory holding costs. Advanced manufacturing and IT can enable smaller economic lot sizes.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is delayed differentiation in logistics?
ANSWER
Shipping generic products as far as possible and customizing/differentiating them closer to the customer — reduces inventory variety while maintaining responsiveness.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is deadheading and how is it reduced?
ANSWER
Deadheading = empty vehicle return trips after delivery. It is reduced by planning backhauls — coordinating return loads to fill the vehicle on the way back.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the benefits of transportation capacity forecasting?
ANSWER
Less spot market reliance, advance carrier procurement notice, decoupled procurement from orders, easier intermodal planning, reduced deadheading, better warehouse staffing, more responsive and cheaper transport.
TERM / CONCEPT
What packaging objectives should logistics consider?
ANSWER
1) Protection against damage, 2) Efficiency of handling/loading/unloading, 3) Communication (labeling for ID, tracking, customs, handling), 4) Low environmental impact.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the inventory vs. transport cost tradeoff?
ANSWER
Full payloads lower per-unit transport cost but require holding more inventory. Distribution control systems that combine forecasting and delivery scheduling can minimize this conflict.
