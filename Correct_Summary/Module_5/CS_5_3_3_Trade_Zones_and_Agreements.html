Free Trade Zones (FTZ)

A free trade zone (FTZ), called a foreign trade zone in the U.S., is a geographic area where normal trade barriers (customs, duties) are suspended to promote commerce. FTZs are organized around major seaports, international airports, and national frontiers.

FTZs are part of a broader category of Special Economic Zones (SEZs). Types of SEZs:

  • FTZs — support domestic or re-export trade
  • Export Processing Zones (EPZs) — enable export manufacturing
  • Urban enterprise zones — urban revitalization
  • Freeports — multi-use, integrated development

Key rule: Retail trade is forbidden in a free trade zone.

Benefits of FTZs for importers and manufacturers:

  • Exemption from customs formalities, duties, or quotas while goods are in the FTZ
  • Exemption from duties/quotas on re-exports from the FTZ
  • Deferral of duties on imports until goods enter the domestic market
  • Avoidance of fines — goods can be processed, remarked, repackaged before going through customs
  • Reduction of import duties on some cargoes via break-bulk before customs
  • Inspection of merchandise before paying duties
  • Avoidance of quota problems by holding cargo in FTZ until quota opens
  • Indefinite, cost-effective storage without local/state inventory taxes
  • Manufacture and assembly without “inverted duties” — when duty on components is higher than on finished goods

Drawback: Host country receives reduced revenue from import duties. However, economic activity, jobs, and trade volume typically offset this.

Trading Blocs
Trade bloc (trading bloc): An agreement between or among countries intended to reduce or remove barriers to trade within member countries. Frequently, but not always, a geographic region.

Types of trading blocs (from least to most integrated):

  • Free trade areas/zones: Reduce/eliminate tariffs among members; each country keeps its own external tariff policy
  • Preferential trade agreements: Preferential (not full) access to certain products from member countries
  • Customs unions: Free trade area + common external tariff policy
  • Common markets: Customs union + free movement of labor and capital; reduced physical/technical/fiscal barriers
  • Economic unions: Common market + unified economic policies
  • Customs and monetary unions: Customs union + shared currency
  • Economic and monetary unions: Common market + customs/monetary union (most integrated)
USMCA (replaced NAFTA 2020)

The USMCA went into effect July 1, 2020, replacing NAFTA. Key changes:

  • Agricultural tariffs: Zero tariffs remain; U.S. can export more dairy to Canada
  • Rules of origin: Higher local-content requirements (especially automotive) to qualify for zero tariffs
  • Certificate of origin: Eliminated — importers can claim favorable tariffs by self-certifying knowledge of origin
  • De minimis levels: Raised to support small/medium enterprises
  • Environmental and labor chapters: U.S., Canada, Mexico must enforce their own environmental and labor laws
  • Sunset clause: USMCA has an expiration date (unlike NAFTA); must be reviewed/renewed every 16 years
Effects of Trading Blocs on Supply Chains

Within blocs — gravity model: Trade volume between two countries is proportional to their economic size and inversely proportional to the distance between them. Bloc membership further amplifies trade between members.

  • Easier negotiation with fewer, larger partners
  • Lesser-developed member countries gain from more stable agreements
  • Reduced complexity for supply chains crossing member borders

Outside blocs:

  • Nonmembers may see their prices and export demand decrease (trade diversion)
  • Nonmembers’ supply chains can be impacted; their ability to negotiate multilateral deals may be reduced
Trade blocs create trade (more trade within the bloc) and divert trade (less trade with nonmembers). The net global economic effect is debated.

What are the key benefits of a free trade zone?
ANSWER
Exemption from duties/quotas on re-exports, deferral of duties until domestic entry, inspection before paying duties, avoidance of quota problems, indefinite storage without inventory taxes, manufacture without inverted duties.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are inverted duties and how do FTZs help?
ANSWER
Inverted duties occur when the duty on imported components is higher than on the finished product. In an FTZ, components can be imported, assembled, and only the (lower) duty on the finished product is paid when entering the domestic market.
TERM / CONCEPT
ASCM definition: Trade bloc
ANSWER
An agreement between or among countries intended to reduce or remove barriers to trade within member countries — frequently, but not always, a geographic region.
TERM / CONCEPT
Rank the 7 types of trading blocs from least to most integrated.
ANSWER
1) Preferential trade agreements, 2) Free trade areas, 3) Customs unions (FTA + common external tariff), 4) Common markets (+ free movement of labor/capital), 5) Economic unions (+ unified policies), 6) Customs and monetary unions (+ shared currency), 7) Economic and monetary unions (most integrated).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between a customs union and a free trade area?
ANSWER
A free trade area reduces/eliminates tariffs among members but each country keeps its own external tariff policy. A customs union adds a common external tariff — members treat non-members identically.
TERM / CONCEPT
When did USMCA replace NAFTA and what is the sunset clause?
ANSWER
USMCA took effect July 1, 2020. The sunset clause means it has an expiration date and must be reviewed/renewed every 16 years — unlike NAFTA, which was in force until removed by members.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the USMCA rules of origin changes?
ANSWER
Higher local content requirements (especially automotive) to qualify for zero tariffs. Products not meeting these requirements face standard tariffs.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the gravity model in trade?
ANSWER
Used to predict trade volume between two countries: proportional to their economic sizes and inversely proportional to the distance between them. Bloc membership amplifies trade between members.
TERM / CONCEPT
How do trading blocs affect nonmember supply chains?
ANSWER
Nonmembers may see export prices and demand decrease (trade diversion). Their ability to negotiate multilateral deals may be weakened as bloc members increasingly trade with each other.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a special economic zone (SEZ)?
ANSWER
A broader category that includes FTZs. Types: FTZs (trade support), Export Processing Zones (manufacturing for export), Urban enterprise zones (revitalization), Freeports (multi-use integrated development).
