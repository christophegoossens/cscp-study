Overview
Warehousing\xa0involves “the activities related to receiving, storing, and shipping materials to and from production or distribution locations” (ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary).

Warehouse strategy must balance ownership type, number, location, facility design, and management systems.

Warehousing can contribute to logistical goals in a number of ways:

Ownership Types:

  • Private: maximum control + market presence; fixed costs persist even when underutilized
  • Public: flexible, pay per use, economies of scale; loss of control and customization
  • Contract: fixed-term; combines flexibility of public + tailored service of private
  • Common strategy: private for base volume + public/contract for seasonal peaks

Materials Handling Equipment (exam-ready comparisons):

  • Conveyors: continuous movement; reduces labor; high capital cost
  • Towlines: move carts in fixed paths; efficient for high-volume repetitive flows
  • Carousels: rotating storage; brings items to picker; space-efficient
  • Robotics: automated picking/sorting; high upfront cost; reduces labor errors
  • Live racks (gravity flow): FIFO-enforcing; ideal for perishables and fast-moving SKUs

Cross-docking means:
A.Storing goods for at least 30 days
B.Transferring inbound freight directly to outbound vehicles with minimal or no storage
C.Sorting by ABC category

Cross-docking transfers goods from inbound trailers directly to outbound trailers, bypassing storage — reducing handling, labour, and lead time.
Question 2
Warehouse slotting assigns storage locations based on:
A.Alphabetical product name order
B.SKU velocity, size, and pick frequency
C.Random assignment for variety
D.Invoice value
Slotting optimizes pick path efficiency by placing fast-moving items in easily accessible locations.
Question 3
Pick accuracy measures:
A.On-time delivery
B.Percentage of order lines picked with the right item and right quantity
C.Inventory turnover
D.Dock-to-stock time
Pick accuracy = % of order lines or orders picked without error. Errors cause returns, customer dissatisfaction, and costly corrections.

Receiving involves unloading, counting, inspecting, and verifying inbound shipments against the PO and ASN — the first critical inventory control point.
