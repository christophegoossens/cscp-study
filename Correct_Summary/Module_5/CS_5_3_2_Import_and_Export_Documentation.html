5.3.2 · Import and Export Documentation

Overview

Import and export documentation is required to legally move goods across international borders. Missing, inaccurate, or incomplete documentation causes customs delays, fines, and shipment holds.

Key Export Documents

📖 Definition:
Commercial invoice: The primary export document — a bill from the seller to the buyer. Used by customs to assess duties. Must include: seller/buyer details, description of goods, quantity, value, currency, and terms of sale (Incoterms).
📖 Definition:
Packing list: A detailed list of the contents of each package in a shipment — including dimensions, weights, and item quantities per package. Used by customs and consignees to verify shipment contents.
📖 Definition:
Bill of Lading (BOL): A legal contract between shipper and carrier documenting: goods accepted for carriage, terms of transport, and delivery terms. For ocean freight, it may also serve as a document of title (negotiable BOL).
📖 Definition:
Certificate of Origin (COO): A document certifying the country in which goods were manufactured. Required to apply preferential duty rates under free trade agreements, and by some countries for all imports.
📖 Definition:
Shipper's Export Declaration (SED) / Electronic Export Information (EEI): Required US export filing for goods above $2,500 or controlled items. Filed electronically through AES (Automated Export System). Reports export statistics and enforces export controls.

Key Import Documents

📖 Definition:
Customs entry: The formal document filed with customs authority to request release of imported goods. Includes goods description, HTS classification, declared value, and duty calculation.
📖 Definition:
Arrival notice: A notification from the freight forwarder or carrier that a shipment has arrived at the destination port. Triggers the importer to arrange customs clearance and final delivery.
  • Letter of Credit (LC): a bank guarantee ensuring the seller is paid when documented proof of shipment is provided — reduces payment risk in international trade
  • Dangerous goods documentation (IMDG/IATA): required for hazardous materials shipments — safety data sheets, proper classification, packaging, and labeling
  • Phytosanitary certificate: certifies that plants/agricultural products are free from pests and diseases
  • ATA Carnet: an international customs document allowing temporary import of goods (trade show samples, professional equipment) without paying duties

DEFINITION
What is a commercial invoice?
ANSWER
The primary export document — a bill from seller to buyer. Used by customs to assess duties. Must include: seller/buyer details, goods description, quantity, value, currency, country of origin, and terms of sale (Incoterms).
DEFINITION
What is a Bill of Lading (BOL)?
ANSWER
A legal contract between shipper and carrier documenting goods accepted for transport, transport terms, and delivery conditions. For ocean freight, a negotiable BOL can also serve as a document of title — transferable to the buyer.
DEFINITION
What is a Certificate of Origin (COO)?
ANSWER
A document certifying the country where goods were manufactured. Required to: claim preferential duty rates under Free Trade Agreements, comply with some country-specific import requirements, and support rules of origin verification.
DEFINITION
What is a packing list?
ANSWER
A detailed list of shipment contents — package by package — including item descriptions, quantities, dimensions, and weights. Used by customs inspectors and consignees to verify what was shipped against what was ordered.
DEFINITION
What is Electronic Export Information (EEI)?
ANSWER
The US electronic export filing (via AES — Automated Export System) required for goods valued over $2,500 or controlled items. Reports export statistics, enables export control enforcement, and provides security screening data.
DEFINITION
What is a customs entry?
ANSWER
The formal document filed with customs authority to request release of imported goods — including goods description, HTS code classification, declared value, and duty calculation. Filed by the customs broker or importer.
DEFINITION
What is a Letter of Credit (LC) in international trade?
ANSWER
A bank guarantee ensuring payment to the seller when they present specified shipping documents to their bank. Reduces payment risk for the seller (guaranteed payment) and documentation risk for the buyer (payment only on proof of shipment).
DEFINITION
What is an arrival notice in international logistics?
ANSWER
A notification from the freight forwarder or carrier that a shipment has arrived at the destination port and is ready for customs clearance and pickup. Triggers the importer to arrange customs clearance, inland transport, and delivery.
DEFINITION
What is an ATA Carnet?
ANSWER
An international customs document allowing temporary import of goods (trade show exhibits, professional equipment, samples) into signatory countries without paying duties — as long as they are re-exported within a specified period.
DEFINITION
What is a phytosanitary certificate?
ANSWER
A certificate issued by a government agricultural authority certifying that plants, plant products, or agricultural commodities are free from pests and diseases and meet the importing country's phytosanitary requirements.
DEFINITION
What is an air waybill (AWB)?
ANSWER
The carrier's receipt and non-negotiable contract for air freight. Unlike an ocean BOL, an AWB is never a document of title — it cannot be transferred to a third party. Always consigned directly to the named consignee.
CONCEPT
What happens when customs documentation is incorrect or missing?
ANSWER
Customs holds the shipment — causing delays, storage fees, and potential fines. Correction may require additional documentation, re-examination, or legal proceedings. For time-sensitive goods, customs holds can cause production stoppages.
CONCEPT
What is HTS code classification and why is accuracy critical?
ANSWER
The HTS code determines the applicable duty rate for imports. Incorrect classification = wrong duty rate. Under-declaration can result in fines and back-duties. Over-declaration results in overpaying duties — both are costly.
CONCEPT
What is dangerous goods documentation (IMDG/IATA)?
ANSWER
Hazardous materials require special documentation: Safety Data Sheets (SDS), proper UN classification codes, IMDG (ocean) or IATA (air) Dangerous Goods declarations, approved packaging, and labeling. Non-compliance risks carrier rejection and safety violations.
CONCEPT
What is a customs broker and what documents do they prepare?
ANSWER
A licensed professional managing customs clearance — classifying goods (HTS), calculating duties, preparing the customs entry, submitting filing to customs authority, paying duties on behalf of the importer, and arranging release of goods.
