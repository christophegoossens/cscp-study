Mode Selection: Value Density vs. Packaging Density

The best transport mode for a product is found by comparing its value density (value per unit of weight) vs. its packaging density (weight per unit of volume).

  • High value density — faster, more expensive mode (air, express)
  • Low value density — slower, cheaper mode (water, rail)
  • High packaging density — cheaper per pound to ship
  • Low packaging density — fills cube before weight limit; more expensive per pound

Rail Transport
  • Best for: Heavy loads, low-value goods, long distances, bulk commodities
  • Advantages: Very fuel-efficient (1 ton of freight ~470 miles per gallon in U.S.), low cost per ton-mile, environmentally friendly, carries virtually any product
  • Disadvantages: Slow, limited accessibility (can’t reach most final destinations), more damage to fragile goods, few carriers (U.S. oligopoly), expensive infrastructure
  • Intermodal: 25% of U.S. railroad revenue; TOFC/COFC (piggyback) combinations with trucks
Motor Carriers (Trucking)
  • Best for: Small-to-medium shipments, high-value goods, short-to-medium distances, door-to-door service
  • Advantages: Highest accessibility, faster than rail/water, low fixed costs, easy market entry, flexible
  • Three segments:
    • TL (Truckload): >15,000 lbs (6,818 kg); single shipper fills the trailer
    • LTL (Less-than-Truckload): <10,000 lbs (4,536 kg); consolidates multiple shippers
    • Specialty carriers: UPS, FedEx, specialized equipment
  • Disadvantages: Variable fuel costs, highway congestion, driver shortages, high variable costs per ton-mile vs. rail
Water Transport
  • Best for: Bulk, low-value, high-density cargo; long international routes
  • Advantages: Lowest cost per ton-mile (<1 cent per ton-mile), huge load capacity, sustainable, relieves highway congestion
  • Disadvantages: Slowest mode, very limited accessibility (ports/waterways only), weather delays, port strike risk, security concerns
  • Containers: Standard 8’ x 8’ wide; lengths 20’–53’. Containerization has revolutionized global trade.
Pipeline Transport
  • Best for: Liquids, gases (petroleum, natural gas, water, chemicals, slurry)
  • Advantages: Available 24/7/365, no weather disruptions, no labor required to operate once built, high safety record, natural monopoly, no return trip needed
  • Disadvantages: Only liquids/liquefiable products, very high construction cost, highly inflexible (fixed route), vulnerable to political disputes in international routes, limited use internationally
Air Transport
  • Best for: High-value, time-sensitive, low-weight/volume cargo; perishables
  • Advantages: Fastest mode, low loss/damage rates, low fixed costs (airports government-provided)
  • Disadvantages: Most expensive per pound, limited accessibility (few airports), weather delays, limited size/weight per shipment
  • Strong competitor with ocean freight for transoceanic carriage of high-value goods
Intermodal Transport
  • Piggyback (TOFC/COFC): Truck trailer or container placed on a flatcar (TOFC = trailer on flatcar; COFC = container on flatcar). Also RoadRailers (trailers with both rubber and steel wheels).
  • Trainship/Containership: Mixed mode including water carriage with rail or truck segments.
  • Truck-plane (birdyback): Air transport with surface legs before/after flight.
  • Benefits: Flexibility, efficiency, reduced costs. Transportation management software helps select optimal combinations.
Types of Carriers
Common (public) carrier: Transportation available to the public that does not provide special treatment to any one party and is regulated in regard to rates and liability. Regulated for public service, assumption of liability, no discrimination, and reasonable rates.
  • Private carrier: A company that owns or leases a fleet to transport its own products. Has control and scheduling flexibility but bears all capital costs. Can haul other goods (since 1980 U.S. deregulation).
  • Contract carrier: Not available to the general public; serves a limited number of shippers under specific contracts. Rates generally lower than common carriers. Independent truckers are a type of contract carrier.
  • Exempt carrier: Not subject to economic regulation of rates. Exemptions for specific commodities (fresh produce, some agricultural goods) or airport zones.
Common carrier = publicly available, regulated. Private = own goods only. Contract = selected clients under contract. Exempt = no rate regulation for specific commodities.

TERM / CONCEPT
What is value density and how does it affect mode selection?
ANSWER
Value density = value per unit weight. High value density → faster, more expensive mode (air, express). Low value density → slower, cheaper mode (water, rail, truck).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is packaging density and why does it matter?
ANSWER
Packaging density = weight per unit of volume. Low-density goods fill a vehicle’s cubic capacity before the weight limit (cube-out) — increasing cost per pound. High-density goods are more economical to ship.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are rail transport’s strengths and weaknesses?
ANSWER
Strengths: very fuel-efficient, low cost for bulk/long-distance, environmentally friendly. Weaknesses: slow, limited accessibility, higher damage risk for fragile goods, few carriers, expensive infrastructure.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the three trucking segments?
ANSWER
TL (Truckload): >15,000 lbs, single shipper fills trailer. LTL (Less-than-Truckload): <10,000 lbs, multiple shippers consolidated. Specialty: express carriers like UPS, FedEx.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is trucking dominant for last-mile delivery?
ANSWER
Highest accessibility (door-to-door), faster than rail/water, low fixed costs, easy market entry, flexible routing — trucks can reach destinations that other modes cannot.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is water transport best suited for?
ANSWER
Bulk, low-value, high-density cargo on long international routes. Lowest cost per ton-mile (<1 cent). Huge load capacity. Main drawbacks: slowest mode and very limited accessibility.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are pipeline transport’s unique characteristics?
ANSWER
Only stationary carrier (doesn’t move the vehicle). Available 24/7/365, no weather disruption, no return trip, no driver needed. Only transports liquids/liquefiable/gas products. Very high construction cost. Natural monopoly.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is air transport best suited for?
ANSWER
High-value, time-sensitive, low-weight cargo. Fastest mode with low damage rates. Most expensive per pound. Limited to airports; impractical for heavy/bulk freight.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is TOFC and COFC?
ANSWER
TOFC = Trailer On Flat Car (piggyback: truck trailer on a rail flatcar). COFC = Container On Flat Car. Both are intermodal combinations combining rail and truck advantages.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a RoadRailer?
ANSWER
A trailer with both rubber tires (for road) and steel wheels (for rail). A simple wheel change allows it to ride on either road or rail track — eliminating the need for a separate flatcar.
TERM / CONCEPT
ASCM definition: Common carrier
ANSWER
Transportation available to the public that does not provide special treatment to any one party and is regulated in regard to rates and liability.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the four regulations on common carriers?
ANSWER
1) Public service (must serve all within license scope), 2) Assumption of liability (carrier bears transport risk), 3) No discrimination (must take all legitimate business), 4) Reasonable rates (rates must be published and fair).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a private carrier?
ANSWER
A company that owns or leases vehicles to transport its own products. Must own/lease vehicles, manage their operation, and not be primarily in the transport business. Since 1980 deregulation, can also carry others’ goods.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a contract carrier?
ANSWER
Not required to serve the general public; makes contracts with a limited number of shippers. Rates generally lower than common carriers. The independent trucker is a special type of contract carrier.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is an exempt carrier?
ANSWER
A carrier not subject to economic rate regulation, with exemptions granted for specific commodities (e.g., fresh produce) or areas (e.g., airport zones). Still responsible for compliance with safety rules.

Question 1
The lowest cost per tonne-kilometre for bulk non-time-sensitive cargo is offered by:
A.Air freight
B.Road (truck)
C.Rail
D.Ocean (sea freight)
Ocean freight has the lowest cost per tonne-km for high-volume non-perishable cargo over long distances, though transit times are longest.
Question 2
Intermodal transportation uses:
A.Multiple carriers of the same mode
B.Two or more transport modes using standard containers without unloading cargo at transfers
C.Only air and ocean combinations
D.Passenger and freight services combined
Intermodal combines modes in sequence using standardized containers, gaining cost and environmental benefits.
Question 3
Air freight is preferred for:
A.Bulk commodities
B.High-value, time-critical, or perishable goods
C.Maximum environmental sustainability
D.All domestic shipments
Air freight's speed and global reach justify the premium cost for high-value, perishable, or urgently needed goods.
Question 4

A.Time from booking to invoice payment
B.Time from cargo pickup to delivery at destination
C.Time to load a truck

Transit time is the elapsed time from carrier pickup to delivery — a key factor in total supply chain lead time.
