Overview

Understanding transportation modes, carrier types, and service offerings enables supply chain managers to select the optimal transportation strategy for each lane, product type, and service level requirement.

Transportation Modes

📖 Definition:
Truckload (TL/FTL): A shipment that fills an entire truck from one shipper to one destination. Cost-effective for high-volume regular lanes. Carrier dedicates the truck to one shipper.
📖 Definition:
Less-than-Truckload (LTL): Multiple shippers' goods consolidated into one truck. Shipped through carrier terminal network with intermediate handling. Per-unit cost higher than FTL; suitable for shipments too large for parcel.
📖 Definition:
Intermodal: Using two or more transport modes — typically truck for first/last mile and rail or ocean for the long-haul portion. Combines cost efficiency (rail/ocean) with flexibility (truck).
📖 Definition:
Air freight: The fastest mode; the most expensive per unit. Used for time-sensitive goods, high-value items, or supply emergencies. Cannot accommodate heavy or bulky goods efficiently.
📖 Definition:
Ocean freight: The cheapest mode per unit for international shipments; slowest. Measured in TEUs (Twenty-foot Equivalent Units). Used for heavy, bulk, or non-urgent international cargo.
📖 Definition:
Rail freight: Cost-effective for heavy, bulk commodities over long distances (coal, grain, chemicals, intermodal containers). Less flexible than truck — limited to rail infrastructure.

Carrier Types

💡 Key Point:
Common carrier: serves any shipper at published tariff rates — no exclusivity. Contract carrier: operates under a contract with specific shippers at negotiated rates. Private carrier: company-owned fleet operated for own goods only. Exempt carrier: exempt from economic regulation (agricultural, produce).
  • Freight broker: an intermediary who arranges shipments between shippers and carriers without taking physical possession — earns a commission
  • Freight forwarder: arranges international shipments including documentation, customs clearance, and carrier coordination
  • Non-Vessel Operating Common Carrier (NVOCC): consolidates ocean freight from multiple shippers without owning vessels
💡 Key Point:
Incoterms determine where risk and cost transfer between buyer and seller — critical for international shipment planning. Key Incoterms: EXW (buyer picks up), FOB (seller to port), CIF (seller pays insurance/freight to destination port), DDP (seller delivers duty paid to destination).

DEFINITION
What is a Full Truckload (FTL/TL) carrier?
ANSWER
A carrier that transports a shipment filling an entire truck from one shipper to one destination. No intermediate terminal handling — direct delivery. Lowest per-unit cost for high volumes on regular lanes.
DEFINITION
What is Less-than-Truckload (LTL) freight?
ANSWER
Multiple shippers share truck space — goods move through carrier terminal network with intermediate handling. Higher per-unit cost than FTL due to consolidation/deconsolidation handling. For shipments too large for parcel, too small for FTL.
DEFINITION
What is a TEU and why is it used in ocean freight?
ANSWER
Twenty-foot Equivalent Unit — the standard measurement unit for ocean freight container capacity. A 40-foot container = 2 TEUs. Ship capacity and port throughput are measured in TEUs.
CONCEPT
What are the four carrier types?
ANSWER
Common carrier (any shipper, published tariffs), Contract carrier (specific shippers, negotiated rates), Private carrier (company's own fleet for own goods), Exempt carrier (exempt from economic regulation, e.g., agricultural produce).
DEFINITION
What is a freight broker?
ANSWER
An intermediary that arranges shipments between shippers and carriers without taking physical possession of goods. Earns a commission on each transaction. Provides shippers with carrier access; provides carriers with freight access.
DEFINITION
What is a freight forwarder?
ANSWER
A company that arranges international shipments on behalf of exporters — including documentation, export/import customs clearance, cargo insurance, and coordination of multiple carriers (ocean, air, truck).
CONCEPT
When is air freight used in supply chains?
ANSWER
For time-sensitive goods (perishables, fashion urgency), high-value items where inventory cost savings justify premium freight, supply emergencies (when a production line is about to stop), or medical/pharmaceutical products requiring fast, controlled delivery.
CONCEPT
When is ocean freight preferred over air?
ANSWER
For heavy, bulk, or low-value goods where transit time is not critical (3-6 weeks acceptable), for international shipments where weight/volume make air cost prohibitive, and for consumer goods with long planning horizons.
DEFINITION
What is intermodal transportation?
ANSWER
Using two or more transport modes in a single movement — most commonly truck for origin/destination and rail or ocean for the long haul. Combines cost efficiency of rail/ocean with truck's flexibility and door-to-door capability.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between a freight forwarder and a Non-Vessel Operating Common Carrier (NVOCC)?
ANSWER
Freight forwarder: agent that arranges shipments, handles documentation, earns fees. NVOCC: issues its own Bills of Lading, consolidates ocean freight from multiple shippers, negotiates rates directly with ocean carriers — acts as a carrier to the shipper.
CONCEPT
What are the key Incoterms and what do they define?
ANSWER
Incoterms define cost/risk transfer points. EXW (buyer picks up at seller's facility), FOB (seller delivers to origin port), CIF (seller pays freight/insurance to destination port), DDP (seller delivers duty-paid to buyer's location).
DEFINITION
What is a Bill of Lading (BOL)?
ANSWER
A legal document between shipper and carrier that details the goods being transported, their quantity, destination, and terms. Serves as a receipt (goods accepted), a contract of carriage, and a document of title for international shipments.
CONCEPT
What is a spot carrier and when is one used?
ANSWER
A carrier hired on the spot market (one-time basis) rather than under a long-term contract. Used when contract carriers lack capacity, for unusual lanes, or during peak demand when contracted capacity is exhausted.
CONCEPT
What is carrier on-time performance and why does it matter?
ANSWER
The percentage of shipments delivered on or before the promised date. Poor carrier OTP directly causes customer service failures. Used in carrier scorecarding and contract negotiations — poor performers may be dropped or penalized.
DEFINITION
What is a dedicated fleet?
ANSWER
A private fleet or contracted fleet of trucks that exclusively serves one company's shipments. Provides maximum service reliability and schedule control but higher fixed cost than using common carriers flexibly.

Question 1
The lowest cost per tonne-kilometre for bulk non-time-sensitive cargo is offered by:
A.Air freight
B.Road (truck)
C.Rail
D.Ocean (sea freight)
Ocean freight has the lowest cost per tonne-km for high-volume non-perishable cargo over long distances, though transit times are longest.
Question 2
Intermodal transportation uses:
A.Multiple carriers of the same mode
B.Two or more transport modes using standard containers without unloading cargo at transfers
C.Only air and ocean combinations
D.Passenger and freight services combined
Intermodal combines modes in sequence using standardized containers, gaining cost and environmental benefits.
Question 3
Air freight is preferred for:
A.Bulk commodities
B.High-value, time-critical, or perishable goods
C.Maximum environmental sustainability
D.All domestic shipments
Air freight's speed and global reach justify the premium cost for high-value, perishable, or urgently needed goods.
Question 4
Transit time is:
A.Time from booking to invoice payment
B.Time from cargo pickup to delivery at destination
C.Time to load a truck
D.Total distance divided by average speed
Transit time is the elapsed time from carrier pickup to delivery — a key factor in total supply chain lead time.
