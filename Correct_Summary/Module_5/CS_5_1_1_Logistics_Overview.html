Overview

Logistics is the SCM subset covering movement, handling, and storage of goods from origin to distribution. Its value proposition balances service and total cost minimization.

  • US definition excludes manufacturing; European definition includes it
  • Logistics must operate 24/7/365 — customers take reliable delivery for granted
  • 2019: US spent $1.63T on logistics; ~65% on transportation (truck dominant at $680B)
  • Any service level is achievable — the constraint is economics, not technology

Logistics Objectives: rapid response · minimize variance · minimize inventory · consolidate shipments · continuous improvement · support full product lifecycle

Enabling Tactics:

  • Coordinate functions: cross-functional teams; improvements in one area affect others
  • Integrate the SC: right countries → warehouse locations → transport modes → partners → IT
  • Substitute information for inventory: JIT, RFID/GPS tracking, postponement, cross-docking
  • Regulate partner count: fewer partners = more efficient; eliminate entire echelons if possible
  • Pool risks: centralized inventory statistically requires less safety stock than dispersed warehouses

Logistics Service Providers:

  • 1PL: company owns and operates its own fleet
  • 2PL: company plans routes; outsources vehicle ownership/operations
  • 3PL: third party performs some or all logistics functions; charges markup
  • 4PL: manages entire logistics operation; subcontracts specific functions; charges fee not markup
  • Trend: from transactional single-service 3PLs → long-term integrated relationships
