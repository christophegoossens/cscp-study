Overview

Logistics is the process of planning, implementing, and controlling the movement and storage of goods, services, and information from origin to destination. It is a core component of supply chain management.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Logistics: In an industrial context, logistics is the art and science of obtaining, producing, and distributing material and product in the proper place and in the proper quantities.
📖 Definition:
Logistics management: The part of supply chain management that plans, implements, and controls the efficient and effective forward and reverse flow and storage of goods, services, and related information between the point of origin and the point of consumption.
📖 Definition:
Third-party logistics (3PL): An outsourced provider of logistics services — transportation, warehousing, distribution, and related activities. A 3PL provider manages logistics on behalf of its clients.
📖 Definition:
Fourth-party logistics (4PL): A supply chain integrator that assembles and manages the resources, capabilities, and technology of its own organization and other organizations to deliver a comprehensive supply chain solution. A 4PL manages 3PL providers.

Logistics Activities

💡 Key Point:
The primary logistics activities: transportation, warehousing, inventory management, order processing, packaging, and logistics information management. Together these activities move products from point of production to point of consumption.
  • Inbound logistics: movement of materials from suppliers to the production facility
  • Outbound logistics: movement of finished goods from production to customers or DCs
  • Reverse logistics: return of goods from customers back through the supply chain
  • Cross-docking: transferring goods from inbound to outbound transport with minimal storage
  • Last-mile delivery: the final delivery from a distribution point to the end customer — typically the most expensive leg

Logistics Trade-offs

💡 Key Point:
The logistics cost-service trade-off: faster, more reliable transportation is more expensive. Logistics managers optimize the total system cost — transportation + inventory + warehousing — while meeting service level requirements.
  • Consolidation: combining small shipments into larger ones reduces per-unit freight cost but increases lead time
  • Intermodal transportation: using multiple transport modes (truck + rail, truck + ocean) to combine cost and service advantages
  • Postponement: delay product finalization to reduce logistics cost and risk

DEFINITION
What is the ASCM definition of logistics?
ANSWER
The art and science of obtaining, producing, and distributing material and product in the proper place and in the proper quantities.
DEFINITION
What is logistics management?
ANSWER
The part of supply chain management that plans, implements, and controls the efficient and effective forward and reverse flow and storage of goods, services, and information between origin and consumption.
DEFINITION
What is a third-party logistics (3PL) provider?
ANSWER
An outsourced provider of logistics services — transportation, warehousing, distribution, and related activities. Using 3PLs allows companies to focus on core competencies while accessing logistics expertise and scale.
DEFINITION
What is a fourth-party logistics (4PL) provider?
ANSWER
A supply chain integrator that manages 3PL providers and assembles comprehensive supply chain solutions on behalf of a client. Provides a single point of accountability for the entire logistics network.
CONCEPT
What are the primary logistics activities?
ANSWER
Transportation, warehousing, inventory management, order processing, packaging, and logistics information management. Together these activities bridge the gap between production and consumption.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between inbound and outbound logistics?
ANSWER
Inbound logistics: movement of materials from suppliers to the production facility. Outbound logistics: movement of finished goods from production to customers or distribution centers (DCs).
DEFINITION
What is cross-docking?
ANSWER
Transferring goods from inbound transport directly to outbound transport with minimal or no storage. Reduces handling and storage cost while maintaining flow. Requires tight timing coordination between inbound and outbound shipments.
DEFINITION
What is last-mile delivery?
ANSWER
The final delivery leg from a distribution point (DC or urban hub) to the end customer. Typically the most expensive and complex part of the delivery network — especially in e-commerce where deliveries go to individual homes.
CONCEPT
What is the logistics cost-service trade-off?
ANSWER
Faster, more reliable transportation is more expensive. Logistics managers optimize total system cost — transportation + inventory + warehousing — while meeting service level targets. More inventory can compensate for slower, cheaper transport.
DEFINITION
What is intermodal transportation?
ANSWER
Using two or more transport modes in a single shipment — e.g., truck to port, ocean freight, then rail/truck to destination. Enables cost optimization (ocean/rail for long distance) with service flexibility (truck for last mile).
CONCEPT
What is freight consolidation?
ANSWER
Combining multiple small shipments into a single, larger shipment to reduce per-unit freight cost. Full Truckload (FTL) is cheaper per unit than Less-than-Truckload (LTL). Consolidation increases savings but may increase lead time.
DEFINITION
What is reverse logistics?
ANSWER
The return of goods from customers back through the supply chain — including returns processing, refurbishment, recycling, and disposal. An increasingly important supply chain function in e-commerce where return rates can exceed 30%.
CONCEPT
What is the bullwhip effect's impact on logistics?
ANSWER
Demand variability amplified by the bullwhip effect creates peak/trough logistics requirements — requiring more peak transportation capacity than average demand would suggest. Poor demand management increases logistics cost and complexity.
CONCEPT
What is postponement in logistics?
ANSWER
Delaying product differentiation or movement until as late as possible — reducing logistics complexity and inventory risk. Example: maintaining undifferentiated product centrally, customizing only when shipped to a specific market.
DEFINITION
What is Total Logistics Cost?
ANSWER
The total cost of all logistics activities — transportation, warehousing, inventory holding, order processing, and reverse logistics — expressed as a dollar amount or percentage of revenue. The key financial metric for logistics optimization.
