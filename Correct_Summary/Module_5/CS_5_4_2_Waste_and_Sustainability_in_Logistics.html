Overview

Sustainability in logistics addresses the environmental and social impacts of freight movement, warehousing, and distribution. Green logistics initiatives reduce carbon emissions, fuel consumption, and waste while often reducing cost.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Sustainability: Meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. In supply chain: balancing economic performance with environmental stewardship and social responsibility.
📖 Definition:
Carbon footprint: The total greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions generated by an individual, organization, or supply chain — measured in CO2 equivalent (CO2e). Transportation is a major component of supply chain carbon footprint.
📖 Definition:
Green logistics: The effort to measure and minimize the ecological impact of logistics activities — reducing fuel consumption, emissions, packaging waste, and land use while maintaining operational performance.

Logistics Emissions

💡 Key Point:
Transportation is the largest source of logistics emissions. Mode selection has a major impact: ocean freight = lowest CO2 per ton-km; air freight = highest CO2 per ton-km (typically 50x ocean). Rail = much lower than truck. Shifting from air to ocean or truck to rail significantly reduces carbon footprint.
  • Fuel efficiency: aerodynamic improvements, route optimization, load maximization, electric/hydrogen vehicles
  • Route optimization: reducing total distance traveled — reduces fuel, time, and emissions simultaneously
  • Consolidation: combining shipments into fuller loads reduces the number of truck/ship movements needed
  • Intermodal: using lower-emission modes for long hauls reduces total transport emissions
  • Last-mile electrification: electric delivery vehicles reduce urban emissions (zero tailpipe)

Packaging Sustainability

📖 Definition:
Sustainable packaging: Packaging designed to reduce material use, increase recyclability, use recycled content, and minimize the overall environmental impact of protecting and delivering products.
  • Right-sizing: matching box size to product — eliminates void fill, reduces dimensional weight charges, and saves materials
  • Recycled content packaging: using post-consumer recycled materials in packaging
  • Reusable/returnable packaging: designing packaging for multiple trips (totes, pallets, containers) rather than single-use
  • Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR): regulations requiring producers to fund packaging recovery and recycling
💡 Key Point:
Scope 3 emissions: greenhouse gas emissions from a company's value chain that are not owned or controlled by the company — including upstream supplier emissions and downstream customer use. Logistics is a major source of Scope 3 emissions.

DEFINITION
What is sustainability in supply chain context?
ANSWER
Meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs — balancing economic performance with environmental stewardship (Planet) and social responsibility (People).
DEFINITION
What is a carbon footprint?
ANSWER
Total greenhouse gas emissions generated by a supply chain activity, measured in CO2 equivalent (CO2e). Transportation is a major contributor. Reducing carbon footprint is a key sustainability goal for supply chains.
DEFINITION
What is green logistics?
ANSWER
The effort to measure and minimize the ecological impact of logistics activities — reducing fuel consumption, GHG emissions, packaging waste, and land use while maintaining service and cost performance.
CONCEPT
How do transportation modes compare in carbon emissions per ton-km?
ANSWER
Air freight = highest (50x ocean). Truck = moderately high. Rail = much lower than truck. Ocean = lowest per ton-km. Shifting from air to ocean or truck to rail significantly reduces supply chain carbon footprint.
DEFINITION
What is Scope 3 emissions in supply chain?
ANSWER
Greenhouse gas emissions from a company's value chain that are not directly owned or controlled by the company — including supplier emissions (upstream) and customer use/disposal (downstream). Logistics is a major source of Scope 3 emissions.
CONCEPT
What are Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions?
ANSWER
Scope 1: direct emissions from company-owned sources (own fleet). Scope 2: indirect emissions from purchased energy (electricity). Scope 3: all other value chain emissions (suppliers, logistics providers, customer use). Scope 3 is typically the largest for manufacturers.
CONCEPT
What is right-sizing in sustainable packaging?
ANSWER
Matching box/container size to the product being shipped — eliminating unnecessary void fill, reducing dimensional weight charges, using less material, and enabling more packages per truck. Saves cost AND reduces environmental impact.
DEFINITION
What is sustainable packaging?
ANSWER
Packaging designed to minimize material use, maximize recyclability, use recycled content, and reduce overall environmental impact while adequately protecting the product. Also includes reusable/returnable packaging designs.
CONCEPT
What is Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) for packaging?
ANSWER
Regulations requiring producers to fund the collection, sorting, and recycling of packaging they place on the market. Common in Europe (EU Packaging Directive). Incentivizes manufacturers to design lighter, more recyclable packaging.
DEFINITION
What are reusable/returnable packaging systems?
ANSWER
Packaging designed for multiple uses — reusable crates, totes, pallets, or containers — rather than single-use. Reduces total material consumption and waste. Common in automotive JIT supply chains and grocery.
CONCEPT
How does freight consolidation support sustainability?
ANSWER
Consolidating multiple small shipments into fuller loads reduces the number of vehicle trips needed — fewer trips = less fuel consumed, less CO2 emitted, lower cost. Maximizing load utilization is both economical and green.
CONCEPT
What is route optimization in logistics sustainability?
ANSWER
Using software to calculate the most efficient delivery routes — minimizing total distance, fuel consumption, and emissions while meeting all delivery time windows. Modern TMS and route planning software applies this automatically.
CONCEPT
What is a low-emission zone (LEZ) and how does it affect urban logistics?
ANSWER
A restricted urban area where only low-emission or zero-emission vehicles may operate — typically ICE trucks are excluded. Forces urban logistics operators to invest in electric vehicles or use smaller, compliant vehicles for last-mile delivery.
DEFINITION
What is the triple bottom line in sustainability?
ANSWER
Measuring organizational performance across three dimensions: Economic (Profit), Environmental (Planet), and Social (People). Sustainability strategy must balance all three — not just minimize cost or carbon alone.
CONCEPT
What is a sustainability report and what supply chain data does it include?
ANSWER
An annual disclosure of environmental and social performance — GHG emissions (Scope 1/2/3), energy use, waste generated, water consumption, supplier labor practices, and community impact. Required by many investors and increasingly by regulation.
