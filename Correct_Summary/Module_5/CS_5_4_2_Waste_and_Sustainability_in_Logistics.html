Waste Hierarchy (The 4 Rs)
Waste hierarchy: A tool that ranks waste management options according to what is most environmentally sound. It gives top priority to preventing waste generation and last priority to disposal. Also called the reverse logistics hierarchy or the four Rs.

In order of environmental priority (most to least preferred):

  1. Reduce resource use
  2. Reuse products or components
  3. Recycle materials
  4. Recover energy (from disposal)
  5. (Dispose in responsible landfill — last resort)
Exhibit 5-32: Waste Hierarchy — ranking from most to least environmentally preferred
Reduce — Most Important Step

Reducing resource use in the first place is the most responsible option. Companies can reduce by:

  • Designing products and packaging for maximum material efficiency
  • Reducing energy consumption in manufacture and use
  • Designing logistics network for efficient use of resources
  • Reducing excess inventory via demand management and visibility
  • Repairing/restoring products rather than replacing them
40/30/30 rule: A rule that identifies the sources of scrap, rework, and waste as 40% product design, 30% manufacturing processing, and 30% from suppliers. Highlights the importance of waste prevention during the design phase.

The reduce step is preventive, ideally implemented during the design and development phase of a product’s life cycle.

Reuse — Second Priority
  • Resell returned products that pass quality control (repackaging and relocating as needed)
  • Donate excess inventory to charities (may provide tax benefit)
  • Remanufacture products to like-new condition (replace worn parts; sell at discount or in trade-in programs)
  • Sell byproducts to organizations that can use them as raw materials
  • Design for disassembly and recyclability — so materials and components can be easily separated
  • Intelligently design product upgrades to extend durable component life (e.g., software upgrades)
Recycle, Recover Energy & Responsible Disposal

Recycle: Third most important. Turn used materials back into usable raw materials. Problems: regional regulatory differences create diseconomies of scale; recycling infrastructure not always available (e.g., lithium-ion batteries).

Recover energy: Disposal with energy recovery (incineration with energy capture) doesn’t return physical materials to service but still provides value compared to landfill.

Responsible landfill: Landfill operations designed to turn waste into recoverable resources, minimize space consumed, and maximize the operational life of the landfill. Last resort — incineration without energy recovery is generally considered worse.
Waste, Hazardous Waste & Waste Exchange
Waste: 1) Any activity that does not add value to the good or service in the eyes of the consumer. 2) A by-product of a process or task with unique characteristics requiring special management control.
Hazardous waste: Waste, such as chemicals or nuclear material, that is hazardous to humans or animals and requires special handling.

Hazardous materials include: chemicals, wastewater, agricultural/human waste and sewage, radioactive materials, construction materials, industrial oils. Special transportation, storage, and disposal methods are required by regulation.

GHS (Globally Harmonized System):

GHS: An international standard created by the United Nations Economic Commission for Europe (UNECE) for classifying chemicals according to health and environmental standards. Specifies how to properly dispose of hazardous materials.

EU Regulations:

  • WEEE (Waste of Electrical and Electronic Equipment): Places the burden of disposing of computers and electronics on the manufacturers — extended producer responsibility.
  • RoHS (Restriction of Hazardous Substances): Aimed at the “reduce” step — restricts use of specific hazardous substances in electrical and electronic equipment during design/manufacture.
Waste exchange: 1) Arrangement in which companies exchange their wastes for the benefit of both parties. 2) An exchange service of valuable information about available waste materials. One organization’s by-product becomes another’s raw material.
Nonprofit information exchanges connect buyers with waste sellers, creating markets for by-products that would otherwise go to landfill. Either party may derive a profit.

ASCM definition: Waste hierarchy
ANSWER
A tool that ranks waste management options according to what is most environmentally sound. Gives top priority to preventing waste generation and last priority to disposal. Also called the reverse logistics hierarchy or the four Rs.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the four Rs of the waste hierarchy in order?
ANSWER
1) Reduce (most important — prevent waste at source), 2) Reuse products or components, 3) Recycle materials, 4) Recover energy from disposal. Responsible landfill is the last resort.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the 40/30/30 rule?
ANSWER
A rule identifying sources of scrap, rework, and waste: 40% from product design, 30% from manufacturing processing, 30% from suppliers. Emphasizes the critical importance of waste prevention during the design phase.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is ‘reduce’ the most preferred option in the waste hierarchy?
ANSWER
Preventing waste from being created in the first place is more efficient than managing it after the fact. It saves resources, energy, and disposal costs — and ideally happens during product design.
TERM / CONCEPT
What activities fall under ‘reuse’ in the waste hierarchy?
ANSWER
Reselling returned products, donating excess inventory, remanufacturing to like-new condition, selling by-products, designing for disassembly/recyclability, software upgrades to extend hardware life.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is remanufacturing?
ANSWER
Restoring a product to like-new condition by replacing worn parts with new parts — then selling at a discount or as part of a trade-in program. More value than recycling (retains more of original material value).

ASCM definition: Waste

1) Any activity that does not add value to the good or service in the eyes of the consumer. 2) A by-product of a process or task with unique characteristics requiring special management control.

ASCM definition: Hazardous waste

What is GHS?
ANSWER
Globally Harmonized System: an international standard by UNECE for classifying chemicals according to health and environmental standards. Specifies how to properly label, handle, and dispose of hazardous materials.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is WEEE legislation?
ANSWER
Waste of Electrical and Electronic Equipment: EU legislation placing the burden of disposing of computers and electronics on the manufacturers (extended producer responsibility).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is RoHS and how does it relate to the waste hierarchy?
ANSWER
Restriction of Hazardous Substances: EU directive restricting use of specific hazardous substances in electronics during design/manufacture. Targets the ‘reduce’ step — preventing hazardous materials from entering products in the first place.
TERM / CONCEPT
ASCM definition: Waste exchange
ANSWER
1) An arrangement in which companies exchange their wastes for the benefit of both parties. 2) An exchange service of valuable information about available waste materials — one company’s by-product becomes another’s raw material.
TERM / CONCEPT
ASCM definition: Responsible landfill
ANSWER
Landfill operations designed to turn waste into recoverable resources, minimize space consumed, and maximize the operational life of the landfill. Considered the last resort — incineration without energy recovery is generally considered worse.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is design for disassembly?
ANSWER
Designing products so their materials and components can be easily separated at end of life for reuse or recycling — reduces reverse logistics costs and enables more material to enter the reuse/recycle stream.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does the 40/30/30 rule inform supply chain strategy?
ANSWER
By showing that most waste originates in design (40%) and manufacturing/suppliers (60%), it directs investment in early-stage waste prevention rather than end-of-pipe solutions. Supplier quality programs and design for sustainability are both implicated.
