Purchase Orders & Invoicing
📖 ASCM Definition — Purchase Order (PO): the purchaser’s authorization used to formalize a purchase transaction with a supplier. When given to a supplier, it becomes a legally binding contract for both parties.
📖 ASCM Definition — Blanket Purchase Order: a long-term commitment to a supplier for material against which short-term releases will be generated to satisfy requirements. Often a year, covering multiple deliveries.

PO process: Purchase requisition → approval → PO creation → supplier acceptance → delivery → receiving → 3-way match (PO + receipt + invoice) → payment approval.

Blanket PO benefits: eliminates repeated competitive bidding; negotiates quantity discounts, delivery lead times, quality terms upfront; releases are made against the blanket as needed.

Order tracking functions:

  • Purchasing: primary party tracking open orders; report exceptions.
  • Sales: notify customers of delays.
  • Finance/Treasury: forecast future AP obligations.
  • Accounting: ensure financial record accuracy.
  • Receiving: forecast inbound workload and space.
  • Traffic: schedule carriers to avoid bottlenecks.

Expediting: rushing or chasing orders needed in less than normal lead time. Should be rare in well-managed systems — frequent expediting signals poor forecasting and increases costs. Achieved by asking suppliers to prioritize your orders above others.

E-Procurement: Portals & Trading Exchanges
📖 ASCM Definition — Portal: a multiservice website providing access to data secured by user role. Users can aggregate data and perform basic analysis. Provides faster access to information.

Types of portals:

  • Consumer portals: personalized home pages, email, shopping — for B2C.
  • Business portals: aggregate and analyze job-relevant data — intranets, extranets.
  • SRM portals: view/react to production changes on supplier availability and capacity — supplier collaboration.

Trading Exchanges: online B2B platforms where buyers and sellers transact. 4 ownership models:

  • Independent Public Trade Exchange (ITX): public marketplace, used for indirect materials and commodities, open to all. Many buyers, many sellers.
  • Private Trading Exchange (PTX): ASCM def: “trade exchange hosted by a single company to facilitate collaborative e-commerce with its trading partners. As opposed to public exchanges, data is secure.” Formed by market-dominant companies. High security, high cost.
  • Consortia Trade Exchange (CTX): ASCM def: “an online marketplace, usually owned by a third party, that allows members to trade with each other. Lowers members’ search and transaction costs.” Some-to-many — within same industry.
  • Virtual Trading Exchange: enables both information integration and e-commerce — deepest integration of systems.

Horizontal marketplace: buyers/sellers from multiple industries (lowers prices through broad competition). Vertical marketplace: buyers/sellers within same industry (lowers prices through industry-specific knowledge).

Auction Types & Exchange Benefits/Risks

5 types of auctions:

  • Classic (forward) auction: one seller, multiple buyers bid UP the price. Highest bid wins.
  • Reverse auction: suppliers bid DOWN the price to win the buyer’s business. Company identities may be concealed. Used for commodities.
  • Dutch auction: one seller, multiple buyers for multiple finite quantities. Price starts high and drops until all units are sold.
  • Demand management auction: clearinghouse to liquidate excess supply (e.g., hotel rooms, airline seats); buyers set their own prices.
  • Stock-market-style auction: dynamic pricing based on buy and sell offers; multiple buyers and sellers for same item simultaneously.

Buyer benefits of trading exchanges: better control over contracts, standard specifications, lower administrative costs, lower unit costs, faster response, improved catalog accuracy.

Buyer risks: lower quality goods, nonconformance to specifications, more rework/returns, long-term loss of skilled suppliers, adversarial relationships.

Supplier benefits: automatic connections, wider market access, faster payment, volume leverage, better catalogs.

Supplier risks: option contracts lock capacity without guaranteed business, tighter margins reduce process investment, risk of being driven out of business, buyer may use seller’s data for lower bids elsewhere.

What is the PO process (steps)?

What is a 3-way match?
ANSWER
Matching the Purchase Order + Goods Receipt + Supplier Invoice to verify accuracy before approving payment. Discrepancies trigger a receiving discrepancy report.

What is an Independent Public Trade Exchange (ITX)?
ANSWER

What services do trading exchanges typically offer?
ANSWER
Supplier/buyer broker services, product search, bidding events, cost-savings identification, access to larger markets, online catalogs, SRM services, automated connections, billing and payment processing, supplier certification.

When should a company use a private exchange (PTX)?
ANSWER
When high data security is required, transaction volumes are very high, and the company is a market-dominant buyer. High cost is justified by exclusive control, security, and deep supplier integration.
