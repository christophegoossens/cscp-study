Overview

Supply chain contracts define the terms under which goods and services are exchanged between buyer and supplier. Well-structured contracts protect both parties, enable collaboration, and allocate risk fairly.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Contract: A legally binding agreement between two or more parties that defines rights and obligations — including price, quantity, quality, delivery terms, payment terms, and remedies for breach.
📖 Definition:
Long-term contract: A purchasing agreement covering multiple years. Provides supply security for the buyer and demand stability for the supplier. Common for strategic materials.
📖 Definition:
Blanket purchase order: A long-term commitment to purchase a fixed quantity of goods from a supplier at an agreed price over a specified time period — with releases called off as needed.
📖 Definition:
Purchase order (PO): A commercial document and legal offer issued by a buyer to a supplier, specifying items, quantities, agreed prices, and delivery requirements.

Contract Types by Risk/Reward

💡 Key Point:
Fixed-price contracts: the buyer pays a set price regardless of supplier cost changes — maximum cost certainty for buyer, maximum cost risk for supplier. Best for stable, well-defined scope.
💡 Key Point:
Cost-plus contracts: the buyer pays the supplier's actual costs plus an agreed profit margin — maximum transparency for buyer, minimum cost risk for supplier. Best for uncertain scope or new technology development.
💡 Key Point:
Time-and-materials (T&M) contracts: buyer pays for actual time (labor rates) and materials used. Flexible but provides little cost certainty for the buyer.

Key Contract Terms

  • Incoterms: standardized international commercial terms defining who bears transportation cost and risk at each stage of delivery
  • Payment terms: Net 30, Net 60, 2/10 Net 30 (2% discount if paid within 10 days)
  • Force majeure: clause releasing parties from obligations due to extraordinary events beyond their control
  • Warranties: seller's promises about product quality and fitness for purpose
  • Intellectual property ownership: who owns designs, tooling, or technology developed under the contract
  • Penalty clauses: financial consequences for failing to meet performance standards
📖 Definition:
Incoterms: A set of internationally recognized commercial terms published by the International Chamber of Commerce (ICC) that define the responsibilities of buyers and sellers for delivery of goods under sales contracts. Examples: EXW, FOB, CIF, DDP.

DEFINITION
What is a contract in supply chain context?
ANSWER
A legally binding agreement defining rights and obligations — price, quantity, quality specifications, delivery terms, payment terms, IP ownership, and remedies for breach. Creates enforceable commitments between buyer and supplier.
DEFINITION
What is a fixed-price contract and when is it used?
ANSWER
A contract where the buyer pays a set price regardless of supplier cost changes. Best for stable, well-defined scope where costs are predictable. Buyer has cost certainty; supplier bears the cost risk.
DEFINITION
What is a cost-plus contract and when is it used?
ANSWER
A contract where the buyer pays the supplier's actual costs plus an agreed profit margin. Used for uncertain scope, R&D, or development work where costs cannot be predicted. Buyer bears cost risk; supplier has guaranteed margin.
DEFINITION
What is a blanket purchase order?
ANSWER
A long-term commitment to purchase specified quantities of goods at agreed prices over a period, with individual releases called off as needed. Reduces per-transaction overhead while maintaining pricing commitment.
DEFINITION
What are Incoterms?
ANSWER
Internationally recognized commercial terms (published by the ICC) defining buyer and seller responsibilities for delivery cost and risk at each stage. Examples: EXW (buyer picks up), FOB (seller delivers to port), DDP (seller delivers to destination, duty paid).
DEFINITION
What does FOB (Free on Board) mean?
ANSWER
Seller is responsible for delivering goods to a named shipping port; risk transfers to buyer when goods are loaded on the vessel. Buyer is responsible for ocean freight, insurance, and destination costs.
DEFINITION
What does DDP (Delivered Duty Paid) mean?
ANSWER
Seller is responsible for all delivery costs including transportation, insurance, customs clearance, and duties — delivering to the buyer's named destination. Maximum seller obligation under Incoterms; minimum buyer burden.
DEFINITION
What is a force majeure clause?
ANSWER
A contract provision releasing parties from obligations when extraordinary events beyond their control (natural disasters, wars, pandemics) make performance impossible. Protects both parties from liability for unforeseeable disruptions.
CONCEPT
What are payment terms and what is '2/10 Net 30'?
ANSWER
Payment terms define when payment is due. '2/10 Net 30' means a 2% discount is offered if paid within 10 days; full amount due in 30 days. Buyers must compare the discount value to their cost of capital.
DEFINITION
What is a long-term contract and what are its advantages?
ANSWER
A multi-year purchasing agreement. Advantages: supply security for buyer, demand stability for supplier, lower transaction costs, enables collaborative investment (tooling, capacity). Disadvantage: less flexibility to change suppliers or prices.
CONCEPT
What is intellectual property (IP) ownership in supplier contracts?
ANSWER
Contract terms specifying who owns designs, formulas, tooling, software, or other IP developed during the contract — the buyer, the supplier, or jointly. Critical for protecting proprietary technology from supplier access.
DEFINITION
What is a service level agreement (SLA)?
ANSWER
A contract component specifying measurable performance standards the supplier must meet — on-time delivery percentage, defect rate, response time — with defined consequences (credits, termination) for failure to meet them.
DEFINITION
What is a penalty clause in a supply contract?
ANSWER
A contract provision imposing financial consequences on the supplier for failing to meet specified performance standards — delivery delays, quality failures, or minimum volume shortfalls. Creates financial accountability.
DEFINITION
What is a termination-for-convenience clause?
ANSWER
A contract provision allowing the buyer to end the contract at any time with proper notice, even if the supplier has not breached the agreement. Provides buyer flexibility but may require compensation to the supplier for stranded costs.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between a contract and a purchase order?
ANSWER
A contract is a comprehensive legal agreement establishing the terms of an ongoing relationship. A purchase order is a transactional document placing a specific order for goods under those terms. The contract governs; the PO executes.

Question 1
A fixed-price contract is most appropriate when:
A.Requirements are uncertain
B.Scope and specifications are well-defined and stable
C.The supplier faces high cost volatility
D.Speed is the priority
Fixed-price contracts work best when scope is clear and stable, transferring cost risk to the supplier.
Question 2
A cost-plus contract shifts cost risk to:
A.The supplier
B.The buyer
C.Both parties equally
D.A third-party insurer
In cost-plus contracts, the buyer reimburses all allowable costs plus a fee — the buyer bears cost risk.
Question 3
An option to purchase in a contract gives:
A.The supplier the right to refuse orders
B.The buyer the right to purchase additional quantities at a predetermined price
C.Both parties price renegotiation rights
D.Automatic contract renewal
An option to purchase gives the buyer the right (not obligation) to buy additional volume at a negotiated price.
Question 4
A take-or-pay contract requires:
A.The buyer to pay only if goods are taken
B.The buyer to pay a minimum amount whether or not the volume is purchased
C.The supplier to guarantee delivery or pay penalties
D.Annual price renegotiation
Take-or-pay contracts commit the buyer to purchase a minimum volume or pay for it regardless — protecting the supplier's investment in capacity.
