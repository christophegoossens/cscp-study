Overview

Supply planning translates demand requirements into procurement and production plans. Material Requirements Planning (MRP) is the foundational logic for calculating what to order, how much, and when — based on dependent demand.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Material Requirements Planning (MRP): A set of techniques that uses the Bill of Materials (BOM), on-hand inventory data, and the Master Production Schedule (MPS) to calculate requirements for materials. Generates planned purchase orders and production orders.
📖 Definition:
Master Production Schedule (MPS): A plan for production of specific end items. It is disaggregated from the S&OP plan and drives the MRP calculation. States what finished products will be produced, in what quantities, and when.
📖 Definition:
Bill of Materials (BOM): A list of all components, subassemblies, and raw materials required to manufacture one unit of a finished product — including quantities and structure (parent-child relationships).
📖 Definition:
Planned order: An MRP-generated order (purchase or production) that is not yet firmed or released. The planner reviews planned orders before releasing them to suppliers or the shop floor.
📖 Definition:
Time fence: A policy/planning parameter that defines the flexibility window around the MPS. Demand time fence: no changes without management approval. Planning time fence: changes allowed but may require expediting.

MRP Logic

💡 Key Point:
MRP answers three questions: 1) WHAT to order (determined by BOM), 2) HOW MUCH to order (net requirement = gross requirement − on-hand − scheduled receipts), 3) WHEN to order (due date − lead time = order release date).
  • Gross requirement: total need for a component from MRP explosion of parent demand
  • Scheduled receipt: a purchase or production order already in progress
  • Projected on-hand: on-hand + scheduled receipts − gross requirements
  • Net requirement: gross requirement − available inventory (negative projected on-hand)
  • Planned order release: net requirement offset by lead time
💡 Key Point:
MRP limitations: it is infinite capacity (assumes unlimited capacity), it is static (must be re-run to reflect changes), and it is deterministic (does not handle demand uncertainty — safety stock must be added separately).
📖 Definition:
Manufacturing Resource Planning (MRP II): An extension of MRP that incorporates capacity planning, financial planning, and simulation. The predecessor to modern ERP systems.

DEFINITION
What is Material Requirements Planning (MRP)?
ANSWER
A set of techniques using the BOM, on-hand inventory, and Master Production Schedule (MPS) to calculate material requirements. Generates planned purchase orders and production orders for dependent demand items.
CONCEPT
What are the three questions MRP answers?
ANSWER
1) WHAT to order (from BOM structure), 2) HOW MUCH to order (net requirement = gross requirement − on-hand − scheduled receipts), 3) WHEN to order (due date − lead time = planned order release date).
DEFINITION
What is the Master Production Schedule (MPS)?
ANSWER
A time-phased plan for production of specific end items, disaggregated from the S&OP aggregate plan. States WHAT will be produced, HOW MUCH, and WHEN — the primary driver of MRP calculations.
DEFINITION
What is a Bill of Materials (BOM)?
ANSWER
A hierarchical list of all components, subassemblies, and raw materials required to produce one unit of a finished product — including quantities and parent-child relationships. The structural input to MRP.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between gross requirements and net requirements in MRP?
ANSWER
Gross requirement: total demand for a component from MRP explosion. Net requirement: actual amount to order = gross requirement − projected on-hand inventory. Net requirements drive planned orders.
DEFINITION
What is a scheduled receipt in MRP?
ANSWER
A purchase order or production order already placed and expected to arrive on a specific date. Counts as available supply in MRP calculations — reduces the net requirement for the arrival period.
DEFINITION
What is a planned order in MRP?
ANSWER
An MRP-generated order recommendation (purchase or production) that has not yet been firmed or released. The planner reviews and approves planned orders before they become actual purchase orders or work orders.
DEFINITION
What is a time fence in MRP/MPS?
ANSWER
A planning parameter defining the flexibility window. Demand time fence (DTF): changes within this window require management approval. Planning time fence (PTF): changes allowed but may require expediting. Protects the MPS from constant instability.
KEY LIMITATION
What is the key limitation of MRP regarding capacity?
ANSWER
MRP is infinite capacity — it calculates what materials are needed without checking whether production capacity exists to build them. Capacity planning (Rough-Cut Capacity Planning, CRP) must be done separately to validate feasibility.
CONCEPT
What does 'lead time offsetting' mean in MRP?
ANSWER
Working backward from when a component is needed (due date) by subtracting its lead time to determine when to place the order (order release date). This is how MRP determines planned order release dates.
DEFINITION
What is Manufacturing Resource Planning (MRP II)?
ANSWER
An extension of MRP II that incorporates capacity planning, labor planning, financial simulation, and business planning — the predecessor to modern ERP systems. Connects manufacturing to the financial plan.
CONCEPT
What is an explosion in BOM/MRP context?
ANSWER
The process of breaking down a parent item's requirements into its component requirements using the BOM structure. The MPS quantity for the finished good is 'exploded' through the BOM to calculate gross requirements for all components.
CONCEPT
What is lot sizing in MRP?
ANSWER
The method for determining the quantity to order when a net requirement exists. Options include: lot-for-lot (order exactly what's needed), fixed order quantity (always order a set amount), Economic Order Quantity (EOQ), or minimum order quantity (MOQ).
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between MRP and reorder point (ROP) systems?
ANSWER
MRP: calculates requirements from demand and BOM — used for dependent demand items. Reorder point: triggers an order when inventory falls below a set level — used for independent demand items. MRP is forward-looking; ROP is reactive.
DEFINITION
What is a firm planned order in MRP?
ANSWER
An MRP planned order that has been 'firmed' — the planner has manually committed to the quantity and date, preventing MRP from automatically changing it. Used when a supplier commitment has been made or production has started.

Question 1
MRP converts the MPS into:
A.Customer order confirmations
B.Time-phased material requirements using the BOM and inventory records
C.Financial budget allocations
D.Production schedules at work centres
MRP takes the MPS, explodes it through the BOM, and generates time-phased planned orders for all required materials.
Question 2
Which is NOT an MRP input?
A.Bill of Materials
B.Inventory records
C.Supplier financial statements
D.Master Production Schedule
MRP requires MPS, BOM, and inventory records. Supplier financials are not an MRP input.
Question 3
Net requirements in MRP are:
A.Gross requirements only
B.Gross requirements minus on-hand inventory and scheduled receipts
C.Total demand plus safety stock
D.Planned order receipts
Net Requirements = Gross Requirements - On-Hand Inventory - Scheduled Receipts. This is the quantity that must be ordered.
Question 4
Safety stock in supply planning:
A.Eliminates all stockouts
B.Protects against demand and supply uncertainty by holding buffer inventory
C.Replaces MRP
D.Is only used in retail
Safety stock is held as a buffer against variability in demand and/or supply, calculated based on desired service level and lead time.
