Overview

Quality management in the supply chain ensures that products and services meet specifications from raw materials through delivery. Quality standards and certifications provide external validation of a supplier's quality management system.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Quality: Conformance to requirements; the degree to which a product or service meets customer expectations. In supply chain, quality applies to materials received, processes executed, and products delivered.
📖 Definition:
Quality management system (QMS): A formalized system documenting processes, procedures, and responsibilities for achieving quality policies and objectives. ISO 9001 is the most widely adopted international QMS standard.
📖 Definition:
ISO 9001: The international standard for Quality Management Systems published by the International Organization for Standardization. Certification demonstrates that a supplier has documented and auditable quality processes.
📖 Definition:
Six Sigma: A quality improvement methodology targeting less than 3.4 defects per million opportunities (DPMO). Uses DMAIC (Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control) methodology for process improvement.

Quality Management Concepts

💡 Key Point:
Prevention vs. inspection: quality prevention (building quality into the process) is more cost-effective than quality inspection (detecting defects after production). The 'cost of quality' includes prevention costs, appraisal costs, internal failure costs, and external failure costs.
  • Incoming inspection: inspecting materials received from suppliers before accepting them into inventory
  • Supplier quality audits: formal assessments of a supplier's quality management system and processes
  • Statistical Process Control (SPC): using statistical methods to monitor process performance and detect when processes go out of control
  • Control charts: graphical tools showing process variation over time — used in SPC
  • Acceptance sampling: inspecting a sample from a batch to decide whether to accept or reject the batch

Industry Standards

📖 Definition:
ISO 14001: The international standard for Environmental Management Systems. Certification demonstrates that a supplier systematically manages environmental impacts.
📖 Definition:
ISO 45001: The international standard for Occupational Health and Safety Management Systems. Demonstrates systematic management of workplace safety.
💡 Key Point:
Total Quality Management (TQM): a management philosophy where every employee at every level is responsible for quality. Quality is built in, not inspected in. Involves continuous improvement, customer focus, and employee engagement.

DEFINITION
What is quality in supply chain context?
ANSWER
Conformance to requirements — the degree to which a product or service meets customer specifications and expectations. Applies to incoming materials, manufacturing processes, and finished goods delivered to customers.
DEFINITION
What is a Quality Management System (QMS)?
ANSWER
A formalized system of documented processes, procedures, and responsibilities for achieving quality policies and objectives. ISO 9001 is the most widely adopted international standard for QMS certification.
DEFINITION
What is ISO 9001?
ANSWER
The international standard for Quality Management Systems. Certification demonstrates that an organization has documented, auditable processes for consistently meeting customer and regulatory requirements. Widely required by major manufacturers from their suppliers.
DEFINITION
What is Six Sigma?
ANSWER
A quality methodology targeting less than 3.4 defects per million opportunities (DPMO). Uses the DMAIC cycle (Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control) for systematic process improvement. Originally developed by Motorola, popularized by GE.
CONCEPT
What is the DMAIC cycle in Six Sigma?
ANSWER
Define (the problem and customer requirements), Measure (current process performance), Analyze (root causes of defects), Improve (implement solutions), Control (sustain the improvement with monitoring systems). The core Six Sigma improvement methodology.
CONCEPT
What is the cost of quality framework?
ANSWER
Total quality cost includes: Prevention costs (training, process design), Appraisal costs (inspection, testing), Internal failure costs (rework, scrap), External failure costs (returns, warranty, reputation damage). Prevention is far cheaper than failure.
DEFINITION
What is Statistical Process Control (SPC)?
ANSWER
Using statistical methods — primarily control charts — to monitor process performance over time and detect when a process goes out of control. Enables proactive intervention before defects reach the customer.
DEFINITION
What is a control chart?
ANSWER
A graphical tool showing process measurements over time with control limits (typically ±3 standard deviations). Points outside limits or non-random patterns indicate the process is 'out of control' — requiring investigation.
DEFINITION
What is Total Quality Management (TQM)?
ANSWER
A management philosophy where every employee at every level is responsible for quality. Key principles: quality built in (not inspected in), continuous improvement culture, customer focus, data-driven decisions, and employee involvement.
DEFINITION
What is supplier quality auditing?
ANSWER
A formal assessment of a supplier's quality management system, processes, facilities, and records. Verifies that the supplier's QMS meets requirements and actually operates as documented. Types: desktop audit, on-site audit, third-party audit.
DEFINITION
What is acceptance sampling?
ANSWER
Inspecting a statistical sample from a batch of received goods to decide whether to accept or reject the entire batch. More efficient than 100% inspection for large quantities. Governed by AQL (Acceptable Quality Level) standards.
DEFINITION
What is Acceptable Quality Level (AQL)?
ANSWER
The maximum percentage of defective items considered acceptable in a lot. AQL is used in acceptance sampling plans — higher AQL tolerates more defects; lower AQL requires stricter sampling. Defined in contract or industry standards.
DEFINITION
What is ISO 14001?
ANSWER
The international standard for Environmental Management Systems. Certification demonstrates that an organization systematically identifies, monitors, and manages its environmental impacts — increasingly required in supply chain sustainability programs.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between quality assurance and quality control?
ANSWER
Quality assurance (QA): proactive, system-level activities to prevent defects — process design, training, SPC. Quality control (QC): reactive, product-level inspection and testing to detect defects after they occur. QA prevents; QC detects.
CONCEPT
What is incoming quality inspection and when should it be replaced?
ANSWER
Incoming inspection checks received materials for conformance before accepting into inventory. As supplier relationships mature and suppliers demonstrate consistent quality (SPC data, certifications), companies can reduce or eliminate incoming inspection — relying on supplier quality systems.
