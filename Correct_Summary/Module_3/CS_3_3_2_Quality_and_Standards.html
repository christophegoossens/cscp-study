Quality Design Methods
📖 ASCM Definition — Quality: conformance to requirements or fitness for use. 5 approaches: (1) Transcendent quality — ideal/excellence. (2) Product-based — based on product attribute. (3) User-based — fitness for use. (4) Manufacturing-based — conformance to requirements. (5) Value-based — excellence at an acceptable price.

Quality has 2 major components: quality of conformance (absence of defects) and quality of design (product designed to meet customer needs).

📖 ASCM Definition — Design for Quality: a product design approach that uses quality measures to capture the extent to which the design meets the needs of the target market.
📖 ASCM Definition — Design for Six Sigma: an approach to designing products and processes that attempts to ensure the firm can provide products or services that meet six sigma quality levels (3.4 defects per million opportunities).
📖 ASCM Definition — Quality Function Deployment (QFD): a methodology to ensure all major customer requirements are identified and subsequently met or exceeded through the final design of a product or service.

QFD extends beyond design to operations and support. QFD benefits: shows relative levels of interactions between desired characteristics so they can be prioritized when in conflict.

Customization: Postponement, Mass Customization & Glocalization
📖 ASCM Definition — Postponement (Delayed Differentiation): a product design or supply chain strategy that deliberately delays final differentiation (assembly, production, packaging, tagging) until the latest possible time, thereby reducing forecasting errors and inventory costs.
📖 ASCM Definition — Product Differentiation: a strategy of making a product distinct from competition on a nonprice basis such as availability, durability, quality, or reliability.

Postponement is a push-pull strategy: product/components are produced in undifferentiated form (push) until customer demand triggers final differentiation (pull). Reduces bullwhip effect — fewer varieties of safety stock needed.

📖 ASCM Definition — Mass Customization: the use of mass production techniques to create large volumes of products in a wide variety. Keeps production costs low while enabling product differentiation.

Mass customization example: HP printers — generic base product shipped globally; power supplies and manuals added at local distribution centers for each market. Requires modular design + real-time communication across supply chain.

📖 ASCM Definition — Glocalization: in a supply chain context, a form of postponement in which a product or service is developed for distribution globally but modified to meet the needs of a local market.

Examples: McDonald’s local menus, GE handheld ECG for rural India (later sold globally = reverse innovation), MTV local music content, Unilever Lipton tea.

Reverse Innovation: product developed for emerging markets (cheaper, simpler) that is then sold successfully in developed markets. Challenges GE’s assumption that developing countries follow the same evolution as wealthy ones.

Sustainable Design
📖 ASCM Definition — Design for the Environment (DFE): considering health, safety, and environmental aspects of a product during the design and development phases to reduce environmental impact throughout its life cycle.

DFE considerations:

  • Provision for reuse or recycling (e.g., motor oil recycling at service stations)
  • Reduced energy consumption (hybrid engines, energy-efficient appliances)
  • Avoidance of hazardous materials (lead-free paint and gasoline)
  • Use of lighter components and less material (lighter car = better fuel economy)
📖 ASCM Definition — Design for Remanufacture: products developed in a manner that allows components to be used in other products. Associated with green manufacturing. Enables lower-cost remanufactured products vs. new — lower customer cost, lower environmental impact.

Design for Reverse Logistics: packaging designed to reduce consumer frustration on returns; simple return process; easy disassembly for repair/replacement. Requires a replacement parts supply chain.

Why is postponement a push-pull strategy?
ANSWER
Push phase: produce undifferentiated base product/components in bulk (efficient). Pull phase: final differentiation triggered by actual customer demand (responsive). Separates economies of scale from customization.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does postponement reduce the bullwhip effect?
ANSWER
By aggregating demand for the undifferentiated base product, forecasting is more accurate. Fewer varieties of safety stock are needed, reducing inventory amplification through the supply chain.

Benefits of mass customization
ANSWER
Economies of scale savings, increased worker efficiency, increased sales volume (different segments), reduced inventory (aggregated demand improves forecast accuracy), local job creation.
TERM / CONCEPT
Tradeoffs of postponement
ANSWER
Requires process, equipment, product, and packaging redesign capital expenditures. Can increase costs if there are few end-product varieties (benefit only comes with variety). Longer supply chain lead times possible.
