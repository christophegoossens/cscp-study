Overview

Supply base management determines how many suppliers to use, how to structure supplier relationships, and how to develop the supply base over time to support supply chain objectives.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Supply base: The group of suppliers that a company uses to purchase goods and services. Managing the supply base involves determining the optimal number of suppliers, their relationship types, and development activities.
📖 Definition:
Supply base rationalization: The process of reducing the number of suppliers a company uses — concentrating volume with fewer, more capable suppliers to achieve better pricing, service, and relationship depth.
📖 Definition:
Single sourcing: The deliberate decision to purchase from only one supplier for a specific item or service. Provides leverage and collaboration depth, but creates supply risk.
📖 Definition:
Sole sourcing: A situation where only one supplier is capable of providing the item — not a deliberate choice, but a market reality (patent holder, proprietary technology).
📖 Definition:
Dual sourcing: Using two suppliers for the same item to balance risk reduction against volume leverage. Provides a backup source while maintaining primary supplier relationship.

Supply Base Strategy

💡 Key Point:
Single vs. multiple sourcing trade-off: single source = lower unit cost, deeper relationship, more collaboration, but 100% dependency risk. Multiple sources = risk mitigation and price competition, but less leverage and more management overhead.
  • Supplier tiers: Tier 1 suppliers directly supply the manufacturer; Tier 2 supply Tier 1; Tier 3 supply Tier 2
  • Supply base rationalization is a primary lever for cost reduction in procurement
  • Preferred supplier status: a designation given to top-performing suppliers, giving them priority for new business
  • Approved supplier list (ASL): the qualified list of suppliers from which purchases may be made
  • Supplier development: investing in a supplier's capabilities to improve their performance
💡 Key Point:
Strategic vs. commodity suppliers: strategic suppliers provide critical, hard-to-substitute materials or services and warrant deep collaboration. Commodity suppliers are interchangeable and should be managed for cost efficiency.

DEFINITION
What is the supply base?
ANSWER
The group of suppliers that a company uses to purchase goods and services. Managing the supply base involves optimizing the number of suppliers, structuring relationship types, and developing supplier capabilities.
DEFINITION
What is supply base rationalization?
ANSWER
The process of reducing the number of active suppliers — concentrating volume with fewer, more capable partners to achieve better pricing, improved service levels, and more collaborative relationships.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between single sourcing and sole sourcing?
ANSWER
Single sourcing: a deliberate decision to buy from only one supplier when alternatives exist. Sole sourcing: forced by market reality — only one supplier is capable (patent holder, unique technology). Single sourcing is strategic; sole sourcing is a constraint.
DEFINITION
What is dual sourcing?
ANSWER
Using two suppliers for the same item to balance supply risk reduction against volume leverage. Provides a backup source and some pricing competition while maintaining depth with the primary supplier.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the fundamental trade-off between single and multiple sourcing?
ANSWER
Single source: lower unit cost, deeper partnership, more collaboration potential, but 100% supply dependency risk. Multiple sources: mitigated risk and price competition, but less leverage per supplier and higher management complexity.
DEFINITION
What is an Approved Supplier List (ASL)?
ANSWER
The qualified list of suppliers from which purchases may be made. Suppliers on the ASL have passed qualification requirements (quality audit, financial stability, compliance). Buyers must use ASL suppliers for standard items.
DEFINITION
What is preferred supplier status?
ANSWER
A designation given to high-performing suppliers, granting them priority consideration for new business, access to the company's roadmap, and collaborative development opportunities. Incentivizes suppliers to continuously improve.
DEFINITION
What is supplier development?
ANSWER
The process of investing in a supplier's capabilities — through training, process improvement support, co-investment, or technology sharing — to improve their quality, delivery, cost, or compliance performance.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between a strategic supplier and a commodity supplier?
ANSWER
Strategic supplier: provides critical, hard-to-substitute materials or services — warrants deep collaboration, joint development, and long-term relationships. Commodity supplier: provides interchangeable goods — managed primarily for cost efficiency.
CONCEPT
What are supplier tiers and why do they matter?
ANSWER
Tier 1 = direct suppliers to the manufacturer. Tier 2 = suppliers to Tier 1. Tier 3 = suppliers to Tier 2. Understanding tiers matters for supply chain risk management — a Tier 2 disruption affects Tier 1 ability to supply you.
CONCEPT
What is the preferred number of suppliers from a risk vs. efficiency perspective?
ANSWER
No universal answer — depends on item strategic importance, market concentration, switching cost, and company's risk tolerance. Strategic items: 2-3 qualified suppliers. Commodity items: managed through rationalization for cost efficiency.
CONCEPT
What does 'supply base rationalization saves cost' mean in practice?
ANSWER
Consolidating volume from 10 suppliers to 3 gives each of the 3 more volume, enabling quantity discounts, lower unit costs, and lower procurement/management overhead. Fewer supplier relationships to maintain reduces transaction and administration cost.
CONCEPT
What is a supply risk from single sourcing?
ANSWER
If the sole supplier experiences disruption (fire, financial failure, capacity shortage), the buyer has no alternative and production or service stops. Recovery time is much longer than with a dual-source arrangement.
CONCEPT
What is a second source qualification and why does it take time?
ANSWER
Qualifying an alternative supplier requires: RFP process, technical audit, sample testing, pilot orders, and full production validation. This process typically takes months — meaning single-source risk cannot be mitigated quickly.
CONCEPT
What is a supplier segmentation approach?
ANSWER
Dividing the supply base into segments (strategic, preferred, approved, conditional) based on spend, criticality, and performance. Each segment receives different management intensity — strategic suppliers get executive engagement, conditional suppliers get improvement plans.
