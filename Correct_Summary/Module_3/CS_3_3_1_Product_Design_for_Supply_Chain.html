Product Design Objectives & Approaches

Design expenses = ~5–15% of product cost, but determine ~70% of total cost. The 6 design objectives:

  • Functionality: fitness for use / ability to satisfy customer requirements.
  • Validity: ratio of desired to unimportant functions relative to competition.
  • Efficiency: ability to design, manufacture, and distribute at a competitive price.
  • Quality: reliable, maintainable, available — a value judgment.
  • Serviceability: ability to maintain the product over its expected life (upgrades, repairs).
  • Returnability/Recyclability: warranty and return processes, sustainability at end of life.

Traditional (sequential) design: Marketing → Engineering → Purchasing → Production (over-the-wall). Problems build in unknowingly; multiple costly redesign rounds.

Collaborative design: Cross-functional team (engineering, purchasing, production, logistics, suppliers, customers) from the start. Everyone agrees before production begins — fewer cost overruns, faster to market, higher quality.

Broad-Based Design Methods
Design for the Supply Chain: enhancement of a product design in consideration of the issues that will arise in the supply chain — faster inventory turnover, lower costs, reduced transit time, quick response.

Design for Logistics (DFL): minimizes supply chain costs by:

  • Minimizing transportation & storage costs (standard box sizes, higher density, lighter weight, stackable packaging)
  • Minimizing manufacture and assembly time
  • Maximizing standardization
Design for X (DFX): a design process ensuring the outcome is manufacturable, maintainable, cost-effective, environmentally sound, and user-friendly. Also called “design for everything.”
Standardization: the process of designing and altering products, parts, processes, and procedures to establish and use standard specifications.
Modular Design Strategy: planning and designing products so that components or subassemblies can be used in current and future products, or assembled to produce multiple configurations.
Modularization: in product development, the use of standardized parts for flexibility and variety. Permits cost reduction through interchangeable components.

3 types of standardization:

  • Modular design: parts used in multiple products; enables postponement and mass customization. Opposite = integral design (Apple iPhone — all components custom for one product).
  • Component commonality: one part replaces a variety of similar parts — fewer SKUs, lower purchasing cost (economies of scale), simpler storage, streamlined production.
  • Universality: designing a product for one market so it can also be sold in others — larger volume, lower unit cost. Tradeoff: may be less suited to any single market.
Simplification Methods

Simplification = improving quality and cutting costs by removing complexity.

Concurrent Engineering (CE): design events happen in parallel rather than sequentially — shortens design cycle. Now largely replaced by DFMA.

Design for Manufacture and Assembly (DFMA): a product development approach that involves manufacturing in initial design stages to ensure ease of manufacturing and assembly. Goals: select materials for ease of production, avoid tight tolerances, reduce part count, reduce handling, use parallel processing, make assembly obvious and easy, simplify process steps, design in easy testing.

DFMA is a further development of CE that also includes suppliers, manufacturing engineers, and warehouse managers.

Design for Service (Design for Maintainability): simplification of parts and processes to improve the after-sale service of a product.

Design for service: product can be maintained, repaired, or upgraded easily. If maintenance is simple, customers have better experiences post-purchase. Also includes logistics of replacement parts supply.

What are the 6 product design objectives?
ANSWER
1. Functionality (fitness for use). 2. Validity (desired vs. unimportant functions ratio). 3. Efficiency (cost-effective to design/make/distribute). 4. Quality (reliable, maintainable). 5. Serviceability (maintainable over life). 6. Returnability/Recyclability (returns, sustainability).
TERM / CONCEPT
Design cost vs. cost determination rule
ANSWER
Design expenses = ~5–15% of product cost, but determine ~70% of total product cost. Getting design right early is the highest-leverage cost reduction opportunity.
TERM / CONCEPT
Traditional (sequential) design — problems?
ANSWER
Marketing → Engineering → Purchasing → Production (“over the wall”). Problems build in unknowingly; multiple redesign rounds; expensive late changes; slow to market.
TERM / CONCEPT
Benefits of collaborative design (vs. traditional)
ANSWER
Fewer cost overruns, new design approaches from supplier expertise, improved customer satisfaction, faster time to market, higher product quality. All functions agree before production begins.

TERM / CONCEPT
Modular vs. integral design
ANSWER
Modular: parts interchangeable across products (e.g., PC components). Integral: all components custom for one specific product (e.g., Apple iPhone — aesthetic, quality, fit & finish prioritized over modularity).
TERM / CONCEPT
Benefits of component commonality
ANSWER
Lower purchasing cost (economies of scale), streamlined production (fewer process/tool changes), simpler and cheaper storage, fewer SKUs, reduced errors.
TERM / CONCEPT
ASCM def: Design for Manufacture and Assembly (DFMA)
ANSWER
A product development approach that involves manufacturing in the initial design stages to ensure ease of manufacturing and assembly. Goals: reduce part count, avoid tight tolerances, simplify assembly, design for easy testing.
TERM / CONCEPT
7 goals of DFMA
ANSWER
1. Select materials for ease of production. 2. Avoid tight tolerances. 3. Reduce part count. 4. Reduce handling instances. 5. Use concurrent/parallel processing. 6. Make assembly obvious/easy. 7. Design in easy testing.
TERM / CONCEPT
ASCM def: Design for Service (Maintainability)
ANSWER
Simplification of parts and processes to improve after-sale service of a product. Lowers total cost of ownership; requires logistics of replacement parts supply.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is concurrent engineering (CE)?
ANSWER
Design events happen in parallel rather than sequentially — shortens design cycle. Emphasizes design collaboration. Now largely replaced by the more complete DFMA methodology.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is universality in product design?
ANSWER
Designing a product initially for one market so it can also be sold in others. Increases sales volume and reduces unit cost. Tradeoff: may be less suitable to any specific market than a specialized product.
TERM / CONCEPT
Benefits of modular design
ANSWER
Reduced cost of design/manufacturing, increased production efficiency, expanded customer base (customization possible closer to end user), easier/cheaper shipping & warehousing.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the over-the-wall problem?
ANSWER
In traditional sequential design, each department completes its work and passes it to the next without feedback. Problems are discovered late — expensive to fix. Collaborative design eliminates this.
