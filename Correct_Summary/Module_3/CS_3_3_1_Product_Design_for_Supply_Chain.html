Overview

Product design decisions have a profound impact on supply chain cost, complexity, and performance. Design for Supply Chain (DFSC) integrates supply chain considerations into the product design process from the beginning.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Design for Supply Chain (DFSC): A product design approach that considers supply chain implications — sourcing, manufacturing, transportation, and end-of-life — during the design phase rather than after the design is fixed.
📖 Definition:
Design for Manufacturability (DFM): A product design approach that simplifies manufacturing processes, reduces part count, and improves quality by designing products that are easier to build.
📖 Definition:
Modular design: Designing products using standardized, interchangeable subassemblies (modules) that can be combined in different ways to create product variety. Enables efficient Assemble-to-Order (ATO) strategies.
📖 Definition:
Postponement: A product design or supply chain strategy where the final configuration of a product is delayed until the latest possible point before customer delivery. Reduces inventory by keeping products in an undifferentiated state longer.

Key Design Strategies

💡 Key Point:
Platform design: creating a common product platform (shared architecture, components, or processes) from which multiple variants can be derived. Reduces total component count and purchasing complexity while enabling product variety.
💡 Key Point:
Parts standardization: using the same component across multiple products reduces the number of unique items, lowers minimum order quantities needed per item, increases volume per item for pricing leverage, and simplifies inventory management.
  • New Product Introduction (NPI): the process of bringing a new product to market — design, prototype, test, pilot production, ramp-up, full launch
  • Product lifecycle: introduction → growth → maturity → decline. Each stage has different supply chain implications
  • Design for disassembly: designing products for easy end-of-life teardown and recycling/reuse
  • Value engineering: systematic analysis of product functions to reduce cost while maintaining performance
  • Early supplier involvement (ESI): involving key suppliers in the design process to leverage their manufacturing knowledge

DEFINITION
What is Design for Supply Chain (DFSC)?
ANSWER
A product design approach that considers supply chain implications (sourcing, manufacturing, transportation, end-of-life) during the design phase. Goal: design products that are easy and cost-efficient to produce, source, and deliver.
DEFINITION
What is Design for Manufacturability (DFM)?
ANSWER
Designing products to simplify manufacturing — reducing part count, using standardized fasteners, minimizing assembly steps, and eliminating complex operations. Results in lower manufacturing cost, faster assembly, and fewer quality defects.
DEFINITION
What is modular design?
ANSWER
Designing products using standardized, interchangeable subassemblies (modules) that can be combined in different configurations. Enables product variety without proportional supply chain complexity — ideal for Assemble-to-Order (ATO) strategies.
DEFINITION
What is postponement in product design?
ANSWER
A strategy that delays final product differentiation (configuration, labeling, packaging) to the latest possible point before delivery. Keeps product in an undifferentiated state longer — reducing inventory while enabling customization.
DEFINITION
What is a platform design strategy?
ANSWER
Creating a common product architecture, shared components, or shared processes from which multiple product variants are derived. Reduces total unique component count, increases volume per part for pricing, and simplifies supply chain complexity.
CONCEPT
What is parts standardization and why does it benefit supply chain?
ANSWER
Using the same component across multiple products. Benefits: fewer unique items to manage, higher volume per part enabling quantity discounts, simpler inventory management, and lower minimum order quantities needed.
DEFINITION
What is early supplier involvement (ESI)?
ANSWER
Involving key suppliers in the product design process before designs are finalized. Allows suppliers to suggest design changes that improve manufacturability, reduce cost, or improve supply chain performance — leveraging supplier expertise.
CONCEPT
What is New Product Introduction (NPI) and what supply chain challenges does it create?
ANSWER
The process of bringing a new product to market. Supply chain challenges: no historical demand data for forecasting, uncertain initial volumes, component qualification from scratch, supply ramp-up risk, and coordination between design and production.
DEFINITION
What is value engineering?
ANSWER
A systematic analysis of product functions to reduce cost while maintaining required performance. Asks: 'What does this component/feature do, and what is the minimum cost way to achieve that function?'
DEFINITION
What is design for disassembly and why is it important?
ANSWER
Designing products so they can be easily disassembled at end of life for recycling or reuse of components. Important for sustainability, regulatory compliance (e.g., WEEE directive in Europe), and circular economy goals.
CONCEPT
How does product design affect inventory levels?
ANSWER
Products with many unique components require more unique inventory items — increasing total inventory. Designs using common/standard parts allow volume consolidation and safety stock sharing — reducing total inventory investment.
CONCEPT
What is the product lifecycle and how does it affect supply chain strategy?
ANSWER
Introduction (uncertain demand, build supply capability), Growth (scale up rapidly), Maturity (optimize efficiency, cost reduction), Decline (plan phase-out, avoid excess inventory, transition supply base). Each stage needs a different supply chain approach.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is form postponement vs. time postponement?
ANSWER
Form postponement: delaying the physical transformation of the product (keep as generic as long as possible). Time postponement: delaying the movement of the product (ship later, but fast when needed). Both reduce inventory risk.
CONCEPT
What is the design freeze and why does it matter for supply chain?
ANSWER
The point at which the product design is finalized and no further changes are allowed without formal change control. After design freeze, the supply chain can fully commit to tooling, component selection, and supplier qualification.
CONCEPT
What is bill of materials (BOM) complexity and how does product design affect it?
ANSWER
BOM complexity = number of unique components and levels of assembly. Complex BOMs mean more items to procure, more supplier relationships, more planning difficulty, and more opportunities for supply disruptions. DFSC aims to simplify the BOM.
