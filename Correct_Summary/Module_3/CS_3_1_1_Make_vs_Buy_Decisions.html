Overview

The make-or-buy (or insource-outsource) decision determines whether a company performs an activity internally or sources it from an external supplier. It is a fundamental strategic supply chain decision.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Make-or-buy decision: An analysis of whether it is more cost-effective and strategically sound to produce an item internally (make) or to purchase it from an outside source (buy). Factors beyond cost include quality, delivery, capacity, strategic importance, and IP protection.
📖 Definition:
Outsourcing: The act of moving some of a firm's internal activities and decision responsibility to outside providers. May be selective (specific activities) or complete (entire functions). Not the same as offshoring, though they often overlap.
📖 Definition:
Offshoring: The practice of basing some of a company's activities or processes in another country, typically for lower labor or material costs.
📖 Definition:
Core competency: A capability or set of skills that provides a competitive advantage that is unique to the organization, difficult for competitors to imitate, and applicable to multiple markets.

Decision Framework

💡 Key Point:
Make activities: those that represent core competencies, require proprietary knowledge, have high strategic value, or need tight quality control. Buy activities: those that are commodity-like, can be sourced efficiently from specialists, or are non-differentiating.
  • Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) is the correct financial comparison — not just unit price
  • TCO includes: purchase price + transportation + customs + supplier management + quality costs + switching costs
  • Strategic factors: core competency alignment, IP protection, supplier dependency risk, flexibility need
  • Operational factors: available internal capacity, required quality level, speed to market
  • Risk factors: single-source dependency, geopolitical risk if offshoring, IP leakage risk
💡 Key Point:
The outsourcing trap: companies that outsource too aggressively may hollow out their capabilities, making them dependent on suppliers and losing the knowledge base needed to manage them effectively.

Outsourcing Considerations

  • Third-party logistics (3PL): outsourcing logistics operations to a specialist provider
  • Contract manufacturing: outsourcing production to a third party that makes the product
  • Business Process Outsourcing (BPO): outsourcing entire business functions (HR, IT, finance)
  • Nearshoring vs. offshoring: nearshoring moves activity to a nearby country (lower transport, cultural proximity); offshoring to a distant country (primarily labor cost arbitrage)

DEFINITION
What is a make-or-buy decision?
ANSWER
An analysis of whether to produce an item internally (make) or purchase it from an outside supplier (buy). Key factors: total cost, quality, capacity, strategic importance, core competency, IP protection, and supply risk.
DEFINITION
What is outsourcing?
ANSWER
Moving some internal activities and decision responsibility to outside providers. May be selective (specific activities) or comprehensive (entire functions). Often done to reduce cost or access expertise, but can create strategic dependencies.
DEFINITION
What is offshoring?
ANSWER
Basing company activities in another country — typically for lower labor or material costs. Not the same as outsourcing (can offshore to a wholly-owned subsidiary).
DEFINITION
What is a core competency and why does it matter in make-or-buy decisions?
ANSWER
A unique capability that provides competitive advantage, is difficult to imitate, and applies across markets. Activities that are core competencies should typically be kept in-house (make). Non-core activities are candidates for outsourcing (buy).
CONCEPT
What is Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) and why is it the right metric for make-or-buy?
ANSWER
TCO = full lifecycle cost of sourcing including purchase price, transportation, customs, supplier management overhead, quality costs, and switching costs. Unit price alone understates the true cost of buying and overstates the savings from outsourcing.
CONCEPT
What is the 'outsourcing trap'?
ANSWER
When companies outsource too aggressively, they hollow out internal capabilities and knowledge — becoming dependent on suppliers and losing the ability to manage them effectively or re-insource if needed.
DEFINITION
What is a third-party logistics provider (3PL)?
ANSWER
A company that provides outsourced logistics services — warehousing, transportation, fulfillment, customs brokerage. Using a 3PL allows companies to focus on core competencies while accessing logistics expertise and scale.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between offshoring and nearshoring?
ANSWER
Offshoring: moving activities to a distant country (primarily for labor cost arbitrage — e.g., Asia). Nearshoring: moving to a nearby country (lower transport cost, cultural proximity, shorter lead time — e.g., Mexico for US companies).
CONCEPT
What are the strategic factors in a make-or-buy decision?
ANSWER
Core competency alignment, intellectual property (IP) protection, supplier power and dependency risk, flexibility requirements, strategic control over quality, and the ability to respond quickly to market changes.
DEFINITION
What is contract manufacturing?
ANSWER
Outsourcing physical production to a third-party manufacturer (contract manufacturer) who makes products to the buyer's specifications. Example: Apple uses Foxconn to manufacture iPhones. The buying company retains design and brand ownership.
CONCEPT
When should a company choose to 'make' rather than 'buy'?
ANSWER
When the activity is a core competency, requires proprietary knowledge or IP, demands tight quality control, has critical supply security implications, or when internal cost is genuinely lower than outsourced TCO including all hidden costs.
CONCEPT
When should a company choose to 'buy' rather than 'make'?
ANSWER
When the activity is not a core competency, an external specialist can do it better/cheaper, internal capacity is constrained, capital investment is prohibitive, or supplier expertise provides better quality or flexibility.
DEFINITION
What is Business Process Outsourcing (BPO)?
ANSWER
Outsourcing entire business functions (HR, payroll, IT, customer service, finance/accounting) to external service providers. Allows companies to focus on core activities while accessing specialized expertise.
CONCEPT
What is IP (intellectual property) risk in outsourcing?
ANSWER
The risk that a supplier gains access to proprietary designs, formulas, or processes and uses them for their own benefit or shares them with competitors. A key reason companies keep core technology activities in-house.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between vertical integration and outsourcing?
ANSWER
Vertical integration = a company owns multiple supply chain echelons (makes more activities in-house). Outsourcing = moves activities to external providers (buys more). Modern supply chains trend toward outsourcing non-core, integrating core.
