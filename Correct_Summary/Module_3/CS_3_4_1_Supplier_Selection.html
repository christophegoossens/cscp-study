Overview

Supplier selection is the process of evaluating and choosing suppliers based on multiple criteria to ensure they can reliably meet the organization's requirements for quality, cost, delivery, and service.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Supplier evaluation: The process of assessing potential or existing suppliers against predefined criteria to determine their ability to meet requirements. Typically covers quality, cost, delivery, service, and financial stability.
📖 Definition:
Weighted evaluation criteria: A scoring approach that assigns importance weights to evaluation criteria, then scores each supplier, enabling objective comparison across multiple factors simultaneously.

Supplier Selection Criteria — QCDSF

💡 Key Point:
The primary supplier selection criteria: Quality (defect rates, certifications, QMS), Cost (total cost of ownership, not just unit price), Delivery (lead time, on-time performance, flexibility), Service (technical support, responsiveness, EDI capability), and Financial stability (creditworthiness, ownership, sustainability as an ongoing supplier).
  • Quality criteria: quality certifications (ISO 9001), historical defect rates, SPC capability, quality system audit results
  • Cost criteria: unit price, volume discounts, payment terms, tooling costs, total cost of ownership
  • Delivery criteria: lead time, on-time delivery history, minimum order quantities, safety stock willingness
  • Service criteria: technical support, engineering collaboration capability, EDI/systems connectivity
  • Financial stability: D&B rating, credit score, revenue trend, customer concentration risk
  • Sustainability/CSR: environmental certifications (ISO 14001), labor practices, conflict minerals policy

Selection Process

💡 Key Point:
Formal supplier selection process: 1) Define requirements → 2) Issue RFI/RFP/RFQ → 3) Evaluate responses against weighted criteria → 4) On-site audit of finalists → 5) Negotiate and select → 6) Qualify supplier → 7) Award business.
📖 Definition:
Request for Information (RFI): A preliminary document sent to potential suppliers to gather general information about their capabilities before issuing a formal RFP or RFQ. Used to narrow the supplier field.

CONCEPT
What is the formal supplier selection process?
ANSWER
1) Define requirements, 2) Issue RFI/RFP/RFQ, 3) Evaluate responses against weighted criteria, 4) On-site audit of finalists, 5) Negotiate and select, 6) Qualify/approve supplier, 7) Award business and monitor performance.
CONCEPT
What are the five primary supplier selection criteria (QCDSF)?
ANSWER
Quality (certifications, defect rates), Cost (TCO), Delivery (lead time, OTD), Service (support, responsiveness, EDI), and Financial stability (creditworthiness, business continuity).
DEFINITION
What is supplier evaluation?
ANSWER
The process of assessing potential or existing suppliers against predefined criteria — quality, cost, delivery, service, and financial stability — to determine their ability to reliably meet requirements.
DEFINITION
What is a weighted evaluation criteria approach?
ANSWER
Assigning importance weights to each criterion (e.g., quality 40%, cost 30%, delivery 20%, service 10%), scoring each supplier on each criterion, then calculating a weighted total score. Enables objective, structured comparison across multiple factors.
DEFINITION
What is a Request for Information (RFI)?
ANSWER
A preliminary document sent to potential suppliers to gather general capability information before issuing a formal RFP. Used to qualify the supplier field — narrow from many potential suppliers to a short list worth further evaluation.
CONCEPT
Why is total cost of ownership (TCO) preferred over unit price in supplier selection?
ANSWER
Unit price alone ignores transportation, customs, inspection, supplier management, risk, and switching costs. A cheaper-priced offshore supplier may have higher TCO than a more expensive local supplier when all costs are included.
CONCEPT
What is financial stability in supplier selection and why does it matter?
ANSWER
A supplier's financial health — assessed via credit ratings, financial statements, customer concentration. A financially unstable supplier risks business failure, which could suddenly cut off your supply with no warning.
CONCEPT
What is an on-site supplier audit in selection?
ANSWER
A visit to the supplier's facility by the buyer's team to verify that the supplier's actual operations match what was presented in the RFP — assessing quality systems, capacity, processes, and management competence.
CONCEPT
What sustainability criteria appear in modern supplier selection?
ANSWER
Environmental certifications (ISO 14001), carbon footprint targets, conflict minerals policy (e.g., Dodd-Frank Act compliance), labor practice standards (no child labor, fair wages), and social responsibility reporting.
DEFINITION
What is supplier qualification?
ANSWER
The formal process of approving a supplier to supply specific items after completing evaluation, audit, and testing. A supplier must be on the Approved Supplier List (ASL) before purchase orders can be issued.
CONCEPT
What is supplier concentration risk?
ANSWER
The risk that a supplier has a very high percentage of revenue from a single customer — making them dependent on that customer's business. If that customer reduces orders, the supplier may have financial difficulty.
DEFINITION
What is early supplier involvement (ESI) in selection?
ANSWER
Involving strategic suppliers in the product design phase — inviting them into engineering discussions before final designs are frozen. Leverages supplier manufacturing knowledge to design more producible, lower-cost products.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between an approved supplier and a preferred supplier?
ANSWER
Approved supplier: meets basic qualification requirements and is on the ASL. Preferred supplier: top-performing approved supplier given priority for new business, closer collaboration, and access to company roadmaps as a reward for excellence.
CONCEPT
How does supplier diversity factor into selection?
ANSWER
Many large companies and government agencies require purchasing from diverse suppliers (minority-owned, women-owned, veteran-owned, small businesses). Supplier diversity is both a compliance requirement and a market access strategy.
CONCEPT
What role does supply chain risk play in supplier selection?
ANSWER
Single-source risk, geographic concentration risk, geopolitical risk, and supplier financial risk all factor into selection. Risk diversification (dual sourcing, geographic distribution) may justify selecting a higher-cost supplier.
