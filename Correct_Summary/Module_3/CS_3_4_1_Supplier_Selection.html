Supplier Selection Criteria
Exhibit 3-14: Functions of Purchasing — supplier selection is step 1

Traditional thinking: minimize price. Supply chain thinking: long-term success of all partners. Key selection criteria:

1. Cost — TCO and COGS:

  • TCO: total sum of all costs in the supply stream. Problem: actual costs of outsourcing are often hidden initially. Example: copper tubing — initial analysis may favor Chinese supplier, but TCO reveals higher costs after transportation, quality, and import duties.
  • COGS: lowering COGS is strategic — SC efficiency has large leveraged effect on profitability (every $1 reduction in COGS saves more than $1 in revenue needed).

Exhibit 3-16: COGS Example — leveraged impact of SC cost reduction on profit

2. Alignment with SC needs: site visits required before establishing partnerships. Consider supplier’s technical communications ability (real-time data exchange, RFID, EDI).

3. Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) Policy: also called corporate citizenship policy. The brand owner is responsible for extended SC compliance. CSR topics in supplier selection:

  • Customer health and safety (product safety, nontoxic)
  • Employee health and safety (risk exposure of supplier employees)
  • Environmental sustainability (energy use, carbon footprint, recycled materials)
  • Maintainability (TCO of supplier’s products)
  • Employment policy (wages vs. regional averages, no child labor)
  • Community reinvestment (local sourcing, transportation cost savings)

4. Supplier Value-Added Services: inventory management at buyer’s site, safety stock maintenance, quick response to volume changes, compatible transfer packaging, break-bulking, CAD/RFID/GPS capabilities, long-term relationship potential.

Negotiation
Negotiation: the process by which a buyer and seller reach agreement on the terms of a purchase.

2 types of negotiation:

  • Competitive bidding (RFQ/ITT): used for commodities and leveragable items — multiple suppliers compete on price and terms.
  • Direct negotiation: used when RFQ/ITT is cumbersome, only one supplier considered, materials have hard-to-define costs/risks, preferred supplier relationships, bottleneck materials, or core-competency strategic partnerships.

Traditional negotiation styles:

  • Hard negotiator: views other party as adversary; takes extreme position; demands concessions; gives none — win/lose.
  • Soft negotiator: values agreement; may disclose bottom line; accepts one-sided agreements — lose/win.

Principled Negotiation (Fisher & Ury, Harvard Negotiation Project) — win/win interest-based bargaining. 4 steps:

  1. Separate people from the problem: negotiators insist on criteria, not on defeating the other side.
  2. Focus on interests, not positions: avoid position-taking or bottom-line anchoring. Understand underlying needs.
  3. Invent options for mutual gain: brainstorm solutions before agreeing; seek creative value-creation options.
  4. Insist on objective criteria: guide disagreements toward measurable, fair standards rather than haggling.

BATNA: Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement — each party should predetermine this before negotiating. Knowing your BATNA determines your walk-away point.

Contract Deployment & Compliance Management

Contract deployment ensures the agreement functions as intended. Activities: navigate legal maze, communicate with winning supplier, promote benefits to internal buyers, load into contract management database, implement order-to-payment procedures, train users and suppliers, validate performance, integrate transaction systems, audit initial invoices.

Compliance management: concentrate purchases with preferred suppliers, monitor off-contract purchases, channel findings to management, audit pricing, monitor contract expirations/renewals.

5 measures to avoid supplier performance pitfalls:

  1. Establish clear performance expectations upfront.
  2. Measure against expectations at regular intervals.
  3. Maintain ultimate responsibility (don’t assume outsourcing transfers accountability).
  4. Coordinate multiple suppliers; share experience and knowledge.
  5. Maintain an exit strategy with a formal backup plan.

ASCM def: Negotiation
ANSWER
The process by which a buyer and seller reach agreement on the terms of a purchase.
TERM / CONCEPT
2 types of negotiation and when to use each
ANSWER
Competitive bidding (RFQ/ITT): commodities and leveragable items. Direct negotiation: complex/hard-to-define costs, preferred suppliers, bottleneck materials, core-competency strategic partnerships.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is principled negotiation? (4 steps)
ANSWER
Fisher & Ury (Harvard). Win/win interest-based: 1. Separate people from problem. 2. Focus on interests not positions. 3. Invent options for mutual gain. 4. Insist on objective criteria.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is BATNA?
ANSWER
Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement — what you will do if negotiation fails. Predetermining your BATNA establishes your walk-away point and prevents accepting unfavorable terms.
TERM / CONCEPT
Hard vs. soft negotiator
ANSWER
Hard: views other as adversary; extreme position; no concessions — win/lose. Soft: values agreement; may reveal bottom line; accepts one-sided deals — lose/win. Principled negotiation achieves win/win.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 4 supplier selection criteria?
ANSWER
1. Cost (TCO and COGS). 2. Alignment with SC needs. 3. Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) policy. 4. Supplier value-added services.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is COGS reduction strategically important?
ANSWER
Each $1 reduction in COGS has a leveraged effect on profitability — it takes much more in additional revenue to generate the same profit improvement. SC efficiency directly impacts bottom line.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a CSR (Corporate Citizenship) Policy?
ANSWER
Organizational self-regulation requiring employees and suppliers to meet standards for: customer health/safety, employee health/safety, environmental sustainability, maintainability, employment policy, community reinvestment.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why are site visits required in supplier selection?
ANSWER
To verify supplier capabilities, culture, and alignment before establishing onshore or offshore partnerships. All key stakeholders who will interact with the supplier should participate.
TERM / CONCEPT
What supplier value-added services may be required?
ANSWER
Inventory management at buyer’s site, safety stock maintenance, quick response to volume changes, compatible transfer packaging, break-bulking, CAD/RFID/GPS capabilities, long-term relationship culture.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is contract deployment?
ANSWER
Getting a signed contract to function as intended. Includes: legal navigation, supplier communication, promoting benefits internally, loading into contract database, order-to-payment procedures, training, performance validation, invoice auditing.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is compliance management?
ANSWER
Ensuring purchases flow to preferred contracted suppliers. Activities: monitor off-contract purchases, channel findings to management, audit pricing accuracy, monitor contract renewals, drive continuous improvements.
TERM / CONCEPT
5 measures to avoid supplier performance pitfalls
ANSWER
1. Clear performance expectations upfront. 2. Regular measurement against expectations. 3. Maintain ultimate responsibility. 4. Coordinate multiple suppliers; share knowledge. 5. Maintain an exit strategy.
TERM / CONCEPT
TCO challenge in outsourcing supplier selection
ANSWER
Initial cost analysis may understate true costs. Hidden costs include quality issues, transportation, duties, management overhead, and compliance. Full TCO analysis often reverses initial low-cost conclusions.
TERM / CONCEPT
When to use competitive bidding vs. direct negotiation?
ANSWER
Competitive bidding: commodities, leveragable items (many suppliers, price-competitive). Direct: preferred suppliers, bottleneck/strategic materials, complex specifications, hard-to-define costs, strategic partnerships.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is ‘triangle talk’ in negotiation?
ANSWER
A negotiation tool (Monczka et al.) that prepares both parties for interest-based discussions by structuring conversations around each party’s underlying needs and constraints — similar in spirit to principled negotiation.
TERM / CONCEPT
What does ‘maintain ultimate responsibility’ mean for outsourcing?
ANSWER
Even after outsourcing, the organization (brand owner) remains legally and reputationally responsible for the activities of its supply chain — cannot transfer accountability to suppliers.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why may RFQ/ITT be inappropriate for strategic materials?
ANSWER
Core-competency and strategic alliance relationships require deep collaboration, customization, and trust — price competition through RFQ/ITT drives wrong behavior and undermines relationship quality.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is an exit strategy in supplier management?
ANSWER
A formal backup plan prepared in advance in case the current supplier relationship ends — ensures business continuity and prevents lock-in. Part of compliance management.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is break-bulking as a supplier value-added service?
ANSWER
Consolidation or deconsolidation of goods from various upstream sources at a supplier’s warehouse — reduces buyer’s logistics complexity and inbound handling costs.

Question 1
What is the typical sequence for a formal supplier selection process?
A.Select supplier, then define requirements
B.Define requirements → Market analysis → RFI (qualify) → RFP/RFQ (evaluate) → Negotiate → Award → Onboard
C.Place order, then evaluate supplier quality
D.Negotiate price first, then evaluate supplier capability
Formal supplier selection follows a structured process: understand requirements, analyze the market, gather supplier information (RFI), evaluate detailed proposals (RFP/RFQ), negotiate, award business, and onboard the supplier.
Question 2
What criteria are typically included in a supplier evaluation scorecard?
A.Only price per unit
B.Quality (defect rates, certifications), delivery (on-time, lead time), cost (price, TCO), service (responsiveness, flexibility), financial stability, and sustainability/compliance
C.Only quality and delivery
D.Only price, quality, and location
A balanced supplier scorecard evaluates multiple dimensions — quality, delivery, cost, service, financial health, and sustainability — to select the supplier that provides the best total value, not just the lowest price.
Question 3
What is a 'request for quotation' (RFQ) vs. a 'request for proposal' (RFP)?
A.They are identical documents
B.RFQ: asks suppliers for price on a specified item/service; RFP: asks suppliers to propose how they would meet the requirements — used when the solution is not fully defined and suppliers should propose their approach
C.RFP asks for price; RFQ asks for capabilities
D.RFQ is for services; RFP is for goods
RFQ is used when specifications are defined and you need a price. RFP is used when requirements are defined but the solution is not — suppliers propose their approach, technology, and pricing, enabling evaluation of value beyond just price.
Question 4
What is 'weighted scoring' in supplier evaluation?
A.Weighing shipments before scoring quality
B.Assigning different weights to evaluation criteria based on their relative importance — then scoring each supplier on each criterion and calculating a weighted total score to enable fair, objective comparison
C.Counting the weight of evidence for each supplier
D.A quality control technique
Weighted scoring makes the evaluation transparent and objective: if delivery is more important than price for this category, it gets a higher weight. The weighted total score reflects the company's actual priorities.
Question 5
Why is supplier financial stability an important selection criterion?
A.Financially stable suppliers always offer the lowest prices
B.A financially unstable supplier risks bankruptcy, creating supply disruptions — financial stability assessment (credit scores, financial ratios) helps identify suppliers who may not be reliable long-term partners
C.Financial stability has no impact on supply chain performance
D.Financially stable suppliers never have quality problems
Supplier financial distress is a leading cause of supply disruptions. Assessing financial health (liquidity, leverage, credit ratings) before awarding significant business identifies suppliers at risk of sudden failure.
Question 6
What is the purpose of a site visit during supplier selection?
A.To enjoy a tour of the supplier's facility
B.To verify that the supplier's actual operations, quality systems, capacity, and workforce match their proposal — 'seeing is believing' for critical capability assessments
C.To negotiate better prices through face-to-face meetings
D.To deliver the contract in person
Site visits let buyers verify supplier claims: actual production capacity, quality processes in practice, working conditions, technology, and management capability — providing evidence that a supplier can actually deliver as promised.
Question 7
What is 'supplier risk assessment' during selection?
A.Evaluating the risk that a supplier will outperform expectations
B.Evaluating financial, operational, geopolitical, environmental, and compliance risks associated with a potential supplier — to avoid awarding business to high-risk suppliers without appropriate mitigation
C.Assessing the company's own risk in changing suppliers
D.Determining the probability of supplier price increases
Supplier risk assessment identifies and evaluates risks before award: financial instability, single-facility concentration, location in high-risk geographies, compliance issues, and environmental risks — enabling informed selection and risk mitigation planning.
Question 8
What is 'total cost of ownership' (TCO) analysis in supplier selection?
A.Comparing only the quoted unit price across suppliers
B.Evaluating all costs associated with each supplier option: price, quality costs, transportation, lead time/inventory costs, transition costs, and business risk — to identify the lowest total cost, not just the lowest price
C.Calculating the cost to manage the supplier relationship
D.Estimating the supplier's production cost
TCO analysis reveals the true cost of each supplier option. A supplier with a higher unit price but better quality, shorter lead time, and lower supply risk may have a significantly lower total cost of ownership.
Question 9
What is 'supplier onboarding'?
A.The process of removing a supplier from the supply base
B.The structured process of setting up a new supplier in all systems (ERP, supplier portal), establishing contacts, communicating requirements, conducting initial quality reviews, and managing the production ramp-up
C.The first purchase order placed with a new supplier
D.Conducting a supplier audit for the first time
Supplier onboarding ensures the supplier is set up for success: EDI/portal connectivity, master data setup in ERP, delivery of requirements documentation, initial quality planning, and production trial orders — getting the relationship off to a good start.
Question 10
What does 'supplier diversity' mean in supplier selection?
A.Selecting suppliers from diverse geographic regions
B.Intentionally considering and awarding business to diverse-owned businesses (minority, women, veteran, LGBTQ+-owned, small) as part of a structured supplier diversity program
C.Selecting a diverse range of product types from each supplier
D.Requiring each supplier to serve multiple customers
Supplier diversity programs intentionally include diverse-owned businesses in the sourcing process. These programs are driven by social responsibility goals, customer requirements (e.g., government contractors), and the belief that diverse perspectives drive innovation.
Question 11
What is the difference between 'preferred supplier' and 'approved supplier' status?
A.They are the same status in most companies
B.Approved = meets minimum requirements and can be used if needed; Preferred = has proven high performance and is actively favored for new business — preferred suppliers typically get first consideration and may have prequalified contracts
C.Approved = domestic; Preferred = international
D.Preferred = cheapest; Approved = highest quality
Approved status is a baseline — the supplier has been qualified. Preferred status is earned through demonstrated performance — these suppliers get priority consideration, streamlined procurement, and often negotiated pricing frameworks.
Question 12
What is a 'contract' in the supplier selection context?
A.A verbal agreement to purchase goods
B.A legally binding agreement that defines the terms of the supply relationship — pricing, specifications, delivery requirements, quality standards, payment terms, IP ownership, and dispute resolution — protecting both parties
C.A purchase order for a single shipment
D.A supplier performance scorecard
Contracts formalize and protect the supply relationship — defining expectations, rights, and obligations for both parties. Without a contract, disputes about price, quality, and liability are much harder to resolve.
