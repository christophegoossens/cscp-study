Overview

Forecasting is the process of estimating future demand. No forecast is perfectly accurate, but a good forecasting process reduces uncertainty and enables better supply chain decisions.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Forecast: An estimate of future demand. A forecast can be constructed using quantitative methods, qualitative methods, or a combination. A forecast is not a plan, but a critical input to planning.
📖 Definition:
Demand forecast: A projection of expected customer demand for a product or service during a specific future time period, based on analysis of historical demand and other influencing factors.
📖 Definition:
Forecast horizon: The number of future periods for which a forecast is made. Longer horizons have higher uncertainty. Strategic plans use long horizons; operational plans use short horizons.
📖 Definition:
Forecast bucket: The time period for which the forecast is stated — daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly. Shorter buckets allow more precision but require more data management.

Fundamental Forecasting Principles

💡 Key Point:
Forecasts are ALWAYS wrong — the goal is not perfect accuracy but understanding and managing the error. A forecast with zero error would require perfect information about the future.
💡 Key Point:
Aggregate forecasts are more accurate than disaggregate forecasts. Forecasting total demand for a product family is more accurate than forecasting individual SKU demand.
💡 Key Point:
Short-term forecasts are more accurate than long-term forecasts. Uncertainty compounds over time.
  • All forecasts should include a measure of forecast error — a forecast without an uncertainty estimate is incomplete
  • Forecast error drives safety stock requirements — higher error requires more safety stock
  • Collaboration with customers improves forecast accuracy (vendor-managed inventory, CPFR)
  • Forecasts should be biased toward the business impact of errors — stockout cost vs. overstock cost

Forecast Inputs

  • Historical demand data — the primary input for time-series forecasting
  • Market intelligence — sales team input, customer plans, competitor activity
  • Economic indicators — GDP, consumer confidence, industry indices
  • Promotional and event plans — planned demand shaping activities
  • New product launch plans — phase-in/phase-out demand

DEFINITION
What is the ASCM definition of a forecast?
ANSWER
An estimate of future demand. A forecast can be constructed using quantitative methods, qualitative methods, or a combination. It is not a plan, but a critical input to planning.
KEY PRINCIPLE
What is the fundamental principle that all forecasts are wrong?
ANSWER
Forecasts always contain error because the future is uncertain. The goal is not perfect accuracy but understanding and managing forecast error to enable better supply chain decisions.
KEY PRINCIPLE
Why are aggregate forecasts more accurate than disaggregate forecasts?
ANSWER
Statistical error at lower levels (SKU, location) does not aggregate — it partially cancels out at higher levels. Forecasting total product family demand is more accurate than forecasting each individual SKU.
KEY PRINCIPLE
Why are short-term forecasts more accurate than long-term?
ANSWER
Uncertainty compounds over time. Factors that influence demand (market conditions, competition, customer behavior) are more predictable in the near term. Long-horizon forecasts are used for strategic planning, not operational commitments.
DEFINITION
What is a forecast horizon?
ANSWER
The number of future periods for which a forecast is made. Longer horizons have higher uncertainty. Strategic plans use long horizons (years); operational plans use short horizons (days/weeks).
DEFINITION
What is a forecast bucket?
ANSWER
The time period for which the forecast is stated — daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly. Shorter buckets allow more precision in planning but require more data management effort.
CONCEPT
What is the relationship between forecast error and safety stock?
ANSWER
Higher forecast error requires more safety stock to maintain the same service level. The safety stock formula uses the standard deviation of forecast error as the key input: SS = z × σ(error) × √lead time.
CONCEPT
What are the five main inputs to a demand forecast?
ANSWER
1) Historical demand data, 2) Market intelligence (sales input), 3) Economic indicators, 4) Promotional/event plans, 5) New product launch plans (phase-in/phase-out).
CONCEPT
What is the purpose of including a measure of error with every forecast?
ANSWER
A forecast without an error estimate is incomplete for planning. Error measures (MAD, MAPE, bias) quantify uncertainty, drive safety stock decisions, and help evaluate and improve the forecasting process.
CONCEPT
What is Collaborative Planning, Forecasting and Replenishment (CPFR) and how does it improve forecast accuracy?
ANSWER
CPFR involves trading partners sharing demand data, promotional plans, and forecasts. Improved visibility into actual sell-through data and customer plans reduces forecast error at the manufacturer level.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is a demand forecast vs. a supply plan?
ANSWER
A demand forecast is an estimate of what customers will want. A supply plan is what the company commits to produce or procure. A demand forecast is the input; the supply plan is the constrained response.
CONCEPT
How does product lifecycle stage affect forecasting approach?
ANSWER
Introduction: rely on analogies, market research, expert judgment (no history). Growth: establish statistical baselines, adjust quickly. Maturity: statistical time-series works well. Decline: project Phase-out curve.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between a baseline forecast and an adjusted (event-driven) forecast?
ANSWER
Baseline forecast: the statistical projection assuming no extraordinary events. Adjusted forecast adds planned demand-shaping events (promotions, price changes, new listings) on top of the baseline — called 'event uplift.'
DEFINITION
What is forecast bias?
ANSWER
A systematic tendency to consistently over-forecast or under-forecast. Bias = Forecast − Actual, summed over time. Positive bias = chronic over-forecasting. Negative bias = chronic under-forecasting. Bias is as dangerous as random error.
CONCEPT
Why is the sales team's input into forecasting both valuable and risky?
ANSWER
Valuable: sales reps have direct customer knowledge and visibility to planned projects. Risky: sales teams may be optimistic (inflating forecasts) or conservative (sandbag to ensure they hit targets). Statistical baselines should anchor the process.

Question 2
Forecast bias occurs when:
A.Random errors cancel out over time
B.Errors consistently skew in one direction
C.The forecast horizon is too short
D.The model is too complex
Bias is systematic over- or under-forecasting. It does not cancel out and must be detected through tracking signals.
Question 3
The principle that aggregate forecasts are more accurate than disaggregate ones is the:
A.Law of large numbers
B.Central limit theorem
C.Aggregation and pooling principle
D.Regression to the mean
The aggregation principle recognizes that random errors tend to cancel when items are combined, making family-level forecasts more reliable.
Question 4
A qualitative forecasting input would be:
A.Moving average
B.Exponential smoothing output
C.Sales force composite estimate
D.Regression model output
Sales force composite, executive opinion, and Delphi method are qualitative forecasting inputs based on human judgment.
Question 5
A causal forecasting model predicts demand by:
A.Projecting historical trends forward
B.Identifying relationships between demand and external variables such as GDP or weather
C.Averaging the last N periods
D.Weighting recent data more heavily
Causal models (regression, econometric) link demand to independent variables (leading indicators) to explain and predict demand changes.
