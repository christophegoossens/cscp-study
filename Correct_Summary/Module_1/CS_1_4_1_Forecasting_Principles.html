ASCM Definitions
📖 ASCM Definition — Forecasting: the business function that attempts to predict sales and use of products so they can be purchased or manufactured in appropriate quantities in advance of demand.
📖 ASCM Definition — Demand Planning: the process of combining statistical forecasting techniques and judgment to construct demand estimates for products or services (both high and low volume, and both stable and erratic demand). Encompasses all forecasting horizons.
💡 Key Point: Everything in the supply network depends on how many customers purchase from the end retailer or direct channel. Inaccurate forecasts propagate upstream and amplify — the bullwhip effect.
5 Principles of Forecasting

1. Forecasts should be based on demand, not orders.

Customer orders differ from actual demand (over-ordering, consolidated orders). Base forecasts on estimated end-user demand, not ordering behavior.

2. Forecasts are (almost) always wrong.

Even sophisticated models produce estimates, not certainties. Forecast misses cause order variation that amplifies upstream — bullwhip effect. Assuming a fixed % increase each period is especially prone to error.

3. Forecasts should include an estimate of error.

Like opinion polls (e.g., 40% ± 3%), demand forecasts should include a margin of error or confidence interval. Lets planners set appropriate safety stock and capacity buffers.

4. Forecasts are more accurate for groups than for single items.

Risk pooling: individual over/under forecasts cancel out when aggregated. Examples: forecast all blue jeans more accurately than one specific style; forecast hospital staffing at regional level better than per-doctor. SKU-level detail is still needed but reserved for shorter horizons.

5. Forecasts for the near term are more accurate than for the long term.

The further out, the more chance and change can derail estimates. Review cadence should match: long-term → annually; medium-term → quarterly; short-term → monthly/weekly.

10-Step Forecasting Process
  • 1. Determine the purpose — manufacturing/purchasing targets, capacity planning, or service staffing?
  • 2. Determine the level of aggregation — total sales, product family, product, or SKU? Specify units of measure.
  • 3. Determine the time horizon — short, medium, or long term? Planning bucket (week, month)?
  • 4. Visualize the data — graph historical data to identify trends or seasonality; informs method selection.
  • 5. Choose the method/model — qualitative, quantitative (time-series or causal), or both.
  • 6. Prepare the data — gather inputs; deseasonalize if strong seasonality exists.
  • 7. Test using historical data — back-cast a few periods and compare to actuals; adjust model as needed.
  • 8. Forecast — run the model; re-add seasonality if removed; apply qualitative adjustments.
  • 9. Perform S&OP — align demand-side, supply-side, and finance on a single demand plan.
  • 10. Review and improve — monitor error levels periodically; continuously refine models.

Why should forecasts be based on demand, not orders?
ANSWER
Customer orders differ from actual end-user demand — they may over-order to protect against stockouts, consolidate orders, or cancel. Orders introduce distortion and contribute to the bullwhip effect.

Why are forecasts almost always wrong?
ANSWER
Even sophisticated models produce estimates, not certainties. Forecast misses cause order variation that amplifies upstream (bullwhip effect). Assuming a fixed % increase each period is especially error-prone.
TERM / CONCEPT
What should a forecast include besides the point estimate?
ANSWER
An estimate of error — a margin of error or confidence interval (similar to a poll's ± figure). This allows planners to set safety stock and capacity buffers at appropriate levels.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why are forecasts more accurate for groups than for single items?
ANSWER
Risk pooling — individual over/under forecasts tend to cancel out when aggregated. Example: all blue jeans can be forecast more accurately than one specific style.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is risk pooling in forecasting?
ANSWER
Aggregating individual demand risks into a pool. The overall variation for the pool is smaller than the sum of individual variations because high and low individual errors offset each other.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why are near-term forecasts more accurate than long-term forecasts?
ANSWER
The further out the forecast, the more chance and change can derail estimates. Events, market shifts, and competitor actions accumulate over time.
TERM / CONCEPT
What review cadence matches each forecast horizon?
ANSWER
Long-term: annually. Medium-term: quarterly. Short-term: monthly or weekly. The review cadence should match the planning horizon.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the bullwhip effect and how does forecasting relate to it?
ANSWER
Amplification of demand fluctuations as they move upstream in the supply chain. Forecast errors at the retail level cause larger order swings at the distributor level, and even larger at the manufacturer level. Demand-based, accurate forecasts reduce the bullwhip.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the first step in the forecasting process?
ANSWER
Determine the PURPOSE — manufacturing/purchasing targets, capacity planning, or service staffing. The purpose drives all subsequent choices (level, horizon, method).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is 'level of aggregation' in forecasting?
ANSWER
The level of detail: total sales, product family, product, or SKU. Higher aggregation = more accuracy (risk pooling). SKU-level forecasts are reserved for shorter horizons.
TERM / CONCEPT
What does visualizing data accomplish in the forecasting process?
ANSWER
Graphing historical data reveals trends, seasonality, cycles, or unusual spikes — informing method selection and whether deseasonalizing is needed before modeling.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is deseasonalizing and when is it used?
ANSWER
Removing the seasonal component from historical data before running a model, so the underlying trend can be identified cleanly. The seasonal factor is added back to the final forecast afterward.
TERM / CONCEPT
What happens at step 9 of the forecasting process?
ANSWER
S&OP — Sales and Operations Planning. Demand-side, supply-side, and finance arrive at a single, agreed-upon demand plan that reconciles sales forecasts with realistic supply capacity.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between qualitative and quantitative forecasting?
ANSWER
Qualitative: based on expert judgment or market research — used when no historical data exists (e.g., NPI). Quantitative: based on historical data using statistical models (time-series or causal). Both can be combined.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a time-series vs. a causal forecasting method?
ANSWER
Time-series: projects future demand based on historical demand patterns (trends, seasonality). Causal: bases forecast on identified demand drivers (e.g., GDP, weather, promotions) that have a measurable impact on demand.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a planning bucket?
ANSWER
The time unit at which the forecast is stated — week, month, or quarter. Matches the planning horizon and decision cycle: short-term uses weekly buckets; long-term uses monthly or quarterly.
TERM / CONCEPT
What does back-testing a forecast accomplish?
ANSWER
Generating a forecast for historical periods and comparing to actuals. Reveals whether the model captures the demand pattern accurately before using it for future periods.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the final ongoing step in the forecasting process?
ANSWER
Periodically review and improve models for accuracy. Monitor error levels (MAD, MAPE, tracking signal) and continuously refine the model to reduce bias and random error.
