Overview

Demand management synchronizes supply and demand plans across all planning horizons. It spans four activities: planning, communicating, influencing, and managing/prioritizing demand.

  • Demand = need for a product from any source: customer order, forecast, interplant, branch WH
  • Demand management = recognizing all demands, prioritizing when supply is constrained
  • Operates at 3 levels: strategic (long-term), aggregate (S&OP/medium-term), item-level (short-term master schedule)
  • Specificity increases at shorter horizons: revenue → product families → individual SKUs
  • Key processes: develop SC personnel → develop infrastructure → plan/schedule → identify logistics → develop logistics → execute
  • For "make" decisions: develop infrastructure, staff, train, manage change
  • For "buy" decisions: select suppliers, develop contracts, monitor relationships
  • Planning steps include: influence demand → forecast → service orders → master scheduling → MRP → DRP → CRP
  • Demand management integrates marketing, sales, and operations into a single plan

Demand management relates to business and customer requirements in that one of its purposes is to influence the organization to produce a product or service that satisfies actual customer requirements and expectations. If marketing professionals succeed in influencing the organization to produce products and services that meet customer requirements, the product/service package should have certain competitive characteristics that enable demand-influencing activities to have a chance to succeed. Supply chain managers can counteract supply chain demand variability such as the bullwhip effect by communicating demand effectively to all parties in the supply chain. On a basic level this involves order processing. Order processing is “the activity required to administratively process a customer’s order and make it ready for shipment or production” (ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary).

Question 1
Demand management in supply chain is primarily concerned with:
A.Producing goods to stock
B.Balancing customer requirements with supply capabilities and influencing demand
C.Selecting transportation carriers
D.Managing warehouse operations
Demand management encompasses all activities to understand, influence, and plan for customer demand.
Question 2
Available-to-Promise (ATP) is used to:
A.Calculate safety stock
B.Determine how much inventory can be committed to new customer orders without impacting existing ones
C.Set reorder points
D.Measure forecast accuracy
ATP checks current inventory and scheduled supply against committed orders to tell salespeople how much product can be promised for specific dates.
Question 3
Which demand management strategy smooths demand peaks by offering price discounts during slow periods?
A.Demand shaping
B.Demand sensing
C.Demand shifting
D.Demand aggregation
Demand shaping uses pricing, promotions, and product alternatives to shift or create demand — smoothing peaks and filling troughs.
Question 4
Which activity is NOT part of demand management?
A.Forecasting
B.Order promising
C.Shop floor scheduling at work centres
D.Demand influencing
Shop floor scheduling is an operations/supply-side activity. Demand management focuses on forecasting, order promising, demand influencing, and prioritizing customer demand.
