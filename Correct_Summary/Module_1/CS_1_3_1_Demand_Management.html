Overview

Demand management is the function of recognizing all sources of demand and ensuring the supply chain can fulfill them in a profitable and timely manner. It bridges customer-facing activities with internal supply planning.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Demand management: The function of recognizing all demands for goods and services to support the marketplace. It involves prioritizing demand when supply is limited and responding to changes. Includes all activities to accept, edit, prioritize, and generate demand signals for planning.
📖 Definition:
Demand signal: Any data that indicates actual or anticipated customer purchasing. Examples include Point-of-Sale (POS) data, customer orders, replenishment orders, and forecasts.
📖 Definition:
Order management: The planning, directing, monitoring, and controlling of the processes related to customer orders, manufacturing orders, and purchase orders. Includes order entry, prioritization, pegging, and confirmation.

Key Demand Management Activities

💡 Key Point:
The four primary demand management activities: 1) Planning (forecasting and demand sensing), 2) Communicating (sharing demand signals with supply planning), 3) Influencing (shaping demand through pricing, promotions, and product availability), 4) Prioritizing (allocating limited supply to highest-value demands).
  • Demand management connects the customer-facing commercial team with internal supply chain planning
  • It must balance customer service objectives (fill rates, lead times) with cost and capacity constraints
  • Effective demand management reduces the bullwhip effect by improving signal quality
  • Order promising and Available-to-Promise (ATP) are key demand management outputs

Order Promising

📖 Definition:
Available-to-Promise (ATP): The uncommitted portion of a company's inventory and planned production; maintained in the Master Production Schedule (MPS) to support customer order promising. Answers the question: can we commit to this order?
📖 Definition:
Capable-to-Promise (CTP): A promise date given to the customer based on planning beyond current ATP — considers whether capacity and materials can be sourced to meet the demand if not currently scheduled.
💡 Key Point:
ATP uses existing scheduled supply. CTP goes further by committing to obtain additional resources (capacity, materials) to fulfill the order — a more powerful but riskier commitment.
  • Order pegging: linking a specific demand (customer order) to a specific supply (production order or purchase order)
  • Priority rules: first-come-first-served, highest margin, strategic customer, delivery date
  • Demand shaping: using pricing, promotions, or lead time offers to shift demand timing or volume

DEFINITION
What is demand management?
ANSWER
The function of recognizing all demands for goods and services and planning how to fulfill them — including prioritizing demand when supply is limited, influencing demand timing, and communicating signals to supply planning.
CONCEPT
What are the four primary activities of demand management?
ANSWER
1) Planning (forecasting, demand sensing), 2) Communicating (sharing demand signals with supply), 3) Influencing (shaping demand through pricing/promotions), 4) Prioritizing (allocating limited supply to highest-value demands).
DEFINITION
What is a demand signal?
ANSWER
Any data that indicates actual or anticipated customer purchasing. Examples: Point-of-Sale (POS) data, customer orders, replenishment orders, and forecasts.
DEFINITION
What is Available-to-Promise (ATP)?
ANSWER
The uncommitted portion of a company's inventory and planned production maintained in the MPS to support customer order promising. Tells planners whether they can commit to a specific customer order based on current schedules.
DEFINITION
What is Capable-to-Promise (CTP)?
ANSWER
A promise date given to a customer based on the ability to obtain additional capacity and materials beyond what is currently scheduled. Goes further than ATP by committing to source new resources if needed.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between ATP and CTP?
ANSWER
ATP uses existing scheduled supply. CTP commits to acquiring additional resources (capacity, materials) to fulfill an order not currently in the plan. CTP is more powerful but requires more commitment and carries more risk.
CONCEPT
What is order pegging in demand management?
ANSWER
The process of linking a specific customer demand (order) to a specific supply source (production order, purchase order, or inventory). Enables traceability and priority management in the planning system.
CONCEPT
How does effective demand management reduce the bullwhip effect?
ANSWER
By improving the quality of demand signals (using POS data vs. distributor orders), sharing real demand data across the supply chain, and reducing order batching and gaming behaviors.
CONCEPT
What is demand shaping?
ANSWER
Using commercial levers (pricing, promotions, product availability, lead time offers) to influence when and how much customers buy — shifting demand timing or volume to better match supply capacity.
DEFINITION
What is order management?
ANSWER
The planning, directing, monitoring, and controlling of processes related to customer orders — including order entry, prioritization, pegging to supply, and confirmation/promising.
CONCEPT
What is the role of Sales and Operations Planning (S&OP) in demand management?
ANSWER
S&OP is the cross-functional process that reconciles demand plans with supply plans at an aggregate level. Demand management feeds the demand plan into S&OP; S&OP outputs constrained demand targets for execution.
CONCEPT
What supply chain problem does demand prioritization solve?
ANSWER
When supply is constrained (e.g., during a shortage or capacity bottleneck), demand prioritization determines which customer orders get filled first — using criteria like customer profitability, strategic importance, or contractual commitments.
CONCEPT
What is the relationship between demand management and customer service?
ANSWER
Demand management balances customer service objectives (fill rates, lead times, order accuracy) with supply constraints and cost targets. Poor demand management leads to stockouts (low service) or excess inventory (high cost).
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between demand management and demand planning?
ANSWER
Demand planning is the forecasting/analytics subset of demand management. Demand management is broader — it includes planning plus communicating, influencing, and prioritizing demand across the supply chain.
CONCEPT
What does 'consuming the forecast' mean in demand management?
ANSWER
When a confirmed customer order arrives, it replaces (consumes) the forecast for that period — preventing both the order and the forecast from being planned simultaneously, which would cause overproduction.
