Overview

A supply chain is the end-to-end network through which raw materials become finished products delivered to customers. Understanding its structure, flows, and integration strategies is fundamental to supply chain management.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Supply chain: The global network used to deliver products and services from raw materials to end customers through an engineered flow of information, physical distribution, and cash.
📖 Definition:
Supply chain management (SCM): The design, planning, execution, control, and monitoring of supply chain activities with the objective of creating net value, building a competitive infrastructure, leveraging worldwide logistics, synchronizing supply with demand, and measuring performance globally.
📖 Definition:
Echelon: A level of supply chain nodes. For example, suppliers, manufacturers, distributors, and retailers each represent an echelon. Each echelon adds cost, inventory, cycle time, and a profit expectation.
📖 Definition:
Channel master (nucleus firm): A supply chain company that orchestrates the overall value-creating activities of the chain; often a powerful manufacturer, brand owner, or dominant retailer.
📖 Definition:
Stakeholder: Any individual or group that has an interest that is affected or could be affected by the organization's activities.
📖 Definition:
Keiretsu: A form of cooperative relationship among companies in Japan in which the companies largely remain legally and economically independent even though they work closely in various ways such as financial backing. A member of a keiretsu generally owns a limited amount of stock in other member companies. A keiretsu generally forms around a bank and a trading company.

The Five Supply Chain Flows

  • Information flow — bidirectional; demand signals travel upstream, supply signals travel downstream
  • Strategic requirements flow — upstream from channel master to suppliers (specifications, standards)
  • Product/goods flow — downstream from suppliers to customers
  • Cash/financial flow — upstream from customers back to suppliers
  • Reverse flow — returns, repairs, recycling back upstream

Integration Strategies

💡 Key Point:
Vertical integration = a company owns multiple echelons end-to-end. Primary benefit: control. Primary drawback: high capital requirement and reduced flexibility.
💡 Key Point:
Lateral (horizontal) integration = outsourcing non-core activities to specialized partners. Now the dominant approach — provides economies of scale, specialized expertise, and global reach.

Production Strategies

💡 Key Point:
Make-to-stock (MTS): produce to forecast before orders arrive — fastest delivery, highest inventory risk. Make-to-order (MTO): produce only after confirmed order — lowest inventory, longest lead time. Assemble-to-order (ATO): keep components in stock, assemble to order — balance of speed and customization. Engineer-to-order (ETO): full design begins after order — most customized, longest lead time.
💡 Key Point:
Push supply chain: production driven by forecasts. Pull supply chain: production triggered by actual customer demand. Most modern chains use a hybrid push-pull model with a decoupling point.
💡 Key Point:
Bullwhip effect: small fluctuations in end-customer demand get amplified into large order swings as you move upstream. Root causes: demand signal distortion, order batching, price fluctuations, shortage gaming.

What is the ASCM definition of 'supply chain'?
ANSWER
The global network used to deliver products and services from raw materials to end customers through an engineered flow of information, physical distribution, and cash.
DEFINITION
What is the ASCM definition of supply chain management (SCM)?
ANSWER
The design, planning, execution, control, and monitoring of supply chain activities with the objective of creating net value, building competitive infrastructure, leveraging worldwide logistics, synchronizing supply with demand, and measuring performance globally.
DEFINITION
What is an echelon in supply chain terms?
ANSWER
A level of supply chain nodes. Suppliers, manufacturers, distributors, and retailers each represent an echelon. Each echelon adds cost, inventory, cycle time, and a profit margin expectation.
DEFINITION
What is a channel master (nucleus firm)?
ANSWER
A supply chain company that orchestrates the overall value-creating activities of the chain — often a powerful manufacturer, brand owner, or dominant retailer. Example: Apple orchestrates its global supply network.
DEFINITION
What is keiretsu?
ANSWER
A Japanese cooperative business structure where companies remain legally independent but work closely through financial backing and limited cross-ownership. Typically organized around a bank and trading company. Example: Toyota and its Tier 1 supplier network.
CONCEPT
What are the five flows in a supply chain?
ANSWER
1) Information (bidirectional), 2) Strategic requirements (upstream), 3) Product/goods (downstream), 4) Cash/financial (upstream), 5) Reverse/returns (upstream).
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between vertical and lateral (horizontal) integration?
ANSWER
Vertical = owning multiple echelons (high control, high capital). Lateral = outsourcing non-core activities to specialists (lower capital, better scale). Lateral is now the preferred approach.
CONCEPT
What is the bullwhip effect?
ANSWER
The amplification of demand variability moving upstream in the supply chain. Small consumer demand changes become large order swings at the manufacturer/supplier level. Caused by signal distortion, order batching, price fluctuations, and shortage gaming.
CONCEPT
What are the four production strategy types in order of increasing lead time?
ANSWER
Make-to-stock (MTS) → Assemble-to-order (ATO) → Make-to-order (MTO) → Engineer-to-order (ETO). MTS is fastest delivery; ETO is longest lead time with highest customization.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between push and pull supply chains?
ANSWER
Push: production driven by forecasts, inventory pushed downstream. Pull: production triggered by actual demand signals. Most modern supply chains use a push-pull hybrid with a decoupling point separating the two.
DEFINITION
What is a stakeholder in supply chain context?
ANSWER
Any individual or group whose interests are affected by or could be affected by the organization's activities — includes employees, shareholders, customers, suppliers, and local communities.
METRIC
What is the cash-to-cash cycle time?
ANSWER
The time from when cash is invested in raw materials to when cash is received from customers. A key supply chain efficiency metric — shorter cycles indicate better working capital management.
CONCEPT
What is the primary SCM goal beyond cost reduction?
ANSWER
To create net value — including building competitive infrastructure, leveraging worldwide logistics, synchronizing supply with demand, and measuring performance globally. Early cost-only approaches often backfired by damaging supplier relationships and flexibility.
DEFINITION
What is engineer-to-order (ETO)?
ANSWER
A production strategy where full engineering design begins only after receiving a customer order. Used for highly customized, complex products. Has the longest lead time of all production strategies.
DEFINITION
What is assemble-to-order (ATO) and when is it used?
ANSWER
A strategy where standard components are kept in stock and assembled only when a customer order is received. Balances customization and delivery speed. Common in PC manufacturing (Dell model) and automotive options-based ordering.
