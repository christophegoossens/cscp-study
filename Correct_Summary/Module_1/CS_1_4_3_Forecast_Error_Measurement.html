Overview

Measuring forecast error is essential for evaluating forecast performance, improving methods, and setting safety stock levels. Multiple error metrics exist because each captures a different aspect of forecast quality.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Forecast error: The difference between actual demand and forecasted demand for the same period. Error = Actual − Forecast. Positive error = underforecast; negative error = overforecast.
📖 Definition:
Mean Absolute Deviation (MAD): The average of the absolute values of the forecast errors over a number of periods. MAD = Σ|Actual − Forecast| / n. Measures the average size of errors regardless of direction.
📖 Definition:
Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE): The average of the absolute percentage errors. MAPE = Σ(|Actual − Forecast| / Actual) / n × 100%. Scales the error relative to demand volume, enabling comparison across products.
📖 Definition:
Bias: The average of the signed forecast errors. Bias = Σ(Forecast − Actual) / n. Positive bias = systematic over-forecasting; negative bias = systematic under-forecasting. A forecast with good MAD but high bias is systematically misleading.
📖 Definition:
Mean Squared Error (MSE): The average of the squared forecast errors. Penalizes large errors more heavily than MAD. Square root of MSE = Root Mean Square Error (RMSE).

Tracking Signal

📖 Definition:
Tracking signal: A measure used to determine whether a forecast is keeping pace with actual demand changes. Tracking signal = Cumulative Forecast Error / MAD. Values outside ±4 to ±6 indicate the forecast needs recalibration.
💡 Key Point:
A large positive tracking signal means the forecast has been consistently over the actual — the forecast is too high. A large negative signal means the forecast is consistently below actual — too low.

Key Principles

💡 Key Point:
MAD vs. MAPE: MAD is an absolute measure (in units). MAPE is a relative measure (percentage) — useful for comparing accuracy across products with very different volumes. MAPE cannot be calculated when actual demand is zero.
  • Safety stock is typically calculated using MAD: SS = k × MAD, where k is the service factor (z-score equivalent)
  • Alternatively: SS = z × σ(error) × √Lead Time, using the standard deviation of forecast errors
  • σ ≈ 1.25 × MAD is a commonly used approximation
  • Forecast error metrics should be reviewed regularly to detect bias and trigger model review
  • MAPE is the most commonly reported metric in business contexts due to its intuitive interpretability

DEFINITION
What is forecast error?
ANSWER
The difference between actual demand and the forecasted demand for the same period. Error = Actual − Forecast. Positive = underforecast; negative = overforecast.
DEFINITION
What is Mean Absolute Deviation (MAD)?
ANSWER
The average of the absolute values of forecast errors: MAD = Σ|Actual − Forecast| / n. Measures the average size of errors regardless of direction. Key input to safety stock calculation.
DEFINITION
What is Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE)?
ANSWER
MAPE = Σ(|Actual − Forecast| / Actual) / n × 100%. Expresses forecast error as a percentage of actual demand, enabling fair comparison across products with different volumes.
DEFINITION
What is forecast bias?
ANSWER
The average of signed forecast errors: Bias = Σ(Forecast − Actual) / n. Positive bias = systematic over-forecasting; negative bias = systematic under-forecasting. Bias is a separate dimension from overall error magnitude.
DEFINITION
What is a tracking signal and what does it measure?
ANSWER
Tracking signal = Cumulative Forecast Error / MAD. Measures whether a forecast is keeping pace with actual demand. Values outside ±4 to ±6 indicate the model needs recalibration due to systematic bias.
CONCEPT
What does a large positive tracking signal indicate?
ANSWER
The forecast has been consistently higher than actual demand (over-forecasting). The cumulative forecast errors are positive, indicating the model needs to be adjusted downward.
FORMULA
How is MAD used to calculate safety stock?
ANSWER
Safety stock = k × MAD, where k is the service factor. Alternatively, using standard deviation: SS = z × σ × √Lead Time, where σ ≈ 1.25 × MAD is a commonly used approximation.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the key difference between MAD and MAPE?
ANSWER
MAD is an absolute measure (in units of demand). MAPE is a relative measure (percentage) — useful for comparing accuracy across products with different volumes. MAPE cannot be calculated when actual demand is zero.
DEFINITION
What is Mean Squared Error (MSE)?
ANSWER
The average of squared forecast errors: MSE = Σ(Actual − Forecast)² / n. Penalizes large errors more heavily than MAD. The square root of MSE is Root Mean Square Error (RMSE), in the same units as demand.
KEY POINT
Why is forecast bias dangerous even if MAD is acceptable?
ANSWER
A forecast with acceptable MAD but high bias is systematically misleading — it consistently over- or under-estimates demand. Over time, this creates chronic inventory imbalances (excess stock or persistent stockouts) that MAD alone wouldn't flag.
FORMULA
What is the approximation relationship between MAD and standard deviation (σ)?
ANSWER
σ ≈ 1.25 × MAD is a commonly used approximation when demand follows a normal distribution. This allows conversion between the two measures for safety stock calculations.
CONCEPT
How should forecast error metrics be used in practice?
ANSWER
Review MAD/MAPE trends regularly to detect forecast quality deterioration. Use tracking signals to flag systematic bias. Use error data to set safety stock. Compare metrics by product, region, or planning horizon to prioritize improvement efforts.
CONCEPT
Why can't MAPE be calculated when actual demand is zero?
ANSWER
MAPE divides by actual demand — division by zero is undefined. For products with intermittent (lumpy) demand that has zero-demand periods, MAPE is not a valid metric. MAD or RMSE are used instead.
DEFINITION
What is the cumulative forecast error (CFE) and how is it used?
ANSWER
CFE = Σ(Forecast − Actual) over time. Used as the numerator in the tracking signal formula. CFE ≠ 0 indicates systematic bias. Tracking signal = CFE / MAD — used to trigger model recalibration.
CONCEPT
What is the service level factor (k or z-score) in safety stock calculation?
ANSWER
The number of standard deviations (or MAD multiples) of safety stock needed to achieve a desired service level. Example: z = 1.65 for 95% service level, z = 2.05 for 98%, z = 2.33 for 99%. Higher service level requires more safety stock.
