Overview

Forecasting methods are qualitative (judgment-based) or quantitative (math-based). Time-series and associative are the two quantitative categories; combination methods blend both.

  • Qualitative = judgment/experience-based; used for new products or highly volatile conditions
  • Bias risk in qualitative forecasting: estimators may skew high or low intentionally or unconsciously
  • Expert judgment / Delphi method: structured, anonymous expert consensus—prevents groupthink
  • Combination methods: quantitative baseline + qualitative adjustment; both can be measured for error separately
  • Time-series = based on historical patterns; assumes past predicts future
  • Time-series steps: visualize raw data → deseasonalize → forecast (SMA/WMA/ES) → reseasonalize
  • Seasonal index = month average / overall average; applied by dividing raw data (deseasonalize) and multiplying (reseasonalize)
  • Simple moving average (SMA): average of n recent periods; good for stable demand
  • Weighted moving average (WMA): assigns higher weights to recent periods
  • Exponential smoothing: Forecast = α × actual + (1-α) × prior forecast; higher α = more reactive
  • Associative (causal) forecasting: uses predictor variables (e.g., housing starts); best for long-term aggregate
  • Correlation ≠ causation; coefficient of correlation r (-1 to +1); r² = coefficient of determination
  • Simple regression: y = a + bx; multiple regression adds more predictor variables

According to the ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary, a leading indicator is “a specific business activity index that indicates future trends.” Leading indicators provide information that enables organizations to anticipate or predict micro- or macroeconomic changes. Acknowledging trends allows a company to prepare to take action to achieve a certain outcome and avoid undesirable circumstances. For instance, if a residential hardware manufacturer researches the residential home market and sees that building permits are down and a recession is near, it may decide that it should order more inexpensive components from its suppliers for its faucet products, since consumers will probably be more interested in making repairs to existing hardware rather than buying brand-new faucets.

Question 1
Which method gives more weight to recent observations using a smoothing factor alpha?
A.Simple moving average
B.Weighted moving average
C.Exponential smoothing
D.Linear regression
Exponential smoothing applies a smoothing factor α so recent data receives exponentially more weight than older data.
Question 2
A 4-period simple moving average for values 100, 120, 80, 100 produces a forecast of:
A.100
B.110
C.102
D.95
SMA-4 = (100+120+80+100) / 4 = 100.
Question 3
The Delphi method involves:
A.A single expert making a forecast
B.Asking a panel of experts independently and iteratively until consensus is reached
C.Averaging all sales force estimates
D.Running a regression model
The Delphi method uses multiple rounds of anonymous expert surveys with controlled feedback to converge on a consensus forecast while avoiding groupthink.
Question 4
Time-series decomposition splits historical demand into:
A.Price, volume, mix, and yield
B.Trend, seasonal, cyclical, and irregular components
C.Mean, variance, skewness, and kurtosis
D.Fixed, variable, step, and random
Time-series decomposition identifies trend, seasonality, cyclical pattern, and irregular (random) components to model each separately.
Question 5
When there is no historical data, the best forecasting approach is:
A.Exponential smoothing
B.Moving average
C.Market research or analogous product forecasting
D.Regression against own history
Without historical data, qualitative methods such as market research, analogous product comparison, or Delphi must be used.
