Overview

Supply chain maturity measures how well an organization's supply chain processes are defined, integrated, and continuously improved — relative to competing supply chains. Organizations evolve through five stages.

The Five Maturity Stages

📖 Definition:
Stage 1 — Multiple Dysfunction (Reactive): Siloed, ad hoc operations. Supply chain viewed only as a cost center. No clear supply chain plan or cross-functional coordination.
📖 Definition:
Stage 2 — Semifunctional Enterprise (Reactive-Efficient): Functional areas are defined and focused on efficiency, but remain largely siloed. Some Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP)/Material Requirements Planning (MRP) tools introduced. Reactive to disruptions.
📖 Definition:
Stage 3 — Integrated Enterprise (Proactive-Efficient): Company-wide cross-functional coordination via ERP/MRP. Supply chain becomes a competitive tool. Proactive planning replaces reactive firefighting.
📖 Definition:
Stage 4 — Extended Enterprise (Strategic Driver): Processes extend beyond company walls to include suppliers and customers. Collaborative planning and forecasting. Supply chain is a key strategic differentiator.
📖 Definition:
Stage 5 — Orchestrated Supply Chain (Consistent and Systematic): Full digital transformation — AI/ML analytics, IoT, real-time network visibility. Supply chain drives business strategy end-to-end.

Key Principles

💡 Key Point:
Maturity is relative to competitors — not an absolute standard. An organization at Stage 4 may lag behind competitors already at Stage 5.
💡 Key Point:
Organizations often display behaviors from two adjacent stages simultaneously. Transition between stages is gradual, not instant.
  • Higher maturity stages bring greater connectivity, visibility, resilience, and responsiveness
  • Technology enablement (ERP, AI/ML, IoT) is a key driver of stage progression
  • Cultural change (breaking down silos) is often harder than technology implementation
  • Different functions within the same company may be at different maturity stages

Specialized Supply Chain Types

📖 Definition:
Service industry company (ASCM): A firm that adds value through labor rather than manufacturing. The output is intangible and cannot be inventoried. Examples: transportation, utilities, healthcare, consulting.
  • Humanitarian supply chains: prioritize speed and flexibility, not profit — used for disaster relief
  • Retail supply chains: high SKU complexity, seasonal demand spikes, replenishment focus
  • Service supply chains: intangible outputs, cannot pre-build inventory; buffer with capacity
  • Manufacturing supply chains: the primary model covered by CSCP curriculum

CONCEPT
What are the five stages of supply chain maturity?
ANSWER
1) Multiple Dysfunction (reactive), 2) Semifunctional Enterprise (reactive-efficient), 3) Integrated Enterprise (proactive-efficient), 4) Extended Enterprise (strategic driver), 5) Orchestrated Supply Chain (consistent and systematic).
CONCEPT
What characterizes Stage 1 supply chain maturity?
ANSWER
Multiple dysfunction: siloed departments, ad hoc reactive behavior, no cross-functional coordination, supply chain perceived only as a cost center with no strategic role.
KEY DISTINCTION
What distinguishes Stage 3 (Integrated Enterprise) from Stage 2 (Semifunctional)?
ANSWER
Stage 3 has company-wide cross-functional coordination through ERP/MRP. Supply chain becomes a competitive tool. Stage 2 has defined functions but they remain siloed with no cross-functional collaboration.
CONCEPT
What characterizes Stage 4 (Extended Enterprise)?
ANSWER
Supply chain processes extend beyond company walls to include suppliers and customers. Collaborative planning and forecasting begins. Supply chain becomes a key strategic differentiator rather than just a cost center.
CONCEPT
What enables the transition to Stage 5 (Orchestrated Supply Chain)?
ANSWER
Digital transformation with AI/ML analytics, IoT connectivity, real-time network visibility across all partners, and autonomous decision-making in routine supply chain operations.
KEY POINT
Is supply chain maturity an absolute or relative measure?
ANSWER
Relative — maturity is always measured relative to competitor supply chains. A Stage 4 company may still be behind competitors already operating at Stage 5.
DEFINITION
How is a service industry company defined by ASCM?
ANSWER
A firm that adds value through labor rather than manufacturing. The output is intangible and cannot be inventoried. Examples include transportation, utilities, healthcare, and consulting.
CONCEPT
Why is cultural change often harder than technology change in maturity advancement?
ANSWER
Breaking down functional silos requires changing incentive structures, reporting lines, and organizational mindsets — changes that affect people's roles and power. Technology can be installed faster than behavior changes.
CONCEPT
Why can't service supply chains use inventory as a buffer?
ANSWER
Services are intangible — they cannot be stored or pre-built. A hospital cannot stockpile surgeries. Service supply chains buffer through spare capacity and rapid labor deployment.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is a humanitarian supply chain and how does it differ from commercial supply chains?
ANSWER
Humanitarian supply chains prioritize speed and flexibility to deliver aid (disaster relief, medical aid) — not profit optimization. They operate with volatile, unpredictable demand and often uncertain supply conditions.
CONCEPT
What technologies are associated with Stage 3 supply chain maturity?
ANSWER
Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) and Material Requirements Planning (MRP) systems that enable cross-functional data sharing and integrated planning within the company.
CONCEPT
What collaborative capabilities appear at Stage 4 maturity?
ANSWER
Collaborative Planning, Forecasting and Replenishment (CPFR), shared demand data with key suppliers and customers, joint S&OP processes, and supplier integration into NPD (new product development).
KEY POINT
Can an organization display behaviors from two maturity stages at the same time?
ANSWER
Yes — maturity transition is gradual. Different functions (procurement, logistics, manufacturing) may be at different stages. An organization often shows behaviors from two adjacent stages simultaneously.
CONCEPT
What is the primary strategic value of supply chain at Stage 5?
ANSWER
At Stage 5, the supply chain IS the business strategy — real-time optimization, predictive analytics, and end-to-end network coordination become core competitive advantages rather than support functions.
DEFINITION
What is Collaborative Planning, Forecasting and Replenishment (CPFR)?
ANSWER
A business process where trading partners (retailers and suppliers) share planning, forecasting, and replenishment data to reduce uncertainty and improve supply chain efficiency. A Stage 4 capability.

Maturity models measure how well processes are documented, standardized, integrated, and optimized across the organization and its partners.
Question 3

A.Optimized
B.Integrated
C.Defined
D.Ad hoc and reactive
In the lowest maturity stage (ad hoc/reactive), there is little process definition or collaboration — each function operates independently.
Question 4
Which SCOR metric category covers delivering the right product, to the right place, at the right time, in the right condition?
A.Responsiveness
B.Agility
C.Reliability
D.Cost
Reliability in SCOR covers Perfect Order Fulfillment — delivering the correct product to the correct location at the right time in perfect condition.
Question 5

A.On-time delivery percentage
B.The ability to respond to unplanned changes in demand or supply
C.Total supply chain cost
D.Asset utilization
SCOR Agility metrics (upside/downside flexibility, overall value at risk) measure responsiveness to unplanned changes such as demand spikes or supply disruptions.
