Overview

Supply and demand alignment is the ongoing process of balancing what customers want with what the supply chain can deliver — at the right time, cost, and service level. Misalignment creates either lost sales (too little supply) or excess cost (too much supply).

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Supply planning: The process of identifying, prioritizing, and aggregating the supply resources required to meet the anticipated demand in a cost-effective manner.
📖 Definition:
Demand plan: A forecast of expected future demand, adjusted for known events (promotions, new product launches), and segmented by product and time period. The primary output of demand planning.
📖 Definition:
Reconciliation: In the context of Sales and Operations Planning (S&OP), the process of balancing the demand plan against supply capabilities and capacity constraints to produce a consensus plan.

Alignment Mechanisms

💡 Key Point:
The primary mechanism for supply-demand alignment is Sales and Operations Planning (S&OP) — a monthly cross-functional process that balances demand plans against supply and capacity plans at an aggregate level.
  • Supply-demand gaps can be closed by: changing supply (production rate, overtime, outsourcing, inventory build) OR changing demand (promotions, pricing, customer prioritization)
  • Unconstrained demand plan vs. constrained supply plan: the S&OP process reconciles the two
  • Inventory serves as a buffer between supply and demand variability
  • Planned stockout risk must be quantified using service level targets

Decoupling Point

📖 Definition:
Decoupling point (Order Penetration Point): The point in the supply chain where a product moves from being pushed (forecast-driven) to pulled (demand-driven). Inventory buffers held at the decoupling point separate forecast-driven upstream from demand-driven downstream.
💡 Key Point:
Positioning the decoupling point strategically balances lead time against customization: moving it upstream reduces lead time but increases forecast risk; moving it downstream reduces risk but increases lead time.
  • Make-to-stock: decoupling point is at finished goods — maximum inventory, minimum lead time
  • Assemble-to-order: decoupling point is at components/subassemblies
  • Make-to-order: decoupling point is at raw materials
  • Engineer-to-order: decoupling point is before engineering design — maximum lead time

DEFINITION
What is supply planning?
ANSWER
The process of identifying, prioritizing, and aggregating the supply resources required to meet anticipated demand in a cost-effective manner — producing production, procurement, and replenishment plans.
DEFINITION
What is a demand plan?
ANSWER
A forecast of expected future demand, adjusted for known events (promotions, launches), and segmented by product and time period. The primary output of demand planning and the primary input to S&OP.
DEFINITION
What is the decoupling point (Order Penetration Point)?
ANSWER
The point in the supply chain where a product transitions from forecast-driven (push) to demand-driven (pull). Inventory buffers held at this point separate the two modes. Its position determines the trade-off between lead time and forecast risk.
CONCEPT
What is the primary mechanism for supply-demand alignment?
ANSWER
Sales and Operations Planning (S&OP) — a monthly cross-functional process that balances the demand plan against supply and capacity plans at an aggregate level to produce a consensus operational plan.
CONCEPT
What are the two ways to close a supply-demand gap?
ANSWER
1) Change supply (adjust production rate, use overtime, outsource, build inventory, expedite) OR 2) Change demand (promotions, pricing adjustments, customer prioritization, demand shaping).
CONCEPT
Where is the decoupling point for a make-to-stock product?
ANSWER
At finished goods inventory — product is made to forecast and held in finished goods. Maximum inventory investment but minimum lead time to the customer.
CONCEPT
Where is the decoupling point for an assemble-to-order product?
ANSWER
At the component or subassembly level — components are stocked to forecast; final assembly waits for the customer order. Balances customization with reasonable lead time.
DEFINITION
What is reconciliation in S&OP?
ANSWER
The process of balancing the (often unconstrained) demand plan against actual supply capabilities and capacity constraints to produce a feasible, consensus plan. Resolves gaps through cross-functional trade-off decisions.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between an unconstrained demand plan and a constrained supply plan?
ANSWER
Unconstrained demand plan: what customers want (no supply limits applied). Constrained supply plan: what the company can actually produce/deliver given capacity and resource limits. S&OP reconciles the two.
CONCEPT
What is the role of inventory in supply-demand alignment?
ANSWER
Inventory buffers the mismatch between supply variability and demand variability. Safety stock specifically buffers against forecast uncertainty and supply variability. Without inventory buffers, every mismatch causes either a stockout or idle capacity.
KEY DISTINCTION
How does moving the decoupling point upstream vs. downstream affect the supply chain?
ANSWER
Moving upstream: reduces customer lead time but increases forecast risk (more inventory pre-built to uncertain demand). Moving downstream: reduces forecast risk but increases customer lead time. A fundamental supply chain trade-off.
CONCEPT
What is a service level target and how does it relate to supply-demand alignment?
ANSWER
A target percentage of demand that the supply chain aims to fulfill without stockouts (e.g., 98% fill rate). Setting service level targets defines how much safety stock is needed and quantifies acceptable stockout risk.
DEFINITION
What is Sales and Operations Planning (S&OP)?
ANSWER
A monthly cross-functional business process that balances demand plans against supply and capacity plans. Involves sales, marketing, operations, finance, and executive leadership to create a consensus plan for the planning horizon.
CONCEPT
What is a supply-demand imbalance and what are its two consequences?
ANSWER
Too little supply vs. demand: stockouts, lost sales, poor customer service, emergency costs. Too much supply vs. demand: excess inventory, obsolescence risk, high holding costs, tied-up capital. Both are costly.
CONCEPT
What is an inventory buffer and how does it support alignment?
ANSWER
Inventory held strategically at the decoupling point or as safety stock to absorb variability in both demand and supply. Without buffers, perfect synchronization of supply and demand would be required — impossible in practice.
