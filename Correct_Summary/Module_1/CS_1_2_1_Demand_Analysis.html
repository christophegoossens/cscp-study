Overview

Demand analysis examines what customers want, when they want it, and in what quantities. It is the foundation for all supply chain planning — without accurate demand analysis, both overstock and stockout problems emerge.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Demand: The need for a particular product or component. The demand could come from any number of sources such as a customer order or a forecast.
📖 Definition:
Independent demand: Demand for an item that is unrelated to the demand for other items; demand created directly by customers. Finished goods and spare parts typically have independent demand — managed through forecasting.
📖 Definition:
Dependent demand: Demand directly derived from or linked to the production requirements of another item. Component parts and raw materials have dependent demand — calculated, not forecast, using Material Requirements Planning (MRP).
📖 Definition:
Derived demand: Demand for a component or raw material that results from demand for the finished product that uses it. Essentially equivalent to dependent demand.
📖 Definition:
Market demand: The total demand for a product category in a defined market at a defined price level.

Planning Approach by Demand Type

💡 Key Point:
Independent demand: managed using forecasting methods and inventory replenishment policies (reorder point, safety stock, Economic Order Quantity/EOQ).
💡 Key Point:
Dependent demand: calculated from the Master Production Schedule (MPS) exploded through the Bill of Materials (BOM) using MRP. No separate forecast needed — it is derived mathematically.

Key Concepts

  • Customer orders (known demand) always take priority over forecasts in planning — consume the forecast
  • Service/spare parts typically have independent demand patterns
  • Raw materials and subassemblies have dependent demand — calculated, not forecast
  • Demand can originate from external customers or internal production requirements
  • Channel demand may include distributor stocking decisions — distorts true end-customer signal
  • Demand sensing: using near-real-time Point-of-Sale (POS) data to detect actual demand faster than traditional forecast cycles
💡 Key Point:
The difference between market demand and channel demand: channel demand may include distributor stocking behavior, not just true end-customer pull — this is a root cause of the bullwhip effect.
  • Primary demand: demand for the product category overall
  • Selective demand: demand for a specific brand within the category
  • Demand analysis must separate signal from noise — systematic variation from random variation

DEFINITION
What is the ASCM definition of 'demand'?
ANSWER
The need for a particular product or component. The demand could come from any number of sources such as a customer order or a forecast.
DEFINITION
What is independent demand?
ANSWER
Demand for an item that is unrelated to the demand for other items — created directly by customers. Finished goods and service/spare parts typically have independent demand, managed through forecasting and replenishment policies.
DEFINITION
What is dependent demand?
ANSWER
Demand directly derived from or linked to the production of another item. Component parts and raw materials have dependent demand — calculated via MRP using the MPS and BOM, not forecast independently.
DEFINITION
What is derived demand?
ANSWER
Demand for a component or raw material that results from demand for the finished product that uses it. Essentially the same concept as dependent demand — components are not purchased for their own sake.
CONCEPT
How is dependent demand calculated in practice?
ANSWER
Through Material Requirements Planning (MRP), which explodes the Bill of Materials (BOM) from the Master Production Schedule (MPS). Component requirements are calculated mathematically — no separate forecast is needed.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the key difference in planning independent vs. dependent demand?
ANSWER
Independent demand uses forecasting and inventory replenishment policies (EOQ, reorder point, safety stock). Dependent demand is calculated from the MPS × BOM — driven by production schedules, not forecast separately.
CONCEPT
What is demand sensing?
ANSWER
Using near-real-time data (such as Point-of-Sale/POS data) to detect actual demand patterns faster than traditional forecasting cycles. Reduces forecast error by shortening the gap between demand occurrence and response.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is the difference between market demand and channel demand?
ANSWER
Market demand = total end-customer demand. Channel demand = orders from distributors/retailers who may order based on their own stocking decisions, not just actual consumer pull. This distortion contributes to the bullwhip effect.
CONCEPT
Why should customer orders take priority over forecasts in planning?
ANSWER
Customer orders represent firm, committed demand — they are certain. Forecasts are estimates with error. Orders should 'consume' the forecast to prevent double-counting and overproduction.
KEY DISTINCTION
What is primary demand vs. selective demand?
ANSWER
Primary demand: demand for the entire product category (e.g., smartphones). Selective demand: demand for a specific brand within that category (e.g., iPhone). Marketing strategies may target both.
DEFINITION
What is a Bill of Materials (BOM)?
ANSWER
A structured list of all components, subassemblies, and raw materials needed to produce one unit of a finished product. The BOM is the core input to MRP for calculating dependent demand.
DEFINITION
What is the Master Production Schedule (MPS)?
ANSWER
A plan that establishes what finished products will be produced, in what quantities, and when. The MPS is the primary input to MRP for calculating dependent demand for components.
FORMULA
What is Economic Order Quantity (EOQ)?
ANSWER
The order quantity that minimizes total inventory costs (ordering costs + holding costs). Used for independent demand items. Formula: EOQ = √(2 × Annual Demand × Ordering Cost / Holding Cost per unit).
CONCEPT
What is the bullwhip effect and what demand phenomenon causes it?
ANSWER
Amplification of demand variability moving upstream — small consumer demand changes become large order swings at the manufacturer/supplier level. Caused by channel demand distortion, order batching, safety stock decisions, and price promotions.
CONCEPT
What is demand management and why is demand analysis its input?
ANSWER
Demand management is the function of recognizing all demands for products/services and planning how to fulfill them. Demand analysis provides the volume, timing, and variability data that demand management plans around.

A.Independent demand
B.Dependent demand

Dependent demand is calculated from the requirements of parent items. Component demand is dependent on finished-goods demand and is not separately forecasted.
Question 2
Which technique categorizes inventory items by annual dollar usage to focus management attention?
A.XYZ analysis
B.ABC analysis
C.EOQ analysis
D.Cycle counting
ABC analysis ranks items by annual dollar usage: A items (high value) get tight control; C items (low value) get minimal oversight.
Question 3
Intermittent demand with many zero-demand periods is best described as:
A.Seasonal
B.Lumpy or erratic
C.Trending
D.Cyclical
Lumpy or erratic demand has long periods of no demand punctuated by irregular spikes, making standard forecasting techniques unreliable.
Question 4
Engine demand derived from automobile production schedules is an example of:
A.Independent demand
B.Stochastic demand
C.Dependent demand
D.Seasonal demand
Engine demand is dependent on the bill of materials for automobiles — it is calculated, not forecasted.
Question 5
Which demand characteristic describes a consistent long-term upward or downward movement in sales?
A.Seasonality
B.Trend
C.Cyclicality
D.Random variation
Trend is the long-term directional movement in demand data, distinct from shorter-term seasonal or random fluctuations.
