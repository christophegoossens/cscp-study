Overview

S&OP is a monthly process that reconciles supply, demand, and financial plans at the aggregate product-family level, resulting in one shared plan for sales, operations, and finance.

  • S&OP: held at least monthly; aggregate (product family) level; links strategy to execution
  • 5-meeting sequence: data gathering → demand planning → supply planning → pre-meeting → executive meeting
  • Demand review: chaired by VP of sales/marketing; product-family level; exception-based
  • Supply review: evaluates capacity feasibility; proposes options to meet or constrain demand plan
  • Financial review: analyzes demand and supply plans in dollars; checks fit with business plan
  • Pre-meeting: cross-functional prep; resolves issues before executive meeting
  • Executive meeting: CEO + demand/supply/finance executives; final consensus; one plan for all functions
  • Production strategy options: Level (stable output, vary inventory) vs Chase (match output to demand) vs Mixed
  • Supply-demand strategies: MTS (from stock), MTO (after order), ATO (assemble after order), ETO (engineer after order), PTO (package after order)
  • MTS: finished goods in inventory; high volume, standard products; schedule based on FGI
  • MTO: make after customer order; many end items, fewer components; longer lead time
  • ATO: assemble standard modules after order; balances customization with speed
  • S&OP benefits: links strategy to tactics, bridges customer value with supply efficiency, drives continuous improvement
  • Demand prioritization: time fences protect committed plans; policy defines who can override

S&OP provides the following organizational benefits:
Bridge between customer value and supply chain efficiency. The S&OP process integrates the tactical focus of the operations side with the customer orientation of the marketing and sales side. There is an inherent tension between the needs of the customer and the evolving quality standards of the supply chain. Reducing cycle times, squeezing out unnecessary inventory, paring down the number of partners, practicing lean manufacturing, and focusing relentlessly on quality may result in a swifter, more agile supply chain, but that can come at the expense of the end customer if marketing isn’t there to keep a close eye on the final product. In common terms, cheaper and faster are not always better from the customer’s perspective. After all, quality is “conformance to requirements or fitness for use” (ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary). On the other hand, the perfect product is not always affordable from an operational standpoint. S&OP integrates the sales and marketing perspective with the operational perspective so the inherent tension between the two can become a creative force that drives the business.

According to the ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary, a level production method is “a production planning method that maintains a stable production rate while varying inventory levels to meet demand.” This method aims for the same output in each period (e.g., each month). The level amount is based on the average of demand forecasts for each period with some modifications for desired inventory levels. For example, Porta Potty, a portable outhouse manufacturer, has seasonal demand spikes but produces the same number of units all year round. Leveling offers the benefits of simplicity and, from an operations viewpoint, predictability (no last-minute hiring of temps or layoffs). The tradeoff is the potential for inventory to pile up during periods of low demand or for stockouts if demand spikes upward.
The ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary defines a chase production method as “a production planning method that maintains a stable inventory level while varying production to meet demand.” This results in demand matching, which aims to match production to demand for each period. For example, restaurants often use chase methods by maintaining a part-time workforce that can be scheduled as needed and altering purchases based on projected demand. Manufacturers sometimes pursue this method by producing different goods for different seasons (e.g., K2 produces skis and in-line skates).

Assemble-to-order (ATO) (or finish-to-order) is “a production environment where a good or service can be assembled after receipt of a customer’s order” (ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary). Assemble-to-order products are partially manufactured and inventoried to await orders. This allows mass customization of products without long lead times and works well with products that can be tailored to customer taste by exchanging a limited number of modular components. The focus of master scheduling in assemble-to-order is on scheduling the manufacture of the modules or components and on final assembly.
A related concept, package to order, is “a production environment in which a good or service can be packaged after receipt of a customer order” (ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary). Package-to-order can be used to accommodate different numbers of units per package or customer language differences.

Question 2
The most common S&OP planning horizon is:
A.1 to 4 weeks
B.1 to 3 months
C.12 to 18 months rolling
D.5 to 10 years
S&OP typically uses a 12 to 18 month rolling horizon — long enough to influence capacity decisions but short enough to remain actionable.
Question 3

A.Differences between GAAP and IFRS
B.Mismatches between unconstrained demand forecasts and available supply capacity
C.Variance between actual and standard cost
D.Gaps in supplier quality performance
Gap analysis compares unconstrained demand with available capacity/supply to surface imbalances that require management decisions.

The pre-S&OP meeting aligns functions — sales, ops, finance — on recommended scenarios before executives make final decisions.
Question 5
S&OP effectiveness is best measured by:
A.Inventory turnover only
B.On-time delivery only
C.A balanced set including forecast accuracy, schedule adherence, and customer service level
D.Number of S&OP meetings held
S&OP effectiveness is measured by a balanced set: forecast accuracy (demand side), schedule adherence (supply side), and customer service level (outcome).
