Overview

Demand patterns can be studied over the long, medium, and short term. Long-term patterns use macroeconomic and microeconomic analyses to understand aggregate market forces. Medium- to short-term patterns look at a product’s average demand over time as well as factors that can make actual demand higher or lower than that average.

💡 Key Point: Supply chain managers study demand patterns to anticipate disruptions, shape supply chain strategy, and separate explainable demand factors (trend, seasonality, promotions) from random variation.
Macroeconomic Demand Patterns

Macroeconomics is the analysis of the behavior of the overall economy (supply and demand in aggregate) in response to variables such as employment, inflation, and monetary policy.

Economies go through cycles between periods of expansion and recession (or depression) in terms of gross domestic product (GDP). Near cycle peaks: low unemployment, production near full capacity. Near cycle troughs: higher unemployment, underutilized capacity.

Exhibit 1-17: Economic Cycles and Long-Term Trend

Real GDP is the total value of all final goods and services produced within a given set of economic boundaries, adjusted for inflation or deflation. Weighted average price tracks overall price levels across the economy.

  • End of recession: weighted average price low, demand high, but suppliers reluctant to invest (previous over-investment).
  • Strong growth phase: prices relatively low, real GDP high, new entrants push supply up and prices down.
  • Peak: prices and real GDP both high; pressure on input costs signals approaching slowdown.
Interpreting Macroeconomic Information

Macroeconomic data cannot be directly applied to a single industry or company—it relates to trends across all industries. Supply chain managers must translate aggregate signals into implications for their specific products and markets.

  • Market adjustments (e.g., housing price corrections) send ripple effects across industries with which they interact.
  • If a country’s net exports increase, aggregate demand increases (and vice versa). Export levels shift due to currency fluctuations, tariffs, trade agreements, and quotas.
  • If input prices increase (e.g., raw material scarcity), supply decreases.
  • Increases in productivity relative to inputs result in larger real GDP at any given price level.

Inflation and Deflation

Inflation: a sustained increase in the general level of prices. Deflation: a sustained decrease. Central banks target a very small, steady rate of inflation to encourage spending over hoarding cash. Very high inflation or deflation both create economic uncertainty.

Recessions and Depressions

A recession is when real GDP declines for two consecutive quarters. If long-range aggregate supply decreases during a recession, it becomes a depression.

Key Macroeconomic Metrics to Track

Beyond GDP: World Bank, IMF (International Monetary Fund), and WTO (World Trade Organization) data. Track metrics for multiple countries and economic regions, especially for global supply chain managers.

Microeconomics
📖 ASCM Definition — Microeconomics: the analysis of the behavior of individual economic decision makers (individuals and firms). Focuses on the price of a product, how sensitive customers are to price changes, cost trends, and employment levels in an industry.

Supply chain managers use microeconomic models to determine when/where to expand output, what product mix to carry, and when to raise or lower prices.

Law of Demand

As the price of a good or service increases, demand will decrease (all things being equal). This is the elasticity of demand. Additional reinforcing forces:

  • Income effect: as prices go down, purchasing power goes up, increasing demand.
  • Substitution effect: consumers shift to lower-priced substitutes as a product’s price rises.
  • Exception — Giffen goods: staple goods whose demand increases as price rises (e.g., subsistence goods when incomes are very low).

Law of Supply

As the price of a good or service increases, supply will also increase. Higher prices give producers more incentive to produce. Related concepts:

  • Economies of scale: per-unit costs decrease as production volume increases.
  • Diminishing returns: beyond a certain production volume, each additional unit costs more to produce.

Equilibrium

On the micro scale, individual supply and demand tend toward an equilibrium price. Supply chain managers must monitor this and adjust when conditions change.

Price Elasticity of Demand
📖 ASCM Definition — Price Elasticity (of Demand): the degree of change in buyer demand in response to changes in product price. Calculated by dividing the percentage change in quantity bought by the percentage change in price.
  • Elastic: demand changes significantly with price (e.g., smartphones, luxury goods). Coefficient > 1.
  • Inelastic: demand barely changes with price (e.g., eggs, fuel in short term). Coefficient < 1.
  • Low-priced items, necessities, and short-term scenarios tend toward inelastic demand.
  • Luxuries, long-term scenarios, and products with close substitutes tend toward elastic demand.
Exhibit 1-18: Price Elasticity of Demand Example

Supply chain managers can use elasticity to optimize price-volume trade-offs. When logistics costs are subtracted from margin-per-unit, the optimal price point may shift from what pure revenue analysis suggests.

Price Elasticity of Supply

Shows how sensitive suppliers are to price changes. Over the short term, suppliers tend to be inelastic (fixed capacity). Over the long term, supply is more elastic as capacity can be added or removed.

Marginal Analysis

Focuses only on marginal utility and marginal cost — the utility and cost of the next unit. Used to decide when adding one more unit of output (or capacity) is still worth it. Example: if a truck costs €1,000 and can carry 10,000 kg, the average cost per kg is €0.10; but if you only need 5,000 kg, the marginal cost doubles — at that volume the truck is not adding full value.

Short- to Medium-Term Demand Patterns

Base demand is the long-term average demand for a product or service. Actual demand fluctuates above and below the base due to several factors:

Trend

📖 ASCM Definition — Trend: the general upward or downward movement of a variable over time (e.g., demand or process attribute). Common types: linear (upward, neutral, or negative) and exponential (skyrocketing). Trends can change direction at any time due to internal or external forces.

Cycles

Periodic upward, neutral, or downward shifts in demand lasting longer than one year. Economic cycles of recession and growth are prime examples. External demand drivers (economic cycles, population growth, major events or disasters) are sometimes used to quantify and model cyclical demand.

Seasonality

📖 ASCM Definition — Seasonality (Seasonal Variation): a predictable, repetitive pattern of demand measured within a year, during which time the demand grows and declines. These are calendar-related patterns that can appear annually, quarterly, monthly, weekly, daily, and/or hourly.
💡 Key Point: Distinguish seasonality from cycles. Seasonality repeats predictably within a year and can be forecasted. Cycles last longer than a year and are harder to predict. Whenever seasonality is significant, it must be removed (deseasonalized) before forecasting, then added back in afterward.

Promotions and Other Internal Drivers

Promotions (discounts, advertising) and internal drivers (deals to gain favorable product placement) create demand spikes. These are knowable in advance and should be incorporated into the forecast model separately.

Random (Irregular) Variation

📖 ASCM Definition — Random Variation: a fluctuation in data caused by uncertain or random occurrences. It is the unpredictable part of a data series — the remaining variation after all other factors (trend, seasonality, cycles, promotions) are accounted for. The goal is to minimize this component by identifying more explainable factors.
💡 Key Point: Random variation is analogous to common cause variation in statistical process control (SPC) — the multitude of small, unidentifiable factors. Trend, seasonality, cycles, and promotions are analogous to special cause (assignable cause) variation. Demand stripped of all special causes should conform to a normal (bell curve) distribution.

What is a Giffen good?
ANSWER
A staple good whose demand actually increases as its price rises — an exception to the law of demand. Occurs when a good is so essential (and income so low) that consumers buy more of it as price rises because they can no longer afford substitutes.

What is the law of supply?
ANSWER
As the price of a good or service increases, supply will also increase. Higher prices give producers more incentive to produce.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are economies of scale vs. diminishing returns?
ANSWER
Economies of scale: per-unit costs decrease as production volume increases. Diminishing returns: beyond a certain volume, each additional unit costs more to produce (the opposite effect kicks in).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the equilibrium price in microeconomics?
ANSWER
The price at which the quantity supplied equals the quantity demanded — the point where supply and demand curves intersect. Both macro and micro supply/demand tend toward equilibrium.
TERM / CONCEPT
What makes a product elastic vs. inelastic in demand?
ANSWER
Elastic (coefficient > 1): demand changes significantly with price — innovative products, luxuries, goods with close substitutes, long-term scenarios. Inelastic (coefficient < 1): demand barely changes — staples, necessities, low-priced items, short-term scenarios.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does price elasticity of supply differ from price elasticity of demand?
ANSWER
Price elasticity of supply measures how sensitive SUPPLIERS are to price changes. Short-term: inelastic (fixed capacity). Long-term: more elastic as capacity can be added or removed.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is marginal analysis?
ANSWER
A decision-making method that focuses only on the marginal utility and marginal cost of the NEXT unit, rather than average costs. Used to determine whether adding one more unit of output or capacity is still worth it.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is base demand?
ANSWER
The long-term average demand for a product or service. Actual demand fluctuates above and below the base due to trend, cycles, seasonality, promotions, and random variation.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between seasonality and cycles?
ANSWER
Seasonality: a predictable pattern repeating WITHIN a year — can be forecasted and must be removed before forecasting, then added back. Cycles: shifts lasting LONGER than a year (e.g., economic recession/growth) — harder to predict.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are internal vs. external demand drivers?
ANSWER
External: economic cycles, population growth, major events or disasters. Internal: promotions (discounts, advertising), deals for favorable product placement. Both should be modeled separately in demand forecasts.
TERM / CONCEPT
In statistical process control (SPC) terms, what is random variation analogous to?
ANSWER
Common cause (general cause) variation — the multitude of small, unidentifiable factors. Trend, seasonality, cycles, and promotions are analogous to special cause (assignable cause) variation.
TERM / CONCEPT
If random variation is the only remaining component, what distribution should demand follow?
ANSWER
A normal distribution (bell curve) — because the special causes (trend, seasonality, cycles, promotions) have all been accounted for and removed.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why must seasonality be removed before forecasting?
ANSWER
Because seasonal spikes and dips distort the underlying demand pattern. The deseasonalizing process removes the seasonal component so the base demand and trend can be forecasted accurately, then seasonality is added back to get the final forecast.
TERM / CONCEPT
How should a supply chain manager interpret macroeconomic data?
ANSWER
Macroeconomic data reflects aggregate trends across ALL industries, not a specific company or product. Managers must translate macro signals into implications for their specific market, considering input costs, export levels, currency changes, and productivity shifts.
TERM / CONCEPT
What macroeconomic institutions publish data relevant to global supply chains?
ANSWER
The World Bank, International Monetary Fund (IMF), and World Trade Organization (WTO). These provide globalized metrics across multiple countries and economic regions — critical for managers buying and selling globally.
