  • Long-term: macro- and microeconomic analyses (economic cycles, GDP, price elasticity).

Macro Environment: environment external to a business including technological, economic, natural, and regulatory forces that marketing efforts cannot control.
Macroeconomic Demand Patterns

Macroeconomics = analysis of the overall economy (aggregate supply and demand). Economies cycle between expansion and recession in terms of real GDP.

  • Real GDP: total value of all final goods/services produced, adjusted for inflation/deflation.
  • Peak: low unemployment, near-full capacity.
  • Trough: high unemployment, underutilized capacity.
  • Recession: real GDP declines for two consecutive quarters.
  • Depression: recession + long-range aggregate supply also decreases.
  • Inflation: sustained price increase. Deflation: sustained price decrease. Central banks target small, steady inflation.

💡 Key Point: Macro data applies to ALL industries — not one company. Managers must translate signals (currency shifts, tariff changes, input price moves) into implications for their specific product.
Microeconomics
Microeconomics: the analysis of the behavior of individual economic decision makers (individuals and firms). Focuses on price sensitivity, cost trends, and employment in a given industry.
  • Law of demand: price up → demand down. Reinforced by income effect (lower price = more purchasing power) and substitution effect (switch to cheaper alternatives). Exception: Giffen goods (staples where demand rises with price).
  • Law of supply: price up → supply up (more incentive to produce).
  • Economies of scale: per-unit cost decreases as volume increases.
  • Diminishing returns: beyond a point, each extra unit costs more to produce.
  • Equilibrium: price at which quantity supplied = quantity demanded.
Price Elasticity
Price Elasticity (of Demand): degree of change in buyer demand in response to changes in product price. Calculated as % change in quantity ÷ % change in price.
  • Elastic (>1): demand changes significantly with price — innovative/luxury goods, long-term, goods with substitutes.
  • Inelastic (<1): demand barely changes — staples, necessities, low-priced items, short-term.
  • Price elasticity of supply: how sensitive suppliers are to price. Short-term = inelastic (fixed capacity); long-term = elastic.
  • Marginal analysis: focus on the cost/utility of the NEXT unit only — used to decide if adding capacity/output is still worth it.

Short- to Medium-Term Demand Patterns

Trend: general upward or downward movement of a variable over time (e.g., linear or exponential). Can change direction due to internal or external forces.

Cycles: periodic demand shifts lasting longer than one year (e.g., economic expansion/recession). Harder to predict than seasonality.

Seasonality: a predictable, repetitive pattern of demand measured within a year, during which demand grows and declines. Calendar-related: annual, quarterly, monthly, weekly, daily, hourly.
💡 Key Point: Seasonality vs. cycles — seasonality repeats within a year and can be forecasted; cycles last over a year and are less predictable. Significant seasonality must be REMOVED before forecasting (deseasonalized), then added back afterward.

Promotions/internal drivers: discounts, advertising, placement deals — knowable in advance, model separately.

Random Variation: fluctuation in data caused by uncertain or random occurrences. The unpredictable residual after trend, seasonality, cycles, and promotions are accounted for. Goal: minimize it by identifying more explainable factors.
💡 Key Point: In statistical process control (SPC) terms — random variation = common cause; trend/seasonality/cycles/promotions = special cause (assignable). Demand stripped of all special causes should follow a normal (bell curve) distribution.

What is macroeconomics?
ANSWER
Analysis of the behavior of the OVERALL economy (aggregate supply and demand) in response to variables such as employment, inflation, and monetary policy.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the ASCM definition of microeconomics?
ANSWER
The analysis of the behavior of individual economic decision makers (individuals and firms). Focuses on price sensitivity, cost trends, and employment levels in a given industry.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the ASCM definition of price elasticity?
ANSWER
The degree of change in buyer demand in response to changes in product price. Calculated as: % change in quantity bought ÷ % change in price.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the ASCM definition of trend?
ANSWER
The general upward or downward movement of a variable over time (e.g., demand or process attribute). Types: linear and exponential. Can change direction at any time.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the ASCM definition of seasonality?
ANSWER
A predictable, repetitive pattern of demand measured within a year, during which demand grows and declines. Calendar-related: can appear annually, quarterly, monthly, weekly, daily, hourly.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the ASCM definition of random variation?
ANSWER
A fluctuation in data caused by uncertain or random occurrences. The unpredictable residual after trend, seasonality, cycles, and promotions are accounted for.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is real GDP?
ANSWER
Total value of all final goods and services produced within a given set of economic boundaries, adjusted for inflation or deflation.
TERM / CONCEPT
Recession vs. depression?
ANSWER
Recession: real GDP declines for two consecutive quarters. Depression: recession where long-range aggregate supply also decreases (deeper, more prolonged).
TERM / CONCEPT
Inflation vs. deflation?
ANSWER
Inflation: sustained increase in the general price level. Deflation: sustained decrease. Central banks target small, steady inflation to encourage spending over hoarding.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the law of demand?
ANSWER
As price increases, demand decreases (all else equal). Reinforced by: income effect (lower price = more purchasing power) and substitution effect (consumers shift to cheaper alternatives). Exception: Giffen goods.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a Giffen good?
ANSWER
A staple good whose demand INCREASES as price rises — an exception to the law of demand. Occurs when the good is so essential and income so low that no cheaper substitute exists.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the law of supply?
ANSWER
As price of a good increases, supply increases. Higher prices give producers more incentive to produce.
TERM / CONCEPT
Economies of scale vs. diminishing returns?
ANSWER
Economies of scale: per-unit costs DECREASE as volume increases. Diminishing returns: beyond a threshold, each additional unit costs MORE to produce.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is price elastic demand vs. inelastic demand?
ANSWER
Elastic (>1): demand changes significantly with price — luxuries, innovative products, goods with substitutes, long-term. Inelastic (<1): demand barely changes — staples, necessities, low-priced items, short-term.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is price elasticity of supply?
ANSWER
How sensitive SUPPLIERS are to price changes. Short-term: inelastic (fixed capacity). Long-term: more elastic as capacity can be added or removed.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is marginal analysis?
ANSWER
Decision method focusing only on the cost and utility of the NEXT unit. Used to determine when adding one more unit of output or capacity is no longer worth it.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is base demand?
ANSWER
The long-term average demand for a product or service. Actual demand fluctuates around it due to trend, cycles, seasonality, promotions, and random variation.
TERM / CONCEPT
Seasonality vs. cycles — key distinction?
ANSWER
Seasonality: repeats predictably WITHIN a year — can be forecasted; must be deseasonalized before forecasting, then added back. Cycles: last LONGER than a year (e.g., recession/growth) — harder to predict.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why must seasonality be removed before forecasting?
ANSWER
Seasonal spikes/dips distort the underlying trend. Deseasonalizing isolates the base demand and trend for accurate forecasting. Seasonality is then re-added to produce the final forecast.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are internal vs. external demand drivers?
ANSWER
External: economic cycles, population growth, major events/disasters. Internal: promotions (discounts, advertising), deals for favorable product placement. Both should be modeled separately.
TERM / CONCEPT
Random variation = what type of variation in SPC terms?
ANSWER
Common cause variation — the multitude of small, unidentifiable factors. Trend/seasonality/cycles/promotions = special cause (assignable cause) variation.
TERM / CONCEPT
What distribution should demand follow after removing all special causes?
ANSWER
A normal distribution (bell curve) — because trend, seasonality, cycles, and promotions have all been removed, leaving only random variation.
TERM / CONCEPT
What global institutions publish key macroeconomic data for supply chains?
ANSWER
World Bank, International Monetary Fund (IMF), and World Trade Organization (WTO). Critical for managers buying and selling across multiple countries.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does macro data differ from data useful for individual supply chain decisions?
ANSWER
Macro data reflects aggregate trends across ALL industries. Managers must translate signals (currency shifts, input prices, export levels, productivity) into implications for their specific products and markets.
TERM / CONCEPT
What happens to demand near a recession trough vs. growth peak?
ANSWER
Trough: weighted average price low, demand high but suppliers reluctant to invest. Growth peak: prices relatively low, real GDP high, new entrants increase supply and push prices down further.

Question 1
Which demand pattern shows a consistent long-term increase or decrease over time?
A.Seasonality
B.Random variation
C.Trend
D.Cyclical variation
A trend is a long-term upward or downward movement in demand. It differs from seasonal variation (which is periodic and repeating within a year) and cyclical variation (which spans multiple years).
Question 2
Seasonal demand patterns are best characterized by:
A.Random, unpredictable fluctuations around an average
B.Demand swings tied to the business/economic cycle over multiple years
C.Predictable, repeating fluctuations within a fixed time period (e.g., annual)
D.A consistent upward or downward drift over time
Seasonal patterns repeat on a fixed cycle (typically annual) and are predictable — e.g., holiday toys, summer beverages, winter clothing. They are tied to the calendar, not the economic cycle.
Question 3
What is 'random variation' (noise) in demand?
A.Demand changes caused by a new product launch
B.Unpredictable fluctuations in demand that cannot be explained by trend, seasonal, or cyclical factors
C.The planned variability built into the production schedule
D.Demand driven by competitor price promotions
Random variation (noise) is the unexplained, irregular part of demand that remains after trend, seasonal, and cyclical components have been identified. It cannot be forecasted and must be managed with safety stock.
Question 4
Cyclical demand variation differs from seasonal variation because it:
A.Is predictable and repeats on an annual calendar
B.Spans multiple years and is tied to economic business cycles rather than the calendar
C.Can be entirely eliminated through better forecasting
D.Is always caused by marketing promotions
Cyclical variation reflects broad economic expansions and contractions (e.g., recession-driven demand drops) that play out over years, not within a single annual cycle like seasonality.
Question 5
A 'lumpy' or 'intermittent' demand pattern is most characteristic of:
A.Fast-moving consumer goods sold daily in high volumes
B.Slow-moving spare parts or capital equipment ordered infrequently in variable quantities
C.Products with a steady, predictable daily demand
D.Seasonal products sold only in summer months
Lumpy/intermittent demand has long periods of zero demand punctuated by occasional large orders — typical of MRO parts, defense equipment, or industrial spare parts.
Question 6
Which statistical measure best captures the variability (spread) in demand data?
A.Mean (average) demand
B.Mode (most frequent value)
C.Standard deviation
D.Median demand
Standard deviation measures the dispersion of demand around its average. Higher standard deviation = more variability = more safety stock needed to maintain a given service level.
Question 7
When demand shows a clear upward trend, which forecasting adjustment is needed?
A.Increase the smoothing constant alpha to react faster to recent data
B.Use a trend-adjusted exponential smoothing method (Holt's method) that incorporates a trend component
C.Switch to qualitative forecasting methods
D.Reduce the forecast review cycle to weekly
Simple exponential smoothing can't capture trends — it will always lag. Holt's method (double exponential smoothing) adds a trend component to track the direction and magnitude of the trend.
Question 8
A product has average weekly demand of 100 units with a standard deviation of 20 units. What does the 20-unit standard deviation imply for inventory planning?
A.The product needs exactly 20 units of safety stock
B.The product has no need for safety stock since the average is predictable
C.Safety stock should be set using a multiple of the standard deviation based on the desired service level
D.The product should be discontinued due to high variability
Safety stock is calculated as: z × σ × √LT (where z is the service-level z-score). The standard deviation of 20 is the key input — higher variability requires more safety stock for the same service level.
Question 9
What is a 'demand signal' in a modern supply chain?
A.The marketing team's annual sales target
B.Any data (POS, orders, inventory levels) that indicates actual or anticipated consumer demand and can trigger supply chain action
C.A written request from the warehouse for replenishment
D.The production schedule issued by the factory
Demand signals are real-time or near-real-time data points that trigger supply chain responses — POS data, EDI orders, and IoT inventory readings are all demand signals.
Question 10
Which pattern is most likely for a new product in its launch phase?
A.Steady state demand with low variability
B.High random variability with an underlying upward trend as the product gains market adoption
C.Seasonal demand peaking in December
D.Lumpy, intermittent demand with no discernible pattern
New product launches typically show trend-driven growth (as market awareness builds) combined with high variability (unpredictable early adoption rates). Forecasting new products is very difficult.
Question 11
What is the 'coefficient of variation' (CV) used for in demand analysis?
A.Calculating the economic order quantity
B.Measuring relative variability of demand (standard deviation / mean) — used to classify items by forecastability
C.Determining the optimal reorder point
D.Measuring supplier on-time delivery performance
The CV (σ/μ) normalizes variability relative to average demand, enabling comparison across products with different scales. High CV = difficult to forecast; used in inventory policy decisions.
Question 12
Promotions and trade deals typically cause which demand pattern distortion?
A.Smoothed, trend-adjusted demand that is easier to forecast
B.Demand spikes followed by a trough as customers consume forward-bought inventory (forward buying / pantry loading)
C.Seasonal demand that peaks 3 months earlier than usual
D.Steady demand growth with no variability
Trade promotions cause customers to buy large quantities upfront (forward buying / pantry loading), creating artificial demand spikes followed by troughs — a major contributor to the bullwhip effect.
