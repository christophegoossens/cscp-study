Overview

Demand patterns span macro- and microeconomic forces and short-term statistical components. Understanding these helps supply chain managers adapt plans to external shifts.

  • Macroeconomics: aggregate supply/demand across the economy; cycles of expansion and recession
  • Real GDP = total value of final goods/services; rises in expansions, falls in recessions
  • Inflation = sustained price increase; deflation = sustained price decrease; central banks target low stable inflation
  • Recession = 2 consecutive quarters of declining real GDP
  • Microeconomics: individual market behavior; law of demand and law of supply
  • Law of demand: as price rises, demand falls (all else equal)
  • Law of supply: as price rises, supply increases (more sellers can profit)
  • Price elasticity of demand = % change in quantity / % change in price; elastic > 1, inelastic < 1
  • Necessities are inelastic; luxuries and innovative products are elastic
  • Base demand + trend + seasonality + cycles + random variation = actual observed demand
  • Seasonality: calendar-based repeating pattern within one year (use seasonal index to adjust)
  • Cycles: demand shifts lasting longer than one year; linked to economic cycles
  • Random variation: unpredictable; caused by uncertain events; distinguish from special/assignable causes

The ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary defines microeconomics as “the analysis of the behavior of individual economic decision makers (individuals and firms).” Microeconomics focuses on specifics such as the price of a product, how sensitive customers are to changes in that price (how much price changes impact demand), cost trends for a business, or employment levels in a given industry. Supply chain managers can use microeconomic theories and models to determine when or where to expand output, what product mix to have where, when to raise or lower prices, and so on.

A trend is the “general upward or downward movement of a variable over time (e.g., demand or process attribute)” (ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary). A trend is a long-term shift, and two common examples are linear (upward-sloping, neutral, or negative) or exponential (skyrocketing upward or downward). Trends can change direction at any time as a result of internal or external forces.

Random variation is “a fluctuation in data that is caused by uncertain or random occurrences” (ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary). It is the unpredictable part of a data series that cannot be explained by the other factors, basically the remaining variation after the other factors such as seasonality are accounted for. The idea is to minimize this component by finding more and more explainable factors.

Question 1
Which demand pattern repeats at fixed calendar intervals?
A.Cyclical
B.Trend
C.Seasonal
D.Random
Seasonal patterns repeat at fixed calendar intervals due to weather, holidays, or events — distinct from multi-year economic cycles.
Question 2
Random variation in demand data is:
A.Predictable fluctuations
B.Unexplained unpredictable noise around the average
C.Long-term growth
D.Calendar-based patterns
Random variation is the unexplained noise in demand data after removing trend, seasonal, and cyclical components. It cannot be forecasted.
Question 3
A demand pattern driven by broad multi-year economic expansions and contractions is called:
A.Seasonal
B.Trend
C.Cyclical
D.Random
Cyclical patterns follow multi-year economic cycles and are generally longer and less predictable than seasonal patterns.
Question 4
Demand decomposition separates demand into:
A.Price, volume, mix, and yield
B.Trend, seasonal, cyclical, and random components
C.Mean, variance, skewness, and kurtosis
D.Fixed, variable, step, and random
Demand decomposition breaks historical demand into component patterns so forecasters can model each component separately.
Question 5
Lumpy demand is characterized by:
A.Steady daily sales with minor fluctuations
B.Higher sales every summer
C.Large infrequent orders interspersed with zero-demand periods
D.Demand that tracks GDP growth
Lumpy demand has large, infrequent spikes separated by periods of zero demand — common for spare parts and capital equipment.
