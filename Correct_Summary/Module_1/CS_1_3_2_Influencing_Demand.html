PDCA Model for Influencing Demand
📖 ASCM Definition — PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Action): a four-step process for quality improvement. Plan: develop a plan to effect improvement. Do: carry out the plan (preferably small scale). Check: observe the effects. Action: study results to determine what was learned and what can be predicted.
  • Plan: product/brand management, marketing, and sales research and develop strategies and tactics.
  • Do: execute the plans — launch campaigns, run promotions, adjust pricing.
  • Check: demand manager reviews metrics vs. plan; documents variances and their causes.
  • Action: demand manager leads replanning to respond to variances and address underlying issues.

Demand Generation

Demand generation translates latent demand ideas into actual sales. Critical for new product introductions — most new products fail. Key marketing responsibilities:

  • Educate customers: buyers must know the product exists. Use appropriate media for the audience (print, TV, internet). Know who the actual buyer is (not always obvious).
  • Educate supply chain partners: distributors and retailers need to understand the product to design, source, stock, and sell it effectively.
💡 Key Point: Demand-influencing activities may include convincing customers to accept substitutions or change purchase timing. Influence over marketing and operations is not automatic — especially in cross-company supply chain relationships.
The Four Ps & Demand Shaping
📖 ASCM Definition — Four Ps: a set of marketing tools to direct the business offering to the customer. The four Ps are product, price, place, and promotion.
📖 ASCM Definition — Demand Shaping: the use of tactics such as price incentives, advertising, product positioning, product modifications and substitutions, or trade programs to entice customers to purchase.

Customer-focused marketing has shifted focus from product-out to customer-in. Key tools:

  • Product: starts with customer need. Increasingly customizable by segment. Must meet quality expectations and minimize challenges for customer use.
  • Price: strategic decision based on competition, perceived value, and brand identity. In customer-focused model, price and product are tightly linked. Must cover costs, return profit, and remain competitive. Price is often the major demand variable.
  • Placement (distribution): getting the product into the customer’s hands via the right channel. Customer-focused = contact channel strategy. Channels must be: accessible, reliable, complete, secure, direct, convenient, fast, and flexible. From the business side: must allow control/consistency and profitability.
  • Promotion: consumer research, segmentation, advertising, publicity, sampling, and customer relationship management (CRM).
📖 ASCM Definition — Branding: the use of a name, term, symbol, design, or a combination of these to identify a product.
💡 Key Point: Brand image can take years to build and be destroyed in an instant. Customer loyalty is increasingly transferring from products to brands. Packaging also serves a marketing purpose: commands attention, reinforces features/benefits, and can promote sustainability goals.
Product Life Cycle Stages

Exhibit 1-28 shows 6 stages of the product life cycle (including pre-launch development):

  • Development: incubation stage — no sales, focused on R&D. Costs are high. Product gets designed, sourced, and built for launch.
  • Introduction: sales low until customers become aware. Marketing spend is high. Qualitative forecasting required.
  • Growth: rapid revenue growth. More customers and distributors. Must commit resources to both satisfying market and managing growth.
  • Maturity: most profitable stage. Sales still increase but at a slower rate. Competition intensifies; price and dependability become key.
  • Decline: sales begin to fall. Market becomes saturated, product becomes outdated or is superseded.
📖 ASCM Definition — Life Cycle Analysis: a quantitative forecasting technique based on applying past patterns of demand data. Covers introduction, growth, maturity, saturation, and decline of similar products to a new product family.
Product Life Cycle Management (PLM)
📖 ASCM Definition — Product Life Cycle Management: the process of facilitating the development, use, and support of products that customers want and need. Helps professionals envision the creation and preservation of product information, both to the customer and along the reverse-logistics portion of the supply chain.

PLM manages new product introductions, existing product life cycles, and end-of-life activities (phase-out, reverse logistics). PLM software provides tools for the full lifecycle.

Exhibit 1-29: Product Life Cycle Management

NPI schedule phases:

  • Innovation: market research, idea generation.
  • Concept: vision vetted for customer demand and financial promise.
  • Development: engineering, manufacturing, and other functions add detail.
  • Prototyping: working model developed; proves feasibility.
  • Engineering: product structure, bills of material, drawings, tolerances.
  • Supply: supply requirements defined, capable suppliers identified, footprint mapped.
  • Manufacturing: production capability confirmed.
  • Launch: marketing and sales launch product and foster awareness.
💡 Key Point: Innovative products require compressed timelines to shorten time to market. Functional products have low margins, making cost the primary factor.
NPI Frequency vs. Demand Uncertainty

New products range from innovative (high uncertainty, short life cycles) to functional (stable demand, long life cycles). Design and supply chain strategy must align with product type.

Exhibit 1-30: NPI Frequency Versus Demand Uncertainty

4 categories (clockspeed = rate of product change):

  • Unstable demand, low clockspeed: long life cycles, integral design. Can use make-to-stock with safety stock.
  • Unstable demand, high clockspeed: short life cycles, high rate of change. Use pull manufacturing; agile/lean supply chains.
  • Stable demand, low clockspeed: staple items, low margins. Produce economically and efficiently; focus on cost.
  • Stable demand, high clockspeed: few finished goods but many component types fit here. High volume, lean manufacturing.

TERM / CONCEPT
What is clockspeed in the context of NPI?
ANSWER
The rate of product change — how quickly products in a category are replaced by new versions. High clockspeed = short life cycles and frequent new product introductions. Low clockspeed = long-lived products with infrequent redesigns.
TERM / CONCEPT
How has customer-focused marketing changed the Four Ps?
ANSWER
Shifted from product-out (we make it, then sell it) to customer-in (customer need drives product design). Product starts with customer need; placement uses a contact channel strategy; price and product are tightly linked; promotion uses CRM to build individualized relationships.
TERM / CONCEPT
What criteria must a customer-facing channel meet (from the customer perspective)?
ANSWER
Accessible, reliable, complete, secure and error-free, direct (decision authority), convenient, fast, and flexible (accommodates non-native speakers, non-computer users, etc.).
TERM / CONCEPT
What criteria must a channel meet from the business perspective?
ANSWER
Control and consistency (promotes intended values), and profitability (minimizes expensive human resources via automation where possible).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the role of packaging in demand influencing?
ANSWER
Packaging serves a marketing purpose: commands attention, reinforces features and benefits, differentiates on the shelf, and can promote organizational goals such as sustainability. Services also have 'packaging' (presentation, environment design, uniforms).
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is brand image described as fragile?
ANSWER
Brand image takes years to build but can be destroyed in an instant by a scandal, quality failure, or public relations crisis. Organizations take great pains to protect brand image because it represents a significant portion of market value.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between demand shaping and demand prioritization?
ANSWER
Demand shaping (influencing): proactively using pricing, promotions, and positioning to shift demand toward what the organization can profitably supply. Demand prioritization: reactive — allocating limited supply when constrained, deciding which customers/orders get served.
