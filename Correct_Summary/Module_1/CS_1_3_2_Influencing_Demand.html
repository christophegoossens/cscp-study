Overview

Influencing demand means using commercial and operational levers to shape customer buying behavior — shifting volume, timing, or product mix — to better align demand with supply chain capabilities.

Demand Shaping Tools

💡 Key Point:
The four primary demand shaping levers: 1) Pricing (price promotions, dynamic pricing), 2) Promotions (advertising, trade deals, sampling), 3) Product availability (limited editions, early access), 4) Lead time offers (expedite fees, reservation systems).
  • Price promotions increase short-term volume but may cause demand borrowing (future demand pulled forward)
  • Trade promotions target retailers/distributors — can distort the demand signal significantly
  • Everyday Low Pricing (EDLP) reduces demand variability compared to Hi-Lo promotional pricing
  • Product bundling and substitution can shift demand between SKUs to manage inventory balance

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Demand shaping: The practice of using the marketing mix and supply chain actions to change customer demand patterns to better match supply capabilities. Includes promotions, pricing changes, product mix shifts, and communication strategies.
📖 Definition:
Price elasticity of demand: The percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in price. A measure of how sensitive demand is to price changes.
📖 Definition:
Trade promotion: A promotional incentive targeted at channel intermediaries (distributors, retailers) rather than end consumers. Examples include volume discounts, slotting fees, co-op advertising.

Key Concepts

💡 Key Point:
Demand borrowing risk: promotions often pull future demand into the current period. When the promotion ends, a demand trough follows. Net effect on total volume may be minimal while supply chain costs increase.
💡 Key Point:
Collaborative Planning, Forecasting and Replenishment (CPFR): trading partners share promotional calendars and demand plans to reduce demand variability and improve forecast accuracy for both parties.
  • Product lifecycle management affects demand: introduction, growth, maturity, and decline stages each require different demand strategies
  • New Product Introduction (NPI) creates demand spikes that must be coordinated with supply ramp-up
  • End-of-life (EOL) management requires demand shaping to drain excess inventory before discontinuation
  • Customer segmentation allows differentiated demand management strategies

DEFINITION
What is demand shaping?
ANSWER
The practice of using marketing mix and supply chain actions to change customer demand patterns to better match supply capabilities — through pricing, promotions, product availability, and lead time management.
CONCEPT
What are the four primary demand shaping levers?
ANSWER
1) Pricing (promotions, dynamic pricing), 2) Advertising/promotions (pull demand up), 3) Product availability (create urgency or manage substitution), 4) Lead time offers (incentivize earlier/later ordering).
DEFINITION
What is price elasticity of demand?
ANSWER
The percentage change in quantity demanded ÷ percentage change in price. A value greater than 1 (elastic) means demand is sensitive to price changes. Less than 1 (inelastic) means demand is relatively unaffected by price.
DEFINITION
What is a trade promotion?
ANSWER
A promotional incentive targeted at channel intermediaries (distributors, retailers) rather than end consumers. Examples: volume discounts, slotting fees, co-op advertising, display allowances.
CONCEPT
What is demand borrowing and why is it a problem?
ANSWER
Demand borrowing occurs when a promotion pulls future demand into the current period — creating an artificial spike now and a trough afterward. Total volume may not increase; supply chain costs do. The demand signal is distorted.
CONCEPT
What is Everyday Low Pricing (EDLP) and how does it benefit the supply chain?
ANSWER
A pricing strategy that maintains consistently low prices instead of alternating between regular and promotional prices. Reduces demand variability, makes planning easier, lowers inventory costs, and reduces the bullwhip effect.
DEFINITION
What is Collaborative Planning, Forecasting and Replenishment (CPFR)?
ANSWER
A business process where trading partners (retailers and suppliers) share planning, forecasting, and replenishment data — including promotional calendars — to reduce uncertainty and improve supply chain efficiency.
CONCEPT
How does the product lifecycle affect demand management strategy?
ANSWER
Introduction: low volume, uncertain demand, build awareness. Growth: rapid demand increase, scale supply. Maturity: stable demand, focus on efficiency. Decline: manage inventory wind-down, plan End-of-Life (EOL).
CONCEPT
What is End-of-Life (EOL) management from a demand perspective?
ANSWER
Using demand shaping (price markdowns, promotions, substitution) to drain excess inventory before a product is discontinued. Prevents excessive write-offs and clears shelf space for new products.
KEY POINT
What is the risk of trade promotions for supply chain planning?
ANSWER
Trade promotions can cause significant demand spikes and troughs at the distributor/retailer level that do not reflect true end-consumer demand — amplifying the bullwhip effect and making planning difficult.
CONCEPT
What is customer segmentation in demand management?
ANSWER
Dividing customers into groups based on characteristics (volume, profitability, strategic importance, demand behavior) to apply differentiated demand management strategies — different service levels, lead times, or pricing.
CONCEPT
What is New Product Introduction (NPI) and why does it challenge demand planning?
ANSWER
NPI creates demand that has no historical data — forecasting must rely on analogous product data, market research, and expert judgment. Supply ramp-up must be coordinated to avoid stockouts during launch.
CONCEPT
How does product bundling serve as a demand shaping tool?
ANSWER
Bundling combines products so customers buy both together — can shift demand from slow-moving items to faster-moving ones, increase average transaction value, and help manage inventory balance across SKUs.
CONCEPT
What is the relationship between demand influencing and inventory management?
ANSWER
By shaping demand timing and volume, companies can better match inventory levels to actual needs — reducing excess stock during slow periods and preventing stockouts during demand spikes.
CONCEPT
What is a demand plan and how does demand influencing affect it?
ANSWER
A demand plan is the expected future demand by product and time period, developed through forecasting. Demand influencing activities (promotions, pricing changes) create 'event uplift' — planned adjustments to the baseline forecast.
