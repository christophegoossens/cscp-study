Overview

Strategic sourcing builds long-term supplier alliances to reduce total cost of ownership, contrasting with tactical buying focused on individual transactions.

  • Strategic sourcing: identify, evaluate, negotiate, configure supply across geographies to reduce risk and cost
  • Tactical buying: transaction-focused, nonstrategic; stable, defined standards, short-term view
  • Strategic sourcing focuses on total cost of ownership; tactical focuses on landed cost only
  • Sole source: only one supplier available (technical/legal barriers); single source: chosen exclusively; multisourcing: multiple suppliers
  • Strategic sourcing reduces expenses by 10-30% for mid-to-large companies
  • Strategic alliance: two or more orgs sharing proprietary info and joint processes to improve both
  • Joint venture (JV): equity investment to create a new entity — distinct from an alliance
  • Successful alliances: individual excellence, interdependence, importance, investment, information sharing
  • Reasons to form alliances: add product value, enable strategic growth, increase market access
  • Factors for deciding alliance: strategic importance, number of available suppliers, complexity, uncertainty
  • Alliance commitment requires: commitment to change, to relationship, and to communication
  • 10 steps to successful alliances: align internally, select partners, win/win deal, ground rules, alliance manager, encourage collaboration, collaborative mindset, manage multifaceted relationships, pulse checks, plan for change

The ultimate goal in strategic sourcing is controlling costs while providing essential goods and services across the supply chain network. Strategic sourcing can reduce costs by consolidating network demand. Multiple plants, supply chain partners, or divisions can allow SRM to consolidate purchasing power. Strategic sourcing could employ a single-source supplier or a small number of suppliers to gain volume discounts or could perform multisourcing to optimize supplier capacities and lead times while providing resilience against disruptions. Many organizations form strategic alliances to increase the performance of their common supply chain. Over time there is alliance development, which is “strengthening the capabilities of a key supplier” (ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary).
image222.png
Alliances should not be confused with joint ventures. A joint venture (JV) is “an agreement between two or more firms to risk equity capital to attempt a specific business objective” (ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary). In joint ventures, the parties typically agree to create a new entity by both contributing equity, and they then share in the revenues, expenses, and control of the enterprise. The venture can be for one specific project only or a continuing business relationship. In contrast, a strategic alliance involves no equity stake by the participants and is a much less rigid arrangement.
