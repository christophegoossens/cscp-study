6.2.2 Overview: Strategic Sourcing vs. Tactical Buying
Strategic sourcing: a comprehensive approach for locating and sourcing key material suppliers, which often includes the business process of analyzing total-spend-for-material categories and identifying achievable cost-reduction alternatives.
Tactical buying: the purchasing process focused on transactions and nonstrategic material buying. Closely aligned with the ordering portion of executing the purchasing cycle.

Key distinctions — Strategic Sourcing vs. Traditional Purchasing:

  • Traditional focuses on landed cost; strategic focuses on total cost of ownership
  • Traditional is transactional; strategic is collaborative
  • Traditional never crosses organizational boundaries; strategic realigns processes and information flows
  • Traditional does not increase supply chain visibility; strategic brings collaborative visibility

Sourcing strategies:

  • Single-source supplier: 100% of business to one supplier though alternatives exist
  • Multisourcing: procurement from more than one independent supplier
  • Sole source: supply available from only one organization (technical/patent barriers)
Strategic Alliances
Strategic alliance: a relationship formed by two or more organizations that share proprietary information, participate in joint investments, and develop linked and common processes to increase the performance of their joint activities.
Strategic alliance ≠ joint venture. A joint venture (JV) involves two or more firms risking equity capital to attempt a commercially profitable innovation — a more formal and financially committed structure.

Nine characteristics of successful alliances (the “8 I’s + Interpersonal”):

  • Individual excellence, Interdependence, Importance, Investment, Information, Integration, Institutionalization, Integrity, Interpersonal skills

Reasons to form alliances: add value, enable strategic growth, increase market access, strengthen operations, increase organizational expertise, build skills, enhance financial strength, better manage risk.

Factors to consider: strategic importance, number of available suppliers, complexity, uncertainty, maturity of relationship.

Commitment and 10 Steps to Successful Alliances
Alliances fail when commitment is insufficient. Three types of commitment required: Commitment to Change, Commitment to Relationship, Commitment to Communication.
Exhibit 6-12: 10 Steps to Successful Alliances
  • Step 1: Align Internally (stakeholder buy-in before entering alliances)
  • Step 2: Select Proper Partners (strategic fit + cultural/operating style fit)
  • Step 3: Negotiate Win/Win Deal (future relationship focus, not just immediate terms)
  • Step 4: Establish Ground Rules (guidelines for working together, conflict resolution)
  • Step 5: Appoint Dedicated Alliance Manager
  • Step 6: Encourage Collaboration (resolve conflicts, negotiate, solve jointly)
  • Step 7: Engage in Collaborative Corporate Mindset (joint goals over individual goals)
  • Step 8: Manage Multifaceted Relationships (partner may also be competitor or customer)
  • Step 9: Conduct Pulse Checks (audit relationship health, not just business objectives)
  • Step 10: Plan for Change (both business changes and relationship evolution)

Single-source vs. sole-source — distinction?
ANSWER
Single-source: 100% of business given to one supplier by choice (alternatives exist). Sole-source: only one supplier available (technical/patent barriers make alternatives impossible).
TERM / CONCEPT
Strategic alliance vs. joint venture — distinction?
ANSWER
A joint venture involves two or more firms risking equity capital to attempt a profitable innovation (more formal, financially committed). A strategic alliance is a deep relationship without necessarily pooling equity.

ANSWER
Individual excellence, Interdependence, Importance, Investment, Information (open communication), Integration (linked operations), Institutionalization (formal status), Integrity (trust), Interpersonal skills.

What are the eight reasons to form strategic alliances?
ANSWER
Add value to products; enable strategic growth; increase market access; strengthen operations; increase organizational expertise; build organizational skills; enhance financial strength; better manage risk.

What are the five factors to consider before forming a strategic alliance?
ANSWER
1) Strategic importance; 2) Number of suppliers available; 3) Complexity of the component/supply chain; 4) Uncertainty in supply; 5) Newness of the relationship.

What are the three types of commitment required for alliance success?
ANSWER
1) Commitment to Change (change is inevitable in today’s supply chain); 2) Commitment to Relationship (ongoing max effort is cost-effective); 3) Commitment to Communication (open, continuous information sharing).

TERM / CONCEPT
What is multisourcing?
ANSWER
Procurement of a good or service from more than one independent supplier — used to reduce supply risk and maintain competitive tension between suppliers.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why do strategic alliances fail?
ANSWER
Insufficient commitment; immature technology; uncertain marketplaces; shifts in corporate strategy; external forces; cultural conflicts; managers who delay or prevent collaboration to protect their positions.
TERM / CONCEPT
What does ‘Exhibit 6-11: Traditional vs. Strategic Alliance’ illustrate?
ANSWER
The butterfly shape depicts traditional relationships: narrow and arm’s-length. Strategic alliances are broader, with deep integration across multiple functions (engineering, marketing, planning, inventory, quality).
