Overview

CRM strategy covers four operational areas — sales operations, analysis, information dissemination, and relationship building — supported by technology across the product life cycle.

  • CRM scope: covers every customer interaction, from order-taking to post-sale support
  • Relationship building goal: develop loyal lifetime customers who lower total marketing costs
  • CRM components: EBS (backbone), web systems, marketing, external data, analytics, service
  • EBS includes: customer database, transaction maintenance, order status, pricing/inventory data
  • CDW (customer data warehouse): integrates data for segmentation and analysis; supports strategic marketing
  • CRM process: reduce non-value-added tasks, maximize concurrent activities, use PDCA loop
  • Org structure: specialized teams by campaign type (media, direct, prospecting, win-back, loyal)
  • Product life cycle x CRM: Development = VOC; Introduction = promotional support; Growth = care and segmentation; Maturity = lure competitors' customers; Decline = service assurance
  • SFA (sales force automation): core CRM technology; collects data from transactions and call centers
  • Marketing automation = campaign management (EMA): media, direct, and e-commerce tools
  • Segmentation technology: computer algorithms based on demographics and historical purchase patterns
  • Integration maturity levels: disconnected → interfacing → internally integrated technology
  • CRM failure reasons: too much focus on technology; insufficient focus on knowing the customer

A data warehouse is “a repository of data that has been specially prepared to support decision-making applications” (ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary). A customer data warehouse (CDW) contains information about an organization’s customers, products, and marketplace. A CDW extracts information from internal and external sources, standardizes and consolidates the information, and stores it for easy access and retrieval. Organizations generally use CDWs in conjunction with their existing information technology infrastructures, which could be an enterprise resources planning (ERP) system or a dedicated CRM transactional system and database. Business intelligence is “information collected by an organization about customers, competitors, products or services, and processes. Business intelligence provides organizational data in such a way that the organizational knowledge filters can easily associate with this data and turn it into information for the organization. ” (ASCM Supply Chain Dictionary). This includes decision support systems (DSS) and data mining tools to generate customer segments and determine customer segment value.
