6.2.4 Overview: CRM and SRM as Two Sides of One Coin

CRM (managing customer relationships) and SRM (managing supplier relationships) are two sides of the same relationship coin. Together they enable:

  • Organizing supplier chains to suit specific customer segment needs
  • Customers or suppliers collaborating to add value for all parties
  • Customer/supplier participation in product development (co-design)

Process for managing customer relationships (CRM): change management to build customer-centric culture; CRM software; customer segmentation; understanding segment needs (data → analysis → tailored packages); lifetime relationship building; sharing customer info with supply chain partners; capturing satisfaction metrics; feedback loop for CRM improvement.

Process for managing supplier relationships (SRM): change management for internal alignment; SRM software; TCO calculation; partner selection; goal development; contract/ground rules; alliance managers; long-term relationship development; realign processes; measure performance; periodic course corrections; periodic strategic fit reevaluation.

Virtual Organizations & Collaboration
Virtual organization: short-term alliances between independent organizations in a potentially long-term relationship to design, produce, and distribute a product. Organizations in virtual organizations share costs, skills, and market access.

Virtual organizations characteristics:

  • Use POS data to replace make-to-stock (push) with make-to-order (pull)
  • Build trust by sharing information — the more shared, the more robust the relationship
  • Speed of adaptation to new business situations is key
  • May need to weaken formal hierarchy to empower individuals/teams in partner-facing roles

Building Collaborative Relationships

Requirements for partner selection (Jordan Lewis framework): adding value, improving market access, strengthening operations, adding technological strength, enhancing strategic growth, sharing insights and learning, increasing financial strength.

Effective partnerships: shared risks + resources + rewards + vision + values. Building them requires: auditable information exchange, deterrence-based arrangements (contracts), incentive-based arrangements, process-based trust-building, leadership with authority, focus on entire supply chain, networkwide visibility, sharing of knowledge (not just data).

Three management tasks to initiate collaboration:

  • Jointly discuss/designate goals and next steps
  • Define roles for each party (avoid redundancy)
  • Create conflict resolution policy and maintain management involvement

Eight barriers to collaboration: suboptimization, individual incentives conflicting with organizational goals, working with competitors, bottlenecks from weak/slow partners, technology barriers, regulations/legal issues, power-based relationships, underestimated benefits, culture conflicts.

Four levels of communication in collaboration:

  • Transactional with information sharing (single data source access)
  • Shared processes and partnership (shared knowledge, collaborative design)
  • Linked competitive vision and strategic alliance (virtual entity, joint strategy)
  • Mergers and acquisitions (backward/forward integration)

Exhibit 6-18: Evolving Relationship with Lifetime Customers — what does it show?
ANSWER

TERM / CONCEPT
What is organizational learning in CRM/SRM context?
ANSWER
The ability of an organization to create, gather, and transfer knowledge and modify behavior to reflect new knowledge. Used by MNCs to challenge paradigms, create formal knowledge steps, and empower decision makers in CRM/SRM improvement.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is “saving face” and its supply chain implication?
ANSWER
An Asian/Latin cultural concept of retaining good image/pride despite being wrong or losing in conflict. In SC negotiations and relationships, managers must avoid situations that cause partners to “lose face” to maintain trust and relationship health.
TERM / CONCEPT
What two major shifts drive customer-centric businesses today?
ANSWER
1) Greater focus on the customer (both internal and external); 2) Greater integration (internally via process reengineering AND externally via supply chain collaboration with partners).

B.Reducing information asymmetry and bullwhip effect, improving forecast accuracy and inventory efficiency
