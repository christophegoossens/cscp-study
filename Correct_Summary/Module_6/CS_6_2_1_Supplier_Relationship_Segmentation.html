Overview

Supplier relationship segmentation classifies suppliers based on strategic importance, spend volume, and risk level — enabling differentiated management approaches that allocate resources where they create the most value.

ASCM Definitions

📖 Definition:
Supplier segmentation: The process of classifying suppliers into groups based on their strategic importance, spend level, and performance — to determine the appropriate level of management attention, relationship depth, and investment.
📖 Definition:
Preferred supplier: A supplier that has demonstrated superior performance and strategic value, designated to receive priority for new business opportunities.

Segmentation Models

💡 Key Point:
Krajlic Matrix — the classic supplier segmentation tool: classifies suppliers by Spend (volume) on one axis and Supply Risk (complexity, scarcity, lead time) on the other: Bottleneck (low spend, high risk) → Leverage (high spend, low risk) → Critical (high spend, high risk) → Routine (low spend, low risk).
  • Leverage items: high spend, low supply risk — use competitive bidding, consolidate volume, negotiate hard on price
  • Critical (strategic) items: high spend, high risk — develop strategic partnerships, joint planning, invest in relationship
  • Bottleneck items: low spend, high risk — ensure supply security, maintain safety stock, develop backup sources
  • Routine items: low spend, low risk — automate ordering, reduce management overhead, use blanket POs

Relationship Levels

💡 Key Point:
Supplier relationship levels: 1) Vendor (transactional — arms-length, price-driven), 2) Preferred Supplier (better performance, price benefits, preferred for new business), 3) Partner (collaborative, shared planning), 4) Strategic Ally (joint innovation, shared risk/reward).
  • Time and resources should be invested proportionally to strategic importance: strategic allies and partners deserve executive attention; routine vendors need only efficient transactional management
  • Supplier rationalization: reducing the total number of suppliers enables deeper relationships with the remaining suppliers
  • Dual sourcing for critical items: maintain two qualified suppliers to balance risk against leverage

DEFINITION
What is supplier segmentation?
ANSWER
Classifying suppliers into groups based on strategic importance, spend volume, supply risk, and performance — to determine the appropriate management approach, relationship depth, and resource investment for each group.
DEFINITION
What is the Krajlic Matrix?
ANSWER
A supplier segmentation tool with two axes: Spend (volume of purchases) and Supply Risk (complexity, scarcity, switching difficulty). Creates four quadrants: Routine, Leverage, Bottleneck, and Critical/Strategic — each requiring a different management strategy.
CONCEPT
How should leverage items be managed?
ANSWER
High spend, low supply risk — use competitive bidding among multiple qualified suppliers, consolidate volume for price leverage, negotiate aggressively on price and terms. Switch suppliers if better value is available.
CONCEPT
How should critical/strategic items be managed?
ANSWER
High spend, high supply risk — develop strategic partnerships with key suppliers. Joint planning, shared roadmaps, co-investment in capacity and innovation. Build interdependence and mutual commitment for supply security.
CONCEPT
How should bottleneck items be managed?
ANSWER
Low spend, high supply risk — ensure supply security through safety stock, long-term agreements, supplier development, and seeking alternative sources. Risk management takes priority over cost optimization.
CONCEPT
How should routine items be managed?
ANSWER
Low spend, low supply risk — minimize management overhead through automation (blanket POs, P-cards, supplier-managed inventory). Focus on efficiency and simplicity rather than deep relationship.
CONCEPT
What are the four supplier relationship levels from least to most integrated?
ANSWER
1) Vendor (transactional, price-driven), 2) Preferred Supplier (consistent quality, preferred for new business), 3) Partner (collaborative planning, shared data), 4) Strategic Ally (joint innovation, shared risk/reward, co-investment).
DEFINITION
What is a preferred supplier designation?
ANSWER
A designation awarded to high-performing suppliers, giving them priority consideration for new business opportunities as a reward for excellent quality, delivery, and service performance.
KEY PRINCIPLE
Why should management attention be allocated proportionally to supplier strategic importance?
ANSWER
Strategic and partner suppliers drive the most business value — they deserve executive engagement, joint planning, and development investment. Routine vendors need only efficient transactional management. Over-investing in routine suppliers wastes resources.
CONCEPT
What is supplier rationalization in the context of segmentation?
ANSWER
Reducing the total number of suppliers — concentrating volume with fewer, more capable suppliers. Enables deeper relationships, better pricing, and lower management overhead. Rational segment: strategic and preferred suppliers get all business; others are phased out.
CONCEPT
Why maintain dual sources for bottleneck or critical items?
ANSWER
A single source for a high-risk, critical item creates 100% supply dependency. Dual sourcing with two qualified suppliers provides a backup when one has problems — balancing supply security against the loss of volume consolidation leverage.
CONCEPT
What is total cost of relationship vs. total cost of ownership in supplier segmentation?
ANSWER
TCO focuses on financial cost. Total cost of relationship also includes relationship investment: management time, joint planning resources, system integration, co-development costs. Strategic allies have high TCO but generate proportionally higher value.
CONCEPT
What is a supplier development investment and for which segment is it justified?
ANSWER
Investing in a supplier's capabilities — training, process improvement, co-investment in equipment — to improve their performance. Justified for strategic and preferred suppliers whose improvement directly benefits your supply chain. Not justified for routine vendors.
CONCEPT
How does segmentation affect contract length?
ANSWER
Routine items: short-term or framework agreements — flexibility to switch. Preferred suppliers: 1-3 year contracts — reward performance stability. Strategic allies: 3-5 year or longer agreements — enable mutual investment in the relationship.
CONCEPT
What is a supplier tiering strategy?
ANSWER
Organizing suppliers into tiers based on their role: Tier 1 suppliers directly supply the manufacturer; Tier 2 supply Tier 1; Tier 3 supply Tier 2. Strategy defines different management expectations, collaboration levels, and development investments for each tier.
