6.2.1 Overview: SRM and Supplier Segmentation
Supplier relationship management (SRM): a comprehensive approach to managing an enterprise’s interactions with the organizations that supply the goods and services it uses. The goal of SRM is to streamline and make more effective the processes between an enterprise and its suppliers.

SRM methodology structures and supports relationships with suppliers to:

  • Reduce procurement and inventory costs
  • Support a customer-centric business delivering customization and quality on time
  • Continuously improve supply processes
Exhibit 6-9: Supply Chain Community
Supplier Segmentation

Supplier segmentation creates categories of suppliers to manage them appropriately — just as you segment customers, you segment suppliers. It is a key tool for a responsive supply chain.

Six forms of supplier segmentation:

  • Product or service type: general category of goods/services supplied
  • Ideal relationship type: what partnership would this supplier be suited for?
  • Supplier capabilities: for niche/differentiation strategies — focus on unique capabilities
  • Customization vs. standardization: responsive/custom vs. efficient/standard
  • Level of innovation: suppliers who contribute creative input to product design
  • Lead times: grouping by lead time for scheduling and performance tracking
SRM applies most to higher-level buyer-supplier relationships. It does not necessarily replace arm’s-length market purchasing for commodity items.
Processes Enabled by SRM
  • Collaborative design
  • Collaborative planning, forecasting, and replenishment (CPFR)
  • Lean and Just-in-Time
  • 3PL/4PL partnerships
  • Supplier- and vendor-managed inventory (VMI)
  • Quick response programs
  • Distributor integration
  • Collaborative transportation management
  • Supplier co-location
Supplier co-location: locating a supplier or multiple suppliers within the buyer’s facility. Benefit to buyer: highly integrated operations. Benefit to supplier: larger and more stable volumes. Co-location may require licensing agreements (patents, software, revenue-sharing).
SRM Technologies

SRM software streamlines connections between purchasers and suppliers, increases efficiency, and enables better supplier selection and performance monitoring.

SRM system components:

  • Strategic sourcing and RFQ/ITT submission and analysis
  • Procurement via internet trade exchanges or auctions
  • Collaborative product design and planning
  • Purchasing/supplier scheduling via EDI or portals
  • Catalog management, category management, supplier segmentation
  • Supplier databases and rating systems

Web-enabled SRM (Exhibit 6-10) has three interfaces: Internal (ERP, procurement history, open orders, accounting, purchasing planning, supplier performance), External supplier interfaces (EDI/internet links), and SRM Analytics (visibility/DSS, strategic sourcing analysis, spend analysis, value analysis, performance analysis).

Exhibit 6-10: Web-Enabled SRM

SRM Analytics types:

  • Spend analysis: who are we buying from, what, when, and at what price?
  • Value analysis: identifies value-added vs. non-value-added procurement activities
  • Performance analysis: measures cost savings, quality, delivery, price, compliance

Question 1
What is 'supplier segmentation' and why is it essential in supply chain management?
A.Organizing suppliers alphabetically in a database
B.The process of categorizing suppliers into distinct groups based on factors such as spend, strategic importance, risk, and market complexity — to focus relationship management effort, service levels, and investment appropriately across the supply base
C.Dividing suppliers by geographic location only
D.A process for rating suppliers on quality performance
Supplier segmentation recognizes that not all suppliers warrant the same management approach: strategic partners need executive relationships and joint innovation; commodity suppliers need efficient transactional processes. Segmentation prevents over-investing in low-value relationships and under-investing in critical ones.
Question 2
What is the 'Kraljic Matrix' and how is it used in supplier segmentation?
A.A mathematical formula for calculating optimal safety stock
B.A 2×2 supplier segmentation tool using supply risk (x-axis) and profit impact (y-axis) to classify suppliers into four categories: Strategic (high risk, high impact), Leverage (low risk, high impact), Bottleneck (high risk, low impact), and Non-critical (low risk, low impact)
C.A quality scoring matrix for supplier audits
D.A matrix for evaluating supplier financial stability
Kraljic segments require different strategies: Strategic → partnership/collaboration; Leverage → competitive tendering, maximize buyer power; Bottleneck → ensure supply continuity, develop alternatives; Non-critical → streamline purchasing, reduce transaction cost.
Question 3
What is a 'strategic supplier' (in Kraljic terms) and how should they be managed?
A.Any supplier that has a formal contract
B.A supplier with high profit impact AND high supply risk — typically a sole source or technically unique supplier providing critical inputs. Managed through long-term partnership, joint innovation, deep integration, and mutual dependency
C.The supplier with the largest annual spend
D.A supplier who is also a customer
Strategic suppliers: build deep partnership (not adversarial), share information (forecasts, product roadmaps), invest in their success (help them develop capability), and create mutual dependency — you're committed to them, they're committed to you. Switching cost is high both ways.
Question 4
What is a 'leverage supplier' (in Kraljic terms) and what purchasing strategy applies?
A.A supplier from whom you borrow raw materials
B.A supplier with HIGH profit impact but LOW supply risk — where many qualified alternatives exist and the buyer has significant purchasing power. Strategy: competitive bidding, volume aggregation, price optimization, and developing backup suppliers
C.A supplier who provides financing for purchases
D.A supplier who leverages the buyer's technology
With leverage suppliers, the buyer has market power: standardize specs (to enable competition), aggregate spend (for volume leverage), run competitive RFPs, and use the threat of switching to negotiate price. Don't lock in to single source without good reason.
Question 5
What is a 'bottleneck supplier' (in Kraljic terms) and what is the key risk?
A.A supplier who creates shipping bottlenecks
B.A supplier with HIGH supply risk but LOW profit impact — often providing specialized components or services with few or no alternatives. Key risk: supply disruption could stop production even though the spending is small
C.A supplier who fails to deliver on time
D.A supplier who has been identified as a performance problem
Bottleneck suppliers are supply chain vulnerabilities: the item may be cheap but critical (a single-source gasket that stops an assembly line). Strategy: qualify backup suppliers, hold strategic safety stock, develop close relationships to get priority allocation, and if possible, redesign to use a more available alternative.
Question 6
What factors are typically used to determine supplier strategic importance?
A.Only the annual spend with the supplier
B.Spend volume, criticality of supplied goods/services (difficulty of substitution), supplier's market uniqueness (sole source vs. commodity market), impact on quality or customer delivery, and strategic alignment with future product/technology direction
C.Only the geographic location of the supplier
D.Only the supplier's financial stability
Strategic importance is multi-dimensional: a supplier providing $50K of sole-source precision components can be more 'strategic' than a $5M commodity supplier. Criticality (what happens if supply stops?), replaceability (how easily can we switch?), and uniqueness all factor in.
Question 7
What is 'supply base rationalization' and what are its benefits and risks?
A.A process for organizing supplier contact information
B.Deliberately reducing the number of suppliers — consolidating spend with fewer, more strategic partners. Benefits: reduced management cost, volume leverage for better pricing, closer relationships, better service. Risk: increased single-source exposure, less competitive tension
C.A process for financial auditing of suppliers
D.The process of adding new suppliers to the approved list
Supply base rationalization reduces complexity (fewer suppliers to manage, audit, onboard) and increases leverage (larger spend with remaining suppliers). The risk is reduced redundancy: fewer suppliers means less flexibility if one fails. Rationalization should stop before creating unacceptable single-source risk.
Question 8
What is 'preferred supplier' status and how is it typically awarded?
A.A status given to suppliers who offer the lowest price
B.A formal designation given to suppliers who have demonstrated superior performance (quality, delivery, service) and strategic fit — typically rewarded with first-right-of-refusal on new business, longer contract terms, and closer collaboration in exchange for investment in the relationship
C.A status automatically given to the largest suppliers
D.A certification awarded by a third-party auditing body
Preferred supplier programs reward top performers: they get preferential access to new business, longer planning horizons (stability to invest), and collaborative relationships — while the buyer benefits from better performance and service. The status must be earned and can be lost.
Question 9
What is 'supplier diversity' and why do companies invest in it?
A.Having suppliers from many different industries
B.A procurement strategy of including minority-owned, women-owned, veteran-owned, and other socially disadvantaged businesses in the supplier base — driven by corporate social responsibility, regulatory requirements, customer demands, and access to innovative suppliers
C.Having suppliers in many different geographic locations
D.A risk management strategy for supply chain disruption
Supplier diversity serves multiple goals: social responsibility (economic inclusion), regulatory compliance (government contractor requirements), customer requirements (some buyers mandate diverse supplier spend reporting), and innovation access (diverse suppliers often bring fresh approaches).
Question 10
What is the difference between a 'transactional' and 'collaborative' supplier relationship?
A.Transactional suppliers are paid by transaction; collaborative suppliers have contracts
B.Transactional: arm's length, price-focused, adversarial negotiations, minimal information sharing. Collaborative: trust-based, joint problem-solving, shared information (forecasts, product plans), aligned incentives, and mutual investment in success
C.Transactional means short-term; collaborative means long-term
D.Transactional is for commodities; collaborative is for services
The relationship model should match the Kraljic segment: transactional efficiency for non-critical and leverage suppliers (where price matters most); collaborative investment for strategic suppliers (where joint innovation and supply security matter more than unit price).
Question 11
What is 'total cost of ownership' (TCO) in supplier segmentation?
A.The cost of the supplier's total product inventory
B.The complete cost of acquiring and using goods or services from a supplier — including purchase price, transaction costs, quality costs (defects, rework), delivery costs (freight, inventory), and risk costs (disruption probability × impact)
C.The supplier's own total operating costs
D.The total cost of the products sold to end customers
TCO-based sourcing evaluates suppliers on total value, not just purchase price: a lower-price supplier in a distant location may have higher total cost when freight, lead time (inventory carrying cost), quality defect rate, and supply disruption risk are included.
Question 12
What is 'single sourcing' vs. 'dual sourcing' and when is each appropriate?
A.Single sourcing means buying one unit; dual sourcing means buying two
B.Single sourcing: one supplier for an item — simplifies management, enables deeper partnership, but creates supply disruption risk. Dual sourcing: two suppliers for the same item — maintains competitive tension and supply backup but divides volume and complicates management
C.Single sourcing is for commodities; dual sourcing is for unique items
D.Single sourcing uses domestic suppliers; dual sourcing uses global suppliers
Single sourcing fits strategic partners where relationship depth matters more than competitive tension. Dual sourcing fits critical items where supply continuity is essential. The split (e.g., 70/30) preserves the primary supplier relationship while maintaining a qualified backup.
