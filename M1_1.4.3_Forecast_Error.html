Why Measure Forecast Error?
Forecast error measurement serves two purposes: (1) evaluate and improve forecast accuracy over time, and (2) size safety stock and capacity buffers to absorb the expected error.
Forecast error: The difference between the actual demand and the forecasted demand for a given period. Error = Actual − Forecast.

A positive error means demand was HIGHER than forecast (risk: stockout). A negative error means demand was LOWER than forecast (risk: excess inventory).

Tracking error trends over time reveals bias — consistently positive errors = systematic under-forecasting; consistently negative = over-forecasting.

Key Error Metrics
Mean Absolute Deviation (MAD): Average of the absolute values of forecast errors. Simple to calculate and interpret. Units are in the same units as demand (e.g., units, dollars). Formula: MAD = Σ|Aₜ - Fₜ| / n
MAD = Σ|Actual - Forecast| / n Interpretation: average error magnitude regardless of direction
Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE): Expresses error as a percentage of actual demand. Allows comparison across products with different volumes. Formula: MAPE = (Σ|Aₜ - Fₜ| / Aₜ) / n × 100%
MAPE = (Σ(|Actual - Forecast| / Actual)) / n × 100% Interpretation: average % error — scale-independent, useful for comparing across SKUs
Mean Squared Error (MSE): Average of squared errors. Penalizes large errors more heavily than MAD. Used in many statistical models to optimize parameters.
MSE = Σ(Actual - Forecast)² / n Interpretation: penalizes large errors heavily; not intuitive (units are squared)
Tracking signal: Cumulative forecast error divided by MAD. Monitors whether a forecast is systematically biased. Formula: Tracking signal = RSFE / MAD
Tracking Signal = RSFE / MAD RSFE = Running Sum of Forecast Errors (signed) Control limits: typically ±4 to ±6 MADs. Outside = bias detected
Use MAD for operational safety stock sizing. Use MAPE for comparing model accuracy across different products. Use tracking signal to detect systematic bias.
Using Error to Size Safety Stock
Safety stock: Buffer inventory held to protect against demand variability (forecast error) and supply variability (lead time uncertainty).
Safety stock formula using MAD: SS = Z × MAD × √LT (where Z = service level z-score, LT = lead time in periods).
Safety Stock = Z × MAD × √LT Z = service level factor (e.g., 1.65 for 95%, 2.05 for 98%) MAD = mean absolute deviation of forecast error LT = replenishment lead time (in same time units as MAD)

Higher forecast error → larger MAD → more safety stock needed. Improving forecast accuracy DIRECTLY reduces inventory investment.

Every % improvement in forecast accuracy translates to a reduction in required safety stock — a direct financial benefit of better forecasting.
Forecast Bias vs. Random Error
Forecast bias: A systematic tendency to consistently over- or under-forecast. Detected by the tracking signal.
Random error: Unexplained variation around the forecast — inherent unpredictability in demand. Cannot be eliminated; can only be buffered with safety stock.
The goal is to ELIMINATE bias (fixable with better models or process) and REDUCE random error (through better data, demand signals). What remains requires safety stock.

Q1 of 5
A company's tracking signal consistently reads +7 over the past 6 months (control limit is ±4). This indicates:
A positive RSFE (Running Sum of Forecast Errors) means actual > forecast consistently = the model is systematically UNDER-forecasting. A tracking signal above +4 (control limit) is a signal to review and correct the forecasting model.
Q2 of 5
Which error metric is best for COMPARING forecast accuracy across two products with very different sales volumes (e.g., 100 units/month vs. 10,000 units/month)?
MAPE is scale-independent — it expresses error as a percentage of actual demand. This allows meaningful comparison across products at very different volume levels. MAD is scale-dependent (units), so a MAD of 50 is massive for a 100-unit product but tiny for a 10,000-unit product.
Q3 of 5
A supply chain manager wants to calculate the safety stock for a product with MAD = 20 units, lead time = 4 weeks, and desired service level (Z = 1.65). What is the safety stock?
SS = Z × MAD × √LT = 1.65 × 20 × √4 = 1.65 × 20 × 2 = 66 units. The square root of lead time accounts for the compounding of uncertainty over the replenishment period.
Q4 of 5
What is the difference between forecast bias and random forecast error?
Bias = systematic, directional error (consistently over or under) = FIXABLE with better models or process adjustments. Random error = inherent demand variability that cannot be predicted = managed with safety stock buffers.
Q5 of 5
What is the primary operational use of MAD in supply chain management?
MAD's primary operational application in supply chain is SAFETY STOCK SIZING. The formula SS = Z × MAD × √LT directly uses MAD to calculate the buffer inventory needed to achieve a target service level given the expected forecast error.
