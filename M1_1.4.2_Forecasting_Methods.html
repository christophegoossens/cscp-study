Qualitative vs. Quantitative Methods
Qualitative forecasting: Methods based on human judgment, experience, and market knowledge rather than historical data. Used when historical data is unavailable (new products) or when market conditions are changing rapidly.
Quantitative forecasting: Methods based on mathematical analysis of historical data. Most appropriate when a product has a stable demand history and the future is expected to resemble the past.
In practice, best-in-class demand planning uses BOTH: quantitative baseline (statistical) + qualitative override (market intelligence). Pure qualitative is biased; pure quantitative ignores market reality.
Key Qualitative Methods
  • Delphi method: Structured expert survey process — experts independently provide estimates, a facilitator summarizes, and experts revise until consensus is reached. Eliminates group-think bias.
  • Market research / surveys: Gathering customer data directly — surveys, focus groups, purchase intent studies. Good for new products.
  • Sales force composite: Each salesperson estimates demand in their territory; totals are aggregated. Risk: optimism bias from salespeople.
  • Executive opinion / jury of executive opinion: Senior leaders provide demand estimates based on industry knowledge. Fast but can suffer from authority bias.
Qualitative methods are essential for NEW PRODUCT FORECASTING where no history exists.
Key Quantitative Methods
Naive forecast: The simplest model — this period's forecast = last period's actual. Zero parameters. Good as a benchmark.
Moving average (MA): Averages the last N periods of demand. Formula: MA = (D₁ + D₂ + ... + Dₙ) / N. All N periods weighted equally. Does NOT respond well to trend or seasonality.
Weighted moving average (WMA): Like MA but more recent periods get higher weights. More responsive to recent changes.
Exponential smoothing (ES): A weighted average where weights decrease exponentially for older data. Only requires last period's forecast and last period's actual. Alpha (α) controls responsiveness: high α = more responsive (more weight to recent demand). Formula: Fₜ = α × Aₜ₋₁ + (1 - α) × Fₜ₋₁
Exponential Smoothing: Fₜ = α × Aₜ₋₁ + (1 - α) × Fₜ₋₁ Where: Fₜ = new forecast, α = smoothing constant (0-1), Aₜ₋₁ = last period actual, Fₜ₋₁ = last period forecast
Linear regression / trend analysis: Fits a trend line to historical data using least squares. Good for data with a clear linear trend.
Seasonal index: A ratio that shows how demand in a given period compares to the annual average. Used to adjust a trend forecast for seasonal patterns.
No single quantitative method is best for all situations. Match the method to the demand pattern: MA for stable demand, ES for demand with slow drift, regression for strong trends, seasonal index for seasonal demand.
Choosing the Right Method
Factors for selecting a forecasting method:
  • Data availability — how much history exists?
  • Demand pattern type — stable, trending, seasonal, or random?
  • Forecast horizon — short-term or long-term?
  • Required accuracy — how costly is error?
  • Complexity vs. simplicity tradeoff — simple models are easier to explain and maintain

Use error metrics (MAD, MAPE, MSE) to compare models on the same data — the model with lowest error is preferred.

A/B testing: run multiple models in parallel and select the one with better out-of-sample accuracy.

TERM / CONCEPT
Qualitative vs. quantitative forecasting
ANSWER
Qualitative: based on human judgment, used when no history exists (new products) or market is changing rapidly. Quantitative: based on statistical analysis of historical data, used when history is stable and future resembles past. Best practice: combine both.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Delphi method?
ANSWER
A structured expert forecasting technique: experts independently estimate → facilitator summarizes → experts revise → repeat until consensus. Eliminates group-think and authority bias.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is exponential smoothing and what does alpha (α) control?
ANSWER
A weighted average where older data gets exponentially less weight. Formula: Fₜ = α × Aₜ₋₁ + (1−α) × Fₜ₋₁. α (0–1): high α = more weight on recent demand = more responsive. Low α = smoother, less reactive to recent changes.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a moving average (MA) forecast?
ANSWER
Average of the last N periods: MA = (D₁ + D₂ + ... + Dₙ) / N. Each period is weighted equally. Simple but does not adjust for trend or seasonality. Good for stable demand patterns.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a seasonal index?
ANSWER
A ratio comparing demand in a specific period to the annual average. Used to adjust a trend-based forecast for known seasonal patterns. Index > 1 = above average; < 1 = below average.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the naive forecast?
ANSWER
The simplest forecast method: this period's forecast = last period's actual demand. Zero parameters. Used as a benchmark — any more complex model should outperform it to be worth using.
TERM / CONCEPT
Sales force composite vs. Delphi method
ANSWER
Sales force composite: salespeople estimate their territory demand, aggregated up. Risk: optimism bias. Delphi: structured expert panel with anonymous iteration until consensus. Eliminates authority bias. More rigorous but slower.
TERM / CONCEPT
How do you select the best quantitative forecasting method?
ANSWER
Match the method to the demand pattern (stable→MA, trending→regression, seasonal→seasonal index). Use error metrics (MAD, MAPE, MSE) to compare models on historical data. The model with the lowest out-of-sample error wins.

Q1 of 5
A company is launching a brand new product with no sales history. Which forecasting approach is most appropriate?
When NO historical data exists for a specific product, quantitative methods cannot be applied. Qualitative methods — market research, expert opinion (Delphi), customer surveys — are the appropriate starting point for new product forecasting.
Q2 of 5
In exponential smoothing, a higher alpha (α) value means:
Alpha controls the weight on the most recent actual demand. High α = more responsive to recent data = faster to react to demand changes (but also more 'noisy'). Low α = smoother forecast that reacts more slowly to change.
Q3 of 5
A forecaster compares three statistical models and wants to choose the most accurate one. The best approach is to:
Model selection should be empirical — calculate error metrics on historical data (or better, on held-out 'test' data) for each candidate model, and choose the model with the lowest error. Complexity and vendor recommendations are not reliable selection criteria.
Q4 of 5
Which quantitative method is best suited for a product whose demand shows a clear, steady upward trend?
Linear regression fits a trend line to historical data — ideal for demand with a clear, directional trend. Moving averages and naive forecasts lag behind a trend. Seasonal index alone doesn't address trend direction.
Q5 of 5
What is the main RISK of using a sales force composite forecast?
Sales force composite relies on individual salesperson estimates, which are subject to optimism bias — salespeople have incentives to forecast high (to avoid stockouts and please customers). This inflates the aggregate forecast and can lead to excess inventory.
