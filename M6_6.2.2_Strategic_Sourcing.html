Strategic Sourcing Process
Strategic sourcing: A systematic, data-driven approach to procurement that analyzes total spend, supply market conditions, and business requirements — to develop sourcing strategies that maximize long-term value, not just minimize unit price.
Strategic sourcing process (7 steps):
  • 1. Category profile: Define scope, spend, and business requirements
  • 2. Supply market analysis: Assess supplier landscape, competitive dynamics, market trends
  • 3. Strategy development: Determine optimal sourcing approach for this category
  • 4. RFI/RFP process: Gather supplier information and competitive bids
  • 5. Supplier evaluation: Assess suppliers on total value (not just price)
  • 6. Negotiation and selection: Negotiate and award contract to best-value supplier
  • 7. Relationship management: Manage ongoing supplier performance and relationship
Spend analysis: Compiling and analyzing all procurement spending — by category, supplier, business unit, and geography — to identify consolidation opportunities, maverick spending, and strategic sourcing priorities.
Make vs. Buy and Outsourcing Decisions
Make vs. buy analysis: A strategic decision comparing the cost and capability of producing a product/service in-house vs. purchasing it from an external supplier — considering TCO, strategic fit, capability, and risk.
Factors favoring 'make' (insource): core competency, proprietary technology, quality control requirements, capacity utilization, long-term cost advantage.
Factors favoring 'buy' (outsource): supplier has superior capability, not a core competency, enables capital focus elsewhere, access to latest technology, flexibility.
Strategic outsourcing: Deliberately outsourcing non-core activities to specialist providers — allowing the company to focus resources on its core competencies while leveraging suppliers' specialized capabilities.
Core competency: The combination of skills, knowledge, and technology that give a company a competitive advantage — activities it does better than anyone else and that are difficult to imitate. Core competencies should typically not be outsourced.
Strategic Alliances
Strategic alliance: A long-term cooperative arrangement between two or more companies that shares resources, risks, and rewards to achieve mutual strategic goals — without a full merger or acquisition.
Types of strategic alliances:
  • Joint venture: Two companies create a new, jointly owned entity — maximum integration and shared investment
  • Equity alliance: One company takes an equity stake in the other — financial alignment of interests
  • Non-equity alliance: Contractual arrangement without equity — licensing, technology sharing, co-development agreements
Alliance success factors: complementary strengths (each brings something the other lacks), clear governance, aligned objectives, trust, and defined exit terms. Most alliances fail due to misaligned expectations or inadequate governance.

Make vs. buy — key decision factors
ANSWER
Favor MAKE (insource) when: • It's a core competency • Proprietary technology / competitive advantage • Superior quality control needed • Have excess capacity to utilize • Long-term lower TCO Favor BUY (outsource) when: • Supplier has superior capability • NOT a core competency • Capital better deployed elsewhere • Need flexibility (supplier scales with you) • Access to supplier's latest technology
TERM / CONCEPT
What is spend analysis?
ANSWER
Compiling and analyzing ALL procurement spending — by category, supplier, business unit, geography. Goals: 1. Consolidation: multiple departments buying same thing from different suppliers → combine volume for better pricing 2. Maverick spend: employees buying outside approved contracts (no leverage, no visibility) 3. Category prioritization: which categories have most savings potential? 4. Supplier rationalization: too many suppliers in a category → consolidate for better terms Requires: clean data from ERP/P2P systems.
TERM / CONCEPT
Core competency — definition and outsourcing implication
ANSWER
Core competency = the combination of skills, knowledge, and technology that gives a company competitive advantage — difficult for competitors to imitate. Examples: Apple's product design and UX, Amazon's logistics, Toyota's manufacturing system. Outsourcing rule: NEVER outsource your core competency — it's your competitive advantage. Outsource everything else to focus resources on what makes you uniquely valuable. Warning: once outsourced, core skills atrophy and become difficult to rebuild.
TERM / CONCEPT
Strategic alliance types
ANSWER
Joint venture: two companies create a NEW jointly owned entity. Shared investment and control. Example: Sony Ericsson (now just Sony). Equity alliance: company A buys equity stake in company B. Financial alignment without new entity. Non-equity (contractual) alliance: license agreement, technology sharing, co-development, preferred partnership — contractual only, no ownership. Most flexible → least flexible: non-equity → equity → JV. More equity = more commitment but harder to exit.
TERM / CONCEPT
RFI vs. RFP vs. RFQ
ANSWER
RFI (Request for Information): gather general supplier info and capabilities. Early stage — market survey. No commitment. RFP (Request for Proposal): ask suppliers to propose their approach, solution, and indicative pricing. Used for complex or service procurement where solution matters as much as price. RFQ (Request for Quote): ask suppliers for a specific price for a defined specification. Used for commodity/standard goods where specs are clear and price drives selection. Process: RFI → RFP → RFQ (narrowing from market survey to competitive bid to final price).

Q1 of 5
A company should NOT outsource activities that are:
Core competencies = what makes the company uniquely valuable and competitive. Outsourcing them transfers the capability to suppliers who may serve competitors, and allows internal capability to atrophy. Once a core competency is lost, it's extremely difficult to rebuild. Examples of what NOT to outsource: Apple's product design, Toyota's manufacturing system. Outsource everything else — focus capital and talent on what makes you better than competitors.
Q2 of 5
Spend analysis helps procurement organizations primarily by:
Spend analysis: compile and categorize all spending → reveal: (1) consolidation opportunities (many departments buying the same category from different suppliers — combine for volume leverage); (2) maverick spending (purchases outside negotiated contracts — fixing this delivers immediate savings); (3) category priorities (which categories have highest spend and lowest maturity = highest savings potential). Without spend analysis, procurement operates blind to where leverage and savings truly exist.
Q3 of 5
The 7-step strategic sourcing process is designed to:
Strategic sourcing: systematic, data-driven process moving from 'find cheapest price' to 'find best total value.' Steps ensure: requirements are clearly defined before going to market (prevents re-work), supply market is analyzed (understand leverage), multiple qualified suppliers compete (maximizes competitive tension), and ongoing relationship management continues after contract award (extracts long-term value). It's a continuous cycle — not a one-time event.
Q4 of 5
A joint venture differs from a non-equity strategic alliance in that:
Joint venture = two companies create a new legal entity they jointly own and govern. Requires significant capital investment and operational commitment from both parties. Very hard to exit. Non-equity alliance = contractual arrangement (technology license, co-development agreement) with no ownership transfer. Easier to form and exit. JVs are appropriate when the shared activity requires dedicated assets, personnel, and long-term commitment that neither party would fund alone.
Q5 of 5
Strategic outsourcing is most beneficial when:
Strategic outsourcing logic: specialize in what you do best, outsource everything else to specialists who do those things best. Example: a pharmaceutical company outsources logistics (to a 3PL), IT infrastructure (to a cloud provider), and manufacturing of generic APIs (to a contract manufacturer) — freeing capital and management focus to invest in drug discovery and clinical development, where its competitive advantage lies. The result: better performance in core areas AND lower cost in non-core areas.
