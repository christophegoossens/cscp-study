Section 1 · SC Strategy & Strategic Value (Q1–Q7)
Q1
Fisher's Model
A consumer electronics company launches a new smartphone with highly uncertain demand, a 6-month product life cycle, and 12 color/storage configurations. According to Fisher's model, which supply chain type is BEST aligned with this product?
Key point: Fisher's model: Innovative products (short life cycle, uncertain demand, high variety) → Responsive supply chain. The smartphone matches all innovative product criteria. An efficient SC (built for predictable demand with long runs and low buffers) would create stockouts on popular configurations and excess inventory on unpopular ones — the fundamental strategic misalignment Fisher's model is designed to prevent.
Q2
Decoupling Point
A furniture manufacturer produces standard semi-finished frames to forecast (push), then applies customer-selected finishes and upholstery only when orders are received (pull). The point where this transition occurs is BEST defined as:
Key point: The decoupling point divides push from pull. Everything upstream (frames built to forecast) = push: long runs, economies of scale, but finished inventory risk. Everything downstream (finishing to specific order) = pull: no finished goods until an order arrives. Strategic positioning: moving the decoupling point downstream shortens customer lead time but requires more finished inventory; moving it upstream reduces finished inventory but extends lead time. This trade-off is the central tension of supply chain network design.
Q3
Postponement Strategy
Benetton manufactures garments in an undyed, generic form and dyes them to specific colors only after seasonal orders reveal actual customer color preferences. This supply chain strategy is BEST described as:
Key point: Postponement deliberately delays the decoupling point — when a generic product becomes a specific finished SKU. Benetton: undyed garments (one generic inventory position) → dyed on order (N colors × M styles). This reduces the risk of excess orange sweaters and a stockout of blue. Key benefit: carry one generic inventory position instead of N × M finished SKUs, while still being able to respond to actual customer color preferences. Dell's original model (standard components, custom-configured PCs on order) is the other classic example.
Q4
Zone of Strategic Fit
The "zone of strategic fit" in Fisher's supply chain framework refers to:
Key point: Fisher's zone of strategic fit: every market has an implied demand uncertainty (how predictable is demand?); every supply chain has an implied supply uncertainty (how flexible and responsive is it?). Strategic fit = aligning these two dimensions. Operating outside: too-efficient SC for volatile market → stockouts and markdowns; too-responsive SC for stable demand → unnecessary cost. Achieving and maintaining fit as markets evolve is THE central task of supply chain strategy.
Q5
Total Landed Cost
A supply chain manager compares two sourcing options: Supplier A in Vietnam at $15/unit; Supplier B in Mexico at $19/unit. In which situation would Supplier B be the BETTER sourcing decision?
Key point: Total Landed Cost = Production Cost + Transportation + Duties/Tariffs + Insurance + Inventory Carrying Cost + Compliance. Vietnam at $15 may have: ocean freight ($3), import duties ($3.75 at 25%), 30-day extra transit → higher safety stock carrying cost. Mexico at $19 may have: truck freight ($1), USMCA zero duty, 5-day lead time → minimal safety stock. Mexico's TLC could be $20–21 vs. Vietnam's $24–27. The fundamental insight: purchase price is not cost. Companies that source based on purchase price alone often end up with the higher actual cost.
Q6
Economic Value Added (EVA)
A supply chain team reduces average inventory by $50 million through improved demand forecasting and vendor-managed inventory. Which of the following BEST describes the impact on Economic Value Added (EVA)?
Key point: EVA = NOPAT − (Capital Employed × Cost of Capital). Inventory is Capital Employed. $50M inventory reduction → Capital Employed drops $50M → at 10% cost of capital, the capital charge falls by $5M/year → EVA improves by $5M/year immediately. Additionally, carrying cost savings (storage, insurance, obsolescence risk) improve NOPAT directly. This is why supply chain professionals say "inventory is capital in disguise" — every dollar of unnecessary inventory has a real annual cost equal to the company's required return on capital.
Q7
Supply Chain Analytics
A supply chain team progresses from producing monthly performance dashboards to running network optimization models that recommend which distribution centers to consolidate to minimize total network cost. This BEST represents a shift from:
Key point: Analytics maturity: Descriptive (what happened? → dashboards, reports) → Diagnostic (why did it happen? → root cause analysis) → Predictive (what will happen? → demand forecasting, ML) → Prescriptive (what should we do? → optimization models, AI recommendations). Monthly dashboards = Descriptive. Network optimization recommending DC consolidation = Prescriptive — the highest maturity level, going from reporting historical performance to making forward-looking optimization recommendations.
Section 2 · Sustainability & Green Supply Chain (Q8–Q14)
Q8
Triple Bottom Line
The "triple bottom line" (TBL) framework requires sustainable supply chains to measure performance across:
Key point: Triple bottom line (TBL): Profit = financial/economic performance; People = labor standards, human rights, community impact, diversity; Planet = carbon footprint, resource consumption, waste, biodiversity. Sustainable supply chain management optimizes all three — not just profit. Why commercially relevant: ESG performance affects investor capital access (institutional investors apply ESG screens), customer requirements (B2B buyers demand supplier ESG compliance), regulatory compliance (CSRD, CSDDD), and talent attraction. TBL is not just a CSR framework — it's a strategic performance system.
Q9
GHG Emissions Scopes
A consumer goods company's manufacturing facilities and purchased electricity represent 15% of its total carbon footprint. The remaining 85% comes from supplier manufacturing, inbound/outbound transportation, and product use and end-of-life disposal. The 85% is BEST classified as:
Key point: Scope 1 = direct from owned operations. Scope 2 = indirect from purchased energy (the 15% in this example). Scope 3 = ALL other indirect emissions — purchased goods (supplier manufacturing), transportation (inbound + outbound), business travel, waste, and product use/disposal. 70–90% of most companies' total footprint is Scope 3. Corporate carbon commitments that exclude Scope 3 are often criticized as greenwashing — you cannot meaningfully reduce your footprint without addressing supply chain emissions. Science-based targets require Scope 3 inclusion for exactly this reason.
Q10
Supply Chain Social Responsibility
A company discovers that its Tier 1 supplier subcontracted production to a Tier 2 factory where child labor violations occurred. The company had a supplier code of conduct requiring Tier 1 compliance. The PRIMARY gap in the company's sustainability program was:
Key point: The most common supply chain social responsibility failure: violations concentrate at Tier 2/3 sub-suppliers that most companies never audit. A supplier code of conduct must: (1) explicitly require Tier 1 to flow down code requirements to their suppliers; and (2) require disclosure of subcontracting relationships. Verification through deeper-tier audits, shared platforms (SMETA/Sedex), or blockchain traceability extends visibility. The EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive now creates legal liability for exactly this type of Tier 2+ violation — companies cannot plead ignorance.
Q11
Science-Based Targets
A company announces it will reduce carbon emissions by 30% by 2035 through energy efficiency at its own facilities only. Why might this commitment NOT qualify as a validated science-based target (SBT)?
Key point: Science-based targets have two critical requirements beyond generic carbon pledges: (1) the reduction must be scientifically aligned with the Paris Agreement 1.5°C pathway — the remaining global carbon budget imposes a specific reduction rate per year; (2) for most sectors, Scope 3 must be included because supply chain emissions represent 70–90% of corporate footprints. The SBTi (Science Based Targets initiative) independently validates that targets are both ambitious enough (aligned with science) and comprehensive enough (includes supply chain). Without Scope 3, the commitment excludes the majority of the carbon footprint.
Q12
Sustainability Standards
A buyer evaluating a supplier's environmental credentials asks for evidence of systematic environmental management. The supplier provides a GRI sustainability report. Why might this be insufficient compared to ISO 14001 certification?
Key point: GRI = a reporting framework — companies disclose sustainability information in a standardized format, but disclosure does not require verification of management system effectiveness. ISO 14001 = a management system standard — requires establishing environmental objectives, controls, procedures, monitoring, and evidence of systematic management. Third-party auditors certify the EMS meets the standard. Key distinction: GRI tells you what a company says it's doing; ISO 14001 gives independent verification that a management system is actually operating. Buyers generally prefer ISO 14001 certification because it is verifiable, not self-declared.
Q13
SMETA Audit
The supply chain problem that SMETA (Sedex Members Ethical Trade Audit) was specifically designed to address is:
Key point: SMETA was created to solve audit fatigue. A large supplier to 20 multinationals might receive 20 separate buyer-specific audits per year — enormous cost and disruption. SMETA solution: standardized audit methodology conducted once and uploaded to Sedex platform → all Sedex-member buyers access the same report. Supplier audited once; multiple buyers' requirements satisfied. SMETA covers 4 pillars: Labor, Health & Safety, Environment, Business Ethics. The sharing mechanism through the Sedex platform is the key innovation — the same principle as RBA-VAP in the electronics industry.
Q14
EU Sustainability Regulation
How does the EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD) fundamentally differ from the EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)?
Key point: The CSRD vs. CSDDD distinction is a common exam topic. CSRD = reporting directive: companies must disclose sustainability information (including supply chain impacts) in a standardized, auditable format. CSDDD = due diligence directive: companies must conduct active due diligence to identify human rights and environmental violations in their supply chains, take action to prevent and address them, and face civil liability for failure. CSDDD is more consequential — it converts supply chain violations from reputational risk to legal liability. Both apply to large EU companies AND large non-EU companies selling in the EU above revenue thresholds.
Section 3 · Emerging Technology in Supply Chains (Q15–Q21)
Q15
Digital Twin
A global manufacturer wants to evaluate the operational impact of closing one of its three distribution centers before committing to the change. Which emerging technology capability BEST enables this type of risk-free evaluation?
Key point: Digital twin = virtual replica of a physical system, continuously updated with real-world data. For supply chain network decisions: instead of closing DC #2 and discovering three months later that service levels in the Northeast have collapsed, you test it first in the virtual model. The digital twin shows: which customer zones move to farther DCs, what happens to lead times, where costs increase or decrease, and whether service targets can still be met. This is the core value proposition of digital twins: test expensive, risky, irreversible decisions in a risk-free virtual environment before committing capital or disrupting operations.
Q16
Blockchain
A food company implements blockchain to track fresh produce from farm to retail shelf. Which blockchain property makes it MORE credible for food safety traceability than traditional paper certificate systems?
Key point: Blockchain's value for traceability: immutability + distributed consensus. Traditional paper certificates (organic certification, HACCP compliance, cold chain records) can be forged. Once data is recorded in blockchain, it's replicated across all network nodes — changing any record requires consensus of all participants, making falsification practically impossible without detection. Food safety example: IBM Food Trust reduced the time to trace a food contamination event from 7 days to 2.2 seconds. Key limitation: "garbage in, garbage out" — blockchain secures data after entry but cannot prevent false data from being recorded in the first place.
Q17
IoT in Supply Chain
A pharmaceutical company ships temperature-sensitive vaccines that must remain between 2°C and 8°C. Which IoT application provides the MOST direct benefit for maintaining cold chain integrity?
Key point: Cold chain integrity: product exposed to out-of-range temperatures may be unsafe even if visually unchanged. Traditional cold chain: driver checks temperature at pickup and delivery — a snapshot, too late to intervene. IoT solution: continuous monitoring with real-time alerting. If temperature exceeds threshold at 3am in a truck overnight, the IoT system triggers an immediate alert → intervention is possible (reroute to cold storage, notify customer, salvage shipment). This transforms cold chain management from periodic-reactive (discover breach on delivery) to continuous-proactive (detect breach in real time, respond before damage is done) — the fundamental IoT value proposition.
Q18
AI/ML Demand Forecasting
A fashion retailer deploys an AI/ML demand forecasting system that ingests social media trend data, weather forecasts, competitor promotional calendars, and local event schedules — in addition to historical sales. Compared to a 12-period moving average, the PRIMARY advantage of the AI system for this retailer is:
Key point: Traditional time-series methods (moving average, exponential smoothing) use only historical sales. For fashion: a viral social media post can cause a demand spike completely unrelated to historical patterns — a moving average sees it only after 12 periods of history, by which time the trend has passed. AI/ML can detect these signals before they materialize as orders: social media mentions rising → position inventory proactively. For stable/predictable demand, moving averages work fine. For volatile, trend-driven products with identifiable external drivers, AI/ML provides materially better forecasts → lower safety stock AND fewer stockouts simultaneously.
Q19
Robotic Process Automation
Which of the following supply chain processes is the BEST candidate for Robotic Process Automation (RPA)?
Key point: RPA is ideal for: repetitive, high-volume, rule-based tasks that currently require humans to follow defined steps. Invoice matching: if invoice amount = PO amount within tolerance AND goods receipt confirms delivery → approve payment. Pure rules-based, no judgment required. 24/7 operation, zero fatigue errors, faster than humans. Options A, B, and C all require strategic judgment, contextual understanding, nuanced decision-making, and handling novel situations — not appropriate for rules-based RPA. RPA does NOT apply to tasks requiring creativity, judgment, or processing truly unstructured information. It automates the mechanical steps humans currently perform.
Q20
Technology Assessment — Build vs. Buy vs. Subscribe
A mid-size logistics company needs a Transportation Management System (TMS) to improve route optimization. They have limited internal IT resources, need deployment within 3 months, and have standard shipping requirements without unique proprietary processes. Which technology acquisition approach is MOST appropriate?
Key point: Decision criteria point to SaaS: (1) Limited IT resources → no bandwidth for custom build or complex on-premise management; (2) 3-month timeline → SaaS deploys in weeks; build takes 12–24+ months; (3) Standard requirements → no unique competitive differentiator justifying custom development. SaaS TMS advantages: vendor handles infrastructure/updates, pay-as-you-grow pricing, rapid time-to-value, lower TCO for standard processes. Custom build is justified only when the technology itself IS the competitive differentiator (e.g., Amazon's proprietary routing algorithms). For a mid-size company with standard needs, SaaS is the correct answer.
Q21
Technology Implementation
A company spends $5M on a new WMS implementation. Six months after go-live, warehouse productivity has dropped 15% and system adoption is below 40%. The MOST likely root cause is:
Key point: Classic WMS failure pattern: the technology works, but people don't use it. Evidence: 40% adoption = 60% of staff working around the system (slower than old process alone = productivity drop). Root causes: (1) No executive champion → no enforcement of adoption; (2) Insufficient training → users don't know how to use the system correctly; (3) Staff not involved in design → system doesn't match actual workflows; (4) Resistance to change → fear of job loss, comfort with familiar processes. Rule of thumb: technology implementations are 30% technology, 70% people and process change. The technology rarely fails — adoption does. Poor data quality would cause wrong outputs, not 40% adoption.
Section 4 · Technology Assessment & Integration Review (Q22–Q25)
Q22
Integration — Scenario
Scenario: TechMart, a global electronics retailer, faces three simultaneous challenges: (1) demand volatility causing frequent stockouts and excess inventory across 8,000 SKUs; (2) investor ESG pressure requiring supply chain emissions reporting and proof of ethical labor practices; (3) a US regulatory requirement to prove no products contain conflict minerals from high-risk zones.
Which combination of supply chain capabilities BEST addresses all three challenges simultaneously?
Key point: This integration question spans three different sections. Challenge 1 (demand volatility) → AI/ML forecasting processes far more variables than traditional methods, significantly improving accuracy for volatile, high-SKU electronics. Challenge 2 (ESG) → GRI framework provides standardized sustainability disclosure; science-based targets provide independently validated credibility for investor relations. Challenge 3 (conflict minerals) → Blockchain's immutability makes it practically impossible to falsify mineral origin records — once certified conflict-free sources are recorded on-chain, the chain of custody cannot be falsified. Options A and B don't credibly address all three. Option D oversimplifies: domestic sourcing doesn't eliminate ESG obligations or conflict mineral compliance requirements.
Q23
Business Process Reengineering
Before implementing a new Order Management System (OMS), a consultant recommends mapping and redesigning the current order process — which currently has 14 sequential approval steps for standard customer orders. The advice to redesign BEFORE implementing technology reflects which principle?
Key point: "Don't automate a broken process." If 14 approval steps exist due to historical organizational politics or workarounds for poor data quality, implementing an OMS that automates those 14 steps delivers a faster but still unnecessarily bureaucratic process. The right sequence: (1) Map current state; (2) Identify non-value-added steps — why does a standard order need 14 approvals? (Most should need zero.); (3) Design improved future state (2–3 approvals max, exceptions auto-flagged); (4) Configure OMS to enable the improved process. BPR + technology = transformation. Technology without BPR = digitized dysfunction at higher speed and cost.
Q24
Data Quality & Technology
A company migrates inventory records from a legacy ERP to a new cloud-based supply chain platform. Three months after go-live, the system generates replenishment orders for over-stocked items and fails to reorder items that are actually below safety stock. The ROOT CAUSE is MOST likely:
Key point: Classic "garbage in, garbage out" scenario. Legacy ERP records commonly contain errors: unrecorded adjustments, theft/damage write-offs never entered, wrong unit-of-measure conversions, phantom inventory (consumed but not deducted), stale safety stock parameters from years-old planning. If these records are migrated unchanged, the new system makes rational decisions based on irrational data: thinks there are 500 units when there are 20 (won't reorder); thinks there are 50 units when there are 500 (triggers unnecessary replenishment). Data cleansing before migration is critical: reconcile system quantities to physical counts, update safety stock levels, remove stale records. Migrating bad data into a new system creates an expensive bad-data system.
Q25
Integration — Capstone
Scenario: MegaDistrib, a global industrial parts distributor, faces three simultaneous strategic decisions: (A) A new competitor offers 24-hour delivery vs. MegaDistrib's current 5-day lead time; (B) A major customer demands a GRI-aligned sustainability report and proof of no forced labor in their supply chain; (C) The board requires any technology investment to show measurable ROI within 18 months.
What strategic approach BEST addresses all three challenges with the right tools and realistic timelines?
Key point: This capstone question tests whether you can integrate competitive strategy, sustainability, and technology ROI — as real supply chain leaders must. (A) Lead time gap → Cloud TMS optimizes carrier/route selection; cloud WMS improves pick efficiency. Cloud deployment goes live in 8–12 weeks, not years. TMS typically delivers 5–15% freight savings; WMS delivers 15–25% labor efficiency — both quantifiable within 18 months for challenge C. (B) SMETA provides the shared social audit evidence the customer requires; GRI provides the reporting framework. Option A's sequential approach leaves MegaDistrib uncompetitive during the ESG phase. Option C misunderstands urgency — competitors are already in the market. Option D transfers control but doesn't ensure service delivery on MegaDistrib's behalf. Option B correctly treats all three challenges as simultaneous, using the right tools for each.
