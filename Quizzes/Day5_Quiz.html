Section 1 · Quality, Customization & Sustainability (Q1–Q4)
Q1
Quality Approaches
According to ASCM, which of the five quality approaches defines quality as "conformance to requirements"?
Key point: The five ASCM quality approaches are: (1) Transcendent = ideal/excellence, (2) Product-based = based on product attributes, (3) User-based = fitness for use, (4) Manufacturing-based = conformance to requirements, (5) Value-based = excellence at acceptable price. Manufacturing-based quality is what internal process control and inspection measure — does the product match its specification?
Q2
Cost of Quality
A product defect escapes quality inspection and is discovered by the customer after delivery. According to the 1-10-100 rule, the cost to fix this defect is approximately how many times more expensive than if it had been prevented at the design stage?
Key point: The 1-10-100 rule: $1 to PREVENT a defect (design controls, process improvement), $10 to FIX in production (rework, scrap), $100 to FIX after customer delivery (warranty claims, recalls, lost customer trust, legal liability). This rule makes the business case for investing in upstream quality prevention rather than relying on late-stage inspection.
Q3
Customization & Postponement
A printer manufacturer builds a standard printer body at its global factory, then adds the appropriate power cable, language manual, and regulatory labels only when a specific customer order is received. This strategy is BEST described as:
Key point: Postponement = delaying final product differentiation to the latest possible point in the supply chain. The generic printer body is "pushed" forward as standard inventory; the country-specific accessories are "pulled" only when an order triggers a specific configuration. This reduces forecast risk (fewer SKUs to forecast), lowers inventory investment, and enables mass customization at low unit cost.
Q4
Sustainability — Triple Bottom Line
A company redesigns its packaging to use 100% recycled materials (increasing cost by 8%), partners with suppliers that pay fair living wages, and publishes annual carbon-reduction targets. This decision BEST reflects which framework?
Key point: The Triple Bottom Line (TBL) measures business performance on three dimensions: People (social — fair wages, community, labor standards), Planet (environmental — carbon, waste, resource use), and Profit (economic). Sustainability = optimizing all three, not trading off environmental or social impact for short-term profit. The Brundtland definition adds: meeting present needs without compromising future generations.
Section 2 · Supplier Selection, Contracts & Purchase Orders (Q5–Q8)
Q5
Supplier Selection — TCO
Supplier A quotes €10/unit with a 2% defect rate and 85% on-time delivery. Supplier B quotes €11.50/unit with a 0.1% defect rate and 98% on-time delivery. Using a Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) approach, which statement is MOST accurate?
Key point: TCO (Total Cost of Ownership) goes beyond unit price to include: quality failure costs (rework, scrap, inspection), late delivery penalties (expediting, lost sales), administrative costs, and risk costs. A 2% defect rate at high volume = significant rework/warranty costs. An 85% OTD rate = frequent expediting and potentially lost customer orders. Modern supply chain thinking requires TCO analysis — not just lowest price selection.
Q6
Contracts — Risk Allocation
A defense contractor is hired to develop a next-generation radar system. The technical requirements will evolve through R&D, and final scope cannot be fully defined upfront. Which contract type BEST protects the supplier from absorbing unforeseeable cost overruns?
Key point: Contract risk allocation rule: Fixed-price = supplier bears cost risk (protects the buyer when scope is clear). Cost-plus = buyer bears cost risk (protects the supplier when scope is uncertain/evolving — common for R&D, complex engineering). Under cost-plus, the buyer pays whatever the supplier's actual costs turn out to be, plus an agreed profit percentage. This is appropriate for the radar system where scope cannot be fully defined.
Q7
Contract Terms
A supply contract specifies: "For every calendar day a shipment is delivered after the contractual delivery date, the supplier will pay the buyer €2,000." This contract provision is called:
Key point: Liquidated damages = pre-agreed financial penalties for specific, defined contract breaches (late delivery, missed quality targets). Agreed upfront — the buyer does not need to prove actual damages in court. This is distinct from: Force majeure (exempts parties during unforeseeable events like natural disasters), Performance-based incentives (positive payments for exceeding targets), and Incoterms (define freight/insurance/risk of loss responsibilities).
Q8
Purchase Orders — 3-Way Match
Accounts payable receives a supplier invoice for 100 units at €50/unit (total: €5,000). The original purchase order was for 100 units at €45/unit. The goods receipt confirms only 95 units were delivered. When performing the 3-way match, which discrepancies will be flagged?
Key point: The 3-way match compares: (1) Purchase Order (what was ordered and at what price), (2) Goods Receipt (what was actually received), (3) Supplier Invoice (what the supplier is charging). Here, two discrepancies exist: price mismatch (€45 PO vs. €50 invoice = €5/unit overage) AND quantity mismatch (95 received vs. 100 invoiced). Both must be resolved before payment is approved. The invoice should only be paid for 95 units at €45 each = €4,275 — pending supplier correction.
Section 3 · Planning Operations & Master Scheduling (Q9–Q17)
Q9
MPC Hierarchy
In the Manufacturing Planning and Control (MPC) hierarchy, which planning level directly follows the Sales & Operations Plan (S&OP)?
Key point: The MPC hierarchy (top to bottom): (1) Business plan → (2) S&OP/Production plan (aggregate, product families) → (3) MPS (item-level, weeks) → (4) MRP (components) → (5) PAC (shop floor execution). The MPS "disaggregates" the S&OP aggregate plan into specific items, quantities, and dates. Each level drives the next — the sequence is critical for the CSCP exam.
Q10
MPS — Primary Role
The Master Production Schedule (MPS) is BEST described as the primary input to which downstream planning process?
Key point: The MPS is the primary driver of MRP. MRP uses the MPS (what finished goods to make and when) + the BOM (what components are needed) + inventory records (what's on hand/on order) to calculate net material requirements and recommend planned orders. Without the MPS, MRP has no way to know what to plan. The flow is: MPS → MRP → purchase orders and production orders.
Q11
Time Fences
Inside the demand time fence of the Master Production Schedule, which of the following is TRUE?
Key point: Time fence rules: Inside the demand time fence = frozen zone. No system-generated changes are allowed — only the master scheduler can manually authorize changes. This protects production already in progress (materials ordered, jobs on the floor). Frequent disruptions inside the fence cause costly "nervousness." Beyond the planning fence = fully flexible (system can auto-adjust). Between fences = limited flexibility with human oversight.
Q12
Available-to-Promise (ATP)
A sales representative wants to promise a customer delivery of 300 units in week 6. Before making this commitment, the MOST appropriate action is to:
Key point: Available-to-Promise (ATP) = the uncommitted portion of planned production and current inventory. Formula: ATP = Planned Production − Already Committed Orders. Sales uses ATP to confirm deliverable quantities before promising customers — preventing over-commitment. Checking ATP is the correct first step before any delivery promise. If ATP shows only 200 units available in week 6, the sales rep should NOT promise 300.
Q13
Capacity Planning — RCCP
Rough-Cut Capacity Planning (RCCP) is performed at which stage of the planning hierarchy, and for what purpose?
Key point: Each planning level has its own capacity check: Resource Planning (S&OP → long-term) → RCCP (MPS → key resources, high-level feasibility check) → Capacity Requirements Planning (MRP → detailed work center) → Capacity Control (PAC → real-time execution). RCCP is a quick, high-level check of critical resources (key machines, labor pools) to confirm the MPS is roughly feasible before investing in the full MRP calculation. It is NOT as detailed as CRP.
Q14
MPS Planning Horizon
A company manufactures industrial pumps. The raw material procurement takes 8 weeks, component fabrication takes 6 weeks, and final assembly takes 4 weeks. What is the MINIMUM planning horizon the MPS must cover?
Key point: The MPS planning horizon must cover at least the cumulative lead time — the total time from placing the earliest material order to completing the finished product. Here: 8 weeks (raw materials) + 6 weeks (fabrication) + 4 weeks (assembly) = 18 weeks minimum. If the horizon is shorter, MRP cannot plan the early-stage materials in time, causing shortages. A shorter horizon means the planning system is "blind" to requirements that fall outside its window.
Q15
MPC Hierarchy — Capacity Checks
Which capacity planning technique operates at the S&OP level and validates long-term resource availability — typically covering years rather than weeks?
Key point: The four capacity planning levels align with the MPC hierarchy: (1) Resource Planning — S&OP level, long-horizon, strategic (facilities, major equipment, workforce headcount). (2) RCCP — MPS level, weeks/months (key machines, critical labor). (3) CRP — MRP level, detailed (all work centers). (4) Capacity Control — PAC, real-time execution. Resource Planning is the most strategic level — it informs investment decisions about building new capacity, not just scheduling existing capacity.
Q16
MPS Inputs
Which combination of inputs drives the Master Production Schedule (MPS)?
Key point: The MPS is driven by three inputs: (1) Customer orders (firm, confirmed demand), (2) Demand forecast (anticipated future demand beyond the order horizon), and (3) Safety stock replenishment (planned buffer to protect service levels). Inside the planning horizon, customer orders typically dominate; further out, the forecast dominates. The master scheduler must balance these inputs — and use RCCP to verify capacity feasibility.
Q17
Master Scheduling — Scenario
Scenario: A key customer calls to place an urgent order for 500 units needed in 4 days. The MPS shows this period falls inside the demand time fence. The Available-to-Promise quantity in that week is 600 units.
What is the MOST appropriate course of action?
Key point: Two rules intersect here: (1) ATP confirms supply exists (600 units ≥ 500 requested — so the order is physically deliverable). (2) Demand time fence — changes can be made, but ONLY with master scheduler manual approval (not automatic system changes). The correct answer: ATP check passes → master scheduler manually authorizes the commitment and updates the MPS. Automatic changes inside the frozen zone would disrupt other orders already in production.
Section 4 · Materials Requirements Planning & BOM (Q18–Q25)
Q18
Bill of Materials
According to ASCM, a Bill of Materials (BOM) is BEST described as:
Key point: The BOM is the product structure — it defines parent-child relationships between finished goods, sub-assemblies, and raw materials, along with usage quantities at each level. Level 0 = finished product, Level 1 = major sub-assemblies, Level 2+ = lower-level components and raw materials. BOM accuracy is critical — a 1% BOM error compounds across every production order and causes MRP to systematically misplan material requirements.
Q19
BOM Structure
In a Bill of Materials (BOM), "Level 0" refers to:
Key point: BOM levels flow top-down: Level 0 = finished product (the top-level item being manufactured). Level 1 = sub-assemblies or major components that go directly into the finished product. Level 2+ = lower-level components and raw materials. MRP "explodes" the BOM top-down, level by level — starting from the MPS quantity at Level 0 and calculating requirements for each lower level using the BOM quantities.
Q20
MRP Inputs
MRP requires three key inputs to calculate material requirements. Which of the following is NOT one of the three standard MRP inputs?
Key point: MRP's three key inputs: (1) MPS — what finished goods to make and when. (2) BOM — what components are needed per unit. (3) Inventory records — what's already on-hand and what scheduled receipts (open POs) are incoming. The S&OP is an input to the MPS, not directly to MRP. MRP works at the detailed item/component level — S&OP operates at the aggregate product family level and is several levels above MRP in the hierarchy.
Q21
MRP Calculation — Net Requirements
MRP shows a gross requirement of 1,000 units of Component X in week 5. On-hand inventory is 200 units. A scheduled receipt of 150 units is due in week 4. What is the net requirement that MRP will plan to order?
Calculation: Net Requirements = Gross Requirements − On-Hand Inventory − Scheduled Receipts = 1,000 − 200 − 150 = 650 units. MRP accounts for BOTH on-hand inventory (already in the warehouse) AND scheduled receipts (already on order, arriving before the need date). This prevents double-ordering. The planned order for 650 units will then be offset backward by the supplier's lead time to determine when the order must be placed.
Q22
Dependent vs. Independent Demand
A bicycle manufacturer produces new bicycles (which require 2 tires each) and also sells replacement tires directly to consumers through a retail channel. The demand for replacement tires sold through retail is BEST classified as:
Key point: Dependent demand = derived from the BOM structure of another item (tires used in new bicycle production = 500 bicycles × 2 tires = 1,000 tires — CALCULATED). Independent demand = not related to any parent item's production plan (retail replacement tires sold directly to consumers = FORECASTED). The same physical item (tire) can have BOTH demand types simultaneously — which is why planners must manage both channels separately.
Q23
BOM Accuracy Impact
A production engineer discovers that the BOM for a product lists 8 fasteners per unit, but actual production consistently uses 10 fasteners per unit. When MRP runs using the incorrect BOM, what is the MOST LIKELY outcome?
Key point: MRP blindly calculates from the BOM. If the BOM says 8 fasteners but 10 are needed, MRP plans orders for 8 × (production quantity) — always 20% short. This causes systematic production stoppages every cycle, not random shortages. Safety stock is designed for demand variability, not for BOM errors. BOM accuracy is foundational — ASCM emphasizes that BOM accuracy must be maintained at >98% for MRP to function reliably. Corrective action: update the BOM to 10 fasteners immediately.
Q24
MRP Exception Messages
MRP generates a "reschedule in" exception message for an existing purchase order. What does this mean, and what action should the planner take?
Key point: MRP exception messages guide planner actions: "Reschedule in" (expedite/move earlier) = demand moved forward, need the material sooner — contact the supplier to accelerate delivery. "Reschedule out" (defer) = demand moved out, delay the order to avoid early inventory buildup. "Cancel" = order no longer needed (demand cancelled or covered by other supply). "Release" = a new planned order needs to be released to a supplier. Exception management is how MRP planners focus attention on what actually needs action, rather than reviewing every order.
Q25
MRP Explosion — Scenario Calculation
Scenario: TechCo plans to produce 500 laptops (Level 0). Each laptop requires 1 display unit (Level 1). Each display unit requires 2 control boards (Level 2). Current on-hand: 50 display units, 0 control boards. Scheduled receipts: 100 display units (due before production), 0 control boards. No safety stock.
What are the MRP net requirements for display units, and how many control boards will MRP plan to order?
Calculation (BOM explosion, level by level):
Level 1 — Display units:
Gross requirement = 500 laptops × 1 display = 500
Net requirements = 500 − 50 (on-hand) − 100 (scheduled receipts) = 350 display units

Level 2 — Control boards:
Gross requirement = 350 (planned displays) × 2 boards each = 700
Net requirements = 700 − 0 − 0 = 700 control boards

MRP explodes the BOM level by level. The net requirement at one level becomes the gross requirement input to explode the next level down. Lead times then offset these planned orders backward to determine the order release dates.
