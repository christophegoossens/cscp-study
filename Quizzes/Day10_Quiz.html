Section 1 · Transport Modes & Carrier Management (Q1–Q6)
Q1
Carrier Types
A company that offers transportation services to any shipper at published tariff rates, with no exclusivity, is BEST classified as a:
Key point: A common carrier serves any shipper at published (tariff) rates — no exclusivity, regulated service (e.g., UPS, FedEx, major railroads). A contract carrier serves specific shippers under a negotiated contract with dedicated capacity. A private carrier is a company-owned fleet. A freight broker is a non-asset intermediary that matches shippers with carriers.
Q2
Rate Strategy
A shipper negotiates rates with a carrier for a 12-month period, with the carrier committing to capacity on specific lanes in exchange for guaranteed volume. This arrangement is BEST described as:
Key point: Contract rates are negotiated for a defined period (typically one year) — the shipper gets price stability and committed capacity; the carrier gets guaranteed volume. This is superior to spot market for high-volume recurring lanes. Spot market pricing is transacted one shipment at a time and is volatile — it can be 50% cheaper or 100% more expensive than contract rates depending on market conditions. Strategy: contracts for base volume, spot/broker for surge.
Q3
Carrier Management
A routing guide lists three carriers for a given shipping lane in priority order. Which of the following BEST describes the primary purpose of a routing guide?
Key point: A routing guide specifies, for each origin-destination lane, which carrier to use (Carrier 1 = preferred, Carrier 2 = backup, Carrier 3 = last resort/spot). This protects pre-negotiated contract rates, ensures preferred carrier relationships are honored, and provides a systematic process for re-tendering when a carrier declines. Without a routing guide, shippers may accidentally use expensive carriers or bypass preferred arrangements.
Q4
Carrier Performance
Scenario: A shipper's routing guide lists Carrier A as the preferred provider for a high-volume lane. Over the past quarter, Carrier A accepted only 55% of load tenders, forcing the shipper to use expensive spot market carriers for the remainder at significantly higher cost.
Which carrier KPI is MOST directly reflected by the 55% acceptance figure?
Key point: Load acceptance rate = the percentage of shipment tenders the carrier actually accepts. A 55% acceptance rate means the carrier refuses nearly half of all loads — unreliable capacity. Preferred carriers should have high acceptance rates (85%+), which is the core value of a contract relationship (the carrier commits capacity to the shipper). Low acceptance forces re-tendering at spot market rates, driving up freight cost. This metric drives routing guide prioritization and annual carrier renewals.
Q5
Mode Selection
A company needs to ship time-sensitive, high-value electronics from Asia to Europe within 48 hours. Which transport mode is most appropriate, even though it is the most expensive option per kilogram?
Key point: Mode selection balances cost, transit time, reliability, and cargo characteristics. For time-sensitive, high-value goods requiring a 48-hour Asia-to-Europe transit, only air freight is feasible — ocean takes 3–5 weeks, Trans-Siberian rail 10–14 days, and road is impractical across continents. The high cost of air is offset by: (1) urgency requirement, (2) high value-to-weight ratio (electronics), and (3) reduced inventory carrying cost during transit. No mode wins on all dimensions — match mode to the dominant selection criterion.
Q6
Carrier Types
A grocery chain operates its own fleet of refrigerated trucks for daily store deliveries on consistent, high-volume routes. The PRIMARY advantage the company seeks by operating a private fleet (rather than using a third-party carrier) is:
Key point: The PRIMARY benefit of a private carrier (company-owned fleet) is control — over schedules, service levels, cargo handling, and for refrigerated goods, cold chain integrity. Private fleet is justified when: volume is high and consistent, specialized handling is required, or delivery is a competitive differentiator. Drawbacks: high fixed costs, fleet management complexity, capital tied up in equipment. Common carriers offer lower fixed cost and scalability, but less control — especially critical for perishables where cold chain compliance is non-negotiable.
Section 2 · Legal, Security & Regulatory (Q7–Q13)
Q7
Legal Documents
A Bill of Lading (BOL) serves THREE distinct legal functions in a shipment. Which answer CORRECTLY identifies all three?
Key point: The BOL has three functions: (1) Receipt — carrier acknowledges receiving goods in stated quantity and condition; (2) Contract of carriage — specifies transport terms, routing, rate, and liability; (3) Title document (negotiable BOL only) — whoever holds the endorsed original has legal claim to the goods. The third function is critical for international trade finance (Letters of Credit). A straight BOL is non-negotiable (names a specific consignee). A negotiable BOL can be endorsed and transferred like a check.
Q8
Legal Documents
A seller ships goods internationally under a Letter of Credit. The bank requires a negotiable (order) bill of lading rather than a straight bill of lading. The PRIMARY reason is:
Key point: Under a Letter of Credit: (1) seller ships goods and receives the original negotiable BOL from the carrier; (2) seller presents the BOL and other complying documents to the bank; (3) bank releases payment to the seller; (4) bank holds the original BOL (= title to goods); (5) buyer pays the bank and receives the BOL; (6) buyer presents the BOL at destination to take delivery. The negotiable BOL is the bank's security — without it, the bank cannot control the goods. A straight BOL names a specific consignee and cannot be transferred.
Q9
Supply Chain Security
The C-TPAT program (Customs-Trade Partnership Against Terrorism) offers companies expedited customs processing and fewer inspections. In exchange, member companies must:
Key point: C-TPAT is a US CBP voluntary program with a risk-based logic: companies that implement CBP-approved security measures (facilities, IT systems, personnel) and extend those standards to their partners (suppliers, carriers, warehouses) get expedited clearance — Green Lane priority, fewer physical inspections. CBP redirects inspection resources to non-vetted companies. The EU/international equivalent is AEO (Authorized Economic Operator). The partner vetting requirement is key — you can't be C-TPAT-compliant if your suppliers aren't secured too.
Q10
Regulatory Compliance
Scenario: A truck driver's 14-hour on-duty window began at 6:00 AM when he arrived at a shipper's facility. After 9 hours of waiting at the loading dock (detention), the driver finally departs at 3:00 PM, leaving only 2 hours of driving allowed within the legal window.
Under US Hours of Service (HOS) regulations, what is the supply chain implication of this situation?
Key point: FMCSA HOS: 11-hour driving limit after 10 hours off-duty; must complete driving within a 14-hour on-duty window — no extension for delays. Excessive detention time (waiting at shipper or receiver docks) is a major HOS problem: time waiting counts against the 14-hour window, leaving fewer hours to drive. This causes: missed delivery appointments, lower driver productivity, and effectively higher shipping costs (shipper may pay detention fees). Supply chain impact: shippers must schedule dock appointments efficiently and minimize driver wait time to protect HOS hours.
Q11
Regulatory Compliance
A company ships lithium batteries by international air freight. Which regulatory body's rules PRIMARILY govern the hazardous materials requirements for this shipment?
Key point: Each transport mode has its own dangerous goods authority: Air = IATA Dangerous Goods Regulations (DGR); Ocean = IMDG Code (IMO); US domestic surface = US DOT. Lithium batteries are specifically restricted under IATA DGR because of fire risk at altitude, pressurization, and temperature extremes. US DOT rules apply to ground transportation within the US only. The differentiation by mode reflects genuinely different risk profiles — air shipments face hazards that don't apply to road or sea transport.
Q12
Legal Framework
A shipper receives a delivery with visible pallet damage. To preserve the right to file a valid freight claim against the carrier, what must the shipper do FIRST?
Key point: A freight claim must be supported by: (1) a delivery receipt with the damage exception noted at time of delivery — if you sign "clear" for a damaged delivery, you surrender your right to claim; (2) photos documenting the damage; (3) the original BOL; (4) the commercial invoice (proof of value). The claim must then be filed within statutory limits (9 months for US domestic loss/damage). Failure to note exceptions at delivery is the most common reason legitimate claims are denied. The statutory filing deadline is an absolute cutoff — claims filed late are rejected.
Q13
Export Controls
Before exporting goods to a foreign buyer, a company must screen the buyer against government denied parties and sanctioned entities lists. This screening requirement applies:
Key point: Denied party/sanctioned entity screening is mandatory for every export transaction. Key lists: OFAC SDN (Specially Designated Nationals), BIS Entity List, State Dept Debarred List. The legal standard is 'knew or should have known' — ignorance is not a defense. Penalties: criminal prosecution of individuals, multi-million dollar fines, permanent loss of export privileges. There is no minimum value threshold and no product category exemption. This is a non-negotiable compliance step in any export compliance program.
Section 3 · Trade Zones, Import/Export & Incoterms (Q14–Q19)
Q14
Trade Documentation
The Harmonized System (HS) code is a 6-digit standardized classification used in 200+ countries. Its PRIMARY function in international trade is to:
Key point: The HS code (first 6 digits globally standardized) determines: (1) the tariff rate applied at import, (2) whether non-tariff requirements apply (import permits, certifications, inspections), and (3) trade statistics. Misclassification = wrong duty rate — underpayment creates penalty risk; overpayment hurts profitability. The US uses a 10-digit HTS code (adds 4 digits for national specificity); the EU uses 8 digits. The first 6 are always universal. A licensed customs broker typically handles correct HS classification because errors are both common and costly.
Q15
Trade Documentation
Under USMCA, a US importer claims preferential (zero) tariff rates on goods manufactured in Mexico. The document that PROVES the goods qualify for USMCA preferential rates is:
Key point: A Certificate of Origin (COO) certifies where goods were manufactured or substantially transformed — and that they meet the applicable FTA rules of origin. This is the document customs authorities require to grant preferential tariff rates. A Mexican supplier address on an invoice only shows where the seller is located, not where goods were manufactured or whether they contain sufficient North American content to qualify for USMCA rates. Without a valid COO, the importer must pay the standard (MFN) tariff rate, not the preferential FTA rate.
Q16
Trade Zones
A manufacturer imports components into a US Foreign Trade Zone (FTZ), assembles finished electronics, and sells them into the domestic US market. The PRIMARY customs duty benefit of this FTZ strategy is:
Key point: FTZ benefits: (1) Duty deferral — no duty paid until goods leave the zone and enter domestic commerce; (2) Inverted tariff benefit — if individual components carry higher tariff rates than the finished product, assembling in an FTZ and paying duty on finished goods is cheaper; (3) Re-export duty-free — goods exported from the FTZ never pay US import duties; (4) Duty reduction on scrap/waste — only pay duty on what enters commerce, not on production waste. Goods sold domestically pay duty when exiting the FTZ — duty is never permanently eliminated for goods entering US commerce.
Q17
Export Controls
Scenario: A US defense contractor's engineer shares detailed technical drawings of a missile guidance system with a foreign colleague during an in-person meeting held at the company's US headquarters. No physical goods cross any border.
Under ITAR (International Traffic in Arms Regulations), this action:
Key point: ITAR's 'deemed export' rule: releasing ITAR-controlled technical data or technology to a foreign national in the US is treated as an export to that person's home country — requiring prior State Department approval. This applies regardless of whether: the person holds a US visa, the meeting is on US soil, or no physical goods move. ITAR (State Dept, covers defense articles) is far stricter than EAR (Commerce Dept, covers commercial and dual-use goods). ITAR violations are treated as national security matters — penalties include criminal prosecution and multi-million dollar fines.
Q18
Trade Zones
A bonded warehouse and a Free Trade Zone (FTZ) both allow imported goods to be held without paying customs duties. What is the KEY operational difference between them?
Key point: The critical distinction: a bonded warehouse is a storage-only facility — goods can be held duty-deferred but cannot be processed, manufactured, or transformed there. A Free Trade Zone (FTZ) allows manufacturing, assembly, processing, repackaging, and other value-adding activities in addition to storage. This makes FTZs far more strategically valuable — enabling the inverted tariff benefit and duty-free re-export of manufactured goods. Both allow duty deferral, but only FTZs enable production activity without triggering duties.
Q19
Trade Agreements
Scenario: A Chinese manufacturer routes its finished products through a Mexican distribution center, re-labels them with a Mexican origin mark, and claims USMCA preferential (zero) tariff rates when exporting to the US.
Why would US Customs REJECT this USMCA tariff claim?
Key point: Rules of origin exist precisely to prevent 'tariff shopping' — routing goods through an FTA country to fraudulently claim preferential rates. USMCA requires that goods be substantially manufactured (or have a minimum percentage of value added) within North America. Simple transshipment, re-labeling, or minimal processing does not constitute sufficient origin change. CBP can investigate the production history and supply chain of any goods claiming USMCA rates. Penalties for false origin claims include duties, fines, and import ban. This is why supply chain design decisions (where to source, where to manufacture) are inseparable from trade compliance strategy.
Section 4 · Reverse Logistics & Waste/Sustainability (Q20–Q25)
Q20
Reverse Logistics
According to ASCM, the logistics definition includes BOTH forward AND reverse flows. Which of the following activities is BEST classified as reverse logistics?
Key point: Reverse logistics = moving goods FROM their typical final destination BACK UP the supply chain to capture value, ensure proper disposal, or comply with regulations. It includes: customer returns, product recalls, end-of-lease returns, warranty repairs, end-of-life recycling, and commercial returns. It is a designed supply chain process — not an afterthought. Drivers of reverse logistics growth: e-commerce (high return rates of 15–30%), Extended Producer Responsibility regulations, consumer expectations for easy returns, and remanufacturing economics.
Q21
Reverse Logistics
In a reverse logistics operation, 'gate-keeping' refers to:
Key point: Gate-keeping is the most critical step in the reverse pipeline — it's the decision point that determines: (1) Is this a valid return? (reject ineligible returns = saves processing cost); (2) What condition is the item in? (routes to right facility); (3) What disposition maximizes value recovery? (resell new → refurbish → remanufacture → cannibalize → donate → recycle → dispose). Poor gate-keeping is costly in two directions: accepting invalid returns wastes processing cost; sending recoverable goods to landfill wastes value. A well-designed gate-keeping process can significantly improve reverse logistics profitability.
Q22
Reverse Logistics
An automotive parts company takes in used alternators, fully disassembles them, inspects every component, replaces worn or failed parts with new or reconditioned equivalents, reassembles, and tests them to original OEM specifications. This process is BEST described as:
Key point: Remanufacturing = complete rebuild to original specifications (full disassembly, component inspection, replacement of worn parts, reassembly, full testing). Result: product sold as equivalent to new. Common in: auto parts, industrial equipment, electronics (printers, copiers, medical devices). Refurbishment = restore to like-new appearance and function (cleaning, testing, repackaging) without full disassembly — less intensive, sold at a discount as 'certified pre-owned.' Recycling = break down to raw materials. Cannibalization = use working parts from one unit to repair another. Full disassembly + replacement to original spec = remanufacturing.
Q23
Waste / Circular Economy
A consumer electronics company has excess unsold inventory. It considers four disposal options: (1) refurbishing and selling as certified pre-owned; (2) donating to schools; (3) recycling for raw material recovery; (4) landfilling. According to the circular economy value-preservation hierarchy, ranked from HIGHEST to LOWEST, the correct order is:
Key point: The circular economy hierarchy (highest to lowest value preservation): Refuse → Reduce → Reuse → Repair → Remanufacture → Refurbish → Donate → Recycle → Recover energy → Dispose (landfill). The principle: keep materials at the highest possible level of use for as long as possible. Refurbishing preserves the product's structure and embedded manufacturing value. Donating provides social value while avoiding disposal cost. Recycling breaks the product back to raw materials — losing the energy and processing that went into making it. Landfill is the last resort — zero value recovery, maximum environmental impact.
Q24
Waste / Sustainability
Under Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) regulations — such as the EU's WEEE Directive for electronics and e-waste — manufacturers are required to:
Key point: EPR = 'you made it, you're responsible for it at end of life.' Manufacturers must plan, fund, and often operate product recovery/recycling programs. This shifts end-of-life cost from government (taxpayer) or consumer to the producer. Key supply chain effects: (1) Reverse logistics obligation — must build or outsource a take-back system; (2) Design incentive — modular design, recyclable materials, fewer hazardous substances reduce compliance cost; (3) Cost pressure — non-recyclable products become expensive to dispose of, making eco-design economically rational. EU WEEE covers electronics; battery EPR covers batteries; packaging EPR covers packaging waste.
Q25
Waste / Sustainability
Scenario: A consumer goods company manufactures products in Asia, ships them by ocean freight to Europe, and distributes to customers via truck. The CEO states the company has "zero direct emissions" from its head office operations. A sustainability analyst argues this statement is misleading from a carbon footprint perspective.
Which concept BEST explains the analyst's concern?
Key point: Emissions scopes: Scope 1 = direct emissions from company-owned operations; Scope 2 = indirect emissions from purchased energy; Scope 3 = all other indirect value chain emissions (supplier production, inbound/outbound logistics, customer use, end-of-life disposal). Scope 3 typically represents 70–90% of a company's total carbon footprint — making it the dominant lever for corporate decarbonization. "Zero direct emissions" (Scope 1) describes only a tiny fraction of total impact. Under ESG reporting frameworks (GHG Protocol, CDP), companies are increasingly required to disclose Scope 3 emissions. Supply chain decarbonization — choosing suppliers with lower emissions, shifting from air to ocean/rail freight, load optimization — is the most impactful action available.
