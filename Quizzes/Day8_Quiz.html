Section 1 · Inventory Types & Costs (Q1–Q6)
Q1
Inventory Types
Which of the following BEST describes anticipation inventory?
Key point: The four functional inventory types are: Cycle stock (batch replenishment, avg = Q/2), Safety stock (buffer against uncertainty), Anticipation inventory (built for a known future spike — seasonal, promotional, shutdown), and Pipeline/in-transit inventory (goods moving through the chain). Option A = pipeline, B = safety stock, C = cycle stock.
Q2
EOQ Calculation
A company has annual demand of 2,500 units, an ordering cost of €50 per order, and a carrying cost of €2 per unit per year. What is the Economic Order Quantity (EOQ)?
Calculation: EOQ = √(2 × D × S / H) = √(2 × 2,500 × €50 / €2) = √(250,000 / 2) = √125,000 ≈ 354 units. At this order quantity, annual ordering cost = annual carrying cost (each ≈ €354). Ordering fewer than 354 units increases ordering cost; ordering more increases carrying cost.
Q3
Inventory Costs
A warehouse holds average inventory of €800,000. The annual carrying cost rate is 25%. What is the annual carrying cost?
Calculation: Annual Carrying Cost = Average Inventory × Carrying Cost Rate = €800,000 × 25% = €200,000 per year. This 25% rate typically encompasses: capital (opportunity cost of money tied up), storage and handling, insurance, obsolescence risk, and damage/shrinkage. The industry range is 20–30% per year.
Q4
Cycle Stock
A company uses an order quantity of 400 units per replenishment cycle. Under the EOQ model, what is the average cycle stock carried between replenishments?
Key point: Average cycle stock = Order Quantity ÷ 2 = 400 ÷ 2 = 200 units. This is because inventory starts at the full order quantity when a batch arrives and depletes linearly to zero before the next order — so the average is half the order quantity. Larger batches → more average cycle stock → higher carrying cost.
Q5
Inventory Cost Tradeoff
A supply chain manager increases the standard order quantity from 100 units to 500 units. Which statement BEST describes the impact on annual inventory costs?
Key point: This is the fundamental inventory cost tradeoff. Ordering cost is fixed per order — so fewer orders per year (larger batches) = lower annual ordering cost. But larger batches mean more average inventory = higher annual carrying cost. EOQ is the point where total cost is minimized — the crossover where ordering cost = carrying cost.
Q6
Safety Stock
A planner is sizing safety stock using the formula SS = Z × σ × √LT. Which factor does NOT directly increase the required safety stock level?
Key point: Safety stock = Z × σ × √LT. The three drivers are: (1) demand variability (σ), (2) lead time (LT), and (3) service level target (Z). EOQ determines cycle stock (how much to order each time) — it has no direct effect on safety stock. Safety stock protects against uncertainty; cycle stock manages the batch replenishment rhythm. These are independent calculations.
Section 2 · Replenishment Strategies (Q7–Q12)
Q7
Reorder Point Calculation
A product has average daily demand of 80 units, a supplier lead time of 5 days, and a safety stock of 200 units. What is the reorder point (ROP)?
Calculation: ROP = (d × LT) + SS = (80 units/day × 5 days) + 200 = 400 + 200 = 600 units. When on-hand inventory hits 600 units, a new order is placed. Under normal conditions, the shipment arrives just as inventory has been drawn down to the safety stock level (200 units). Option B (400 units) is demand during lead time only — ignoring safety stock.
Q8
Q System vs P System
A warehouse manager monitors inventory levels continuously and places an order for exactly 600 units every time stock falls below 250 units. This BEST describes which replenishment system?
Key point: The Q (continuous review) system = FIXED ORDER QUANTITY ordered at VARIABLE TIMES (whenever inventory hits the ROP). The P (periodic review) system = VARIABLE ORDER QUANTITY ordered at FIXED TIME INTERVALS. The exam frequently tests this distinction. Clue words: "fixed 600 units" + "whenever stock falls below" = Q system.
Q9
Vendor-Managed Inventory
Under a VMI arrangement, the PRIMARY benefit to the SUPPLIER is:
Key point: VMI transfers the replenishment decision to the supplier. The supplier receives real-time inventory and sales data from the customer — meaning it sees actual demand signals, not distorted purchase orders. This visibility lets the supplier plan production smoothly, reduce its own safety stock, and avoid the Bullwhip Effect (over-ordering due to uncertainty about real demand).
Q10
Kanban / Lean Pull
A production floor uses two bins for each component. When a worker empties the first bin, it is returned to the supply area as a replenishment signal, while the second bin is pulled into use. This is BEST described as:
Key point: The two-bin system is a simple, visual form of Kanban. The empty bin is the physical kanban signal — it triggers replenishment of exactly one bin's worth. No complex inventory tracking required. This is a lean pull system: replenishment happens only in response to actual consumption, not a forecast or schedule push.
Q11
Just-in-Time (JIT)
Which condition is MOST critical for a Just-in-Time (JIT) replenishment system to function effectively?
Key point: JIT eliminates all buffer inventory — which means there is no cushion to absorb late or defective deliveries. If a supplier delivers late or delivers poor-quality material, production stops immediately. This is why JIT demands: (1) extremely reliable, frequent supplier deliveries, (2) near-perfect quality (no buffer for rework), and (3) stable, predictable demand. Without these, JIT creates costly production stoppages.
Q12
Min-Max System
An item's min-max parameters are: minimum (reorder point) = 500 units, maximum = 2,000 units. Current inventory is 420 units — below the minimum — so an order is triggered. How many units should be ordered?
Calculation: In a min-max system, the order quantity = Maximum − Current Inventory on Hand = 2,000 − 420 = 1,580 units. Option C (1,500) is the difference between max and min — which would be correct only if inventory were exactly at the minimum. Option D (2,000) ignores existing inventory. The goal is to always return to the maximum level.
Section 3 · Traceability, Accuracy & Disposition (Q13–Q17)
Q13
Inventory Traceability
An aerospace manufacturer assigns a unique identifier to EVERY individual component and can trace each unit back to its raw material source, production date, operator, and test results. This level of traceability BEST describes:
Key point: Serial number tracking assigns a unique ID to each individual item — the highest level of traceability. Required in aerospace, medical devices, and high-value industries where full unit history must be documented. Lot/batch tracking (option A) assigns one number to a group of items produced together — sufficient for food/pharma recalls but not for individual component history. RFID tracks location, not product history.
Q14
Cycle Counting
A logistics company replaces its annual physical inventory count with a cycle counting program. Which advantage does cycle counting provide that the annual count CANNOT?
Key point: Cycle counting's three advantages over annual counts: (1) Frequency — errors caught sooner, so root causes are easier to identify and fix; (2) No operational shutdown — the warehouse keeps running while a subset of items is counted daily/weekly; (3) ABC prioritization — A items (highest value/movement) are counted most frequently, where errors have the greatest financial impact. Annual counts treat all items equally and require a costly, disruptive shutdown.
Q15
Inventory Record Accuracy
According to ASCM best practice, effective MRP planning requires inventory record accuracy to be at or above what minimum threshold?
Key point: The industry benchmark is ≥95% inventory record accuracy for reliable MRP. Below 95%, the system records diverge too much from physical reality. MRP plans based on what the system thinks is available — not what is actually there. This creates phantom inventory (system shows stock that doesn't exist → shortages) or missed available inventory → excess orders. Both destroy MRP's value.
Q16
ABC Analysis in Cycle Counting
Under an ABC-based cycle counting program, which items should be counted LEAST frequently?
Key point: ABC cycle counting frequency follows Pareto logic — invest audit effort where it matters most financially. A items: top 20% of items representing ~80% of inventory value → counted most frequently (weekly or monthly). B items: monthly to quarterly. C items: lowest value (bottom ~50% of SKUs but only ~5% of total value) → counted least frequently (quarterly to annually). An error on a C item costs little; an error on an A item can seriously disrupt operations.
Q17
Inventory Disposition
A food manufacturer discovers 10,000 units of a product that have passed their expiration date. They cannot be sold, returned to the supplier, donated, or repurposed. Which inventory disposition option applies?
Key point: When no recovery option is possible (expired, unsaleable, no return/rework path), the only remaining disposition is write-off and disposal. The five CSCP disposition options in value-recovery order are: (1) Return to supplier, (2) Rework/remanufacture, (3) Donate, (4) Sell as scrap/secondary market, (5) Write off and dispose. Prevention is better than cure: shorter order quantities, better forecasting, and regular slow-mover reviews reduce obsolescence risk.
Section 4 · Performance Metrics & Continuous Improvement (Q18–Q25)
Q18
Inventory Turnover
A company reports annual COGS of €6 million and maintains average inventory of €500,000. What is the inventory turnover?
Calculation: Inventory Turnover = COGS ÷ Average Inventory = €6,000,000 ÷ €500,000 = 12 times per year. This means the company sells and replenishes its average inventory 12 times per year — approximately every 30 days. Higher turnover = more efficient use of inventory investment. Note: use COGS (not sales revenue) — sales includes profit margin which would overstate efficiency.
Q19
Cash-to-Cash Cycle Time
A company has the following metrics: Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) = 45 days, Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) = 30 days, Days Payable Outstanding (DPO) = 60 days. What is the cash-to-cash (C2C) cycle time?
Calculation: C2C = DIO + DSO − DPO = 45 + 30 − 60 = 15 days. The company collects from customers relatively quickly (DIO 45 + DSO 30 = 75 days from inventory purchase to cash receipt) AND has 60 days to pay suppliers — so it only needs to finance 15 days of operations with its own cash. A negative C2C (like Amazon or Walmart) means the company collects cash before paying suppliers — the supply chain funds itself.
Q20
Days of Supply
A distribution company has average inventory valued at €900,000 and annual COGS of €3,285,000. How many days of supply does current inventory represent?
Calculation: Daily COGS = €3,285,000 ÷ 365 = €9,000/day. Days of Supply = Average Inventory ÷ Daily COGS = €900,000 ÷ €9,000 = 100 days. This tells managers: at the current consumption rate, existing inventory will last 100 days before stockout. Used to set reorder points, evaluate safety stock adequacy, and identify potential overstock or understock situations.
Q21
Inventory & Return on Assets
A company has net income of €2M and total assets of €20M (including €8M of inventory), giving ROA = 10%. After a lean initiative, inventory is reduced by €2M (net income unchanged). What is the new ROA?
Calculation: ROA = Net Income ÷ Total Assets. New total assets = €20M − €2M = €18M. New ROA = €2M ÷ €18M = 11.1%. This illustrates a key linkage: reducing excess inventory lowers total assets → improves ROA (with same income). It also reduces DIO → shorter C2C → better cash flow. This is why lean inventory management has a direct financial statement impact.
Q22
MRP Inputs
Which of the following are the THREE inputs required to run Material Requirements Planning (MRP)?
Key point: MRP's three mandatory inputs: (1) MPS — what finished goods to produce and when; (2) BOM (Bill of Materials) — the recipe: what components/raw materials are needed per unit at each level; (3) Inventory records — what's already on hand or on order. MRP combines these to answer: what to order, in what quantity, and when. Options A and C describe EOQ/safety stock inputs; D describes S&OP inputs.
Q23
PDCA Cycle
After identifying a recurring defect in a packaging process, a quality team designs a corrective process change and implements it on ONE of five packaging lines for 30 days. The 30-day trial on the single line represents which PDCA phase?
Key point: The PDCA sequence: Plan (analyze root cause, design solution) → Do (implement on a small scale / pilot) → Check (measure results vs. expected) → Act (standardize if successful, or repeat cycle). Implementing on ONE line for 30 days = Do (small-scale pilot). If the question said "measure defect rates after the 30-day pilot," that would be Check. If it said "roll out to all 5 lines," that would be Act.
Q24
Lean 8 Wastes (TIMWOODS)
At a manufacturing facility, production workers spend significant time walking to a centralized tool storage room at the far end of the floor every hour to collect tools they use constantly. Under Lean's TIMWOODS framework, this waste is BEST classified as:
Key point: TIMWOODS distinction: Transportation = moving MATERIALS/PRODUCTS unnecessarily. Motion = unnecessary movement of PEOPLE. Workers walking = Motion. The Lean solution is 5S — specifically "Set in order" (Seiton): place tools at the point of use, within arm's reach of where they are needed, eliminating the walk entirely. A common exam trap is confusing Transportation (goods move) with Motion (people move).
Q25
Lean & Financial Performance — Scenario
Scenario: GlobalParts Co. has annual COGS of €6 million and average inventory of €2 million, giving an inventory turnover of 3×. After a lean improvement initiative — tighter replenishment, improved forecasting, and cycle counting — average inventory is reduced by €500,000.
What is GlobalParts Co.'s new inventory turnover after the lean initiative?
Calculation: New average inventory = €2,000,000 − €500,000 = €1,500,000. New Inventory Turnover = COGS ÷ Average Inventory = €6,000,000 ÷ €1,500,000 = 4×. COGS is unchanged (same sales volume), but the denominator shrinks → turnover improves. This also reduces DIO (365 ÷ 4 = 91 days vs. 365 ÷ 3 = 122 days), shortening cash-to-cash cycle by 31 days. Lean improvements directly and measurably improve financial metrics.
