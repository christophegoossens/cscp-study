Section 1 · Module 1 Review — SC Foundations, Demand & Forecasting (Q1–Q7)
Q1
Make-vs-Buy / Vertical Integration
A manufacturing company is deciding whether to continue making a component in-house or outsource it to a specialist supplier. In which situation is OUTSOURCING the MOST strategically appropriate choice?
Key point: Outsourcing is strategically justified when (1) the activity is non-core — it doesn't differentiate the company competitively, and (2) a specialist can do it better or cheaper. The primary reason to keep an activity in-house (vertical integration) is when it IS a core competency or when control is critical. Excess internal capacity (option D) is an argument to insource, not outsource.
Q2
Demand Signals & Bullwhip Effect
A retailer begins sharing its daily point-of-sale (POS) data directly with its upstream manufacturer, instead of communicating only through weekly purchase orders. What is the PRIMARY benefit of this change?
Key point: One of the four main causes of the Bullwhip Effect is demand signal distortion — each tier orders based on what the tier below it orders rather than actual end-consumer demand. By sharing POS data, the manufacturer sees true demand and can plan accordingly. This is one of the most effective remedies for the Bullwhip Effect, and forms the basis of CPFR. Note: POS sharing alone does not create VMI — VMI is a formal replenishment responsibility transfer.
Q3
Aggregate Planning — Level Strategy
A toy manufacturer faces near-zero demand in January–April and peak demand in November–December each year. A LEVEL aggregate planning strategy would MOST LIKELY result in:
Key point: Level strategy = constant output rate, stable workforce, inventory absorbs the gap. The trade-off: lower labor costs (no hiring/firing) but higher inventory carrying costs. Chase strategy = adjust workforce/output to match demand — lower inventory but higher labor flexibility costs. Option A describes chase. Option B describes a mixed approach with overtime. Level strategy favors industries where hiring/firing is very expensive or workforce stability is critical.
Q4
Forecasting Principles
Which of the following statements about forecasting is INCORRECT according to ASCM principles?
Key point: Option A is the incorrect statement. ASCM explicitly warns against basing forecasts on purchase orders rather than market demand — orders are a distorted signal (Bullwhip Effect). Forecasts should be based on demand, not orders. The other three statements are correct ASCM principles: (B) shorter horizon = more accurate; (C) aggregate = more accurate due to error cancellation; (D) always include an error estimate.
Q5
Bullwhip Effect — Remedies
A 4-tier supply chain (retailer → distributor → manufacturer → raw material supplier) is experiencing severe demand amplification. Which single action would MOST effectively reduce the Bullwhip Effect across ALL tiers simultaneously?
Key point: The four main Bullwhip causes are: demand signal distortion, order batching, price fluctuations, and the rationing game. Option B directly attacks the root cause — demand signal distortion. Options A and C actually worsen the Bullwhip: more safety stock amplifies the signal, and less frequent ordering creates bigger batch spikes. Option D (price promotions) is actually one of the causes of the Bullwhip — promotions trigger forward buying and demand spikes.
Q6
Tracking Signal — Calculation & Interpretation
Over 5 consecutive periods, the forecast errors (Actual − Forecast) were: +10, +8, +12, +6, +4. The MAD is 8. What is the tracking signal, and what action should the planner take?
Calculation: Cumulative Error = +10 + 8 + 12 + 6 + 4 = +40. Tracking Signal = Cumulative Error ÷ MAD = 40 ÷ 8 = +5.0. A positive TS means actuals consistently exceed the forecast (underestimating). At +5.0, this is approaching the ±4 to ±6 alert threshold AND all 5 errors are positive — no oscillation — which independently signals systematic positive bias. The planner should revise the forecast upward. Note: MAD = (10+8+12+6+4)/5 = 40/5 = 8 — confirming the given value.
Q7
Planning Hierarchy — DRP vs MRP
A regional distribution center manager wants to determine how much finished product to replenish from the central warehouse over the next 8 weeks, accounting for current stock, safety stock targets, forecast demand, and replenishment lead times. Which planning tool is MOST appropriate?
Key point: DRP is the distribution equivalent of MRP — it calculates when and how much to replenish at each DC node, factoring in demand forecasts, safety stock, and lead times. MRP calculates material component requirements using the BOM for manufacturing. RCCP validates the MPS against key resources. S&OP operates at product family level. The planning flow: S&OP → MPS → MRP (manufacturing side) / DRP (distribution side).
Section 2 · Module 2 Review — Global Networks, IT, Metrics & SCOR (Q8–Q14)
Q8
Efficient vs Responsive Supply Chain
A generic pharmaceutical company produces aspirin and ibuprofen for major retail pharmacy chains under long-term private-label contracts. Demand is highly predictable, products are standardized, and competition is primarily on price per tablet. Which supply chain design is MOST appropriate?
Key point: The efficient vs. responsive decision is driven by demand uncertainty and required inventory volume. Generic pharmaceuticals have stable, predictable demand and compete on cost — a textbook case for an efficient supply chain (high capacity utilization, lean inventory, minimal waste). Agile and leagile suit volatile demand and short product lifecycles. Resilience is a separate strategic dimension — valid for supply risk management but doesn't change the fundamental efficiency orientation.
Q9
IT Systems — APS
A complex discrete manufacturer needs to simultaneously optimize production scheduling across 6 facilities, respecting material constraints, machine capacity limits, and customer due-date priorities. Standard ERP scheduling modules cannot solve this multi-constraint problem efficiently. Which system is MOST appropriate?
Key point — system map: APS sits on top of ERP to add optimization capabilities — it uses algorithms (constraint-based, linear programming) to generate feasible, optimized schedules across complex multi-facility environments. ERP has good transaction management but limited optimization. WMS manages warehouse operations. TMS manages transportation. This distinction — ERP for transactions vs. APS for optimization — is frequently tested on the CSCP exam.
Q10
SCOR Performance Attributes
A supply chain team measures "upside supply chain flexibility" — defined as the number of days required to achieve an unplanned 20% increase in production output from current levels. Which SCOR performance attribute does this metric PRIMARILY measure?
Key point — SCOR 5 attributes: (1) Reliability = Perfect Order Fulfillment. (2) Responsiveness = Order Fulfillment Cycle Time (speed for a standard order). (3) Agility = ability to respond to unplanned/unusual changes — upside flexibility and downside adaptability. (4) Costs = SC cost as % of revenue. (5) Asset Management = C2C, inventory turns, ROA. "Upside flexibility" is a classic SCOR agility metric — it measures how adaptable (not just how fast) the supply chain is.
Q11
SCOR — Enable Process
In the SCOR model, which of the following activities BEST represents the "Enable" process?
Key point — SCOR process map: Plan = demand/supply balancing. Source = supplier mgmt, purchasing. Make = manufacturing, testing, release (Option C). Deliver = order mgmt, outbound, last mile. Return = reverse logistics (Option D). Enable = the foundational layer — governance, IT systems, data standards, people, contracts, and policies that make all other processes possible. It "enables" the rest of the model. Option B describes DRP, which falls under Deliver/Plan.
Q12
Financial Metrics — Gross Margin
A company reports annual Revenue of $5,000,000 and Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) of $3,500,000. What is the gross margin?
Calculation: Gross Margin = (Revenue − COGS) ÷ Revenue × 100 = ($5M − $3.5M) ÷ $5M = $1.5M ÷ $5M = 30%. Option A (70%) is COGS as a % of revenue — the inverse. Option D is the gross profit in dollars, not the gross margin percentage. Supply chain managers must connect COGS-reduction initiatives to gross margin improvement — every dollar saved in direct materials or production costs flows directly to gross profit.
Q13
Cash-to-Cash Improvement
A supply chain team wants to improve its Cash-to-Cash Cycle Time (currently 90 days). They are evaluating three separate actions, each requiring equal effort. Which action produces the LARGEST reduction in C2C?
Key point: C2C = DIO + DSO − DPO. Each action's impact: (A) Reduce DSO by 10 → C2C improves by 10 days. (B) Reduce DIO by 15 → C2C improves by 15 days. (C) Extend DPO by 30 → C2C improves by 30 days — the largest single impact. (D) Increasing safety stock INCREASES DIO, making C2C WORSE. Companies like Amazon famously achieve negative C2C partly by extending DPO to 90+ days. This is why supplier payment terms are a key working capital lever.
Q14
Fill Rate vs Perfect Order Fulfillment
A company reports a 99% fill rate but only a 91% Perfect Order Fulfillment rate. Which explanation BEST accounts for the gap between these two metrics?
Key point: Fill rate = % of orders shipped complete on first shipment (quantity only). Perfect Order Fulfillment = % of orders that are complete AND on time AND damage-free AND correctly documented — ALL four simultaneously. An order can achieve 99% fill rate (right quantities) but still fail Perfect Order if 8% are late, damaged, or misdocumented. This is why Perfect Order is always a lower number than fill rate — it multiplies multiple performance dimensions together. Perfect Order is the SCOR composite reliability metric.
Section 3 · Integration & Mixed Topics (Q15–Q20)
Q15
Demand Patterns + Forecasting Method Selection
A consumer electronics company finds that its wireless headphone line has experienced a consistent upward trend over 18 months, with recurring sales spikes in Q4 each year. Which forecasting approach BEST captures BOTH of these demand patterns?
Key point: When demand has both a trend and seasonality, neither a simple moving average (which ignores both) nor naive forecasting (which ignores trend) is adequate. The correct approach is a model that explicitly decomposes demand into: (1) base level, (2) trend, and (3) seasonal component. Holt's double exponential smoothing captures trend; adding seasonal indices (Holt-Winters) captures seasonality too. With 18 months of data available, qualitative methods are no longer needed — quantitative models are now more appropriate.
Q16
Leagile SC Strategy + Technology
A sports apparel company uses a leagile supply chain: plain base garments are mass-produced efficiently in Asia and held at a European DC. Final customization (team printing, branding) is done only after receiving actual retailer orders. Which combination of IT systems BEST supports this end-to-end leagile structure?
Key point: The leagile model has two distinct zones requiring different IT support: (1) Upstream (lean/push): ERP to plan and control efficient mass production. (2) DC holding point: WMS to manage the semi-finished goods buffer. (3) Downstream (agile/pull): TMS to execute rapid, flexible outbound delivery in response to actual orders. Option C maps one system to each zone of the leagile model. Option D (CPFR) supports collaborative forecasting but doesn't address the operational execution stack. Option A addresses traceability, not the core leagile execution need.
Q17
Bullwhip Effect + SCOR
Reducing the Bullwhip Effect is PRIMARILY addressed by which SCOR process, and through what mechanism?
Key point: The Bullwhip Effect is fundamentally a planning and information problem — demand signals are distorted as they travel upstream. The SCOR Plan process directly addresses this: it encompasses demand planning, supply planning, and balancing the two. Bullwhip remedies (sharing POS data, reducing order batching, collaborative forecasting, eliminating artificial promotions) are all planning interventions. Make and Source adaptations can help operationally but don't eliminate the root cause — distorted demand signals in the planning process.
Q18
S&OP + IBP — Process Failure
A company has a mature S&OP process that consistently produces an operational consensus plan. However, the CFO repeatedly rejects it because the plan does not align with the approved annual financial budget. The MOST likely root cause is:
Key point: The CFO rejecting the S&OP plan due to financial misalignment is the classic symptom of immature financial integration in S&OP. In a traditional S&OP, finance participates as a reviewer; in IBP (Integrated Business Planning), financial modeling is embedded throughout the process — every plan is simultaneously evaluated for P&L, cash flow, and balance sheet impact. The progression: S&OP (operational consensus) → IBP (operational + financial + strategic alignment). This gap between operations and finance is one of the primary reasons companies advance to IBP.
Q19
VMI — Impact on SCOR Metrics
A supplier takes over inventory management at a customer's warehouse under a VMI agreement. On-shelf availability at the customer improves from 91% to 97%, and the customer's average inventory holding drops by 25%. Which SCOR performance attributes improve MOST directly for the CUSTOMER?
Key point: VMI improves two customer SCOR attributes: (1) Reliability — the supplier, with real-time visibility into the customer's stock, replenishes proactively → higher fill rate and fewer stockouts. (2) Asset Management — in most VMI agreements the supplier owns the inventory until it is consumed, so the customer's DIO (Days Inventory Outstanding) decreases → lower C2C cycle time and higher ROA. Option D is wrong: in true VMI the supplier retains ownership until consumption — the customer's balance sheet asset (inventory) actually decreases.
Q20
Leagile + SCOR Processes
In a leagile supply chain, the Customer Order Decoupling Point (CODP) marks the transition from lean/push to agile/pull. From a SCOR model perspective, which two processes are MOST directly split by the CODP?
Key point: The CODP is the point in the supply chain where the mode switches from push (forecast-driven) to pull (order-driven). In SCOR terms: the Make process upstream of the CODP is executed based on forecasts (lean, efficient). The Deliver process downstream is triggered by actual customer orders (agile, responsive). The CODP itself is often a semi-finished goods buffer (strategic inventory). Option D (Source and Make) is wrong because both Source and Make are typically on the upstream/lean side — the split occurs between Make and Deliver.
Section 4 · Advanced Scenarios (Q21–Q25)
Q21
Advanced Scenario — Inventory Turns vs Service Level
Scenario: MediSupply has achieved 12 inventory turns per year — well above the industry average of 8 turns. However, its Perfect Order Fulfillment rate has fallen to 78% against an industry benchmark of 95%. Leadership proposes further reducing inventory to push turns to 15.
What is the MOST LIKELY diagnosis, and what action should be recommended?
Key point: Inventory turns and service level are in tension — there is no free lunch. At 12 turns (vs. industry 8), MediSupply has likely cut inventory below the safety stock threshold for some items, causing frequent stockouts that drive down Perfect Order. Pushing to 15 turns would worsen this. The correct action is an SKU-level service-level analysis: identify which items are stocking out, calculate their appropriate safety stock given lead time and demand variability, and target lean inventory where demand is stable while maintaining adequate buffers for critical items. This balances efficiency and reliability — the classic SCOR Costs vs. Reliability trade-off.
Q22
Advanced Scenario — Planning Hierarchy & Mix
Scenario: TechCo's S&OP produces an accurate product family forecast of 10,000 units/month for its "Smart Home" family. The MPS team disaggregates this into: Model A = 5,000 units, Model B = 3,000, Model C = 2,000. Actual consumer demand reveals Model A at 7,000 units while B and C together total only 3,000 — the family aggregate was correct, but the mix was wrong.
What is the MOST appropriate corrective action?
Key point: This scenario illustrates the distinction between aggregate accuracy and mix accuracy. The S&OP aggregate forecast (10,000 units) was correct — so the S&OP process itself is working. The error is in how the MPS team disaggregates the family total into individual SKUs. The fix is at the MPS level: improve the mix forecasting assumptions (possibly using POS data per SKU, price elasticity, or causal variables). Option D (CPFR) helps get better data but wouldn't directly fix the disaggregation logic. Option B reduces the aggregate — wrong, since the aggregate was right. Option C confuses the supply strategy with the planning problem.
Q23
Advanced Scenario — Resilience Investment Analysis
Scenario: GlobalPharma single-sources a critical API from one supplier in Southeast Asia. A typhoon disrupted the plant for 6 weeks, causing $25M in lost sales. Management now proposes: (1) qualify a second supplier (+$800K/year), and (2) maintain 8 weeks of safety stock of the API (+$2M/year in inventory carrying costs). Total annual resilience cost: $2.8M.
Which statement BEST evaluates whether this resilience investment is justified?
Key point: Resilience investments are evaluated like insurance: Annual Cost vs. (Probability × Impact). With a $25M event, the breakeven is: $25M ÷ $2.8M ≈ 8.9 years — meaning if a similar disruption could realistically occur once per decade or more frequently, the investment is financially justified. Southeast Asia faces typhoon risk annually, so this threshold is not unreasonable. Option C is technically correct in principle but avoids the answer — the data IS sufficient to frame the decision. Single-sourcing for efficiency at the expense of resilience is a classic supply chain risk trade-off that the CSCP exam tests from multiple angles.
Q24
Advanced Scenario — Technology Gap Analysis
Scenario: A mid-size consumer goods company has an ERP system managing finance, purchasing, and manufacturing. However: (1) once goods leave the factory, there is zero visibility into outbound shipment status until delivery; (2) the distribution center tracks inventory using shared Excel spreadsheets; and (3) suppliers send demand forecast input via email, making collaborative planning impossible.
Which technology roadmap MOST completely addresses ALL THREE gaps?
Key point — system-to-gap mapping: Gap 1 (no shipment visibility after factory) → TMS: real-time carrier tracking, freight visibility. Gap 2 (Excel at DC) → WMS: warehouse operations (receiving, put-away, picking, inventory location management). Gap 3 (email-based supplier collaboration) → CPFR platform or EDI portal: structured collaborative forecasting and replenishment. Option C is the only one that addresses all three gaps with the right tools. Option B misses the DC gap. Options A and D address manufacturing problems that weren't described.
Q25
Advanced Scenario — Full M1 + M2 Integration
Scenario: GlobalConnect's supply chain review reveals: Inventory turns = 4/year (industry: 8). Cash-to-Cash Cycle Time = 92 days (industry: 45 days). Perfect Order Fulfillment = 88% (target: 95%). Tracking signal has been at +4.8 consistently for 3 months (all errors positive). S&OP aggregate forecasts are accurate; however, SKU-level forecasts show high error and the product mix is frequently wrong.
Which diagnosis and integrated action plan is MOST comprehensive and accurate?
Key point — integrated diagnosis: This scenario requires connecting all the data: (1) Tracking signal +4.8, consistent, all-positive → systematic positive bias at SKU level (underestimating specific SKUs) even though aggregate S&OP is accurate → fix mix disaggregation in MPS. (2) 4 turns vs. 8 benchmark, 92-day C2C → strongly suggests excess overall inventory (high DIO) OR very long payment terms → lean inventory and/or negotiate DPO extension. (3) 88% Perfect Order despite excess aggregate inventory → inventory is likely concentrated in the wrong SKUs (mix problem) → rebalance after fixing mix forecast. Option D is wrong — the data shows excess inventory (high turns benchmark gap) which suggests DIO is the likely C2C driver, not DSO. Option A oversimplifies: fixing forecast alone won't reduce inventory already built up or immediately fix supplier terms. Option C is irrelevant — the problems are management/process issues, not a structural outsourcing case.
