Section 1 · Strategic Sourcing & Supplier Performance (Q1–Q7)
Q1
Strategic Sourcing Process
In the 7-step strategic sourcing process, what activity takes place in Step 3?
Key point: The 7-step sequence is: (1) Category profile → (2) Supply market analysis → (3) Strategy development → (4) RFI/RFP → (5) Supplier evaluation → (6) Negotiation and selection → (7) Relationship management. Step 3 (Strategy development) determines the RIGHT approach for the category before going to market — the analysis in Steps 1 and 2 informs what that strategy should be. This is a frequently tested sequence on the CSCP exam.
Q2
Make vs. Buy / Core Competency
A pharmaceutical company is considering outsourcing its proprietary drug synthesis process to a lower-cost contract manufacturer. Which strategic principle MOST strongly argues against this decision?
Key point: Drug synthesis is the pharmaceutical company's core competency — what makes it uniquely valuable and difficult for competitors to imitate. Outsourcing it transfers the capability to a contract manufacturer who may serve competitors, and allows the internal team's skills to atrophy. Once lost, core competencies are extremely difficult to rebuild. The rule: outsource non-core activities (logistics, facilities, IT infrastructure) to specialists — never outsource your source of competitive advantage.
Q3
Spend Analysis
A procurement team's spend analysis reveals that 8 departments are independently purchasing identical MRO supplies from 14 different suppliers at widely varying prices, with no coordinated contracts. The MOST appropriate first response is:
Key point: This scenario describes classic maverick spending — purchases made outside of negotiated contracts. The spend analysis reveals a consolidation opportunity: many departments buying the same category from many different suppliers at suboptimal prices. The solution is to develop a category strategy — consolidate volume to preferred suppliers, negotiate preferred pricing and terms, and establish compliant procurement channels. Immediate elimination of all suppliers to one (Option B) skips the competitive evaluation step and creates concentration risk.
Q4
RFI vs. RFP vs. RFQ
A logistics director wants to understand what third-party logistics (3PL) providers exist in the market and what capabilities they offer — before defining the company's outsourcing requirements. Which document should be issued at this stage?
Key point: RFI (Request for Information) = early-stage market survey. Used when requirements are not yet defined and the buyer wants to understand the supply market. No commitment. RFP (Request for Proposal) = ask suppliers to propose solutions and indicative pricing — used when requirements are defined and solution quality matters. RFQ (Request for Quote) = ask for a specific price for a clearly defined specification — used for commodities where specs are fixed and price drives selection. Sequence: RFI → RFP → RFQ as the process narrows.
Q5
Supplier Scorecard — QCDS
A supplier scorecard uses the QCDS framework with equal weighting across dimensions. A supplier scores 95/100 for Quality, 88/100 for Delivery, 72/100 for Cost, and 90/100 for Service. In the next Quarterly Business Review (QBR), the improvement discussion should focus PRIMARILY on:
Key point: The supplier scorecard is an objective, multi-dimensional tool — and the QBR should address the largest gaps first. With equal weighting, Cost at 72/100 has the biggest gap (28 points below perfect vs. 5, 12, and 10 for the others). The QCDS framework (Quality, Cost, Delivery, Service) is designed to prevent buyers from focusing only on price — but when cost is the largest measured gap, it deserves priority attention. The scorecard removes subjectivity from that prioritization decision.
Q6
PPM Calculation
A supplier delivers 800,000 components per year. Quality inspection identifies 56 defective units across all deliveries. What is the supplier's defect rate in PPM (Parts Per Million)?
Calculation: PPM = (Defective units ÷ Total units received) × 1,000,000 = (56 ÷ 800,000) × 1,000,000 = 0.00007 × 1,000,000 = 70 PPM. PPM is used instead of percentage for high-volume components because percentages are too coarse — 70 PPM = 0.007%, which sounds negligibly small, but for a plant running millions of assemblies, it generates significant defect counts. Six Sigma quality = 3.4 PPM. A common exam trap is confusing the raw defect count (56) with the PPM rate (70).
Q7
Corrective Action Request (CAR)
A buyer issues a Corrective Action Request (CAR) to a supplier after defective seals caused a production line shutdown. According to quality management best practices, the supplier's IMMEDIATE first step upon receiving the CAR should be:
Key point: The CAR process follows a strict sequence: (1) Containment first — stop the bleeding; prevent more defective product from reaching the buyer. (2) Root cause investigation. (3) Permanent corrective action implementation. (4) Verification of effectiveness. (5) Documentation and submission to buyer. Containment must come FIRST because while root cause analysis takes time, every day without containment means more defective parts potentially entering the buyer's process. The supplier's speed and quality of response to a CAR is itself a service performance metric.
Section 2 · Supply Chain Relationship Management (Q8–Q10)
Q8
Integration Types
Two competing beverage manufacturers agree to share distribution trucks and warehousing infrastructure in rural regions where neither has sufficient volume to justify dedicated assets. This arrangement is BEST described as:
Key point: Horizontal integration = collaboration between companies at the SAME level of the supply chain (two manufacturers, two retailers, two distributors). It enables scale benefits (shared fixed costs, better asset utilization) without the capital and complexity of vertical integration. Distinguish from vertical integration (owning different levels: manufacturer owns a retailer). The beverage companies remain competitors in their market but cooperate on non-core distribution logistics — a classic horizontal collaboration pattern.
Q9
Information Asymmetry — Scenario
Scenario: A retail buyer has real-time POS data showing a 30% demand surge for a seasonal product. To protect its negotiating position, the buyer does not share this information with its key supplier. The supplier, using only historical order data, plans production based on prior-year patterns.
What is the MOST LIKELY outcome of this information asymmetry?
Key point: Information asymmetry — one party holds demand information the other doesn't — is a root cause of the Bullwhip Effect. The buyer's withholding creates a gap between what the supplier plans and what is actually needed. Result: stockout at the buyer, emergency orders, production scramble at the supplier, and amplified volatility throughout the chain. The short-term negotiating advantage is vastly outweighed by supply disruption costs. Solution: collaborative forecasting (CPFR), VMI, or shared demand signal platforms — so both parties plan from the same data.
Q10
SC Partnership Foundations
According to supply chain relationship management principles, the MOST important foundation for a productive long-term strategic supply chain partnership is:
Key point: Sustainable strategic partnerships require: (1) Complementary capabilities — each party brings something the other lacks; (2) Aligned goals — both benefit when the partnership succeeds; (3) Trust — willingness to share sensitive information; (4) Mutual dependency — balanced power. Power-based relationships (Option A) typically produce short-term wins for the dominant party but long-term fragility — the weaker party under-invests, withholds information, and exits at the first opportunity. Purely price-driven relationships don't create conditions for collaborative value creation.
Section 3 · Risk Management Overview & Identification (Q11–Q17)
Q11
SCRM Definition
Supply Chain Risk Management (SCRM) is BEST described as:
Key point: The SCRM definition covers the full risk management cycle: Identify → Assess → Treat/Respond → Monitor → Review. It is NOT about eliminating all disruptions (impossible) — it is about building the capability to anticipate, adapt, and recover (resilience). The CSCP exam distinguishes SCRM's broad scope from narrower tools like supplier audits or contract management. Key output: supply chain resilience — the ability to absorb disruptions and return to normal operations with minimal customer impact.
Q12
Risk Categories
A consumer electronics company sources 80% of its components from a single manufacturing cluster in a coastal region. A typhoon causes severe flooding, shutting down all local factories for 6 weeks. Which supply chain risk category BEST describes this event?
Key point: Environmental risk = disruptions from natural events (earthquakes, floods, hurricanes, climate change impacts). This scenario also illustrates geographic concentration risk — 80% sourcing from a single region amplifies the typhoon's impact. While the event also has financial consequences (Option B), the primary risk category by cause is environmental. The mitigation is geographic diversification — qualifying suppliers in different regions so no single natural event can disable the majority of supply. This was a key lesson from the 2011 Tohoku earthquake disruptions.
Q13
Efficiency vs. Resilience Trade-off
COVID-19 revealed critical vulnerabilities in highly optimized supply chains. Which statement MOST accurately captures the core lesson regarding the efficiency-resilience trade-off?
Key point: The efficiency-resilience trade-off: lean JIT supply chains minimize cost by eliminating buffers — but create fragility. Any disruption hits with no buffer capacity or alternative options. COVID-19 exposed this: highly optimized single-source, zero-inventory supply chains (especially in auto, electronics, healthcare) collapsed when disruptions hit simultaneously across multiple nodes. The CSCP lesson: strategic redundancy is the correct framing — it is risk management investment, not inefficiency. The optimal level depends on the disruption probability × consequence of service failure.
Q14
Risk Register
Which of the following MOST accurately describes a risk register that delivers real value to a supply chain organization?
Key point: A risk register is only valuable if it is: (1) Complete — all identified risks included; (2) Current — regularly updated as supply chains, markets, and geopolitics change; (3) Owned — each risk has an accountable person driving mitigation; (4) Actionable — drives mitigation investment and contingency planning, not just documentation. A one-time annual document becomes stale within weeks. New suppliers added, geopolitical shifts, new product launches — all change the risk profile. Without regular review, the risk register becomes a filing exercise.
Q15
FMEA — Risk Priority Number
In Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA), the Risk Priority Number (RPN) is calculated as:
Key point: RPN = Severity × Occurrence × Detection. Each factor is rated 1–10: Severity = how bad is the effect if the failure occurs; Occurrence = how frequently does this failure mode happen; Detection = how likely is it to be caught BEFORE causing harm (10 = impossible to detect). Highest RPNs receive priority mitigation investment. Note: "Probability × Impact" (Option A) is the Expected Value formula — different tool, different purpose. Detection is what makes FMEA distinct — a hard-to-detect risk gets a higher RPN even at low severity/occurrence.
Q16
Supply Chain Mapping — Tier Risk
A global automotive manufacturer has qualified two separate Tier 1 suppliers for a critical microcontroller, believing it has eliminated single-source risk. An audit later reveals that both Tier 1 suppliers source the same semiconductor wafers from a single Tier 2 supplier. This situation BEST illustrates:
Key point: This is the classic multi-tier supply chain risk trap — and the core lesson of COVID-19's semiconductor shortage. The auto company correctly dual-sourced at Tier 1. But because both Tier 1 suppliers share a common Tier 2 supplier, they are not actually independent. If that Tier 2 supplier fails, BOTH Tier 1 suppliers fail simultaneously — worse than single-sourcing because the manufacturer believed it was protected. Solution: supply chain mapping must extend to Tier 2 and Tier 3 to reveal these hidden shared dependencies. "Visible" dual sourcing can mask invisible single-source risk.
Q17
PESTLE Analysis
A global freight forwarder's supply chain team is analyzing external factors that could impact its operations. New carbon emission regulations in the EU are forcing a structural shift from air to sea freight for certain categories. Under PESTLE analysis, this risk factor is classified as:
Key point: PESTLE = Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental. Carbon emission regulations fall under both Environmental (climate change driving the regulation) and Legal (the regulatory mandate itself). On the exam, when a question involves government-imposed environmental rules, the primary PESTLE category is Environmental/Legal. PESTLE is a macro-environment scanning tool — it identifies external factors the supply chain cannot control but must monitor and plan for. It feeds the risk identification step of the SCRM process, catching strategic risks at the regulatory and market level.
Section 4 · Risk Analysis & Risk Responses (Q18–Q25)
Q18
Risk Matrix Quadrants
A risk matrix assessment places a supply chain risk in the HIGH probability / LOW impact quadrant. According to risk matrix best practices, the MOST appropriate response strategy is:
Key point: The four risk matrix quadrant responses: HIGH probability + HIGH impact = Critical — mitigate urgently. LOW probability + HIGH impact = Monitor — contingency plans. HIGH probability + LOW impact = Reduce — process improvement. LOW probability + LOW impact = Accept — monitor only. High probability / low impact risks are typically recurring operational issues (minor quality defects, occasional delivery delays) — the right response is process improvement to reduce their frequency, not transfer to insurance (wasteful) or immediate escalation as critical (over-reaction).
Q19
Expected Value Calculation
A risk analyst estimates a 20% probability that a key supplier's production capacity will be disrupted during peak season, causing $750,000 in lost revenue and emergency sourcing costs. The Expected Value (EV) of this risk is:
Calculation: EV = Probability × Financial Impact = 0.20 × $750,000 = $150,000. Expected value converts risk into a comparable financial basis for prioritization. Practical application: any mitigation costing less than $150,000 that eliminates this risk entirely has a positive ROI. A $50,000 dual-sourcing qualification program that reduces the probability from 20% to 5% reduces EV from $150,000 to $37,500 — a $112,500 expected benefit for $50,000 investment (2.25:1 ROI). EV doesn't say the risk WILL cost $150K — it's the probability-weighted expected cost.
Q20
Inherent vs. Residual Risk
A supply chain manager originally scored a critical component supply risk at 4 (probability) × 5 (impact) = 20 (high). After implementing dual sourcing and maintaining 6 weeks of safety stock, the re-assessed score drops to 2 × 3 = 6. The score of 6 represents:
Key point: Inherent risk (score of 20) = raw exposure BEFORE any mitigation. Residual risk (score of 6) = what remains AFTER mitigations are applied. The risk management process: Identify inherent risk → Apply mitigations → Assess residual risk → Compare to risk appetite → Decide if sufficient. If residual risk ≤ risk appetite → accept and monitor. If residual risk > risk appetite → implement additional mitigations. The goal is not to eliminate all risk (impossible and uneconomic) but to reduce residual risk to within the organization's acceptable threshold.
Q21
4 T's of Risk Response
A consumer goods company identifies that new import tariffs in a major market could make its products uncompetitive. The company decides to shift its sales and distribution focus to other markets and reduce its dependency on the affected country. This risk response is BEST classified as:
Key point: The 4 T's of risk treatment: Tolerate/Accept (live with the risk — monitor only); Treat/Reduce (mitigate probability or impact — dual source, safety stock); Transfer (shift financial consequence — insurance, hedging, contract clauses); Terminate/Avoid (stop the risky activity entirely). Shifting focus away from the affected market = Terminate/Avoid — the company is reducing its exposure to the geopolitical risk by exiting or reducing presence in that risk context. It doesn't eliminate all risk but avoids the specific risk source.
Q22
Dual Sourcing Trade-offs
An aerospace company sources a specialized titanium alloy from a single supplier. To reduce supply concentration risk, it qualifies a second supplier and allocates 70% of volume to the primary and 30% to the secondary. The PRIMARY trade-off of this dual-sourcing strategy is:
Key point: Dual sourcing benefits: eliminates single point of failure; maintains competitive pressure on the primary supplier. Key trade-off: splitting volume reduces leverage with each supplier (especially the primary, who may lose preferred pricing at lower volumes) and creates a commitment risk with the secondary (if 30% volume isn't enough to keep the secondary engaged and invested, it may deprioritize your business). The 70/30 split is a common compromise. When to use dual sourcing: for critical, high-value components with long qualification lead times where a stockout is very costly — the resilience benefit outweighs the cost of lower volume leverage.
Q23
Business Continuity Plan
According to Business Continuity Planning (BCP) best practices, which characteristic is MOST critical for a BCP to deliver value when a major supply chain disruption occurs?
Key point: BCP value = being prepared before the disruption. When a crisis hits, there is no time to design a response — you execute the pre-planned one. Pre-planning delivers: faster response (know exactly what to do), better decisions (made when calm, not in crisis mode), clear ownership (who activates, who communicates externally), and tested procedures (known to actually work). BCPs created reactively during a disruption are slow, inconsistent, and miss critical elements. Testing is also essential — an untested BCP may fail at the moment it's most needed. Update frequency should be annually at minimum, not every 5 years.
Q24
RTO vs. RPO
During BCP planning, a supply chain director states: "If our warehouse management system goes down, we must be able to resume all receiving and shipping operations within 4 hours." This statement defines the system's:
Key point: RTO (Recovery Time Objective) = maximum acceptable downtime for a critical function. It is a time target for restoration. The RTO drives the design of recovery capabilities — redundant systems, failover architecture, or manual backup processes that can activate within the RTO window. RPO (Recovery Point Objective) = maximum acceptable data loss measured in time (e.g., "we can lose at most 1 hour of transactions"). RPO drives backup frequency. Shorter RTO and RPO = more resilient but more expensive recovery infrastructure. Both are set based on how much operational disruption the business can absorb.
Q25
Risk Management — Integration Scenario
Scenario: A consumer electronics company sources 85% of its display panels from a single manufacturer in Taiwan. A 7.8-magnitude earthquake destroys the factory and halts production entirely. The company has no qualified backup supplier, no strategic safety stock beyond 10 days of coverage, and no pre-tested Business Continuity Plan for this scenario. Recovery takes 5 months, during which the company loses $120M in revenue and significant customer loyalty.
Which combination of pre-emptive risk management actions would have MOST reduced the impact of this disruption?
Key point: This scenario integrates all of Module 7's core concepts. The three gaps that caused the 5-month recovery: (1) No dual sourcing → no backup when primary failed; (2) No strategic safety stock → only 10 days of coverage before stockout; (3) No BCP → improvised, slow response. Option B directly addresses all three gaps with the proven toolkit: dual sourcing (eliminates single point of failure), strategic safety stock (buffer time while backup ramps up), and a pre-tested BCP (faster, better-quality response). Option C (control tower) improves visibility and may provide a few hours' early warning — but doesn't prevent the disruption or provide backup supply. Option D diversifies distribution but doesn't address the supply-side gap.
