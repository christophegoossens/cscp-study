Section 1 · Supply Chain Models & Structure (Q1–Q6)
Q1
Supply Chain Foundations
According to ASCM, which of the following BEST describes the primary objective of supply chain management?
Key point: The ASCM definition is comprehensive — it covers the full lifecycle (design → monitoring) with the goal of creating net value. Options A and D describe only partial goals. This definition is frequently tested verbatim on the exam.
Q2
Supply Chain Flows
In a supply chain, cash flows in which direction?
Key point: Cash flows upstream (customers pay → retailers → distributors → manufacturers → suppliers). Goods flow downstream. Information flows in both directions. Reverse flows handle returns.
Q3
Integration Types
A consumer electronics company decides to acquire its chip manufacturer and open its own retail stores. This strategy is BEST described as:
Key point: Vertical integration = owning multiple levels of the supply chain (upstream suppliers and/or downstream distributors/retailers) within one organization. The PRIMARY benefit is CONTROL. The drawback is loss of scale economies and increased complexity.
Q4
SC Maturity
A manufacturing company shares real-time demand data with its key suppliers and jointly plans production schedules across the network. Which stage of supply chain maturity does this BEST represent?
Key point: Stage 4 (Extended Enterprise) involves sharing data and collaborating with external supply chain partners. Stage 5 goes further — full synchronization and orchestration across ALL partners. Stage 3 is internal integration only. The distinction between Stage 4 and 5 is a common exam trap.
Q5
Bullwhip Effect
A retailer notices a 5% increase in consumer sales and orders 15% more inventory from its distributor. The distributor, in turn, orders 30% more from the manufacturer. Which phenomenon does this BEST illustrate?
Key point: The Bullwhip Effect is demand variability that amplifies as it moves upstream. A small retail fluctuation becomes an increasingly large order swing at each upstream echelon. Causes: forecast error, batch ordering, price fluctuations, and the rationing game.
Q6
SC Concepts
The term "echelon" in supply chain management refers to:
Key point: An echelon is a level of supply chain nodes. A three-echelon system might have: factory warehouses → regional distribution centers → retail stores. Option C describes Keiretsu, a Japanese cooperative relationship model.
Section 2 · Demand Analysis & Patterns (Q7–Q12)
Q7
Demand Types
A bicycle manufacturer needs 2 tires, 1 frame, and 2 brakes for every bicycle sold. The demand for tires is BEST described as:
Key point: Dependent demand is derived from demand for a higher-level item. Tires are needed because bicycles are being manufactured — you calculate tire demand directly from the production plan (MRP). The bicycles themselves face independent demand (unpredictable, comes from the market).
Q8
Demand Patterns
A retailer sells significantly more winter coats in October–February each year, with demand dropping in spring and summer. This demand pattern is BEST classified as:
Key point: Seasonality = a predictable, recurring pattern within a year. Distinguish from: Trend (long-term directional change), Cycle (multi-year economic oscillation), Random variation (unpredictable noise). Seasonality repeats on an annual basis.
Q9
Demand Analysis
A supply chain manager is conducting a PEST analysis. Which of the following factors would be examined under this framework?
Key point: PEST = Political, Economic, Social, Technological. It's a macro-environment scanning tool. SWOT analysis covers Strengths/Weaknesses (internal) + Opportunities/Threats (external). PEST informs the external portion of a SWOT.
Q10
Demand Patterns
Over the past 10 years, demand for a product has steadily increased by approximately 3% annually, with normal seasonal fluctuations. The 3% annual increase BEST represents which demand pattern?
Key point: A trend is a long-run directional increase or decrease in demand over time. Seasonality recurs annually. Cycles span multiple years and relate to economic expansion/recession. Random variation has no pattern. The 3% annual growth is a clear upward trend.
Q11
Macro Environment
A company observes that its product sales decline significantly during recessions and recover during economic expansions, with this pattern repeating over 5–7 year intervals. This is MOST likely the result of:
Key point: Cycles are multi-year oscillations tied to macroeconomic conditions (expansion/recession). They differ from seasonality (within-year, predictable) and trends (long-term directional). A 5–7 year recession/recovery pattern is a classic economic cycle.
Q12
Demand Management
Which of the following is the MOST accurate description of demand management according to ASCM?
Key point: The ASCM definition of demand management emphasizes two things: (1) recognizing ALL demands (orders, forecasts, interplant, etc.) and (2) prioritizing when supply is constrained. It operates at strategic, tactical, and operational levels.
Section 3 · Forecasting Methods & Error Measurement (Q13–Q20)
Q13
Forecasting Principles
Which of the following statements about forecasting is MOST accurate according to ASCM principles?
Key point: Key forecasting principles: (1) Aggregate forecasts are MORE accurate than detailed ones — random errors cancel out. (2) Short-term forecasts are MORE accurate than long-term. (3) Forecasts should be based on demand, not orders (to avoid the Bullwhip Effect). (4) Every forecast should include an error estimate.
Q14
Forecasting Methods
A company is launching a completely new product category with no historical sales data. Which forecasting approach is MOST appropriate?
Key point: Qualitative methods (like Delphi) are used when no historical data exists — new products, new markets. All time-series methods (moving average, exponential smoothing, naive) require historical demand data. The key risk of qualitative methods is bias.
Q15
Exponential Smoothing
Last month's forecast was 500 units. Actual demand was 550 units. Using exponential smoothing with α = 0.2, what is the forecast for next month?
Calculation: Ft = Ft-1 + α(At-1 − Ft-1) = 500 + 0.2 × (550 − 500) = 500 + 0.2 × 50 = 500 + 10 = 510 units. With a low α of 0.2, the forecast adjusts slowly toward actual demand — giving more weight to the old forecast than to the new actual.
Q16
Forecasting — Alpha Value
A supply chain planner switches the exponential smoothing alpha (α) from 0.1 to 0.8. What is the MOST LIKELY result?
Key point: Higher α = more weight on recent demand = more responsive forecast. But "responsive" is a double-edged sword — it also means the forecast overreacts to random noise/variation. Low α (close to 0) = slow, stable forecast. High α (close to 1) = almost a naive forecast (just use last period's actual).
Q17
Forecast Error
Over four periods, the forecast errors were: +8, −4, +6, −10. What is the MAD (Mean Absolute Deviation)?
Calculation: MAD = average of ABSOLUTE errors = (|+8| + |−4| + |+6| + |−10|) ÷ 4 = (8 + 4 + 6 + 10) ÷ 4 = 28 ÷ 4 = 7 units. Note: the cumulative error = +8 − 4 + 6 − 10 = 0, which would suggest no bias — but the MAD correctly shows there IS still forecast error (it just oscillates).
Q18
Tracking Signal
A forecast tracking signal has been consistently at +5.8 for the past three months. What action should the supply chain planner take?
Key point: A tracking signal of +5.8 is at the edge of the acceptable ±4 to ±6 range AND has been consistently high — both are red flags. A positive tracking signal means actual demand is consistently HIGHER than the forecast (the forecast is too low = positive bias). The planner should review and raise the forecast. Some sources use ±4 as the threshold, others ±6 — the exam typically uses ±4 to ±6.
Q19
Delphi Method
In the Delphi forecasting method, which feature distinguishes it from a standard expert panel discussion?
Key point: The defining feature of the Delphi method is anonymity + iteration. Experts answer independently (anonymously), results are compiled and shared, then another round occurs. This eliminates dominant-personality bias and groupthink. It's used for new products, new markets, or technological forecasting.
Q20
Causal Forecasting
An appliance manufacturer uses housing construction start data to predict washing machine sales 6 months in advance. This is an example of:
Key point: Associative (causal) forecasting uses a leading indicator — a variable that changes before the variable being forecast. Housing starts are a classic leading indicator for appliances. The key requirement is that the leading indicator must change reliably BEFORE the dependent variable.
Section 4 · S&OP & Operations Planning (Q21–Q25)
Q21
S&OP
Which of the following BEST describes the purpose of Sales and Operations Planning (S&OP)?
Key point: S&OP bridges demand and supply at the aggregate product family level to create a monthly consensus plan. It links strategic plans to operational execution. Done at product family level — not individual SKUs (that's the Master Production Schedule). IBP (Integrated Business Planning) is an advanced form that more deeply integrates financial planning.
Q22
S&OP Process
In the S&OP cycle, which meeting step typically comes IMMEDIATELY BEFORE the Executive S&OP meeting?
Key point: The S&OP sequence is: (1) Data gathering → (2) Demand planning → (3) Supply planning → (4) Pre-S&OP / Financial reconciliation → (5) Executive S&OP meeting. The pre-meeting reconciles gaps between demand and supply plans and aligns with financial targets before presenting to leadership.
Q23
MPS vs MRP
A company has completed its S&OP process and produced a consensus production plan. What is the NEXT step in the planning hierarchy?
Key point: The planning hierarchy flows: S&OP (aggregate) → MPS (specific items/dates) → MRP (material requirements) → Purchasing/Production execution. RCCP is performed alongside the MPS to check capacity feasibility. The MPS "disaggregates" the S&OP plan into item-level detail.
Q24
Influencing Demand / 4Ps
During a period of excess inventory, a supply chain manager recommends a temporary price reduction to stimulate demand. Which of the 4Ps of marketing is being used?
Key point: The 4Ps = Product, Price, Placement (distribution), Promotion. A temporary price reduction = Price lever. Promotion refers to advertising/marketing campaigns. Note: Price reductions to clear excess inventory can contribute to the Bullwhip Effect if they cause customers to stockpile during the promotion.
Q25
Integration — Scenario
Scenario: RetailCo's supply chain team notices that its 3-month moving average forecast has been consistently 10–15% below actual sales for the past 6 months. The tracking signal has moved from +4.2 to +6.8. Meanwhile, marketing has just launched a new promotional campaign that is expected to increase demand further.
What should RetailCo do FIRST?
Key point: Multiple signals demand action: (1) Tracking signal rose from +4.2 to +6.8 — clearly outside the ±4 to ±6 range, indicating systematic positive bias (forecast consistently too low). (2) A new promotion will further increase demand. Increasing the moving average periods would make it less responsive — the opposite of what's needed. The correct first step is to review/revise the forecast model, potentially adding a promotional uplift factor (qualitative override).
