Section 1 · Make-vs-Buy, Outsourcing & Offshoring (Q1–Q6)
Q1
Sourcing Process
According to the ASCM 7-step sourcing framework, which of the following is the FIRST step in the strategic sourcing process?
Key point: The 7-step sourcing process begins with (1) Develop/validate the supply plan — designing the supply network strategy before any tactical buying begins. Step 2 is research the supply market. Step 4 is evaluate/select suppliers. Step 5 is negotiate and contract. Getting the sequence right is a common exam question. Sourcing is strategic; procurement (steps 6–7) is operational execution.
Q2
Make-vs-Buy Decision
Which of the following is the STRONGEST argument for keeping a manufacturing process in-house (make) rather than outsourcing it?
Key point: Protecting proprietary technology and core competencies is the most compelling argument for insourcing. If the process drives competitive advantage, outsourcing risks IP leakage and transferring that knowledge to suppliers who may serve competitors. Option C (excess capacity) is a factor but only addresses short-term availability — it doesn't justify locking in an insource decision long-term. Lower external cost (A) and multiple capable suppliers (D) both favor buying.
Q3
Outsourcing & Offshoring — Definitions
Which statement BEST distinguishes outsourcing from offshoring?
Key point: These are commonly confused. Outsourcing = in-house process → external supplier (geography irrelevant). Offshoring = domestic process → different country (the supplier can be an internal "captive" operation or external). Combined = "offshore outsourcing." Option A describes offshoring. Option B describes outsourcing. Option C describes reshoring/onshoring — bringing production back home.
Q4
Nearshoring
A German automotive supplier relocates its component production from Shenzhen, China to Wrocław, Poland to reduce transportation time and improve supply chain resilience. This strategic move is BEST described as:
Key point: Nearshoring = offshoring to a geographically closer country. Poland is much closer to Germany than China — shorter lead times, lower freight cost, better supply chain visibility, easier management. It is NOT reshoring because production stays outside Germany. The scenario makes no mention of a change in ownership (external supplier), so outsourcing doesn't apply.
Q5
Post-COVID Sourcing Trends
The post-COVID supply chain environment has broadly driven companies toward which sourcing strategy?
Key point: COVID-19, port congestion, the semiconductor shortage, and geopolitical disruptions exposed the fragility of long, lean, globally dispersed supply chains. The dominant response has been nearshoring (to regional suppliers) and reshoring (domestic production) — accepting higher unit costs in exchange for resilience, speed, and supply certainty. Single-source global suppliers (C) would increase risk, not reduce it.
Q6
Make-vs-Buy — Factors Favoring Buy
Which of the following scenarios MOST strongly favors a "buy" (outsource) decision?
Key point: The buy decision is strongest when BOTH conditions align: (1) the activity is non-core — internal resources are better deployed on higher-value strategic activities, AND (2) an external supplier has a cost/quality advantage. Options B and D are classic "make" arguments (IP protection and unique design). Option C favors making based on capacity and cost. When A's two conditions are simultaneously true, the case for outsourcing is compelling and well-grounded in TCO analysis.
Section 2 · Sourcing Requirements & Total Cost of Ownership (Q7–Q12)
Q7
Sourcing Documents — RFI / RFP / RFQ
A procurement team sends a document to 18 potential suppliers asking about their manufacturing capacity, ISO quality certifications, financial stability, and engineering capabilities — with no pricing information requested at this stage. This document is BEST described as:
Key point: An RFI is used early in the sourcing process to build a qualified supplier list — gathering information about capabilities, certifications, and capacity WITHOUT requesting prices. Sequence: RFI (qualify suppliers) → RFP (get technical proposals + pricing) or RFQ (get price for defined spec). The RFI narrows a broad market down to qualified candidates before investing time in RFP or RFQ analysis.
Q8
Total Cost of Ownership — Cost Categories
Under a Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) framework, which of the following is classified as a POSSESSION cost?
Key point: TCO has four cost categories: Acquisition (purchase price, tooling, qualification — one-time costs to get the item), Possession (carrying cost, storage, receiving, inspection — costs of HOLDING the item over time), Usage (quality failures, scrap, rework, warranty, buffer inventory — costs of using or the item failing), End-of-life (disposal, switching costs). Option D (rework from quality failures) is a usage cost. Options A and B are acquisition costs.
Q9
Total Cost of Ownership — Calculation
Supplier A in Vietnam offers a component at $4.50/unit. Additional costs identified in the TCO analysis: freight = $0.80/unit, import duty = 8% of purchase price, lead-time safety stock carrying cost = $0.45/unit, incoming inspection cost = $0.25/unit. What is Supplier A's TCO per unit?
Calculation: TCO = $4.50 (price) + $0.80 (freight) + $0.36 (duty: 8% × $4.50) + $0.45 (safety stock carrying cost) + $0.25 (inspection) = $6.36/unit. If a domestic supplier quotes $6.00/unit with near-zero add-ons, TCO favors the domestic supplier despite its higher list price. This is the core message of TCO — apparent price advantages from distant suppliers often disappear once all lifecycle costs are included.
Q10
Sourcing Requirements
A supplier's on-time delivery rate, ability to meet tight delivery windows, and scheduling flexibility during peak and off-peak demand periods are all components of which key sourcing requirement?
Key point: Delivery performance covers: (1) on-time delivery rate (% of orders delivered on or before the promised date), (2) adherence to delivery windows, and (3) scheduling flexibility across volume swings. This is distinct from lead time (the elapsed time from order placement to receipt) and capacity (the volume a supplier can handle). All seven sourcing requirements are evaluated independently — don't combine them in your analysis.
Q11
Sourcing Documents — RFP vs RFQ
A company needs to source a complex IT managed services contract where the solution approach, service levels, and pricing model are not yet fully defined. The MOST appropriate sourcing document to issue is:
Key point: An RFP is used when the solution is not fully defined — you want suppliers to propose their approach, methodology, and pricing together. The selection is based on technical approach AND price. An RFQ requires a fully defined specification (you know exactly what you want and just need a price). For complex services where HOW the work will be done is part of the evaluation, an RFP is the correct choice. An RFI would be used even earlier (before you're ready to solicit).
Q12
Total Cost of Ownership — Switching Costs
A manufacturer switches from one raw material supplier to another and incurs $85,000 in re-qualification testing, process re-certification, retooling, and updated quality documentation. Under TCO, these costs are BEST classified as:
Key point: Switching costs fall under end-of-life costs in TCO — they are incurred when terminating a supplier relationship. They are often severely underestimated and can effectively lock companies into poor supplier relationships because the cost of leaving is prohibitively high. High switching costs give existing suppliers significant pricing leverage. Including switching costs in the initial supplier selection TCO is critical to avoiding this trap.
Section 3 · Supply Plans, Categorization & Supply Base Management (Q13–Q19)
Q13
Sourcing Strategy — Sole vs Single Source
A pharmaceutical manufacturer requires a specific chemical reagent used in its API production process. After extensive market research, it finds this reagent is produced by only one global manufacturer. This supply situation is BEST described as:
Key point: Sole source = no alternatives exist in the market — the company has no choice. This is the highest-risk supply situation and requires maximum mitigation (safety stock, long-term contracts, qualification of potential alternates even if not yet approved). Single source = alternatives exist but the company strategically chooses one supplier (a deliberate decision for volume leverage or partnership). The distinction is critical: sole source is a market reality; single source is a strategic choice.
Q14
Kraljic Matrix — Strategic Items
A manufacturer spends $8M annually on rare earth metals essential for its electric motor production. These metals are available from only two global suppliers, both located in geopolitically sensitive regions. In the Kraljic Matrix, this purchase is BEST classified as:
Key point: Strategic items sit in the high-spend + high-supply-risk quadrant of the Kraljic Matrix. Recommended strategy: deep supplier partnerships, long-term contracts, joint supply planning, and active risk management. The $8M spend rules out "bottleneck" (which is low spend). Two geopolitically concentrated suppliers confirms high supply risk. Do NOT confuse with leverage items — those have high spend but LOW risk (many suppliers, standard commodity).
Q15
Kraljic Matrix — Bottleneck Item Strategy
A food manufacturer spends $12,000/year on a specialty flavor concentrate available from only one approved supplier. Without it, production halts entirely. What is the BEST Kraljic Matrix-aligned sourcing strategy?
Key point: Bottleneck items = low spend but high supply risk (single source, production-critical). The strategy focus is NOT cost reduction (spend is too small to matter) — it is ensuring supply continuity. Tactics: safety stock, qualify alternates, long-term contract to lock in availability. Strategic co-development (D) is overkill for $12,000 of spend. Non-critical strategy (A) ignores the production-critical risk. This is a common exam trap: low spend ≠ low importance.
Q16
Supply Base Management — Rationalization
Which of the following BEST defines "supply base rationalization"?
Key point: Supply base rationalization = right-sizing the supply base by strategically reducing supplier count where beneficial. The goal is the optimal count — not the minimum. Benefits: volume leverage (higher spend per supplier → better pricing), simpler relationship management, deeper performance partnerships, and more consistent quality. Option B (down to one supplier per category) risks over-rationalization and eliminates resilience. Option C confuses price ranking with supplier count management.
Q17
Spend Analysis — Pareto by Supplier Count
In a spend analysis, a Pareto analysis by NUMBER OF SUPPLIERS per spend category is primarily used to:
Key point: Three Pareto analyses support a complete spend analysis: (1) by spend category = find where the money goes (80/20); (2) by supplier count per category = find oversupplierized categories where spend is fragmented and no single supplier has sufficient volume to be fully motivated — these are consolidation candidates; (3) by spend-per-supplier = verify leverage levels. Option C describes the first Pareto analysis (by spend), not the second (by supplier count).
Q18
Supply Plan — Centralized vs Decentralized Sourcing
Which of the following is the PRIMARY ADVANTAGE of decentralized sourcing compared to centralized purchasing?
Key point: Decentralized sourcing = each site or business unit sources independently. The PRIMARY advantage is local flexibility — faster response to local needs, local market knowledge, and ability to use regional suppliers. The tradeoff: you lose the volume leverage, standardization, and supply base rationalization benefits that centralized purchasing delivers. Options A, B, and C are all advantages of CENTRALIZED sourcing. Supply plans must explicitly address this tradeoff for each spend category.
Q19
Supply Base Management — Undersized Risk
After aggressive supply base rationalization, a company has only two approved suppliers for a critical electronic component. One supplier unexpectedly declares bankruptcy. The company cannot fulfill customer orders for 4 months while qualifying a new supplier. This scenario BEST illustrates:
Key point: Supply base right-sizing is about finding the optimal number of suppliers — not the minimum. Reducing to only two suppliers for a critical component eliminates the backup options needed when a supplier fails. The 4-month disruption is the direct cost of insufficient resilience. The lesson: resilience (multiple qualified options) and cost efficiency (fewer, higher-volume supplier relationships) must be explicitly balanced when making supply base decisions.
Section 4 · Product Design for Supply Chain (Q20–Q25)
Q20
Product Design — Lifecycle Cost Impact
Product design decisions account for only 5–15% of a product's total production cost, but they determine approximately what percentage of the product's total lifecycle cost?
Key point: This is a frequently tested CSCP statistic: design = 5–15% of production cost but approximately 70% of total lifecycle cost. Why? Design locks in material choices (and their supply chains), manufacturing processes, required tolerances, component complexity, and distribution packaging — all long before production begins. This is the fundamental justification for DFSC and concurrent engineering: supply chain and procurement must be involved during design, not after.
Q21
Concurrent Engineering
Which of the following is the PRIMARY advantage of concurrent engineering (simultaneous design) over traditional sequential product development?
Key point: Concurrent engineering = all functions (engineering, procurement, manufacturing, marketing) work in parallel rather than sequentially. Benefits: faster time-to-market, supply chain constraints discovered before design is finalized, fewer expensive late-stage redesigns. In sequential design (Marketing → Engineering → Manufacturing → Procurement), procurement discovers single-source or long-lead parts only AFTER the design is locked — making correction extremely expensive. Concurrent engineering prevents this entirely.
Q22
Design for Supply Chain (DFSC)
A consumer electronics manufacturer redesigns its product line so that 14 of 18 components are shared across all product models, and all shared components are available from at least three qualified suppliers. This redesign BEST exemplifies:
Key point: Design for Supply Chain (DFSC) focuses on designing products with supply chain implications explicitly in mind: component commonality across product lines (fewer unique parts to source and stock), standardized components (available from multiple suppliers), and packaging suited to distribution. DFM focuses on assembly simplicity. DFSS focuses on building quality in. Modular design focuses on creating product variety from standard modules. DFSC is the correct term when the primary driver is supply chain simplification and risk reduction.
Q23
Product Design Quality — Design vs Conformance
A medical device company produces a blood glucose monitor that is manufactured exactly to specification — all tolerances met, defect rate below 0.5%. However, clinical data shows that patients cannot accurately read the display under typical home lighting conditions. This is a failure of:
Key point: Quality of design = how well the specification captures what customers actually need. Quality of conformance = how consistently the product meets its specification. This product has PERFECT conformance (made exactly to spec) but POOR design quality (the spec missed the real usability requirement). A product can be perfectly manufactured and still fail customers if the specification was wrong from the start. Both must be managed. Option D may have contributed but is not the direct cause described.
Q24
Modular Product Design
A smartphone manufacturer offers customers 96 different product configurations using only 8 standardized internal modules (processor, screen, battery, camera, memory, connectivity, housing, ports). This approach is BEST described as:
Key point: Modular design = building products from standardized, interchangeable sub-assemblies (modules). The result is mass customization — many customer-visible product variants from a small set of standard modules. Supply chain benefits: fewer unique components to source and stock, higher volume per module (better leverage per supplier), simpler supplier relationships, and easier serviceability. The 8-module / 96-configuration example is a classic illustration of how design choices directly simplify the supply chain.
Q25
Product Design — Integrated Scenario
Scenario: MechCo's engineering team completes the design for a new industrial pump and passes the specification to procurement. Procurement discovers that 3 of the pump's 5 critical seals are specified with proprietary dimensions only produced by one supplier with a 16-week lead time. Engineering declines to redesign, stating the specification is final and approved.
What process failure MOST contributed to this situation, and what should MechCo implement going forward?
Key point: The root cause is sequential design — procurement was handed a finished, approved design with no ability to influence it. By the time supply chain issues are discovered post-design, changing them is expensive and organizationally painful (as MechCo learned). Concurrent engineering fixes this by involving procurement, manufacturing, and supply chain during the design phase — when material choices are still flexible. DFSC principles (use standard components, ensure multi-source availability, consider lead times) would have prevented single-source proprietary seals from being specified. Supply base rationalization (A) and spend analysis (B) are valuable tools but address different problems — they do not fix design-stage sourcing decisions.
