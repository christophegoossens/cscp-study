Section 1 · SC Strategy & IT Requirements (Q1–Q6)
Q1
Efficient vs. Responsive SC
A furniture company sells a standard product catalogue with very stable, predictable demand and competes primarily on price. Which supply chain strategy is MOST appropriate?
Key point: Stable, predictable demand + price competition = Efficient supply chain. Efficiency is achieved through high capacity utilization, lean inventory, minimal overhead, and standardized processes. Responsive SCs are for volatile demand. Resilient SCs protect against disruptions — but add cost that is not justified here.
Q2
Efficient vs. Responsive SC
According to ASCM, the two key variables that differentiate an efficient supply chain from a responsive one are:
Key point: The two differentiating variables are: (1) Inventory volume — how much buffer is needed, and (2) Demand uncertainty — how predictable is demand? High certainty + low inventory needed → efficient. High uncertainty + high inventory buffer needed → responsive. Companies cannot maximize both simultaneously because the required design choices conflict.
Q3
SC Resilience
A pharmaceutical company qualifies a second supplier for its most critical active ingredient and maintains a dedicated 90-day safety stock buffer at a separate warehouse to protect against disruptions. This investment is PRIMARILY in:
Key point: Resilience is the third strategic dimension — the ability to withstand and recover from supply chain disruptions. It requires redundant capacity, diversified sourcing, and safety buffers. These add cost but reduce risk from disruptions (supplier failure, natural disasters, geopolitical events). Efficiency minimizes cost; responsiveness maximizes speed; resilience minimizes disruption risk.
Q4
Technology Analysis — BCA
A company's benefit-cost analysis for a new Warehouse Management System (WMS) estimates total benefits of $800,000 and total costs (implementation, licensing, training, 3-year maintenance) of $1,000,000, yielding a Net Present Value of −$200,000. What should the company do?
Key point: A negative NPV means the investment destroys value — total costs exceed total benefits in present value terms. The correct decision is to reject or redesign. The company should look for ways to reduce implementation costs, expand the scope of captured benefits, phase the rollout, or find an alternative solution. Intangible benefits should have been included in the original analysis and do not justify proceeding when the quantified NPV is negative.
Q5
Technology Optimization
A company spends €2.5M implementing an advanced ERP system. Eighteen months later, the planning team is still using spreadsheets because no user training was delivered and process redesign was skipped. This BEST illustrates the failure of:
Key point: Technology optimization means ensuring installed technology is configured and used to its full potential. Installing software without process redesign and training = underutilization — the organization pays the full cost but captures only a fraction of the potential benefit. Many supply chain IT failures are optimization failures, not technology failures. The distinction: technology analysis evaluates whether to invest; technology optimization ensures the investment actually delivers value.
Q6
IT Benefits — Tangible vs. Intangible
When building a business case for a supply chain IT investment, the recommended approach for estimating intangible benefits (e.g., improved decision speed, better supplier relationships) is:
Key point: Consensus-based estimation reduces individual optimism bias — particularly from the project sponsor who is motivated to see the investment approved. Multiple stakeholder perspectives surface different risks and lead to more credible, balanced benefit estimates. This also increases organizational buy-in for the investment decision. The project sponsor's individual estimate is the MOST biased source because they have the strongest personal interest in approval.
Section 2 · SC Technology Applications & Connectivity (Q7–Q13)
Q7
SC Technology Systems
A logistics manager wants to automate carrier selection for each outbound shipment, optimize truck loads for cost efficiency, audit freight invoices, and provide customers with real-time shipment tracking. Which system is specifically designed for these capabilities?
Key point: TMS (Transportation Management System) manages transportation operations: carrier selection, load and route optimization, freight audit and payment, and shipment tracking. Distinguish from: WMS (warehouse operations — receiving, putaway, pick/pack/ship), ERP (enterprise-wide backbone), SRM (supplier performance and contracts). Each system has a distinct domain; best-in-class supply chains use all of them integrated.
Q8
EDI
A global retailer sends purchase orders, advance ship notices, and invoices electronically to 400 suppliers. Each document follows a standardized format (ANSI X12) that the suppliers' systems process automatically without manual re-entry. This B2B integration method is BEST described as:
Key point: EDI (Electronic Data Interchange) is the structured electronic exchange of standard business documents (POs, invoices, ASNs) between trading partners using a standardized format (ANSI X12 in North America, EDIFACT internationally). EDI was the original B2B integration technology and remains dominant in retail, automotive, and healthcare. API integration is faster and more flexible but does not replace EDI where batch document exchange is the established standard.
Q9
APS vs. ERP
A supply chain manager needs a system that simultaneously considers customer demand requirements AND available production capacity constraints to generate a feasible, optimized production and procurement schedule across multiple plants. Which system category is designed for this purpose?
Key point: APS (Advanced Planning System) is constraint-based planning software — it simultaneously considers demand, supply, and capacity constraints to generate feasible plans. Standard ERP planning modules do not perform true constraint-based optimization. An APS handles demand planning, supply planning, and production scheduling together. It is more sophisticated than basic MRP/ERP because it accounts for real-world resource limitations rather than generating infinite-capacity plans.
Q10
SC Technology Perspectives
A supply chain technology architect describes the 'demand channel' component of the company's integrated system architecture. The PRIMARY role of the demand channel is:
Key point: The three SC technology perspectives are: (1) Process value supply chain — the operational make/source/deliver chain; (2) Value delivery supply chain — how value and service are delivered to customers; (3) Demand channel — the information-gathering function that collects demand signals (POS data, orders, forecasts) from the market and feeds them to supply chain planners. Without an effective demand channel, the planning function is operating blind.
Q11
IS Architecture
According to ASCM, an information system architecture is a model of how the organization operates regarding information. Which option correctly lists all four factors it considers?
Key point: The ASCM definition of IS architecture considers four factors: (1) Organizational functions — what business processes exist; (2) Information requirements — what data each function needs; (3) Data attributes — the characteristics of that data (frequency, volume, format); (4) Information technologies — the systems that collect, store, and deliver it. This is a business-driven framework, not just a technology inventory — it starts with business need, then selects technology to match.
Q12
SC Connectivity
A company wants to view its key suppliers' inventory levels and production schedules in real time. Their IT team reports that no system-to-system connectivity exists between the company's ERP and the suppliers' systems. What is the PRIMARY operational consequence of this gap?
Key point: Connectivity is the foundation of supply chain visibility. Without system-to-system connectivity, partner data (inventory levels, production schedules, shipment status) must be collected via phone, email, or spreadsheets and re-entered manually. This introduces: (1) time delay — data is stale by the time it arrives; (2) errors — manual re-entry error rates; (3) cost — labor to collect and enter data. Connectivity eliminates all three problems simultaneously.
Q13
Data Sharing — Legal Risks
Three competing food manufacturers agree to share current retail pricing data with each other as part of an industry supply chain benchmarking initiative. Which legal risk is MOST directly triggered by this arrangement?
Key point: Sharing competitive pricing data between direct competitors is a classic antitrust risk — it can constitute or facilitate price-fixing, which is illegal in most jurisdictions. The three legal risks of supply chain data sharing are: (1) Data privacy (GDPR/CCPA) — for personal data; (2) Intellectual property — sharing product specifications or formulas; (3) Antitrust — sharing competitive pricing or market-share data across industry rivals. Pricing data shared between competitors falls squarely into category 3.
Section 3 · Master Data & Data Governance (Q14–Q19)
Q14
Master vs. Transactional Data
Which of the following BEST distinguishes master data from transactional data in a supply chain context?
Key point: Master data = core, relatively static reference data (customer master, product master, supplier master, location master). It changes infrequently — a product's weight and unit of measure don't change with every order. Transactional data = dynamic event data that changes constantly — every purchase order, shipment, receipt, and invoice is a new transaction. The distinction matters because master data errors affect ALL transactions using that record, while transactional data errors affect only a single event.
Q15
Master Data Management
Master Data Management (MDM) is BEST described as:
Key point: MDM (Master Data Management) is a data governance discipline — not a specific software product. It governs master data throughout its entire life cycle: Plan → Create → Use → Maintain → Archive/Delete. Key MDM activities include: defining data ownership (data stewardship), establishing validation rules, deduplication, and ensuring the same customer, product, or supplier record is consistent across all systems (ERP, WMS, TMS, APS). Poor MDM is a leading cause of supply chain system failures.
Q16
Bill of Materials
A Bill of Materials (BOM) is a supply chain master data file that specifies:
Key point: A BOM (Bill of Materials) is the product structure master — it lists every component (raw material, sub-assembly) and the exact quantity needed to produce one unit of a finished product. It is the critical link between demand planning (how many finished goods are needed) and material requirements planning (which components must be ordered/produced). An incorrect BOM directly causes production errors, material shortages, and costly rework. The BOM is one of the five critical master data files in supply chain.
Q17
Master Data Life Cycle
During a post-merger ERP consolidation, the data governance team identifies 6,800 duplicate supplier records, multiple items with incorrect units of measure, and thousands of inactive customers who should be removed from the active supplier base. Resolving these issues falls under which stage of the master data life cycle?
Key point: The five master data life cycle stages are: Plan → Create → Use → Maintain → Archive/Delete. Maintain = ongoing data stewardship: cleansing (removing errors), deduplication (merging duplicate records), validation (checking data quality), and security. Without active maintenance, master data quality degrades over time — duplicate records multiply, corrections never happen, and outdated records pile up. The distinction between Archive and Maintain: Archive is for data no longer in active use (historical); Maintain is for data still in active use.
Q18
Master Data Quality Impact
An item master record for a critical component shows a supplier lead time of 4 days. The actual supplier lead time is 14 days. The system uses this lead time to calculate planned order release dates. What is the MOST LIKELY operational consequence?
Key point: Lead time is a critical master data field — the planning system calculates order release dates by subtracting lead time from the required date. If the system thinks lead time is 4 days but it's actually 14, purchase orders will be released 10 days too late, creating a supply gap. This leads to stockouts, production stoppages, and missed customer deliveries. This is a systemic error — it affects every order for this component until the master data is corrected. Master data quality errors have supply chain-wide, recurring consequences.
Q19
Master Data Governance Principles
The MDM principle that "data collection should be driven by its expected USE" is specifically intended to prevent which problem?
Key point: Collecting more data than you use creates two problems: (1) Maintenance burden — unused data fields still need to be kept accurate, secure, and current; (2) Compliance risk — under GDPR and similar regulations, organizations cannot hold personal data beyond what is necessary for the stated purpose (the "data minimization" principle). The MDM principle of "collect what you will use" keeps data governance manageable and keeps the organization compliant with privacy regulations.
Section 4 · Performance Metrics & SCOR (Q20–Q25)
Q20
SCOR Performance Attributes
The SCOR (Supply Chain Operations Reference) model organizes supply chain performance into five performance attributes. Which option correctly lists all five?
Key point: The SCOR model's five performance attributes are: (1) Reliability — right product, right place, right time, right condition (metric: Perfect Order Fulfillment); (2) Responsiveness — speed of fulfillment (metric: Order Fulfillment Cycle Time); (3) Agility — adaptability to market changes; (4) Costs — SC cost efficiency (metric: SC cost as % of revenue); (5) Asset Management — working capital efficiency (metric: Cash-to-Cash Cycle Time). Option A lists the six SCOR processes (Plan/Source/Make/Deliver/Return/Enable) — a common exam trap.
Q21
Perfect Order Fulfillment
A customer receives an order that is delivered on time, contains the correct quantity of all items requested, and arrives in perfect condition — but the accompanying paperwork shows an incorrect purchase order number, causing a dispute in accounts payable. Under the Perfect Order Fulfillment metric, this order is classified as:
Key point: Perfect Order Fulfillment is a strict composite metric — ALL four criteria must be met simultaneously: (1) Complete (correct quantity), (2) On time, (3) Damage-free, (4) Correctly documented (correct PO, invoice, ASN). Missing even ONE criterion makes the order imperfect. There is no "near-perfect" — it is binary. This is intentional: it gives a holistic view of supply chain reliability and prevents gaming the metric by optimizing only one component. Incorrect documentation causes real costs downstream (payment delays, disputes, returns).
Q22
Cash-to-Cash Cycle Time — Calculation
A company reports the following working capital metrics: Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) = 50 days, Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) = 35 days, Days Payable Outstanding (DPO) = 45 days. What is the Cash-to-Cash Cycle Time, and what does it indicate?
Calculation: C2C = DIO + DSO − DPO = 50 + 35 − 45 = 40 days. This means the company must finance 40 days of operations from its own cash — paying suppliers before it collects from customers. Lower is better. Ways to improve: reduce DIO (leaner inventory), reduce DSO (collect faster from customers), or increase DPO (negotiate longer payment terms with suppliers). A negative C2C (like Amazon's model) means customers pay before suppliers are paid — an extremely favorable working capital position.
Q23
Metrics Design Principles
A warehouse manager establishes a KPI of "number of pallets shipped per hour" without any corresponding accuracy metric. Within three months, pickers dramatically increase their speed to hit the target, but order error rates climb from 1.2% to 6.8%, generating a surge in customer returns and complaints. This scenario BEST illustrates which measurement principle?
Key point: "You get what you measure" is the foundational principle of supply chain performance management. People optimize for whatever is measured — especially when metrics are tied to bonuses or evaluations. Measuring throughput alone without accuracy creates a perverse incentive. Well-designed metrics: (1) measure the RIGHT outcome (not just the easiest-to-count activity), (2) are balanced (speed AND accuracy, not just one), (3) cut across functions so departments can't optimize locally at the cost of global performance. This is why SCOR's Perfect Order Fulfillment combines multiple dimensions.
Q24
Inventory Turns — Calculation
A company reports annual COGS of €12 million and average inventory value of €2 million. What are the inventory turns and the approximate Days of Supply?
Calculation: Inventory Turns = COGS ÷ Average Inventory = €12M ÷ €2M = 6 turns. Days of Supply = 365 ÷ Inventory Turns = 365 ÷ 6 ≈ 61 days. This means the company sells and replenishes its entire inventory 6 times per year and holds about 2 months of stock at any given time. Higher turns = more efficient use of working capital, lower holding costs. Too high turns = risk of stockouts. Note: Days of Supply can also be calculated as Average Inventory ÷ Average Daily COGS = €2M ÷ (€12M ÷ 365) ≈ 61 days.
Q25
Integration — Financial Impact Scenario
Scenario: GlobalParts Co. implements a supply chain improvement programme. Average inventory falls from €8M to €5M. The order-to-cash cycle shortens by 15 days. Order error rates drop by 30%, reducing returns. Net income holds at €2M, total assets were €20M (now €17M), and annual COGS remains €15M.
Which combination of financial and operational metrics shows the MOST DIRECT improvement as a result of this programme?
Key point: This scenario shows how supply chain improvements create multi-dimensional financial impact: (1) ROA improves: ROA = Net Income ÷ Total Assets = €2M ÷ €17M = 11.8% (vs. 10% before) — lower assets, same income; (2) C2C improves: DIO decreases because average inventory dropped €3M — less cash tied up; (3) Inventory turns improve: €15M COGS ÷ €5M inventory = 3 turns (vs. 1.9 before); (4) Perfect Order Fulfillment improves: 30% fewer errors = more orders meeting all four criteria. This is why supply chain excellence is a strategic financial lever — it simultaneously improves the P&L, balance sheet, and cash flow.
