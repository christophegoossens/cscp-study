Section 1 · Make vs. Buy, Outsourcing & Sourcing Strategy (Q1–Q6)
Q1
Make vs. Buy
A biotechnology company manufactures a proprietary enzyme central to its product line. A supplier offers to produce it at 12% lower unit cost. Based on CSCP principles, the STRONGEST reason to keep production in-house is:
Key point: The primary factor favoring MAKE is when the activity is a core competency that drives competitive differentiation — especially when it involves proprietary technology or IP. Outsourcing it risks leaking that knowledge to suppliers who may share it with competitors. A 12% unit cost saving must be weighed against strategic loss using Total Cost of Ownership (TCO), not unit price alone.
Q2
Outsourcing vs. Offshoring
A company transfers its IT helpdesk function to a third-party firm based in the same country. This is BEST described as:
Key point: Outsourcing = transferring a previously internal function to an external supplier. No geographic change is required. Offshoring = moving to a different country (can be in-house or outsourced). The two dimensions are independent: you can outsource domestically, offshore in-house (captive), or do both simultaneously (offshore outsourcing).
Q3
Nearshoring / Reshoring
Post-COVID, a European manufacturer shifts production from a low-cost Asian supplier to a Polish supplier to reduce lead times and supply chain risk. This is BEST classified as:
Key point: Nearshoring = moving production to a nearby country (Poland is near Western Europe — shorter transit times, aligned time zones, lower geopolitical risk than Far East). Reshoring would mean bringing production back TO the home country (e.g., France). The post-COVID trend toward nearshoring prioritizes supply chain resilience over lowest-cost sourcing.
Q4
Total Cost of Ownership
A make-vs-buy analysis shows an external supplier's unit price is 15% lower than internal production cost. After adding transportation, incoming inspection, supplier management, and quality risk costs, the total difference narrows to only 2%. This scenario BEST demonstrates the importance of:
Key point: TCO (Total Cost of Ownership) captures ALL costs associated with a sourcing decision — purchase price, transportation, quality failure costs, incoming inspection, supplier management overhead, transition costs, and strategic risk. A 15% price advantage can shrink dramatically once TCO is applied. CSCP examiners frequently test this distinction: price ≠ TCO.
Q5
Sourcing Process
In the 7-step sourcing process, "evaluating and selecting suppliers" comes IMMEDIATELY AFTER which step?
Key point: The 7-step sourcing process in order: (1) Develop/validate supply plan → (2) Research supply market → (3) Create/refine category strategy → (4) Evaluate and select suppliers → (5) Negotiate and contract → (6) Conduct procurement → (7) Continuously improve. Supplier evaluation follows category strategy definition, which determines what kind of supplier relationship and criteria are needed.
Q6
Sourcing vs. Procurement
A purchasing agent places a purchase order for raw materials with an already-approved supplier at a negotiated price. This activity is BEST classified as:
Key point: Sourcing is STRATEGIC — deciding what to buy, from whom, under what terms (make-vs-buy, category strategy, supplier selection). Procurement is OPERATIONAL — executing the day-to-day buying (placing POs, receiving goods, paying invoices). Placing a PO with an existing approved supplier at a pre-negotiated price is a textbook procurement activity.
Section 2 · Supplier Selection, Qualification & Contracts (Q7–Q15)
Q7
Supplier Evaluation Criteria
A manufacturer is selecting a supplier for a high-volume commodity component where many qualified suppliers exist and the specification is standardized. Which evaluation criterion should receive the HIGHEST weight in this situation?
Key point: For a commodity component with many qualified suppliers and a standardized spec, cost/TCO and delivery are the primary differentiators — the product itself is interchangeable. Innovation and R&D matter for strategic partnerships on differentiated components. ESG and financial stability matter but are secondary for commodity sourcing. The category type drives which criteria are weighted highest.
Q8
Approved Vendor List
A procurement analyst identifies a new chemical supplier offering 18% lower pricing. The supplier is not on the company's Approved Vendor List (AVL). According to supply chain best practice, the analyst should:
Key point: The Approved Vendor List (AVL) is a governance tool — purchasing should ONLY order from pre-qualified, approved suppliers. Using a non-approved supplier — even once, even for cost savings — bypasses quality and risk controls. The correct action is to put the supplier through the formal qualification process (financial review, capability audit, quality system assessment, first article inspection) before placing any orders.
Q9
Supplier Scorecard Calculation
A supplier scorecard rates a supplier 7/10 on cost, 9/10 on quality, and 5/10 on delivery, with weights of 30%, 40%, and 30% respectively. What is the supplier's weighted total score?
Calculation: Weighted score = (7 × 0.30) + (9 × 0.40) + (5 × 0.30) = 2.10 + 3.60 + 1.50 = 7.2. Note that the high quality score (weighted 40%) lifts the total above a simple average of 7.0. The supplier's weak delivery score (5/10 at 30% weight) is partially offset by excellent quality. Scorecards allow objective, weighted comparison across multiple criteria.
Q10
Contract Types
A company contracts with a logistics firm to manage its distribution network. The scope is fully specified — fixed routes, fixed service levels — and is unlikely to change during the contract term. Which contract type is MOST appropriate?
Key point: Fixed-price is best when scope is clear and stable — the supplier can accurately price the work and bears the cost risk if their actual costs exceed the estimate. Cost-plus is appropriate for uncertain or evolving scope (R&D, complex projects) where the buyer accepts cost risk in exchange for flexibility. A well-defined logistics contract with fixed routes is a textbook fixed-price scenario.
Q11
Incoterms
Under EXW (Ex Works) Incoterms, the transfer of risk from seller to buyer occurs:
Key point: EXW (Ex Works) = maximum obligation on the buyer. Risk transfers at the seller's factory door — the buyer is responsible for all transportation, export clearance, insurance, and risk from that point forward. Contrast with: FOB (seller loads goods on board the ship), CIF (seller pays cost/insurance/freight to destination port), DAP (seller delivers to named place). EXW is the seller's minimum obligation Incoterm.
Q12
Contract Terms
Which contract clause provides a buyer with automatic financial compensation when a supplier delivers late, WITHOUT requiring the buyer to prove actual damages in court?
Key point: Liquidated damages are pre-agreed financial penalties for specific contract breaches — typically late delivery or missed performance targets. Both parties agree upfront so the consequences are certain and automatic; the buyer does NOT need to prove actual damages. Force majeure exempts both parties from obligations during unforeseeable events. CISG governs international sales law. These are commonly confused on CSCP exam questions.
Q13
Contract Risk Allocation
A defense contractor signs a cost-plus contract to develop a new guidance system. During the project, raw material prices increase by 30%. Who bears this cost increase?
Key point: In a cost-plus contract, the buyer pays actual costs + an agreed profit. If costs rise, the buyer absorbs the increase. This is why cost-plus is appropriate for uncertain/evolving scope — the supplier has no incentive to cut corners to protect margin. The tradeoff: the buyer loses cost certainty. Fixed-price would put the cost risk on the supplier — but at the expense of a risk premium built into the contract price.
Q14
Integration Scenario — M3
Scenario: A company outsources its logistics operations to a 3PL under a performance-based contract. After 6 months, the 3PL consistently misses on-time delivery targets (72% vs. the contracted 95%). The contract includes both performance-based pricing and a liquidated damages clause.
What should the supply chain manager do FIRST?
Key point: The contract already provides the tools: (1) invoke liquidated damages for the proven breach, and (2) issue a formal corrective action request (CAR) giving the 3PL the opportunity to fix the root cause. Immediate termination is disproportionate at this stage and ignores the contract's built-in remedies. Increasing safety stock treats the symptom, not the cause. Renegotiating contract type doesn't address the performance failure.
Q15
Integration Scenario — M3
Scenario: MedTech Co. currently manufactures medical device housings in-house. A supplier offers to produce them at 20% lower unit cost. However, the supplier failed its ISO 13485 (medical device quality) certification audit last year and is located in a distant country with long lead times.
Which factor should MOST influence the decision to keep production in-house?
Key point: For a regulated medical device, quality system compliance (ISO 13485) is a regulatory requirement — not optional. A supplier that failed this certification represents serious quality risk that could result in product recalls, patient harm, and regulatory action. TCO for this scenario must include: quality risk costs, regulatory non-compliance penalties, long lead time inventory costs, and supply chain disruption risk. These factors strongly favor keeping production in-house until a qualified supplier is identified.
Section 3 · Module 4 Preview: Capacity Types & Planning Levels (Q16–Q20)
Q16
Rated Capacity Calculation
A stamping press has a design capacity of 500 parts/hour. It operates 16 hours out of a possible 24 (utilization = 66.7%) and produces at 95% of its standard rate (efficiency = 95%). What is the rated (effective) capacity per day?
Calculation: Rated Capacity = Design Capacity × Utilization × Efficiency = 500 parts/hr × 16 hrs × 0.95 = 8,000 × 0.95 = 7,600 parts/day. Alternatively: 500 × 24 hrs × 0.667 × 0.95 = 12,000 × 0.633 = 7,600. Both routes give the same answer. Design capacity (12,000) is theoretical maximum; rated capacity (7,600) is the practical planning baseline.
Q17
Capacity Types — Definitions
A production planner reviews 12 months of historical output records to establish a baseline for capacity planning. Which type of capacity does this BEST represent?
Key point: Demonstrated capacity = actual historical output — what the system has actually produced over time. The four types: Design (theoretical max, 24/7 no downtime), Rated/Effective (practical max = design × utilization × efficiency), Demonstrated (actual historical output), Available (rated minus already-planned work). Demonstrated capacity is often used when actual performance differs significantly from rated capacity.
Q18
Capacity Planning Hierarchy
An S&OP team approves a significant production volume increase for Q3. Which capacity planning tool is used at the S&OP level to validate that the organization has sufficient long-term resources?
Key point: The four capacity planning levels aligned to the manufacturing hierarchy: Resource Planning (long-term, S&OP level — facility and workforce decisions), RCCP (medium-term, MPS level — key resource validation), CRP (detailed, MRP level — every work center against every work order), I/O Control (real-time shop floor). At S&OP level, resource planning checks whether major capacity investments are needed to support the aggregate plan.
Q19
RCCP vs. CRP
Which statement BEST describes the key difference between Rough-Cut Capacity Planning (RCCP) and Capacity Requirements Planning (CRP)?
Key point: RCCP = fast, high-level check — is the MPS feasible at key work centers? Runs BEFORE MRP to avoid generating a detailed material plan that cannot be executed. CRP = most detailed — checks EVERY work center against EVERY MRP-generated work order, using actual routings, setup times, and run times. CRP runs AFTER MRP. A common exam trap is reversing the sequence: RCCP → MPS approval → MRP run → CRP.
Q20
Bottleneck / Theory of Constraints
A plant has five work centers. Work center C processes 80 units/hour while all others handle 120+ units/hour. Work-in-process inventory is piling up in front of work center C. According to the Theory of Constraints (TOC), management should FIRST:
Key point: TOC 5-step process: (1) Identify the bottleneck → (2) Exploit it — maximize output from existing capacity (eliminate idle time, reduce setup, prioritize quality) → (3) Subordinate everything else to the bottleneck's pace → (4) Elevate — add capacity if still needed → (5) Repeat. Step 2 (Exploit) comes before Step 4 (Elevate/invest). Many exam questions test this ordering: exploit BEFORE adding capacity.
Section 4 · Module 4 Preview: Production Activity Control & Integration (Q21–Q25)
Q21
Theory of Constraints — Step 3
According to the Theory of Constraints, "subordinating everything to the constraint" (Step 3) means:
Key point: "Subordinate" means aligning ALL non-bottleneck activities to support the constraint's throughput rate. Non-bottlenecks should NOT run faster than the bottleneck can process — that only creates WIP pileup and inventory cost. The entire system's rhythm is set by the bottleneck. This is the essence of the Drum-Buffer-Rope concept in TOC: the bottleneck is the "drum" setting the pace.
Q22
Production Activity Control
Which of the following activities falls WITHIN the scope of Production Activity Control (PAC)?
Key point: PAC (Production Activity Control) is the EXECUTION layer — it translates MRP output into real shop floor action: releasing work orders, scheduling operations to work centers, dispatching jobs (prioritizing the queue), and tracking/reporting progress. S&OP and MPS are planning activities above PAC. MRP generates the material requirements. PAC executes what MRP planned. The exam distinguishes "planning" (S&OP → MPS → MRP → RCCP/CRP) from "execution" (PAC → I/O control).
Q23
Dispatching Rules
A shop floor manager wants to minimize the AVERAGE FLOW TIME (the total time work orders spend in the system from release to completion). Which dispatching priority rule should be used?
Key point: Each dispatching rule optimizes a different objective: EDD minimizes the number of late jobs (lateness). SPT minimizes average flow time and average WIP — by clearing short jobs quickly, the queue shrinks and average waiting time falls. FCFS is fair but not optimal. CR manages urgency dynamically. A common exam trap: confusing "minimize late jobs" (EDD) with "minimize average flow time" (SPT). Know which rule achieves which objective.
Q24
Critical Ratio Calculation
A work order is due in 4 days and has 6 days of work remaining. What is the Critical Ratio and what action should the dispatcher take?
Calculation: CR = (Due Date − Today's Date) / Remaining Work Days = 4 / 6 = 0.67. Since CR < 1, this order is BEHIND schedule — there is less time available (4 days) than work remaining (6 days). A CR of <1 signals the highest priority for dispatching. CR = 1 means exactly on track. CR > 1 means ahead of schedule. The Critical Ratio is recalculated dynamically as dates and remaining work change.
Q25
Integration Scenario — M4 Preview
Scenario: A production planner completes the MPS for the next 8 weeks. Before running MRP, they perform RCCP and discover that work center 5 will be loaded at 135% of rated capacity in weeks 3 and 4. The overload is caused by three large customer orders due in week 5.
What should the planner do NEXT?
Key point: The purpose of RCCP is to catch overloads BEFORE running MRP. If an overload is found, the MPS must be revised — not ignored. Running MRP on an infeasible MPS generates purchase orders and work orders that cannot be executed, wasting time and confusing suppliers. Options to resolve: (1) overtime/extra shifts at work center 5, (2) move some orders to earlier or later weeks, (3) subcontract work center 5 operations. Option D (run CRP first) is wrong — CRP runs AFTER MRP, not as a diagnostic before MPS revision.
