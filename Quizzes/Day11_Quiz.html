Section 1 · Customer Relationships (Q1–Q6)
Q1
Customer-Centric Supply Chain
According to ASCM principles, which of the following BEST describes the starting point for designing a demand-driven supply chain?
Key point: The demand-driven approach starts with the customer — determining what delivery speed, flexibility, customization, and cost customers actually value — and then designing the supply chain backward from those requirements. This contrasts with supply-driven design, which pushes what can be produced most efficiently. The customer is the ultimate source of supply chain performance requirements.
Q2
Customer Segmentation
A supply chain manager finds that 15% of customers generate 70% of revenue but require same-day delivery, dedicated safety stock, and specialized packaging. The BEST supply chain response is to:
Key point: Customer segmentation is the foundation of differentiated supply chain strategy. Not all customers have the same value or the same requirements. Serving all customers identically leads to over-investing in low-value accounts (eroding margin) and under-investing in strategic relationships (risking defection). The goal is to match service investment with customer lifetime value — maximum service where it generates maximum retention value.
Q3
Voice of the Customer (VOC)
The Voice of the Customer (VOC) process in supply chain management is PRIMARILY used to:
Key point: VOC = structured methods (surveys, interviews, focus groups, NPS, complaint analysis, win/loss analysis) to understand customer requirements. In supply chain, VOC answers: what delivery lead time do customers actually need? How important is order accuracy vs. delivery speed? What is the cost of a stockout to them? These inputs drive supply chain design decisions — inventory positioning, transportation mode choices, and service-level commitments.
Q4
Customer Lifetime Value (CLV)
Customer A generates $50,000/year in revenue with standard service requirements. Customer B generates $30,000/year but requires expedited delivery, dedicated inventory, and weekly account management meetings. Based on Customer Lifetime Value (CLV) and cost-to-serve principles, what should the company do FIRST?
Key point: CLV and cost-to-serve analysis must be combined. Revenue alone is insufficient — Customer B's $30K revenue may be barely profitable or even loss-making after factoring in expedited freight, dedicated inventory carrying cost, and account management time. Conversely, Customer A's $50K may be highly profitable with simple standard service. The correct first step is always to calculate true profitability, then make evidence-based decisions about service levels and pricing adjustments.
Q5
Service Level Agreements (SLAs)
A company signs a 3PL logistics contract that specifies "excellent service and fast delivery." Six months later, a dispute arises because the 3PL considers 3-day delivery to be "fast" while the company expected 1-day delivery. This situation MOST LIKELY results from:
Key point: SLAs convert vague service expectations into measurable, contractual commitments. A well-written SLA defines specific targets (e.g., "95% of orders delivered within 24 hours of shipment confirmation"), measurement methodology, reporting frequency, and remedies for non-performance. "Excellent service" and "fast delivery" are subjective — without quantification, both parties can have different, equally valid interpretations. The SLA is the mechanism that creates shared, accountable service expectations.
Q6
Customer Satisfaction vs. Loyalty
A customer satisfaction survey shows 90% of customers are "satisfied" with service, yet 20% of customers defect to competitors each year. The MOST likely explanation for this paradox is:
Key point: Satisfaction = meeting current expectations ("this order was fine"). Loyalty = an ongoing preference to keep buying from you even when alternatives exist. Satisfied customers have met their threshold — they won't complain — but if a competitor offers something better, they have no strong reason to stay. Loyalty requires exceeding expectations, building emotional connection, and delivering consistent value that makes switching feel like a loss. This is why NPS (likelihood to recommend) correlates better with retention than satisfaction ratings.
Section 2 · CRM Systems & Collaboration Tools (Q7–Q13)
Q7
CRM Systems
Which of the following BEST describes the role of a Customer Relationship Management (CRM) system in supporting supply chain operations?
Key point: CRM sits at the intersection of commercial relationships and supply chain execution. For supply chain, CRM provides: (1) demand signals — order history informs forecasting; (2) contract terms — SLA commitments become operational targets; (3) customer segmentation data — tier customers to differentiate service; (4) order management integration — feeds orders to WMS and TMS. CRM connects the customer-facing commercial function to the supply chain's operational execution.
Q8
Vendor-Managed Inventory (VMI)
In a Vendor-Managed Inventory (VMI) arrangement, who makes replenishment decisions and what is the PRIMARY benefit to the supplier?
Key point: In VMI, the supplier monitors the customer's inventory levels (via shared POS or inventory data) and proactively replenishes without the customer placing a purchase order. The primary benefit to the supplier is demand signal quality — instead of seeing customer purchase orders (inflated or deflated by ordering behavior), the supplier sees actual consumption. This removes one layer of the Bullwhip Effect, enabling more accurate production planning and lower safety stock requirements.
Q9
CPFR
CPFR (Collaborative Planning, Forecasting, and Replenishment) MOST directly reduces the Bullwhip Effect by:
Key point: The Bullwhip Effect compounds when each supply chain tier independently forecasts and adds safety buffer — small retail fluctuations become large swings upstream. CPFR solves this by having trading partners jointly develop ONE agreed forecast using shared POS data, inventory positions, and promotion plans. With a single source of truth flowing up the chain, each tier doesn't independently forecast and buffer — dramatically reducing forecast error and total supply chain inventory cost while improving service.
Q10
Key Account Management (KAM)
A company generating 40% of revenue from a single customer assigns a dedicated cross-functional team (sales, supply chain, finance, and technical support) to manage that relationship, with joint annual business planning. This is BEST described as:
Key point: KAM dedicates specialized resources to the most strategically important customer relationships. A customer representing 40% of revenue is clearly a key account — losing them would be a major financial event. KAM investment is justified by the Pareto principle: 20% of customers typically generate 80% of revenue. KAM provides dedicated team, joint planning, customized supply chain services, and deep operational integration — converting a buyer-seller relationship into a true strategic partnership that is more resilient and more profitable for both parties.
Q11
Electronic Data Interchange (EDI)
A major retailer requires all suppliers to transmit EDI transaction 856 (Advance Ship Notice) before shipments arrive at distribution centers. The PRIMARY operational benefit for the retailer is:
Key point: The EDI 856 ASN (Advance Ship Notice) tells the retailer's WMS exactly what is on the inbound truck — item, quantity, lot, expiration, pallet configuration — before the truck arrives. This enables: (1) pre-building receiving dock assignments; (2) pre-allocating labor; (3) pre-staging put-away locations; (4) automated receiving check-in (scan vs. expected ASN). The result is significantly faster receiving, fewer errors, and lower labor cost. Without ASN, every receiving transaction requires manual verification — slow and error-prone.
Q12
Collaboration Maturity
SupplyCo and RetailMart currently share weekly point-of-sale data with each other, but each company creates its own independent forecasts and replenishment plans. Based on the supply chain collaboration maturity model, they are at which stage, and what would represent the NEXT level up?
Key point: The four collaboration maturity stages are: (1) Transactional — buy/sell, no sharing; (2) Information Sharing — share data, but each party uses it independently; (3) Coordinated — share data AND align plans; (4) Synchronized (CPFR) — joint forecasting, single shared plan. Sharing POS data but maintaining independent forecasts = Stage 2. The next step (Stage 3) is to use that shared data as a common input into coordinated planning — both parties aligning on the same demand and supply picture before creating their respective plans.
Q13
CRM Collaboration Tools — Scenario
A consumer goods manufacturer wants to simultaneously reduce its forecast error for its top 5 retail accounts AND improve those retailers' in-stock rates. Which supply chain collaboration tool BEST achieves BOTH objectives?
Key point: CPFR is uniquely positioned to achieve both objectives simultaneously. Shared forecasting reduces the manufacturer's forecast error (better demand signal → less safety stock needed, less waste). Better-planned replenishment improves retailer in-stock rates (right product, right time, right quantity). Neither EDI nor customer portals address forecast quality — they improve transaction processing efficiency, not demand visibility. ABC costing addresses cost understanding, not service quality or forecast accuracy.
Section 3 · Customer Service Metrics (Q14–Q19)
Q14
OTIF Calculation
A supplier achieves 97% on-time delivery and 93% order fill rate over the past quarter. What is their OTIF (On-Time In-Full) performance?
Calculation: OTIF = % On-Time × % In-Full = 0.97 × 0.93 = 0.9021 = 90.21%. OTIF is multiplicative — a shipment must be BOTH on-time AND complete to count as a success. A shipment that arrives on time but short by even one unit counts as an OTIF failure. This explains why OTIF is always lower than either component metric alone. Major retailers (Walmart, Target, Amazon) use OTIF as the standard and impose financial chargebacks for failures below threshold.
Q15
Net Promoter Score Calculation
A company surveys 200 customers about likelihood to recommend (0–10 scale). Results: 80 customers score 9–10, 60 customers score 7–8, and 60 customers score 0–6. What is the Net Promoter Score (NPS)?
Calculation: % Promoters (9–10) = 80/200 = 40%. % Detractors (0–6) = 60/200 = 30%. Passives (7–8) are excluded from the formula. NPS = % Promoters − % Detractors = 40 − 30 = 10. NPS ranges from −100 to +100. Score of 10 = acceptable but below the "good" benchmark of 50+. Key exam trap: passives are NOT included in the NPS calculation, even though they are part of the survey sample.
Q16
Service Metric Hierarchy
Which of the following customer service metrics is the MOST stringent and will therefore result in the LOWEST percentage if all underlying performance dimensions are equal?
Key point: These metrics form a hierarchy of increasing stringency: Order Fill Rate (availability only) ≥ On-Time Delivery (timing only) ≥ OTIF (on-time AND complete) ≥ Perfect Order (on-time + complete + damage-free + correctly invoiced). Each level adds another dimension that must be satisfied simultaneously. Since Perfect Order requires ALL four dimensions to be perfect for a single order to count, it will always produce the lowest percentage. A common exam scenario: OTIF of 90% and damage-free of 98% → Perfect Order = 90% × 98% = 88.2%.
Q17
Cost-to-Serve Analysis — Scenario
Cost-to-serve analysis reveals that Customer X (generating $200K gross margin) is net-unprofitable due to frequent small orders, partial-pallet deliveries, and high return rates. What is the MOST appropriate FIRST action?
Key point: When cost-to-serve analysis reveals an unprofitable customer, the sequence is: (1) diagnose which cost drivers are creating the problem; (2) explore whether changes (minimum order size, delivery surcharges, SKU rationalization, returns policy) can make the relationship profitable; (3) renegotiate pricing; (4) as last resort, exit the relationship. Immediate termination skips the opportunity to fix the problem — the customer may be perfectly willing to change behavior if the cost implications are explained. "Absorb the cost" is not a strategy.
Q18
SCOR Model Customer Metrics
Under the SCOR model, the Reliability performance attribute is primarily measured by which metric?
Key point: SCOR's customer-facing performance attributes and their primary metrics: Reliability = Perfect Order Fulfillment (did we do exactly what we promised?); Responsiveness = Order Fulfillment Cycle Time (how fast?); Agility = Upside/Downside Adaptability (how flexible?). Internal attributes: Costs = Total SC Management Cost; Asset Management = Cash-to-Cash Cycle Time. Reliability is the reliability of the supply chain to deliver the right product, to the right place, at the right time, in the right condition and quantity — hence Perfect Order Fulfillment.
Q19
Activity-Based Costing vs. ABC Inventory
A supply chain analyst uses Activity-Based Costing (ABC costing) to determine that two customers with identical revenue are not equally profitable. A colleague mistakenly thinks ABC costing is the same as ABC inventory classification. What is the KEY distinction between these two concepts?
Key point: These share only the "ABC" name — otherwise unrelated. ABC inventory classification (Pareto): ranks SKUs by annual demand value to prioritize inventory control effort (A items = tight control, C items = minimal attention). Activity-Based Costing (ABC costing): an overhead allocation method that traces costs to activities (order processing, picking, delivery) and then assigns those activity costs to customers/products based on their actual consumption — revealing true profitability. Traditional costing spreads overhead evenly, hiding the true cost of complex, demanding customers.
Section 4 · Supplier Relationships (Q20–Q25)
Q20
Supplier Segmentation — Kraljic Matrix
The Kraljic Matrix is used in supply chain management to segment suppliers. Which two dimensions does it use to classify suppliers?
Key point: The Kraljic Matrix classifies suppliers using: (1) Profit impact (high/low) — how strategically important is this item to the business? and (2) Supply risk (high/low) — how difficult is it to source or switch suppliers? The four quadrants: Bottleneck (high risk, low profit → secure supply), Strategic (high risk, high profit → partnership), Leverage (low risk, high profit → competitive bidding), Non-critical (low risk, low profit → simplify/automate). This determines the appropriate relationship type and management investment for each supplier segment.
Q21
Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)
Supplier A quotes $10/unit with 98% quality yield, 1-week domestic lead time, and reliable delivery. Supplier B quotes $8/unit with 94% quality yield, 8-week overseas lead time, and variable delivery. Using Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) principles, which conclusion is MOST appropriate?
Key point: TCO = purchase price + all other ownership costs. Supplier B's lower price may be negated by: (1) 6% defect rate vs. 2% = 4× more rework, scrap, warranty cost; (2) 8-week lead time = significantly more pipeline inventory + safety stock; (3) overseas = import duties, ocean freight, customs clearance cost; (4) variable lead time = emergency air freight when delays occur; (5) supply disruption risk premium. A 'cheap' supplier with poor quality and long/variable lead times often has a higher true TCO than a seemingly more expensive local supplier.
Q22
Open-Book Costing
A buyer proposes open-book costing to a preferred supplier to identify collaborative cost reduction opportunities. The supplier declines. The MOST likely reason for the supplier's refusal is:
Key point: Open-book costing requires the supplier to share its detailed cost structure — materials, labor, overhead, margin. This is only rational if the supplier trusts the buyer not to use that information as leverage to squeeze margin to zero. In a preferred (but not yet strategic) relationship, this trust may not yet exist. The supplier correctly reasons: "If I show them I make 20% margin, they'll immediately demand 15% price reduction." Open-book costing requires a foundation of genuine partnership, gain-sharing agreements, and demonstrated mutual benefit before suppliers will expose their financial structure.
Q23
Sole Source vs. Single Source
A manufacturer uses only one supplier for a critical electronic component. Three other qualified suppliers exist in the market, but the manufacturer chose to concentrate all volume with the incumbent due to volume discounts and delivery consistency. This is BEST described as:
Key point: The critical distinction: Sole source = only ONE supplier in the market is capable of supplying the item — no alternatives exist. Single source = multiple capable suppliers exist, but the buyer deliberately chooses to use only one (for economies of scale, relationship investment, or quality consistency reasons). Both create supply concentration risk. Single sourcing rationale: higher volume to one supplier → better price, priority capacity, closer relationship. Risk mitigation best practice: dual-qualify a backup supplier even if the primary supplier receives 100% of normal volume.
Q24
Gain Sharing
A buyer and supplier jointly identify a packaging redesign that saves $0.60/unit in material and labor costs. They agree in advance to split the savings 50/50: the buyer's unit cost decreases by $0.30, and the supplier retains $0.30/unit of improved margin. This supply chain arrangement is called:
Key point: Gain sharing is the mechanism that aligns buyer-supplier incentives for cost reduction. Without it, any cost reduction the supplier implements benefits the buyer (via lower price pressure) while the supplier gets nothing → no incentive to find improvements. With gain sharing, both parties benefit from every dollar of savings found → the supplier proactively brings ideas. Why gain sharing requires open-book costing: both parties need to verify the actual savings to implement the split correctly. Gain sharing converts adversarial price negotiation into positive-sum collaborative improvement.
Q25
Strategic Supplier Relationships — Scenario
Scenario: ManuCo has a single-source critical supplier with a 5% defect rate (vs. 1% target) and 70% on-time delivery (vs. 95% target). The supplier is a small, specialized firm with unique technical capabilities that no other supplier in the market currently possesses. Qualifying an alternative supplier would take 18 months.
What is the MOST appropriate strategy for ManuCo?
Key point: This scenario tests supplier relationship strategy under constraint. Termination is not viable — no alternative supplier exists and qualification takes 18 months. Multi-bid cannot work — no other qualified bidders. Accepting poor performance is never the right answer. The only viable path is supplier development — investing ManuCo's own resources (engineers, training, capital) to help the supplier fix its quality and delivery problems. This is the classic case where supplier development is both strategically necessary and financially justified: the supplier's unique capabilities make them irreplaceable in the short-to-medium term, so improving them is improving your own supply chain.
