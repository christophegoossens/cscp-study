Section 1 · Forecasting Methods & Calculations (Q1–Q8)
Q1
Moving Average Calculation
Using a 3-period moving average, what is the forecast for April given the following data?
January: 100 units · February: 120 units · March: 110 units
Calculation: Moving Average = (100 + 120 + 110) ÷ 3 = 330 ÷ 3 = 110 units. A simple moving average gives equal weight to all N periods. The key limitation: it lags behind trends and reacts slowly to demand changes.
Q2
Weighted Moving Average
Using a weighted moving average with the weights below, what is the forecast for April?
Most recent period (March): weight 0.5 · Previous period (February): weight 0.3 · Oldest period (January): weight 0.2 Demand: January = 80 · February = 90 · March = 100
Calculation: Forecast = (100 × 0.5) + (90 × 0.3) + (80 × 0.2) = 50 + 27 + 16 = 93 units. The weighted moving average gives more influence to recent periods — making it more responsive than a simple moving average. Note: weights must always sum to 1.0.
Q3
MSE vs MAD
Why is Mean Squared Error (MSE) less commonly used as a primary forecast error metric compared to MAD?
Key point: MSE squares each error — so a single large error (e.g., 10²=100) gets far more weight than ten small errors (e.g., 10 × 1²=10). This makes MSE sensitive to outliers and results are in squared units (hard to interpret). MAD is in the same units as demand and easier to communicate. MSE is still useful when large errors are particularly costly.
Q4
MAPE Calculation
Actual demand was 200 units and the forecast was 180 units. What is the MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error) for this period?
Calculation: MAPE = |Actual − Forecast| ÷ Actual × 100 = |200 − 180| ÷ 200 × 100 = 20 ÷ 200 × 100 = 10%. Note: MAPE divides by Actual, not Forecast. Option C (11.1%) would result from dividing by the forecast instead — a common trap. MAPE is useful for comparing forecast accuracy across products with different volumes.
Q5
Tracking Signal — Bias Direction
A product's tracking signal has been consistently at −5.5 for the past four months. This indicates that the forecast is:
Key point: Tracking Signal = Cumulative Error ÷ MAD. Cumulative Error = Σ(Actual − Forecast). A negative tracking signal means cumulative error is negative → actuals are consistently LOWER than the forecast → the forecast is too HIGH (overestimating). Also note: "consistently" at −5.5 is a warning sign regardless of whether it's within ±4 to ±6 range — the lack of oscillation signals bias.
Q6
Forecast Accuracy Principle
According to ASCM forecasting principles, which of the following forecasts would GENERALLY be the MOST accurate?
Key point: Two ASCM principles combine here: (1) Aggregate forecasts are more accurate — random errors cancel out across products. (2) Shorter-horizon forecasts are more accurate — less uncertainty. The best combination is: short horizon + aggregate level = a 3-month product family forecast beats all other options listed.
Q7
Qualitative vs Quantitative Forecasting
A pharmaceutical company is preparing to launch a first-in-class drug with no comparable products on the market. Which forecasting approach is MOST appropriate for initial demand planning?
Key point: When there is no historical data for the specific product, qualitative methods are required. All time-series methods (moving average, exponential smoothing, naive) need prior demand data. The Delphi method or market research panels allow expert judgment to fill the gap. The main risk of qualitative methods is bias.
Q8
Naive Forecasting
A planner uses naive forecasting. Actual demand in March was 450 units. What is the forecast for April?
Key point: The naive forecast is the simplest possible method: next period's forecast = last period's actual demand. In this case, April forecast = 450 units. It requires no historical analysis and is often used as a benchmark to test whether more complex methods add value. Naive forecasting with α=1 in exponential smoothing produces the same result.
Section 2 · S&OP & Planning Hierarchy (Q9–Q12)
Q9
S&OP Output
What is the PRIMARY output of a successful S&OP process?
Key point: S&OP produces a consensus plan at product family level — not individual SKUs (that's the MPS). The consensus plan aligns sales, marketing, supply chain, and finance around one agreed number. This plan then feeds into the MPS, which disaggregates it into specific items and dates.
Q10
IBP vs S&OP
Integrated Business Planning (IBP) is considered an advancement over traditional S&OP primarily because it:
Key point: IBP extends S&OP by connecting the supply chain plan more tightly to financial targets and long-term business strategy. In standard S&OP, finance is a participant; in IBP, financial modeling is embedded throughout. The planning horizon is typically extended (18–36 months vs. 12–18 in S&OP).
Q11
Aggregate Planning Strategies
A company maintains a constant workforce throughout the year and builds up finished goods inventory during slow periods to meet peak demand later. This aggregate planning approach is BEST described as a:
Key point: Level strategy = constant production rate, stable workforce, use inventory/backorders to absorb demand variation. Chase strategy = match production to demand — adjusting workforce size or output up and down. Mixed strategy combines elements of both. Level is preferred when workforce stability matters; chase when holding inventory is costly.
Q12
DRP
DRP (Distribution Requirements Planning) is primarily used to:
Key point: DRP is the distribution equivalent of MRP — it determines when and how much to replenish at each distribution center based on demand forecasts and lead times. MRP calculates material needs from the BOM. RCCP checks capacity feasibility for the MPS. The planning flow is: S&OP → MPS → MRP (manufacturing) / DRP (distribution).
Section 3 · Supply Chain Design — Lean, Agile & Network (Q13–Q17)
Q13
Agile vs Lean — Scenario
A fast-fashion retailer launches new clothing styles every 4–6 weeks with highly unpredictable demand and a short selling window. Which supply chain strategy is MOST appropriate?
Key point: Agile supply chains prioritize speed and flexibility over cost efficiency — ideal for volatile demand, short product life cycles, and high customization needs (like fast fashion). Lean suits stable, high-volume, predictable products. Leagile is a hybrid — lean to the decoupling point, agile downstream from it.
Q14
Leagile / Decoupling Point
In a leagile supply chain, what happens at the Customer Order Decoupling Point (CODP)?
Key point: In a leagile supply chain, upstream of the CODP = lean/push (production driven by forecasts, optimized for cost). Downstream of the CODP = agile/pull (driven by actual customer orders, optimized for speed/flexibility). The CODP is where the switch from forecast-driven to order-driven production occurs — essentially where you "postpone" commitment to a final product.
Q15
Responsive Supply Chain
A supply chain designed primarily for RESPONSIVENESS (rather than cost efficiency) would MOST LIKELY feature:
Key point: A responsive supply chain sacrifices cost efficiency to gain speed and flexibility. This means: excess capacity (to scale up quickly), flexible processes (to handle variety), and premium logistics options (faster but costlier). Efficient chains do the opposite — they minimize cost at the expense of flexibility.
Q16
Network Design
A company is redesigning its global supply chain network. Which of the following is a PRIMARY factor to consider in this network design decision?
Key point: Supply chain network design decisions center on: (1) Facility location and sizing, (2) Inventory positioning (where to hold stock in the network), (3) Transportation modes (cost vs. speed trade-off), (4) Make-vs-buy (vertical integration decisions), and (5) Number of echelons. These structural choices create the cost/service profile of the supply chain.
Q17
ERP Systems
Which of the following BEST describes the role of an ERP system in supply chain management?
Key point: ERP = the enterprise backbone that integrates all core business functions. Option A describes a WMS; Option C describes a TMS; Option D describes the Delphi method. APS sits on top of ERP to add advanced optimization. Common ERP examples: SAP S/4HANA, Oracle, Microsoft Dynamics.
Section 4 · Technology, Connectivity & Systems (Q18–Q21)
Q18
WMS vs TMS
A logistics director wants to optimize carrier selection, plan multi-stop delivery routes, and track freight costs across all shipments. Which system is MOST appropriate?
Key point — system map: WMS = manages warehouse operations (receiving, put-away, picking, packing, shipping). TMS = manages transportation (carrier selection, route optimization, freight audit & payment). ERP = enterprise backbone. APS = advanced planning and optimization algorithms. Knowing which system handles which function is a frequent exam topic.
Q19
Blockchain in Supply Chain
A food company wants to give regulators the ability to trace any product back to its farm of origin within seconds, with a tamper-proof audit trail. Which technology is MOST appropriate?
Key point: Blockchain creates an immutable, distributed ledger — once recorded, records cannot be altered. This makes it ideal for provenance tracking and compliance audit trails. RFID tracks physical location in real time; IoT gathers sensor data; EDI is a document exchange protocol. The key differentiator for blockchain is immutability + shared access across parties.
Q20
CPFR vs VMI
A grocery retailer shares its point-of-sale data and promotional calendar with its key supplier. They jointly build a 12-week demand forecast and agree on replenishment quantities. This collaboration is BEST described as:
Key point — CPFR vs VMI: CPFR = joint planning, forecasting, AND replenishment by both parties. VMI = the supplier alone manages and replenishes customer inventory (the customer is passive). The key distinction: CPFR is collaborative (both parties participate in forecasting). VMI shifts responsibility to the supplier. If the question mentions "jointly" or "co-develop," it's CPFR.
Q21
SCOR Processes
A supply chain team auditing the SCOR "Make" process would MOST LIKELY be examining:
Key point — SCOR process map: Plan = aggregate demand/supply balancing. Source = supplier management, purchasing, inbound. Make = manufacturing, assembly, testing, packaging, release. Deliver = order management, outbound logistics, last mile. Return = reverse logistics. Enable = foundational capabilities (data, rules, IT, people). Know what each process covers — this is a frequent exam format.
Section 5 · Supply Chain Metrics & Calculations (Q22–Q25)
Q22
Perfect Order Fulfillment
A company ships 1,000 orders in a month. Of these: 20 were delivered late, 15 were incomplete (missing items), and 10 had incorrect documentation — some orders had more than one issue. If exactly 940 orders had absolutely no problems, what is the Perfect Order Fulfillment rate?
Key point: Perfect Order Fulfillment = orders meeting ALL criteria simultaneously ÷ total orders. An order that's late but complete and correctly documented still fails the perfect order standard. The correct measure is: 940 ÷ 1,000 = 94%. A common exam trap is averaging the individual metrics (2% + 1.5% + 1% = 4.5% → 95.5%) — that's wrong because some orders had multiple defects.
Q23
Cash-to-Cash Cycle Time
A company reports: Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) = 45 days, Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) = 30 days, Days Payable Outstanding (DPO) = 25 days. What is the cash-to-cash cycle time?
Calculation: Cash-to-Cash = DIO + DSO − DPO = 45 + 30 − 25 = 50 days. DIO = how long cash is tied up in inventory. DSO = how long to collect from customers. DPO = how long the company delays paying suppliers (this extends your cash cycle favorably). Companies aim to minimize C2C — negative C2C (like Amazon) means you collect from customers before paying suppliers.
Q24
Inventory Turns & Days of Supply
A company's annual Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) is $24 million and average inventory is $4 million. What is its approximate Days of Supply?
Calculation (two methods): Method 1: Inventory Turns = COGS ÷ Avg Inventory = $24M ÷ $4M = 6 turns/year. Then Days of Supply = 365 ÷ 6 = ≈ 61 days. Method 2: Daily COGS = $24M ÷ 365 = $65,753/day. Days of Supply = $4M ÷ $65,753 = ≈ 61 days. Option A (6 days) confuses inventory turns with days of supply — a common trap.
Q25
Integration Scenario
Scenario: GlobalRetail has a fully implemented ERP system connecting finance, purchasing, and inventory. However, their distribution center team still uses spreadsheets to manage picking, put-away, and inventory locations. Shipment tracking relies on phone calls to carriers, and they cannot see estimated delivery times until goods actually arrive.
Which combination of systems would BEST address GlobalRetail's operational gaps?
Key point: The two gaps described are: (1) Warehouse operations — spreadsheets for picking, put-away, locations → WMS fixes this. (2) Shipment visibility — no carrier tracking → TMS fixes this. Blockchain/IoT would help with supply chain visibility but don't directly address DC operations. APS is for planning/scheduling. CPFR is for supplier collaboration. Always match the tool to the specific problem described.
