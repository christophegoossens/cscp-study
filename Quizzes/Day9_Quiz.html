Section 1 · Quality Tools & SPC (Q1–Q4)
Q1
Quality Tools
Which of the 7 basic quality tools (Ishikawa's toolkit) is MOST appropriate for detecting whether a manufacturing process is producing variation due to common causes or special causes?
Key point: A control chart plots process measurements over time with Upper Control Limit (UCL) and Lower Control Limit (LCL). Points within the limits in a random pattern = common cause variation (process is "in control"). A point outside UCL/LCL or a non-random pattern = special cause present. The other options cannot distinguish between these two types of variation — they reveal distribution shape (histogram), priorities (Pareto), or correlation (scatter).
Q2
Quality Tools
A quality manager wants to identify which 3 defect types account for the majority of customer returns so improvement resources can be focused most effectively. Which quality tool is BEST suited?
Key point: A Pareto chart ranks defect types from most to least frequent, with a cumulative percentage line — directly visualizing the 80/20 rule (80% of problems come from 20% of causes). It tells management exactly where to focus improvement effort for maximum return. The fishbone diagram helps find causes AFTER priorities are set; the check sheet collects raw data; the control chart monitors over time.
Q3
Quality Tools / Root Cause Analysis
A production team is investigating why a CNC machine intermittently produces parts that are 0.3mm oversized. They want to brainstorm and organize ALL possible root causes by category before investigating further. Which tool should they use?
Key point: The fishbone (Ishikawa) diagram is specifically designed for brainstorming and organizing potential causes of a problem. It structures causes by the 6 M categories: Man, Machine, Method, Material, Measurement, Mother Nature. This structured approach prevents teams from fixating on the most obvious cause while missing others. Once the fishbone is built, the team can prioritize which branches to investigate — often using the 5 Whys.
Q4
Statistical Process Control
A process has a capability index of Cp = 1.05. What does this indicate, and what action is warranted?
Key point: Cp measures the ratio of the specification width to the process spread. Cp = 1.0 means the process just fits within specifications — any shift in the mean will produce defects. The industry standard for a capable process is Cp ≥ 1.33 (sometimes 1.67 for critical processes). Cp = 1.05 is marginally capable — the process is not out of control (that's determined by control charts), but it lacks the cushion needed for sustained quality. Note: Cp does not account for process centering — Cpk does.
Section 2 · Continuous Improvement: Lean, Six Sigma & TOC (Q5–Q8)
Q5
Theory of Constraints
According to the Theory of Constraints 5 Focusing Steps, AFTER identifying the system constraint, the NEXT immediate step is to:
Key point: The 5 Focusing Steps in order: (1) Identify the constraint → (2) Exploit it (maximize its output with what you already have — reduce downtime, eliminate waste at the bottleneck, improve scheduling) → (3) Subordinate everything else (all other stations exist to feed and protect the constraint) → (4) Elevate (invest capital to increase constraint capacity if still limiting) → (5) Repeat. The exam frequently tests the ORDER of these steps. "Exploit before elevate" is the key principle — never spend money until you've maximized current resources.
Q6
Six Sigma / DMAIC
A Six Sigma project team has just completed a root cause analysis revealing that 78% of product defects are caused by three specific variables in the curing process. They are currently in which DMAIC phase?
Key point: Analyze is where the team uses data, fishbone diagrams, regression, and hypothesis testing to find the root causes of defects and variation. The sequence: Define (scope and VOC) → Measure (baseline data) → Analyze (root causes) → Improve (solutions) → Control (sustain). If the team has already identified WHICH variables cause most defects, they are finishing the Analyze phase and about to enter Improve. A common exam trap: "implementing solutions" = Improve; "finding what causes defects" = Analyze.
Q7
Lean Manufacturing / Takt Time
A factory operates two shifts of 7.5 hours each per day and must produce 360 units to meet customer demand. What is the takt time?
Calculation: Takt Time = Available Production Time ÷ Customer Demand Rate. Available time = 2 shifts × 7.5 hours × 60 min = 900 minutes. Takt Time = 900 ÷ 360 = 2.5 minutes per unit. This means one unit must exit the production line every 2.5 minutes to exactly match customer demand. Any workstation with a cycle time longer than 2.5 minutes is a bottleneck. Takt time sets the production "heartbeat" — neither faster (overproduction waste) nor slower (missed demand).
Q8
Lean vs. Six Sigma
Which statement BEST distinguishes Lean manufacturing from Six Sigma?
Key point: Lean (from Toyota Production System) targets waste and speed — removing non-value-added activities to make processes flow faster. Six Sigma (developed at Motorola/GE) targets variation and defects — using statistical tools to make processes more consistent. Option A has the certifications backwards (Six Sigma uses belt certifications). Option C has the origins reversed. Lean Six Sigma integrates both: faster AND more consistent. Both apply across manufacturing and service industries.
Section 3 · Logistics Foundations & Warehousing (Q9–Q17)
Q9
Logistics Fundamentals
Which element of the CSCMP definition of logistics distinguishes it from a simple "forward movement of goods" definition?
Key point: The CSCMP definition is: "the planning, implementation, and control of the efficient, effective forward and reverse flow and storage of goods, services, and related information between the point of origin and the point of consumption to meet customer requirements." The reverse flow component covers reverse logistics: customer returns, product recalls, end-of-life recycling, warranty repairs, and remanufacturing. With e-commerce return rates of 20–30%, reverse logistics is a major operational challenge — not an afterthought.
Q10
Total Logistics Cost
A company is considering switching from a slow, inexpensive ocean carrier to a faster, more expensive air freight option. Under a total logistics cost framework, this change is financially justified when:
Key point: Total logistics cost (TLC) = transportation + warehousing + inventory carrying + order processing + packaging + administration. The key principle: optimize the TOTAL, not each component independently. Faster transport reduces lead time → less safety stock needed → lower inventory carrying costs. If the inventory savings exceed the transport premium, the switch lowers TOTAL cost even though the transport line item goes up. This trade-off thinking is a core CSCP exam concept.
Q11
Logistics Activities
According to the logistics activity classification, which of the following is a PRIMARY logistics activity (one that directly creates time and place utility)?
Key point: Primary logistics activities directly create time and place value: (1) Transportation, (2) Inventory management, (3) Order processing. Secondary (supporting) activities enable the primary ones: warehousing, materials handling, packaging, procurement, and logistics information systems (WMS, TMS). This is a commonly tested classification — particularly the surprising fact that warehousing is classified as SECONDARY, supporting storage and flow but not directly creating customer value in the way transportation does.
Q12
Logistics Network / Cross-Docking
Cross-docking is MOST beneficial and operationally feasible for which type of product and supply chain situation?
Key point: Cross-docking requires tight supply-demand synchronization — goods flow directly from inbound vehicles to outbound vehicles with minimal dwell time. This works when: (1) demand is high and predictable (fast-movers), (2) supplier deliveries can be timed to match outbound schedules, (3) products don't need storage-based VAS. It fails for slow-movers (no outbound vehicle ready), items needing VAS (requires processing time), or high-variability products (buffers are needed). Walmart's distribution network is the classic cross-docking example.
Q13
Warehouse Types
A fast-growing e-commerce startup expects order volumes to triple over the next 18 months but has high uncertainty about regional demand patterns. Which warehouse type BEST fits this situation?
Key point: Public warehouses (rented by space/time) or contract warehouses (dedicated 3PL space under agreement) are the right fit for high-growth, uncertain demand scenarios: no large capital outlay, scale up as volumes grow, change footprint as regional demand patterns become clear. A private warehouse locks in fixed cost and capacity — risky when you don't know how much space you'll need and where. Cross-docking requires fast-moving, predictable flows — not ideal for a startup with uncertain demand.
Q14
Warehouse Technology / WMS
A Warehouse Management System (WMS) improves picking accuracy PRIMARILY by:
Key point: A WMS manages warehouse execution — it tells workers exactly where to go, what to pick, and in what sequence, then confirms the pick via barcode scan or RFID. This eliminates memory-based picking (prone to errors) and verifies accuracy in real time. A WMS also optimizes pick paths (reducing travel), manages wave planning, tracks labor productivity, and provides real-time inventory visibility. Note: D describes an ERP or inventory planning system, not a WMS.
Q15
Value-Added Services / Postponement
A consumer electronics company stores generic base units in its European DC and only installs country-specific power adapters, applies language-specific packaging, and adds regulatory labels when a confirmed order arrives. This practice BEST supports which supply chain strategy?
Key point: Postponement = delaying the point of product differentiation as close to actual demand as possible. Instead of stocking 20 country-specific SKUs (each with its own forecast error), the company stocks one generic SKU and customizes it via value-added services (VAS) in the warehouse only when an order is placed. Benefits: fewer finished-goods SKUs to forecast, lower total inventory, faster response to actual demand mix. The warehouse becomes a "last-mile" customization center, not just a storage facility.
Q16
Warehouse Layout / Slotting
In warehouse slotting optimization, fast-moving "A" items (top 20% of SKUs by pick frequency) should be located:
Key point: Slotting principle: place items where they are needed most frequently closest to shipping. Since A items are picked dozens to hundreds of times per day, even a 30-second travel reduction per pick multiplied across thousands of picks creates significant labor savings. Secondary slotting rules: heavy/bulky items at floor level (ergonomics), items often picked together stored near each other (reduce multi-location trips), seasonal rotation as demand patterns shift. Good slotting alone can reduce warehouse picking labor by 10–20% with no capital investment.
Q17
Warehouse Operations — Scenario
A distribution center manager analyzes labor allocation: receiving 10%, putaway 15%, storage 5%, order picking 57%, packing & shipping 13%. Where should the manager FOCUS process improvement efforts FIRST to achieve the greatest cost reduction?
Key point: Order picking typically accounts for 50–60% of total warehouse labor cost — making it the highest-leverage function to optimize. Improvements in picking (slotting, pick-to-light, voice picking, route optimization, batching) deliver the largest absolute cost savings. This is a classic application of the Pareto principle in operations: focus where the most resources are consumed. While receiving errors do cascade, they represent a fraction of total labor cost. The exam often presents this as a resource allocation or Pareto-type scenario.
Section 4 · Transportation Strategy & Distribution Services (Q18–Q25)
Q18
Transportation Modes
A pharmaceutical company must ship temperature-controlled biological samples from its lab in Brussels to a clinical trial site in Singapore. The samples degrade if transit exceeds 48 hours and the shipment weighs only 12 kg. Despite the very high cost, the only viable mode is:
Key point: Mode selection is driven by the intersection of time, cost, and product characteristics. The 48-hour constraint eliminates all other modes: ocean takes 3–4 weeks, rail takes 10–15 days, pipeline is irrelevant for biological samples. Air freight is the only option — despite costing 8–10× ocean per kg. For low-weight, extremely time-sensitive, or very high-value goods, air freight cost per unit of value is often justified. The exam frequently tests mode selection based on speed-cost trade-offs; memorize the mode ranking: Air = fastest + most expensive; Ocean = slowest + cheapest per ton-mile.
Q19
Transportation Strategy / Safety Stock
A company's current ocean carrier delivers in 18–24 days (average 21 days, ±3 days variability). A competing carrier offers an average of 22 days but with only ±1 day variability. If the company switches carriers, what is the MOST LIKELY impact on inventory levels?
Key point: Safety stock covers TWO types of uncertainty: demand variability AND supply lead-time variability. With the current carrier (±3 days), the planner must buffer for worst-case delivery (24 days). With the new carrier (±1 day), the worst case is only 23 days — allowing safety stock reduction even though average transit is 1 day longer. Reducing lead-time variability is often more valuable than reducing average lead time for safety stock purposes. This is a high-frequency exam concept: consistent, reliable suppliers → lower safety stock requirements.
Q20
Intermodal Transportation
A shipper uses a truck to pick up goods at a factory and haul them to a rail terminal, where the truck trailer is loaded directly onto a flatcar for a 1,400-mile haul across country, then a local truck delivers the trailer to the customer. This logistics arrangement is BEST described as:
Key point: TOFC (Trailer On FlatCar) is a specific form of intermodal transportation where a truck trailer is loaded onto a railroad flatcar for the long-haul portion, then picked up by a local truck for final delivery. Benefits: truck provides door-to-door flexibility at both ends; rail provides dramatically lower cost per mile for the long-haul portion. The key advantage of intermodal: no reloading of cargo — the container or trailer moves intact between modes. COFC (Container on Flatcar) is the container equivalent.
Q21
Transportation Management / LTL vs FTL
A regional manufacturer ships approximately 5,500 lbs of finished goods monthly to a retailer located 300 miles away. The minimum weight for Full Truckload (FTL) pricing is 20,000 lbs. What is the MOST cost-effective shipping option?
Key point: At 5,500 lbs against a 20,000 lb FTL threshold, FTL would mean paying for 14,500 lbs of empty space — extremely wasteful. LTL is designed for shipments that don't fill a truck: the carrier consolidates loads from multiple shippers at a hub, each shipper pays for their portion. Trade-offs of LTL vs FTL: LTL = more handling touches (damage risk), slower (consolidation/deconsolidation adds time), but cost-effective for small volumes. FTL = direct, fast, fewer touches, economical at scale. For 300 miles at 5,500 lbs, LTL is clearly the right answer.
Q22
Distribution Services / Perfect Order
A distributor reports the following monthly performance metrics: 96% on time, 98% in full, 99% damage-free, and 98% invoice accuracy. What is the approximate Perfect Order rate?
Calculation: Perfect Order % = % On Time × % In Full × % Damage Free × % Invoice Accurate = 0.96 × 0.98 × 0.99 × 0.98 = 0.9408 × 0.9702 = approximately 91.2%. The multiplicative formula is critical — it means that ALL dimensions must be right for an order to be "perfect." Four 98%-ish scores combine to only ~91%. This illustrates why companies must drive ALL dimensions simultaneously. Even small improvements across all four (e.g., each to 99%) dramatically lift the combined score: 0.99⁴ ≈ 96%.
Q23
Distribution Services / E-Commerce Logistics
Last-mile delivery is considered the most expensive component of e-commerce logistics PRIMARILY because:
Key point: A truck delivering pallets to a supermarket makes 5–10 stops per route. A parcel delivery truck might make 100–150 stops per route — each stop consuming fixed time (park, walk, attempt delivery). Fixed vehicle and driver costs are spread over many fewer units at each residential stop, pushing cost per package high. Compounding factors: failed deliveries (no one home = costly re-delivery attempts), urban traffic, and customer expectations of same/next-day delivery. Last-mile = 40–50% of total e-commerce delivery cost — why logistics innovation (drone, autonomous, locker networks) focuses here.
Q24
Omnichannel Distribution
A retailer wants to implement "buy online, pick up in store" (BOPIS) and allow customers to return online purchases at any physical location. The single MOST critical operational capability required for this omnichannel strategy is:
Key point: Omnichannel = fulfilling from any channel, delivering or picking up through any channel, returning through any channel. The foundational enabler is unified real-time inventory visibility across all nodes. Without it: BOPIS orders are accepted for items that are actually out of stock or held in the wrong location; customers can't return at a different store than where purchased. An Order Management System (OMS) typically provides this unified inventory view across stores, DCs, and in-transit positions. A TMS (Option C) is important but secondary — you first need to know WHERE the inventory is before you can route it.
Q25
Distribution Channel Strategy — Scenario
Scenario: ManufactureCo sells all products exclusively through a network of regional distributors. A competitor has launched a direct-to-consumer (D2C) website, capturing premium customers with personalized offers and 25% better margins by eliminating distributor markup. ManufactureCo's board is considering launching its own D2C channel while keeping its existing distributor relationships.
The PRIMARY risk ManufactureCo must manage when adding a D2C channel alongside its distributor network is:
Key point: Channel conflict is the primary strategic risk when a manufacturer adds direct channels alongside existing indirect channels. Distributors who built their business on ManufactureCo products now face competition from the manufacturer itself — they may retaliate by de-emphasizing the brand, pushing competitor products, or renegotiating terms. Managing channel conflict requires: clear segment separation (D2C for premium/direct consumers; distributors for SMB or geographic reach), pricing discipline (no undercutting), and transparent communication. Option B (logistics offsetting gains) is a risk but is often manageable; channel conflict is the relationship and strategic risk that's harder to fix once damaged.
