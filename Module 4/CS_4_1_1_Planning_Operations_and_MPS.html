Manufacturing Planning and Control Hierarchy

The MPC (Manufacturing Planning and Control) system links business planning to shop floor execution through four cascading planning levels, each with its own capacity check:

  • Business / S&OP — aggregate volumes by product family; capacity check via Resource Planning
  • Master Scheduling (MPS) — specific end items, weekly buckets; capacity check via RCCP (Rough-Cut Capacity Planning)
  • MRP — component/material requirements; capacity check via CRP (Capacity Requirements Planning)
  • Production Activity Control (PAC) — shop floor execution; capacity check via Capacity Control
Exhibit 4-1: Manufacturing Planning and Control
Each planning level feeds the next: S&OP → MPS → MRP → PAC. Problems at one level propagate downward.
Master Scheduling (MPS)

Master scheduling disaggregates the S&OP production plan (product families, monthly) into specific end-item quantities per week.

Master schedule: a format that includes time periods (dates), the forecast, customer orders, projected available balance, available-to-promise, and the master production schedule. Takes into account backlog, availability of material and capacity, and management policies.
Master schedule item: a part number managed by the master scheduler (not auto-calculated) due to its critical impact on lower-level components or key resources. May be an end-item, component, pseudo number, or planning bill.
Exhibit 4-2: Master Scheduling Grid

MPS grid rows:

  • Forecast — demand expected each period
  • Customer Orders (Backlog) — firm orders received
  • Projected Available Balance (PAB) — on-hand inventory projected forward
  • Available-to-Promise (ATP) — uncommitted inventory available for new customer promises
  • MPS — planned production quantity per period
Available-to-promise (ATP): the uncommitted portion of a company’s inventory and planned production maintained in the master schedule to support customer-order promising.
Time Fences

Time fences divide the planning horizon into zones with different rules for making changes:

  • Demand time fence (DTF) — near-term zone; inside this point the master scheduler controls changes manually; customer orders replace forecast
  • Planning time fence (PTF) — medium-term zone; changes require master scheduler approval; system may suggest but not auto-execute changes
  • Beyond PTF (liquid zone) — system can auto-replan; forecast drives the schedule
Cumulative lead time: the longest planned length of time to accomplish the activity in question, found by reviewing the lead time for each bill of material path below the item. The path that adds up to the greatest number defines cumulative lead time.
The closer to the delivery date, the more expensive a change becomes. Time fences protect schedule stability while allowing flexibility further out.
Rough-Cut Capacity Planning (RCCP)

RCCP validates MPS feasibility against key bottleneck resources before committing to the plan:

  • Uses a bill of resources (time per unit at each critical work center)
  • Faster and less detailed than CRP — checks only critical resources
  • If RCCP finds overload, the master scheduler adjusts the MPS before running MRP
  • Three common RCCP methods: Overall factors, Capacity bills, Resource profiles
Commitment Decision Points

When sharing demand plans with suppliers, organizations use decision points to clarify purchasing obligations:

  • Frozen zone (inside firm commitment point) — buyer commits to firm purchase orders with specific delivery dates
  • Slushy zone (between firm and volume commitment points) — buyer commits to weekly quantities but can rearrange timing
  • Liquid zone (beyond volume commitment point) — demand information represents no contractual commitment
Exhibit 4-5: Use of Decision Points (Purchase Commitment Zones)
Decision points function like time fences for buyer–supplier agreements — they replace ambiguity with clear contractual zones.
MPS Purposes and Benefits
  • Acts as a contract between sales and operations: sales promises customers what operations commits to produce
  • Provides ATP quantities week by week for customer promising
  • Enables efficient use of plant, labor, and equipment
  • Minimizes holding costs and stockouts simultaneously
  • Drives MRP: the MPS is the primary input to material requirements planning

What is a Master Schedule?
ANSWER
A format with time periods, forecast, customer orders, projected available balance, available-to-promise, and the MPS. Takes into account backlog, material/capacity availability, and management policies.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a Master Schedule Item?
ANSWER
A part number managed by the master scheduler (not auto-calculated) because of its critical impact on lower-level components or key resources. May be an end-item, component, pseudo number, or planning bill.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Available-to-Promise (ATP)?
ANSWER
The uncommitted portion of a company's inventory and planned production in the master schedule, used to support customer-order promising.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Cumulative Lead Time?
ANSWER
The longest planned time to accomplish an activity, found by reviewing all BOM paths below the item and taking the path with the greatest total lead time.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 4 levels of the Manufacturing Planning and Control hierarchy?
ANSWER
1) Business/S&OP (resource planning check), 2) Master Scheduling/MPS (RCCP check), 3) MRP (CRP check), 4) Production Activity Control (capacity control check).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Rough-Cut Capacity Planning (RCCP)?
ANSWER
A high-level check of MPS feasibility against critical bottleneck resources using a bill of resources. Faster but less detailed than CRP. Prevents the MPS from overloading key work centers.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 5 rows of the MPS grid?
ANSWER
1) Forecast, 2) Customer Orders (Backlog), 3) Projected Available Balance (PAB), 4) Available-to-Promise (ATP), 5) MPS (planned production quantity).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Demand Time Fence (DTF)?
ANSWER
The near-term zone in the MPS where customer orders replace forecast and the master scheduler manually controls changes. Inside this zone, the system does not auto-reschedule.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Planning Time Fence (PTF)?
ANSWER
The medium-term zone where the system may suggest changes but requires master scheduler approval before executing. Beyond the PTF, the system can auto-replan.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 3 commitment zones used in buyer-supplier decision point agreements?
ANSWER
1) Frozen zone — firm PO commitment, 2) Slushy zone — volume quantity committed (timing flexible), 3) Liquid zone — no contractual commitment.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does the MPS serve as a contract between sales and operations?
ANSWER
Sales promises customers delivery based on ATP quantities; operations commits to produce those quantities. Both sides are held to the MPS numbers.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between the Demand Time Fence and Planning Time Fence?
ANSWER
DTF: customer orders replace forecast; master scheduler controls changes manually. PTF: system can suggest changes but needs approval. Beyond PTF: auto-replanning allowed.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why are time fences important in MPS?
ANSWER
They protect schedule stability. Changes near the delivery date are expensive (rescheduling, overtime, expediting). Time fences signal where changes are routine vs. costly.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the relationship between S&OP and MPS?
ANSWER
S&OP creates an aggregate production plan by product family at monthly level. The master scheduler disaggregates this into specific end items by week in the MPS.
TERM / CONCEPT
What does MPS drive downstream?
ANSWER
The MPS is the primary input to MRP, which calculates all material and component requirements needed to execute the production plan.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a Projected Available Balance (PAB)?
ANSWER
The running on-hand inventory projected forward period by period, calculated as: prior PAB + MPS — greater of (forecast or customer orders).
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 3 RCCP methods?
ANSWER
1) Overall factors (simple ratio), 2) Capacity bills (time per end item at each resource), 3) Resource profiles (time-phased load by resource).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the purpose of commitment decision points in supply chain collaboration?
ANSWER
They formalize what demand information obligates the buyer to purchase, replacing ambiguity with three clear zones: frozen (firm PO), slushy (volume commitment), liquid (forecast only).
TERM / CONCEPT
In a Make-to-Order (MTO) environment, what drives the MPS?
ANSWER
Actual customer orders (backlog) drive the MPS rather than statistical forecasts, since production is only authorized by firm orders.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the typical MPS planning horizon?
ANSWER
3 to 6 months — long enough to plan procurement and capacity, short enough to remain actionable.

Question 1
What is the Master Production Schedule (MPS)?
A.The annual strategic production target
B.A time-phased plan specifying what finished products (or key sub-assemblies) will be produced, in what quantities, and when — the foundation for MRP and production scheduling
C.A supplier delivery schedule
D.A capacity plan for all work centers
The MPS translates the S&OP aggregate plan into a detailed schedule of specific finished goods — disaggregating the family plan to item level and time-phasing it. It drives all downstream MRP, purchasing, and scheduling activities.
Question 2
What is the difference between the 'planning horizon' and the 'time fence' in MPS?
A.The planning horizon is shorter than the time fence
B.The planning horizon is the full period covered by the MPS; time fences are boundaries within the horizon that define how easily changes can be made — protecting execution within the fence from disruption
C.They refer to the same concept
D.The time fence is always equal to the supplier lead time
The planning horizon covers the full MPS period. Time fences segment it: within the demand time fence, customer changes are restricted; within the planning time fence, production changes require approval; beyond both, planning is flexible.
Question 3
What is the 'demand time fence' and why is it important?
A.The time required to manufacture a product
B.The period closest to the present within which changes are frozen or heavily restricted — protecting customer commitments and firm production orders from being disrupted by new demand
C.The forecast horizon for demand planning
D.The lead time for critical raw materials
Within the demand time fence (typically 1–4 weeks), actual customer orders dominate and changes require management approval — protecting committed delivery dates and stable short-term production from constant plan changes.
Question 4
What is 'master scheduling' and how does it differ from 'detailed scheduling'?
A.Master scheduling is more detailed than shop floor scheduling
B.Master scheduling plans what to produce at the finished goods (or major subassembly) level over a medium-term horizon; detailed scheduling determines the exact sequence and timing of operations at each work center
C.They are the same process
D.Master scheduling covers daily operations; detailed scheduling covers annual plans
Master scheduling is the strategic/tactical plan (what and when at product level). Detailed scheduling (finite scheduling, sequencing) determines the exact order of operations at each machine/work center to execute the master schedule.
Question 5
What is 'rough-cut capacity planning' (RCCP) and when is it used?
A.A detailed capacity analysis of every work center
B.A high-level capacity check of key resources (bottlenecks) against the MPS — quickly identifying if the MPS is feasible before investing in detailed MRP calculations
C.A method for reducing production setup times
D.An analysis of raw material requirements
RCCP is a fast, high-level check: it multiplies MPS quantities by resource profiles for key bottleneck resources to identify obvious capacity conflicts — before running the full MRP explosion that would reveal detailed materials and capacity requirements.
Question 6
What does 'planning bill of materials' mean?
A.A simplified BOM that removes all optional components
B.A BOM created for planning purposes — often using average percentages for product options (e.g., 40% of customers choose blue, 60% choose red) — to enable master scheduling for configurable products without pre-building every variant
C.A BOM that has not yet been approved by engineering
D.A BOM listing only the most expensive components
A planning BOM (or modular BOM or super BOM) includes options with their historical frequency percentages — enabling the MPS to plan production of standard modules (which can be assembled to any configuration) rather than forecasting every variant.
Question 7
What is 'final assembly scheduling' (FAS)?
A.The first step in production planning
B.A schedule that determines when to assemble finished products from standard sub-assemblies based on actual customer orders — used in configure-to-order or assemble-to-order environments
C.A long-term capacity plan
D.The schedule for raw material deliveries
FAS operates within the demand time fence, scheduling the final assembly of customer-specific products from generic sub-assemblies that were produced to a planning BOM. It enables postponement (build sub-assemblies to forecast; finish to actual orders).
Question 8
What is 'production planning' in the context of S&OP and MPS hierarchy?
A.The same as detailed shop floor scheduling
B.An intermediate planning level between S&OP (aggregate, by family) and MPS (specific items) — setting aggregate production rates by product family that become the targets the MPS must detail out
C.A system for managing production orders
D.The planning of raw material procurement
Production planning sits between S&OP and MPS in the planning hierarchy. It sets the production rate (units/month by family) that the MPS must disaggregate into specific items, quantities, and time periods.
Question 9
What is 'make-to-stock' (MTS) vs. 'make-to-order' (MTO) production strategy?
A.MTS builds only custom products; MTO builds standard products
B.MTS produces to a forecast and holds finished goods inventory; MTO produces only when a customer order is received — the choice depends on lead time requirements vs. demand predictability
C.MTS uses pull scheduling; MTO uses push scheduling
D.MTO always has shorter lead times than MTS
MTS uses a forecast to build inventory before orders arrive — providing short delivery lead times but carrying inventory risk. MTO waits for actual orders before producing — eliminating inventory risk but requiring customers to wait the full production lead time.
Question 10
What is 'assemble-to-order' (ATO) production strategy?
A.Assembling products only after customer complaints
B.Building standard sub-assemblies to forecast, then completing final assembly only when a specific customer order is received — combining MTS speed (sub-assemblies are ready) with MTO customization
C.Building products in large batches and storing them
D.A strategy used only for spare parts
ATO (like Dell's original model) holds standard modules in WIP inventory (built to planning BOM forecast), then assembles to customer specification when orders arrive. It provides fast delivery with customization — the postponement sweet spot.
Question 11
What are 'frozen zones' in master scheduling?
A.Cold storage areas in the warehouse
B.Periods in the MPS where no changes can be made without senior management approval — protecting production stability, supplier commitments, and customer delivery promises from constant replanning
C.Time periods excluded from the MPS
D.Periods when production is shut down for holidays
Frozen zones protect the near-term schedule: no changes without management approval. This stability allows suppliers to deliver materials, shop floor to execute the plan, and customers to receive promised orders on time.
Question 12
What is the 'replanning frequency' for an MPS?
A.MPS is only replanned annually during budgeting
B.How often the MPS is regenerated — weekly or monthly in most companies — balancing responsiveness (frequent replanning catches new demand) with stability (infrequent replanning avoids MRP nervousness)
C.MPS must be replanned daily to reflect actual production
D.The MPS automatically updates in real time from ERP
Most companies replan the MPS weekly or monthly. Too frequent replanning creates MRP nervousness (constant reschedule messages). Too infrequent misses important demand/supply changes. Time fences and frozen zones provide stability within the replanning cycle.
