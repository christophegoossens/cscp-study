Material Requirements Planning (MRP)
Material requirements planning (MRP): a set of techniques that uses bill of material data, inventory data, and the master production schedule to calculate requirements for materials. It makes recommendations to release replenishment orders for material. Further, since it is time-phased, it makes recommendations to reschedule open orders when due dates and need dates are not in phase.
Dependent demand: demand that is directly related to or derived from the bill of material structure for other items or end products. Such demands are calculated, not forecasted.
Independent demand: demand for an item that is unrelated to the demand for other items. Demand for finished goods, parts for destructive testing, and service parts requirements are examples.
MRP plans dependent demand (components, raw materials). Finished goods are independent demand — subject to forecasting. Components are calculated from the MPS, not forecasted.

MRP Inputs
  • Master Production Schedule (MPS) — planned orders for end items (the demand driver)
  • Bill of Material (BOM) — complete list of components and quantities to make one parent unit
  • Inventory status — on-hand quantities, open orders, scheduled receipts
  • Planning factors — safety stock, lead times, lot sizes, scrap allowances

Order types in MRP:

Planned order: a suggested order quantity, release date, and due date created by the planning system when it encounters net requirements in the future. Not yet released — still subject to change.
Firm planned order (FPO): a planned order that is frozen in quantity and time. The system is not allowed to change it automatically; this is the responsibility of the planner.
Open order (released order): a released manufacturing order or purchase order.
Scheduled receipt: an open order that has an assigned due date.
Bill of Material (BOM)
Bill of material (BOM): a listing of all the subassemblies, intermediates, parts, and raw materials that go into a parent assembly showing the quantity of each required to make an assembly.
Multilevel BOM: a display of all components directly or indirectly used in a parent, together with the quantity required of each. Shows all levels of the product structure.

BOM terminology:

  • Parent item — the item produced from one or more components
  • Component — any item used in the production of a parent item
  • Modular (planning) bill — pertains to a module not a finished product but used as a planning device for items produced/stocked in that form
Exhibit 4-7: Multilevel BOM for Electric Motor
MRP Outputs
  • Planned order receipts — quantities planned to be received in a future period
  • Planned order releases — dates when orders should be released (accounting for lead time offset)
  • Exception reports — flags items where due dates and need dates are out of alignment
  • Net requirements = Gross requirements − On-hand inventory − Scheduled receipts
Pegging: in MRP, the ability to identify for a given item the sources of its gross requirements and/or allocations. Links each component requirement back to its parent, enabling planners to trace the impact of a change.
Routing and Lot Sizing

A routing file describes the sequence of operations required to manufacture an item, including work centers, setup times, and run times.

Exhibit 4-8: Routing for Orange Juice

Lot sizing in MRP:

  • Lot-for-lot (L4L) — order exactly what is needed each period; no excess inventory; best for JIT
  • Fixed order quantity (FOQ) — order a fixed quantity each time regardless of demand variation
  • Period order quantity (POQ) — orders cover a fixed number of future periods
Exhibit 4-9: Lot-for-Lot Scheduling

Offsetting: counting backward from a due date by the lead time to determine when an order must be released. Components with different lead times are offset so they all arrive simultaneously.

Exhibit 4-10: Component Orders Offset by Lead Times
Evolution of MRP Software
  • MRP — calculates material requirements from BOM, inventory, and MPS; assumes infinite capacity
  • Closed-loop MRP — adds capacity feedback; checks available capacity and feeds execution data back to planning
  • MRP II (Manufacturing Resource Planning) — integrates finance, sales, and all manufacturing resources
MRP II: a method for the effective planning of all resources of a manufacturing company. Addresses operational planning in units, financial planning in dollars, and has a simulation capability to answer what-if questions.

System nervousness — excessive, frequent plan changes triggered by minor input variations. Controlled by time fences and pegging.

Distribution Requirements Planning (DRP)
Distribution requirements planning (DRP): the function of determining the need to replenish inventory at branch warehouses. A time-phased order point approach using the same logic as MRP.

Push vs. Pull in distribution:

Push system: replenishing field warehouse inventories based on centrally generated schedules. Upstream determines when and how much to ship downstream.
Pull system: replenishing field warehouse inventories where replenishment decisions originate at the field warehouse itself — serial autonomy with each partner ordering independently.

Bullwhip effect in pull systems — orders amplify as they travel upstream because each DC makes independent ordering decisions without visibility into the full chain.

DRP (hybrid): combines push coordination (centralized planning) with pull responsiveness (DC-generated demand signals). Orders originate downstream but are evaluated upstream before confirmation.

What is Material Requirements Planning (MRP)?
ANSWER
A set of techniques that uses BOM data, inventory data, and the MPS to calculate material requirements and make recommendations to release replenishment orders. Time-phased to recommend rescheduling when due dates and need dates diverge.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Dependent Demand?
ANSWER
Demand directly related to or derived from the BOM structure for other items. Calculated from the MPS, not forecasted. Examples: raw materials, components, subassemblies.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Independent Demand?
ANSWER
Demand for an item unrelated to the demand for other items. Subject to forecasting. Examples: finished goods, service parts, parts for destructive testing.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 4 inputs to MRP?
ANSWER
1) Master Production Schedule (demand driver), 2) Bill of Material (product structure), 3) Inventory status (on-hand + scheduled receipts), 4) Planning factors (lead times, safety stock, lot sizes).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a Planned Order vs. a Firm Planned Order (FPO)?
ANSWER
Planned order: system-suggested order, can be changed automatically. FPO: frozen in quantity and time — system cannot change it automatically; planner controls it.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a Scheduled Receipt?
ANSWER
An open order (released manufacturing or purchase order) that has an assigned due date — it is inbound inventory already in process.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a Bill of Material (BOM)?
ANSWER
A listing of all subassemblies, intermediates, parts, and raw materials that go into a parent assembly, showing the quantity of each required to make one unit.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between a parent item and a component?
ANSWER
Parent item: the item produced from one or more components. Component: any item used in the production of a parent item. A component can itself be a parent to lower-level items.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a Multilevel BOM?
ANSWER
A display of all components directly or indirectly used in a parent, together with the quantity required of each — shows all levels of the product structure, not just the first level.
TERM / CONCEPT
How is MRP net requirement calculated?
ANSWER
Net requirements = Gross requirements − On-hand inventory − Scheduled receipts. Only net requirements trigger new planned orders.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 3 MRP outputs?
ANSWER
1) Planned order receipts, 2) Planned order releases (offset by lead time), 3) Exception reports (flags where due dates and need dates are out of phase).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Offsetting in MRP?
ANSWER
Counting backward from a component's due date by its lead time to determine its order release date. Components with different lead times are offset so they all arrive simultaneously at the work center.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Lot-for-Lot (L4L) scheduling?
ANSWER
An MRP lot sizing technique that orders exactly the quantity needed each period — no more, no less. Minimizes carrying costs but may increase ordering frequency. Best suited for JIT environments.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Pegging in MRP?
ANSWER
The ability to identify, for a given item, the sources of its gross requirements and/or allocations — tracing a component requirement back up to its parent. Helps planners assess the impact of schedule changes.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is System Nervousness in MRP?
ANSWER
Excessive, frequent plan changes triggered by minor input variations. Controlled by time fences (preventing auto-rescheduling inside certain horizons) and pegging (tracing changes to their true source).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is MRP II?
ANSWER
Manufacturing Resource Planning — an upgrade of MRP that integrates all manufacturing resources including finance, sales, and capacity. Provides financial visibility, simulation capability, and translates operational plans into financial statements.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between MRP, Closed-Loop MRP, and MRP II?
ANSWER
MRP: calculates material requirements, assumes infinite capacity. Closed-loop MRP: adds capacity feedback and execution data back to planning. MRP II: integrates finance, sales, and all resources; includes simulation.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Distribution Requirements Planning (DRP)?
ANSWER
The function of determining the need to replenish inventory at branch warehouses using time-phased order point logic — the same logic as MRP applied to a distribution network.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Bullwhip Effect in pull distribution?
ANSWER
Order amplification as orders travel upstream — each DC makes independent ordering decisions without visibility of the full chain, causing increasing variability and overordering upstream.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does DRP combine push and pull?
ANSWER
Orders originate at downstream DCs (pull element) but are evaluated at supplying locations before confirmation (push element). Combines centralized coordination with downstream responsiveness.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 4 DRP inputs?
ANSWER
1) Demand forecasts from DCs, 2) Safety stock levels, 3) Lead time information, 4) Distribution network mapping (analogous to a BOM for the distribution structure).

Question 1
What is a 'bill of materials' (BOM) explosion?
A.A catastrophic failure of the BOM system
B.The process of multiplying a parent item's demand by the quantity per in the BOM to calculate the gross requirements for all its components — working down through all BOM levels
C.Deleting obsolete components from the BOM
D.Printing the BOM for production use
BOM explosion is the core of MRP: starting with MPS quantities, multiply by each component's quantity-per-parent at each BOM level to calculate gross requirements for all materials. This cascades down through all BOM levels.
Question 2
What is 'BOM level' or 'low-level code'?
A.A security access level for BOM changes
B.The position of an item in the BOM hierarchy — level 0 is the finished good; level 1 is direct components; level 2 is components of components. Items that appear at multiple levels are assigned the lowest level where they appear
C.The priority assigned to a BOM item
D.The quality grade of a component
BOM levels define the hierarchy. Low-level coding ensures that when MRP plans an item that appears at multiple BOM levels, it only plans it once (at the lowest level it appears) — avoiding double-counting.
Question 3
What is 'phantom' item in a BOM?
A.An item that doesn't really exist in the product
B.A subassembly that is assembled and consumed immediately in the next step — never stocked as a separate item. MRP passes through phantom items without planning them as separate items
C.A deprecated component no longer used
D.A component available only from a single supplier
Phantoms (also called blow-through items) are intermediate assemblies that are built and consumed in the same operation. MRP explodes through them to their components directly, since they are never held in inventory as a separate stock item.
Question 4
What is an 'engineering change order' (ECO) and why is BOM accuracy critical for ECOs?
A.An order for engineering services
B.A formal document that changes the design, specification, or BOM of a product — BOM accuracy is critical because even a small BOM error causes MRP to order wrong quantities or wrong parts, leading to production delays
C.A change to production work instructions only
D.A quality non-conformance report
ECOs modify the BOM. If the ECO is applied incorrectly, MRP plans the wrong materials — either continuing to order superseded parts or failing to order new parts. Effective ECO management requires precise BOM maintenance and effective date control.
Question 5
What is the difference between a 'single-level' and 'multi-level' BOM?
A.Single-level BOM is more complex than multi-level
B.Single-level shows only the direct components of a parent item (one level down); multi-level shows the full hierarchy from finished good down to raw materials — including all sub-assemblies and their components
C.Single-level is only used for simple products; multi-level is only for electronics
D.They contain the same information in different formats
A single-level BOM shows the immediate components of a parent. A multi-level (indented) BOM shows the complete product structure — all levels, from finished good to raw material — enabling full material explosion.
Question 6
What is 'where-used' analysis and why is it useful?
A.Analyzing where in the warehouse an item is stored
B.A BOM inquiry that identifies all parent items (products or assemblies) that use a specific component — useful for assessing the impact of an engineering change, supply shortage, or price change on multiple products
C.Analyzing where a product is sold geographically
D.Tracking where a shipment is in transit
Where-used analysis (the reverse of BOM explosion) shows all the products that incorporate a specific component. Critical for impact analysis: if a part is discontinued or becomes scarce, where-used reveals all affected products.
Question 7
What is 'co-product' vs. 'by-product' in a BOM?
A.Co-products are high value; by-products are always waste
B.Co-products are planned, significant outputs of a production process that have commercial value (e.g., beef and leather from cattle); by-products are incidental, secondary outputs with limited value
C.They are the same concept
D.Co-products are inputs; by-products are outputs
Co-products are joint outputs that are planned and valued (both are primary products). By-products are secondary outputs with lower value that arise incidentally (e.g., sawdust from lumber, glycerin from soap production).
Question 8
What is 'scrap' factor in BOM planning?
A.The quantity of finished goods that fail quality inspection
B.A percentage added to component requirements to account for expected losses during production — ensuring that enough material is ordered to produce the required quantity of good output despite expected waste
C.A safety stock multiplier for raw materials
D.The yield rate of a production process
Scrap factors in the BOM inflate component requirements to account for process losses: if 5% of parts are scrapped in production, MRP orders 100/0.95 = 105.3% of the net requirement to yield the needed 100 good parts.
Question 9
What is 'component availability check' in production planning?
A.Inspecting components for quality before production
B.Verifying that all required materials will be available when a production order is needed — identifying shortages before they halt production
C.Checking whether components are certified
D.A count of all components in the warehouse
Component availability (or shortage) checking verifies that MRP-planned supply (orders + on-hand) will be sufficient when each production order needs its materials — enabling proactive resolution of shortages before they shut down production.
Question 10
What is 'yield' in production and how does it affect materials planning?
A.The profit generated by a product
B.The percentage of inputs that are converted into acceptable output — lower yield means more raw material must be consumed per unit of finished goods, which MRP must account for in its materials calculations
C.The speed of a production machine
D.The number of shifts a factory operates
Yield is the good output / total input ratio. MRP uses yield (or scrap) factors to inflate component requirements — for example, if yield is 90%, the BOM or process routing must indicate that 1/0.9 = 1.11 units of input are needed per unit of good output.
Question 11
What is the purpose of a 'routing' in production planning?
A.A GPS route for delivery trucks
B.A document describing the sequence of operations (work centers, setup times, run times) required to manufacture a component — used to calculate capacity requirements and schedule production
C.A shipping lane from supplier to factory
D.A list of approved suppliers for a component
Routings define how to make something: the sequence of work centers, setup and run times for each operation. MRP/MPS uses routings to calculate when to start each operation (lead time offsetting) and how much capacity each work center needs (CRP).
Question 12
What is 'modular BOM' and how does it support configure-to-order manufacturing?
A.A BOM with components that can be easily moved between products
B.A planning BOM structure where options and features are represented with percentage factors — enabling a master scheduler to plan at the feature level rather than exploding thousands of end-item combinations
C.A BOM used only for modular furniture products
D.A simplified BOM with only 2–3 levels
Modular BOMs represent configurable products as a set of options with their expected demand split (e.g., 40% AC, 60% no AC). This allows planning at the option level without creating a separate BOM for every possible product configuration.
