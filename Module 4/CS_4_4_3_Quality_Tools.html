Seven Basic Tools of Quality

The seven basic tools are graphical/visual tools for understanding and improving processes. They require no advanced statistics and can be used by front-line workers.

1. Process Map (Flowchart)

Process map (flowchart): a diagram of the flow of a production or service process through the production system. Standardized symbols represent operations, decisions, flows, and delays. The starting point for most process improvement efforts.
Exhibit 4-38: Process Map of Order Fulfillment Process

2. Control Chart

Control chart: a graphic comparison of process performance data with computed control limits. The process data are displayed as a time series of points. Differentiates between common cause variation (random, inherent to process) and special cause variation (assignable, requires investigation).
  • UCL (Upper Control Limit) and LCL (Lower Control Limit) are set statistically (typically ±3 sigma from the mean)
  • Points outside the control limits or non-random patterns indicate special causes
  • Distinguish from specification limits: control limits are process-based; spec limits are customer-defined
Exhibit 4-39: A Control Chart

3. Pareto Chart

Pareto chart: a bar graph that displays the results of a Pareto analysis (80/20 rule). Bars are arranged in descending order of frequency or impact. Identifies the "vital few" causes that account for the majority of defects or problems.
Exhibit 4-40: Pareto Chart

4. Cause-and-Effect Diagram (Fishbone / Ishikawa)

Links an effect with its possible causes. Common categories (5Ms): Machines, Methods, Materials, Measurements, Manpower (People). Also includes Environment. Used to identify root causes using the "Five Whys" technique.

Exhibit 4-41: Cause-and-Effect Diagram

5. Histogram

A bar graph showing the frequency distribution of measurement data. Reveals: natural vs. unnatural distributions, whether variation falls within specification limits, and whether data is skewed. Unlike the Pareto chart, a histogram shows frequency distribution of measured values, not frequency ranking of categories.

Exhibit 4-42: Histogram Example

6. Check Sheet

A simple tally-count tool for recording event occurrences (defect types, error frequencies). The starting point for CI projects — provides raw data for other tools. Easy to use at the point of production without special skills or software.

Exhibit 4-43: Check Sheet

7. Scatter Diagram

Shows the relationship between two variables. A diagonal pattern of dots indicates correlation (positive or negative). Does NOT prove causation — only correlation. Useful for testing hypotheses from cause-and-effect analysis.

Exhibit 4-44: Scatter Diagram (Positive Correlation)
Seven New (Management) Tools of Quality

Developed in the mid-1970s by Japanese engineers to handle non-numerical, language-based planning and quality data. More useful for planning and design phases.

1. Affinity Diagram

Organizes a large number of brainstormed ideas into natural groupings. Each group is given a descriptive title. Used for complex problems with many unstructured ideas; helps teams discover patterns.

Exhibit 4-45: Affinity Diagram

2. Tree Diagram

Lists tasks in increasingly finer detail to meet a specific goal. Shows hierarchical breakdown from goal to sub-tasks to specific actions. Similar to a WBS (work breakdown structure).

Exhibit 4-46: Tree Diagram

3. Matrix Diagram

Shows relationships between two or more groups of information, the strength of the relationship, and the role of each group. Used to identify which causes most strongly affect which effects, or to map customer requirements to product features.

4. Process Decision Program Chart (PDPC)

Visually captures things that might go wrong in a plan and identifies countermeasures. Used in two ways: (1) to identify preventive measures for potential failures; (2) to brainstorm contingency responses.

Exhibit 4-48: Process Decision Program Chart

5. Relationship Diagram (Interrelationship Diagram)

Illustrates cause-and-effect relationships among factors. Uses arrows to show which factors cause others. Goal: identify the major cause (most arrows out) and the most interrelated factor (most arrows in + out).

Exhibit 4-49: Relationship Diagram

6. Matrix Data Analysis Chart

Shows relationships between groups of information using quantitative data plotted in a matrix format. More analytical than the matrix diagram — uses actual data points to reveal clusters and patterns.

Exhibit 4-50: Matrix Data Analysis Chart

7. Activity Network Diagram (Arrow Diagram)

Shows the sequence of tasks and dependencies in a project plan. Also called a critical path method (CPM) chart. Identifies which tasks can be done in parallel and which must be sequential; finds the critical path.

Exhibit 4-51: Activity Network Diagram
Choosing the Right Tool
  • Nonquantitative tools: process flowchart, cause-and-effect diagram, affinity diagram, tree diagram, PDPC, relationship diagram
  • Quantitative tools: scatter diagram, Pareto chart, histogram, control chart
  • Combination tools: matrix diagram (can be either)
If one tool doesn’t yield actionable information, try another. The tools are complementary — e.g., use a check sheet to gather data, a Pareto chart to identify the top problem, a cause-and-effect diagram to find root causes, and a control chart to verify the fix worked.

What is a Control Chart used for?
ANSWER

What is the difference between control limits and specification limits?
ANSWER
Control limits are statistically derived from the process itself (±3 sigma); they indicate process stability. Specification limits are set by the customer and define acceptable product. A process can be in control but still produce out-of-spec parts.

What is the Five Whys technique?
ANSWER
A root cause analysis method that involves asking 'Why?' repeatedly (typically 5 times) for each answer, until the root cause is revealed rather than a symptom. Used with the cause-and-effect diagram.

ANSWER
Histogram: shows frequency distribution of measured values (bell curve, skew, outliers). Pareto: ranks categories of defects/problems by frequency — uses discrete categories, not a continuous distribution. Both are bar charts but answer different questions.

ANSWER

What is common cause variation vs. special cause variation?
ANSWER
Common cause: random variation inherent to the process — always present, managed by changing the process. Special cause: assignable variation from specific, identifiable sources — investigated and eliminated. Control charts distinguish the two.

Question 1
What is a 'fishbone diagram' (also called Ishikawa or cause-and-effect diagram)?
A.A supply chain network mapping tool
B.A structured problem-analysis tool that identifies potential causes of a quality problem organized into categories (typically: Machines, Methods, Materials, Measurement, Man, Environment) — helping teams find root causes rather than symptoms
C.A chart tracking defect rates over time
D.A diagram showing product quality specifications
The fishbone diagram (shaped like a fish skeleton) maps all potential causes of a problem across the 6M categories. It structures brainstorming to ensure root cause analysis considers all cause categories, not just the first obvious cause.
Question 2
What is a 'Pareto chart' in quality management?
A.A chart showing production output by time of day
B.A bar chart ranking defects, failures, or problems from most to least frequent — based on the Pareto principle (80/20 rule). It identifies the vital few problems that account for most defects, focusing improvement effort where it has the greatest impact
C.A chart comparing supplier quality scores
D.A trend line showing quality improvement over time
A Pareto chart visually identifies that a small number of defect types (20%) typically cause most defects (80%). It directs quality improvement resources toward the highest-impact problems rather than spreading effort across all defect types equally.
Question 3
What is a 'control chart' (also called Shewhart chart) used for?
A.Controlling employee headcount in a quality department
B.Monitoring a process over time to distinguish between common cause variation (inherent, expected) and special cause variation (unusual, indicating a process change) — the foundation of statistical process control (SPC)
C.Tracking the cost of quality over time
D.A visual management tool for scheduling quality inspections
Control charts plot process output over time with upper and lower control limits (± 3σ). Points within limits indicate common cause variation (process in control). Points outside limits or non-random patterns indicate special causes requiring investigation.
Question 4
What is a 'scatter diagram' in quality analysis?
A.A diagram showing the scatter of inventory locations
B.A chart that plots two variables against each other to reveal whether a correlation exists between a potential cause (X-axis) and a quality outcome (Y-axis) — used to validate suspected cause-effect relationships
C.A map of quality problems by geographic region
D.A diagram showing the relationship between quality cost categories
Scatter diagrams test hypotheses about cause and effect: does temperature (X) correlate with defect rate (Y)? A pattern or cluster confirms a relationship; random scatter suggests no relationship. This helps validate root causes before implementing fixes.
Question 5
What is a 'check sheet' in quality management?
A.A checklist for quality managers to inspect suppliers
B.A structured data collection form for recording how often specific quality problems occur during production — transforming raw observations into organized data for further analysis (often using other quality tools)
C.A list of quality standards and certifications
D.A form for customer quality complaints
Check sheets are simple but powerful data collection tools: workers tally each defect type each time it occurs. The accumulated data provides factual input for Pareto analysis, control charts, and root cause analysis — replacing guesswork with data.
Question 6
What is the '5 Whys' analysis technique?
A.A method requiring exactly five root causes to be identified
B.An iterative interrogation technique — asking 'Why?' five times in succession — to drill down from a problem's symptom to its fundamental root cause. Stops when the cause identified can no longer have a 'why' asked of it
C.A team meeting format for discussing quality issues
D.A customer complaint resolution process
5 Whys is a simple but powerful root cause technique: keep asking 'Why did this happen?' until you reach a root cause that can be addressed. Five iterations is typical — but the actual number needed varies by problem complexity.
Question 7
What is a 'histogram' in quality analysis?
A.A historical record of quality defects
B.A bar chart displaying the frequency distribution of a continuous variable (e.g., product dimensions, cycle times) — showing the shape, center, and spread of the data to assess process capability and identify abnormal distributions
C.A chart tracking quality costs by month
D.A diagram showing quality improvement steps
Histograms visualize the distribution of process data — are dimensions centered on the target? Is the distribution bell-shaped or skewed? Wide distribution = poor process control; narrow distribution near target = capable process.
Question 8
What is 'process capability' (Cp and Cpk)?
A.The maximum speed of a production process
B.Measures of how well a process fits within its specification limits. Cp = (USL - LSL) / (6σ) measures potential capability; Cpk = min(USL - mean, mean - LSL) / (3σ) measures actual capability including centering. Cpk ≥ 1.33 is typical target
C.The maximum output rate of a process
D.The reliability of a machine over time
Cp measures whether the process spread fits within specification limits (if centered). Cpk measures actual capability including any centering offset. A process can have high Cp but low Cpk if it is centered far from nominal. Both must be ≥ 1.33 for capable processes.
Question 9
What are the 'seven basic quality tools' (7QC tools)?
A.5 Whys, 8D, DMAIC, Kanban, Poka-yoke, Gemba, Heijunka
B.Check sheet, Histogram, Pareto chart, Cause-and-effect (fishbone) diagram, Scatter diagram, Control chart, and Stratification (or flow chart) — the set of basic statistical tools taught to all quality practitioners
C.ISO 9001, Six Sigma, TQM, Lean, 5S, Kaizen, FMEA
D.SPC, FMEA, PFMEA, APQP, Control Plan, MSA, SPC
The 7 QC tools (attributed to Kaoru Ishikawa) are the foundation of practical quality improvement — simple tools accessible to all workers for data collection, analysis, and problem-solving without advanced statistics.
Question 10
What is FMEA (Failure Mode and Effects Analysis)?
A.A financial metric for measuring quality cost
B.A structured proactive risk analysis that identifies potential failure modes in a product or process, assesses their likelihood, severity, and detectability, and prioritizes countermeasures by Risk Priority Number (RPN = Severity × Occurrence × Detection)
C.A quality certification standard
D.A supplier audit methodology
FMEA is a preventive tool: for each failure mode, evaluate Severity (1–10), Occurrence likelihood (1–10), and Detection capability (1–10). RPN = S × O × D. High RPN items receive improvement actions to reduce risk before problems reach customers.
Question 11
What is 'gauge repeatability and reproducibility' (Gauge R&R)?
A.A method for calibrating production gauges
B.A measurement system analysis technique that assesses whether a measurement tool provides consistent results when the same item is measured multiple times (repeatability) and by different operators (reproducibility) — ensuring measurement data is trustworthy
C.A customer survey repeatability analysis
D.A production scheduling method
Gauge R&R determines whether variation in measurements comes from the process (real variation) or the measurement system (measurement error). If measurement error is large relative to process variation, decisions based on that data are unreliable.
Question 12
What is 'statistical sampling' in quality inspection?
A.Inspecting every item in a production batch
B.Inspecting a representative sample of a production batch and using statistical inference to draw conclusions about the entire population — balancing inspection cost against the risk of passing defective goods
C.A random selection of production employees for training
D.Selecting a random subset of suppliers for audit
Statistical sampling uses probability theory to decide how many items to inspect from a batch and what acceptance criteria to apply. It balances the cost of 100% inspection against the risk of accepting a batch with an unacceptable defect rate.
