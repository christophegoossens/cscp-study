Inventory Types

Inventory exists in multiple forms throughout the supply chain:

Raw materials: materials purchased from suppliers that have not yet entered the production process. Represent upstream inventory buffer.
Work-in-process (WIP): materials that have been released to manufacturing but are not yet finished goods. Includes items on the shop floor being processed or waiting in queues.
Finished goods: completed products ready for sale or distribution to customers.
MRO (Maintenance, Repair, and Operating supplies): items used in support of manufacturing but not incorporated into the product (e.g., lubricants, tooling, spare parts).

Inventory by function:

  • Cycle stock — the portion of inventory consumed during a replenishment cycle; average = Q/2 for fixed order quantity
  • Safety stock — buffer inventory held against demand and supply variability
  • Anticipation inventory — built ahead of seasonal demand peaks or planned events (promotions, shutdowns)
  • Pipeline inventory — inventory in transit between locations; a function of demand rate and transit lead time
  • Hedge inventory — held against price increases or supply disruptions
  • Decoupling stock — used to decouple adjacent operations to allow each to run independently
ABC Classification

ABC analysis (Pareto-based) classifies inventory by annual dollar usage (unit cost × annual demand volume):

  • A items — top ~20% of items by count = ~80% of total dollar value. Tight controls, frequent review, low safety stock, careful forecasting.
  • B items — middle ~30% of items = ~15% of total dollar value. Moderate controls, periodic review.
  • C items — bottom ~50% of items = ~5% of total dollar value. Loose controls, high safety stock acceptable, simple reorder methods (e.g., two-bin system).
ABC classification enables differentiated management — applying the tightest controls where the most money is at stake (A items), not uniformly across all items.

ABC can be applied by criteria other than dollar value: risk of stockout, lead time, criticality to production, supplier reliability.

Inventory Costs

Four major categories of inventory cost:

  • Ordering (acquisition) costs — fixed cost per order: PO preparation, receiving, inspection. Drive large orders (EOQ logic)
  • Holding (carrying) costs — cost of storing inventory: capital tied up, storage space, obsolescence, shrinkage, insurance, taxes. Typically 20–40% of item value per year. Drive smaller orders.
  • Shortage (stockout) costs — lost sales, backorders, expediting, customer goodwill damage. Drive higher safety stock.
  • Unit (purchase) costs — price paid per unit; can vary with quantity (price breaks).
EOQ balances ordering costs (decreasing with larger orders) against holding costs (increasing with larger orders). The optimal order quantity minimizes total inventory cost.
Cycle Counting
Cycle counting: an inventory accuracy audit technique where inventory is counted on a cyclic schedule rather than once a year. More frequent counting of A items, less frequent counting of C items.

Cycle counting vs. physical inventory:

  • Cycle counting is continuous (year-round) — physical inventory is annual
  • Cycle counting does not require shutting down operations
  • Cycle counting catches and corrects errors faster, maintaining higher ongoing accuracy
  • A-item frequency example: count every 30 days; B: every 90 days; C: every 180 days
Inventory accuracy: a measure (%) of how closely on-hand records match the actual physical count. Target: typically 95%+ for MRP to function effectively.
Inventory Record Accuracy

MRP and S&OP depend on accurate inventory records. Common causes of inventory inaccuracy:

  • Unrecorded material movements (picking without transacting)
  • Scrap not reported
  • Returns not recorded
  • Counting errors
  • Timing differences between physical movement and system update

Solutions: cycle counting, real-time scanning (barcodes/RFID), disciplined transaction recording, access controls on warehouse locations.

MRP effectiveness is directly tied to inventory record accuracy. "Garbage in, garbage out" — MRP cannot generate correct requirements if the inventory data is wrong.

What is Cycle Stock?
ANSWER
The working inventory that is consumed and replenished during a regular order cycle. Average cycle stock = Q/2 for a fixed order quantity Q.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Safety Stock?
ANSWER
Buffer inventory held to protect against variability in demand and/or supply lead time. Provides a cushion against stockouts during the replenishment lead time.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Anticipation Inventory?
ANSWER
Inventory built up in advance of a predictable surge in demand — seasonal peaks, promotions, planned plant shutdowns. Traded off against holding costs.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Pipeline Inventory?
ANSWER
Inventory in transit between supply chain locations. Calculated as: Pipeline inventory = Demand rate × Transit lead time.

What is Decoupling Stock?
ANSWER
Inventory held between adjacent production operations to decouple them — allowing each to operate at its own rate independently of the other. Common at bottlenecks.

How does ABC classification work?
ANSWER
Items ranked by annual dollar usage (cost × volume). A items: ~20% of items, ~80% of value — tight controls. B items: ~30% of items, ~15% of value — moderate. C items: ~50% of items, ~5% of value — loose controls.

What is the management implication of A vs. C items?
ANSWER
A items: tight controls, frequent cycle counts, careful forecasting, low safety stock relative to value. C items: loose controls, simple reorder methods (two-bin), higher safety stock acceptable.

What are the 4 categories of inventory cost?
ANSWER
1) Ordering/acquisition costs (fixed per order), 2) Holding/carrying costs (~20–40% of item value/year), 3) Shortage/stockout costs, 4) Unit/purchase costs (may vary with quantity discounts).

What is the trade-off that EOQ balances?
ANSWER
EOQ balances ordering costs (which decrease per unit as order size increases) against holding costs (which increase with order size). The optimal order quantity minimizes total cost.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Cycle Counting?
ANSWER
An inventory accuracy audit technique where inventory is counted on a continuous, cyclic schedule rather than once per year. Frequency varies by item criticality — A items counted most often.

What is the advantage of cycle counting over annual physical inventory?
ANSWER
Cycle counting is continuous (no shutdown required), catches and corrects errors faster, maintains higher ongoing accuracy, and assigns counting frequency based on item importance (ABC).

What inventory accuracy level does MRP require?
ANSWER
Typically 95%+ accuracy is required for MRP to generate reliable requirements. Inaccurate inventory records cause MRP to over- or under-plan, leading to stockouts or excess.

What is MRO inventory?
ANSWER
Maintenance, Repair, and Operating supplies — items consumed in support of manufacturing but not incorporated into the finished product. Examples: lubricants, cleaning supplies, spare machine parts, tooling.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Hedge Inventory?
ANSWER
Inventory purchased in advance to protect against anticipated price increases or supply disruptions. A strategic buffer, not a tactical one.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are common causes of inventory inaccuracy?
ANSWER
Unrecorded movements, scrap not reported, returns not recorded, counting errors, timing differences between physical movement and system update.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the two-bin system?
ANSWER
A simple reorder technique for C items: two bins are kept for each item. When the first bin is emptied, it triggers a replenishment order; the second bin supplies demand during the replenishment lead time.
TERM / CONCEPT
What criteria other than dollar value can ABC classification use?
ANSWER
Risk of stockout, production criticality, supplier lead time or reliability, regulatory/compliance importance, or any factor that differentiates management intensity needed.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the relationship between ABC class and cycle count frequency?
ANSWER
A items are counted most frequently (e.g., every 30 days), B items periodically (e.g., every 90 days), C items least often (e.g., every 180 days). Higher-value items get tighter count discipline.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between Carrying Cost and Shortage Cost?
ANSWER
Carrying cost: cost of holding inventory (capital, space, obsolescence) — drives smaller order quantities. Shortage cost: cost of not having inventory (lost sales, expediting, damage to customer relationships) — drives higher safety stock.

Question 2
What is 'cycle stock' (cycle inventory)?
A.Inventory that completes a full production cycle
B.The portion of inventory that results from ordering in batches — it cycles between a peak (just after replenishment) and near zero (just before the next replenishment). Average cycle stock = order quantity / 2
C.Inventory held to cover seasonal demand peaks
D.Emergency stock held for supply disruptions
Cycle stock results from batch ordering — even with perfectly predictable demand, you hold an average of Q/2 units (where Q is order quantity) because you order in batches. Smaller batches reduce cycle stock but increase ordering costs.
Question 3
What is 'safety stock' and what drives the amount needed?
A.Stock held permanently as a minimum level regardless of demand
B.Buffer inventory held above cycle stock to protect against demand uncertainty and supply variability — the amount needed is driven by demand variability (standard deviation), lead time, and the desired service level
C.Stock held specifically for premium customers
D.All inventory held in safety storage conditions
Safety stock = z × σ_demand × √(lead time). The amount needed depends on three factors: demand variability (σ), lead time length (more LT = more exposure), and desired service level (higher service = higher z-factor = more safety stock).
Question 4
What is 'ABC classification' of inventory?
A.A quality grading system for raw materials
B.Classifying inventory items into three groups based on their value contribution: A-items (typically top 20% of items = 80% of value), B-items (30% of items = 15% of value), C-items (50% of items = 5% of value) — to focus management attention appropriately
C.Classifying items by their alphabetical description
D.A classification of inventory by storage location
ABC analysis (Pareto) focuses management effort: A-items deserve frequent review, tight controls, and accurate forecasting. C-items can be managed with simpler, less frequent review. The 80/20 principle ensures resources are focused where value is highest.
Question 5
What is 'dead stock' or 'obsolete inventory'?
A.Inventory stored in a basement warehouse
B.Inventory that is no longer needed or sellable — due to product discontinuation, engineering changes, or market shifts — requiring write-off or disposal
C.Inventory that has not been moved in 24 hours
D.Inventory held specifically for discontinued product service parts
Dead/obsolete stock has zero value and consumes space and working capital. It results from poor demand planning, excess ordering, late engineering changes, or product discontinuation without proper phase-out planning. Prevention is far cheaper than write-offs.
Question 6
What is 'SKU proliferation' and why is it a supply chain problem?
A.Increasing production of the fastest-moving SKUs
B.The uncontrolled growth in the number of unique Stock Keeping Units (product variants) — increasing forecast complexity, reducing volume per SKU (more safety stock per item), adding supply chain cost, and creating inventory complexity
C.Having too many suppliers for the same SKU
D.Too many distribution centers for the same SKU
SKU proliferation spreads demand across more variants, making each harder to forecast (higher CV), requiring more safety stock per SKU, increasing supplier complexity, and driving up total supply chain cost — often without proportional revenue benefit.
Question 7
What is 'inventory turnover' (or turns) and what does a high turnover rate indicate?
A.The frequency of physical inventory counts
B.COGS / Average Inventory — the number of times inventory is replenished per year. High turns indicate efficient inventory use (less cash tied up) and fast-moving stock; low turns may indicate excess or slow-moving inventory
C.The rate at which inventory is damaged
D.The speed at which goods move through the warehouse
High inventory turns mean inventory is selling/moving quickly — less working capital tied up in stock, lower obsolescence risk, higher asset efficiency. World-class retailers may turn inventory 20+ times per year; manufacturers often target 10–15 turns.
Question 8
What is 'inventory accuracy' and how is it typically measured?
A.The percentage of orders delivered accurately
B.The degree to which physical inventory quantities match the quantities recorded in the system — measured by cycle counting. High accuracy is essential for reliable MRP planning and order fulfillment
C.The accuracy of the demand forecast
D.The percentage of inventory in acceptable condition
Inventory record accuracy is the percentage of items whose actual count matches the system record. Low accuracy causes MRP to plan on wrong data — ordering unneeded materials or failing to order needed ones. 99%+ accuracy is the target for effective MRP.
Question 9
What is a 'cycle count' and how does it differ from an annual physical inventory?
A.Counting inventory at the end of each production cycle
B.A continuous inventory auditing process where a subset of items is counted on a rotating schedule (A-items more frequently) — maintaining high accuracy without shutting down operations, unlike an annual full physical count
C.Counting inventory at the end of each fiscal year
D.Counting only damaged or returned inventory
Cycle counting counts a portion of inventory every day/week (A-items most frequently) so that all items are counted regularly without halting operations. It identifies and corrects errors continuously, maintaining higher accuracy than annual physical counts.
Question 10
What is 'MRO inventory' and what makes it difficult to manage?
A.Manufactured, Ready-to-Order inventory
B.Maintenance, Repair, and Operating supplies — items needed to support operations but not incorporated into products (tools, lubricants, spare parts). MRO is hard to manage due to high SKU count, unpredictable demand, and multiple internal customers
C.Materials Returned from Operations — items returned from production
D.Minimum Reorder Quantity inventory
MRO is challenging: thousands of SKUs, highly unpredictable and often intermittent demand, many internal 'customers' (maintenance, production, facilities), high criticality (a missing spare can shut down production), and often poor data quality.
Question 11
What is 'consignment inventory' from the buyer's perspective?
A.Inventory the buyer sends to customers on consignment
B.Inventory at the buyer's facility that is owned by the supplier until consumed — the buyer pays only when using the inventory. It reduces the buyer's working capital investment and inventory risk
C.Inventory damaged during transit
D.Inventory reserved for a specific customer
Consignment inventory is a working capital benefit for the buyer — the supplier carries the inventory investment risk. The buyer has material available on-site but doesn't pay until consumption, improving cash flow and reducing inventory write-off risk.
Question 12
What drives the decision between 'make-to-stock' and 'make-to-order' inventory strategy?
A.Company size and number of employees
B.The trade-off between customer lead time requirements (MTS provides immediate availability; MTO requires waiting) and demand predictability (MTS requires accurate forecasting; MTO eliminates finished goods forecast risk)
C.The cost of raw materials
D.The location of the manufacturing facility
MTS vs. MTO depends on: (1) customer tolerance for wait time — if customers won't wait the full production lead time, stock is needed; (2) demand predictability — if demand is too variable, MTS creates too much inventory risk.
