Six Sigma Overview
Six sigma: a methodology that furnishes tools for the improvement of business processes. The intent is to decrease process variation and improve product quality by using statistical methods and disciplined problem-solving. Goal: reduce defects to 3.4 DPMO (defects per million opportunities).

Six sigma objective: achieve 6 standard deviations (σ) between the process mean and the nearest specification limit. Traditional quality targets only 3 sigma on each side of the mean.

Exhibit 4-59: Six-Sigma Control
At 3 sigma quality: ~66,807 DPMO. At 6 sigma quality: 3.4 DPMO. Six sigma represents near-perfection: 99.99966% of products defect-free. The difference is enormous in high-volume processes.
Three Elements of Six Sigma

Attaining six-sigma quality requires attention to three elements:

  • Customer — quality is defined by the customer’s acceptable defect rate. Customer expectations (voice of the customer) determine what constitutes a defect.
  • Process — process mapping and flowcharting identify unique steps. Each step is assessed for defect potential and variation. CTQ (Critical to Quality) characteristics are identified.
  • Employee — full employee participation is essential (W. Edwards Deming principle). Training occurs at multiple levels with certification options.

Six Sigma belt levels:

  • Champion — executive sponsor; removes organizational barriers
  • Master Black Belt — expert trainer and mentor; leads complex projects
  • Black Belt — leads improvement projects full-time; deep statistical expertise
  • Green Belt — participates in projects part-time; applies six sigma tools in their own area
  • Yellow Belt — basic awareness; supports team activities
DMAIC — Six Sigma Process Improvement

The five-phase six sigma methodology for improving an existing process:

  • D — Define: Determine the nature of the problem; identify the project scope, team, and customer CTQ requirements
  • M — Measure: Measure existing performance; record data and facts about the process; establish baseline capability
  • A — Analyze: Study the data to determine root causes of the problem; use statistical and graphical tools
  • I — Improve: Develop and implement solutions to the problem; eliminate root causes
  • C — Control: Monitor the process until solutions become ingrained; create control plans to sustain gains
DMAIC is for improving existing processes. DMADV (Define, Measure, Analyze, Design, Verify) is for creating new processes or products. Both require thorough, reliable, measurable data — the statistical basis of six sigma requires good data.
DMADV — Design for Six Sigma (DFSS)

The five-phase six sigma methodology for creating new processes or products:

  • D — Define: Define goals consistent with customer demands and enterprise strategy
  • M — Measure: Measure and identify CTQs (Critical to Quality), product capabilities, process capabilities, risks
  • A — Analyze: Analyze to develop and design alternatives; create a high-level design
  • D — Design: Design details, optimize, and plan for design verification
  • V — Verify: Verify the design through pilot runs, implement the production process, and hand off to process owners
Theory of Constraints (TOC)
Theory of Constraints (TOC): a management philosophy developed by Eliyahu M. Goldratt that applies to any organization. It holds that every system has at least one constraint limiting its output. Improvement efforts should focus on the constraint because it determines the maximum output of the entire system.

Core TOC principle: A chain is only as strong as its weakest link. The constraint (bottleneck) limits the entire system — improving any non-constraint yields zero system improvement. Only improving the constraint increases throughput.

There is no benefit to speeding up processes before or after the constraint. Upstream speedup creates WIP pile-up before the constraint; downstream speedup creates idle capacity after it. All improvement effort should be concentrated on the constraint.
Five-Step TOC Process
  1. Identify the constraint — find the bottleneck (look for excessive queues or WIP piling up; that’s where the constraint is)
  2. Exploit the constraint — maximize output of the constraint without major investment; ensure it is never idle, never starved, never processing bad parts
  3. Subordinate other processes to the constraint — align all non-constraint resources to support the constraint’s schedule; adjust input rates so constraint is always fed but not overwhelmed
  4. Elevate the constraint — if steps 1–3 are not sufficient, invest in increasing constraint capacity (new equipment, overtime, outsourcing)
  5. Repeat — once the constraint is resolved, a new constraint emerges; restart the five steps (continuous improvement)
The sequence matters: exploit first (free improvement), then subordinate (align everything else), then elevate (invest). Do not skip to elevation — you may be investing in something that is not the true constraint.
Drum-Buffer-Rope (DBR)
Buffer (in TOC): a quantity of materials awaiting further processing. In DBR, it protects the constraint from being starved due to variability upstream.

DBR metaphor (from the "hiker in a line" analogy):

  • Drum — the pace set by the constraint (the drumbeat of the system). All production is scheduled to the drum rate.
  • Buffer — a time buffer of WIP placed immediately before the constraint to protect it from starvation caused by upstream variability
  • Rope — the scheduling mechanism that limits the release of work into the system to match the drum rate; prevents WIP from building up excessively upstream
DBR implements the TOC principle of pulling orders through the system at the constraint’s pace. The rope prevents overloading the system; the buffer prevents starvation at the constraint.
Integrating CI Methods

Lean, Six Sigma, and TOC are complementary and can be used together:

  • TOC identifies where to improve (the constraint)
  • Lean identifies and eliminates the waste causing the problem
  • Six Sigma provides statistical tools to reduce variation at the constraint

Example: When TOC has a system running at the speed of the constraint, six sigma can be applied to reduce defects at the constraint, and lean can be applied to eliminate waste around it — each methodology contributes its strength to the overall improvement.

What are the 3 elements of Six Sigma?
ANSWER
1) Customer — defines acceptable defect rate (Voice of Customer, CTQ). 2) Process — process mapping identifies variation sources and defect potential. 3) Employee — full participation at all levels with belt certification.

What are the 5 Six Sigma belt levels?
ANSWER
Champion (executive sponsor), Master Black Belt (trainer/mentor), Black Belt (full-time project leader, deep statistics), Green Belt (part-time, own area), Yellow Belt (basic awareness, team support).

What is DMAIC?
ANSWER
The 5-phase Six Sigma methodology for improving EXISTING processes: Define (problem/scope), Measure (baseline performance), Analyze (root causes), Improve (implement solutions), Control (sustain gains with control plans).

What is DMADV?
ANSWER
The 5-phase Six Sigma methodology for creating NEW processes or products (Design for Six Sigma): Define, Measure (CTQs/risks), Analyze (develop alternatives), Design (detailed design), Verify (pilot and hand off).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between DMAIC and DMADV?
ANSWER
DMAIC: improves an existing process (find and fix problems). DMADV: designs a new process or product to meet six sigma quality from the start. DMAIC is the more common application.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Theory of Constraints (TOC)?
ANSWER
A management philosophy by Eliyahu Goldratt holding that every system has at least one constraint limiting its output. Improvement must focus on the constraint — it determines maximum system output. A chain is only as strong as its weakest link.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 5 steps of the TOC process?
ANSWER
1) Identify the constraint, 2) Exploit the constraint (maximize its output without major investment), 3) Subordinate other processes to the constraint, 4) Elevate the constraint (invest if needed), 5) Repeat (new constraint will emerge).
TERM / CONCEPT
Why should you Exploit before Elevating in TOC?
ANSWER
Exploitation extracts maximum output from the constraint for free — by ensuring it never idles, never starves, and never processes defects. Only if exploitation is insufficient should you invest in elevation (new equipment, overtime).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Drum in Drum-Buffer-Rope (DBR)?
ANSWER
The pace set by the constraint — the drumbeat of the entire system. All production scheduling is aligned to the drum rate. The constraint’s schedule dictates the rhythm of the whole factory.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Buffer in Drum-Buffer-Rope?
ANSWER
A time buffer of WIP placed immediately before the constraint to protect it from starvation caused by variability or disruptions upstream. Ensures the constraint is always fed and never idle.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Rope in Drum-Buffer-Rope?
ANSWER
The scheduling mechanism that limits the release of work into the system to match the drum (constraint) rate. Prevents WIP from building up excessively upstream; the rope ties the input rate to the constraint’s pace.
TERM / CONCEPT
What does TOC say about improving non-constraint processes?
ANSWER
There is no benefit to improving non-constraint processes — the system output is still limited by the constraint. Upstream speedup creates WIP pile-up before the constraint; downstream speedup creates idle capacity. All effort should focus on the constraint.
TERM / CONCEPT
How do Lean, Six Sigma, and TOC complement each other?
ANSWER
TOC identifies WHERE to improve (the constraint). Lean identifies and eliminates WASTE causing the problem. Six Sigma provides statistical tools to reduce VARIATION at the constraint. Together they cover focus, waste, and variability.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the Critical to Quality (CTQ) in Six Sigma?
ANSWER
Product or process characteristics that are most important to the customer — where a defect would cause customer dissatisfaction. CTQs are the starting point for six sigma projects: if it’s not CTQ, it’s not worth a six sigma project.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a Constraint in TOC?
ANSWER
Any factor that limits a system from achieving higher performance. Most visible indicator: an excessive queue or WIP pile-up immediately before it. Also called a bottleneck. Every system has at least one constraint.
TERM / CONCEPT
What happens to the constraint after you successfully Elevate it in TOC?
ANSWER
The original constraint is resolved, but a new constraint emerges elsewhere in the system. TOC is a continuous improvement cycle — step 5 (Repeat) means you restart the 5-step process to find and work on the new constraint.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Subordination in TOC?
ANSWER
Adjusting all non-constraint resources and processes to support the constraint’s schedule and maximize its output. Non-constraints should run only fast enough to feed the constraint — running faster creates unnecessary WIP and queues.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the core statistical requirement of Six Sigma?
ANSWER
Thorough, reliable, measurable data. Six sigma is statistically based — without good data, you cannot measure baseline performance (Measure phase), identify root causes (Analyze), or verify improvements (Control). 'If you can’t measure it, you can’t improve it.'

Question 4
What is 'throughput accounting' in TOC?
A.Standard cost accounting for manufacturing
B.A TOC accounting approach that focuses on three metrics: Throughput (rate of revenue generation), Inventory/Investment (money tied up in the system), and Operating Expense (all money spent). It challenges traditional cost accounting's focus on local efficiency
C.A method for calculating overhead absorption rates
D.A customer profitability analysis method
Throughput accounting reframes performance measurement: maximize Throughput (T), minimize Inventory (I), minimize Operating Expense (OE). Unlike standard cost accounting, it does not penalize idle time (building excess inventory is the real waste, not idle capacity).
Question 5
What is the 'drum-buffer-rope' (DBR) in TOC scheduling?
A.A production music system for worker motivation
B.A TOC-based scheduling method: Drum (the constraint sets the production pace), Buffer (time/inventory buffer protects the constraint from variability), Rope (coordinates the release of materials to feed the constraint at the right time)
C.A safety system for production equipment
D.A quality control method for defect detection
DBR translates TOC into a scheduling method: the constraint (drum) sets the beat for the whole system. Time buffers protect the constraint. The rope synchronizes material release to the constraint's consumption rate — preventing both starvation and overloading of the bottleneck.
Question 6
What distinguishes 'improvement projects' (e.g., Six Sigma) from 'continuous improvement culture' (e.g., Kaizen)?
A.Six Sigma is easier to implement; Kaizen is more expensive
B.Improvement projects are periodic, significant, expert-led initiatives targeting breakthrough improvements (DMAIC projects); continuous improvement culture drives daily incremental improvements from all employees — both are needed for sustained performance
C.They address the same types of problems
D.Projects target cost; culture targets quality
Six Sigma projects tackle complex, significant problems requiring statistical expertise — breakthrough improvements. Kaizen cultivates daily small improvements from all workers — incremental gains that compound. Best organizations use both: projects for breakthroughs, culture for continuous small improvements.
Question 7

Hoshin Kanri (or policy deployment) ensures that strategic priorities are translated into specific improvement targets at each organizational level, with two-way dialogue ('catch-ball') to create realistic, aligned plans. It connects daily improvement activity to strategic goals.
Question 8
What is 'benchmarking' in continuous improvement?
A.Setting internal performance targets
B.Comparing an organization's processes and performance against best-in-class peers or industry leaders — to identify performance gaps, learn from better practices, and set ambitious improvement targets

Benchmarking identifies gaps between current performance and best-in-class. It provides external reference points for improvement targets (not just improving on last year's performance) and can reveal innovative practices from other industries.
Question 9
What is 'A3 thinking' in lean and continuous improvement?
A.A paper size standard for documentation
B.A Toyota problem-solving and communication methodology using a structured one-page (A3 paper) format: problem statement, current state, target state, root cause analysis, countermeasures, implementation plan, and expected results
C.A quality audit scoring methodology

A3 thinking (named for the A3 paper size) disciplines problem-solving: everything important must fit on one page, forcing clarity and conciseness. The structured format ensures systematic root cause analysis and a clear, testable improvement plan.
Question 10
What is 'mistake-proofing' (poka-yoke) in the context of continuous improvement?
A.Correcting mistakes after they are made
B.Designing processes or devices that prevent errors from occurring in the first place — eliminating the root cause of recurring defects rather than inspecting or reworking after they occur
C.Training employees to avoid mistakes
D.A quality inspection technique for detecting defects
Poka-yoke builds quality into the process: design the process so the error cannot be made, or so that the error is immediately detected before causing a defect. It is more effective than inspection and training because it addresses the physical root cause.
Question 11
What is the relationship between lean and Six Sigma?
A.Lean is better than Six Sigma for all applications
B.Lean focuses on eliminating waste and improving flow; Six Sigma focuses on reducing variation and defects. 'Lean Six Sigma' combines both — using lean tools for flow improvement and Six Sigma's DMAIC for data-driven defect reduction
C.They address completely different problems and cannot be combined
D.Six Sigma is a subset of lean manufacturing
Lean and Six Sigma are complementary: Lean removes non-value-added activity and creates flow; Six Sigma reduces variation in the value-added activities that remain. Lean Six Sigma combines both toolsets — most improvement programs use elements of each.
Question 12
What is 'total productive maintenance' (TPM)?
A.A maintenance budget planning system
B.A maintenance philosophy that involves all employees (including production operators) in maintaining equipment — targeting zero breakdowns, zero defects, and zero accidents through proactive, preventive, and autonomous maintenance
C.A system for managing maintenance work orders

TPM engages operators in basic maintenance (cleaning, inspection, lubrication) and promotes preventive maintenance — reducing unplanned downtime, improving OEE, and creating shared ownership of equipment performance between maintenance and production teams.
