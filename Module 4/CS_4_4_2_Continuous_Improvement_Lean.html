Waste (Muda) and the 8 Deadly Wastes
Waste (muda): (1) Any activity that does not add value to the good or service in the eyes of the consumer. (2) A by-product of a process or task with unique characteristics requiring special management control.

Toyota’s Taiichi Ohno identified 7 deadly wastes; the 8th (unused talent) was added later:

  • Transportation — unnecessary movement of materials
  • Inventory — excess stock beyond immediate needs
  • Motion — unnecessary movement of people
  • Waiting — idle time waiting for materials, machines, or information
  • Overproduction — producing more or sooner than needed (the worst waste — causes other wastes)
  • Overprocessing — doing more work than required by the customer
  • Defects — scrap, rework, returns
  • Unused talent/skills — not leveraging employee knowledge and creativity (8th waste)
Mnemonic: TIM WOOD U. Overproduction is the worst waste because it causes all other wastes (excess transportation, inventory, motion, waiting).
Three Broad Types of Waste (Muda, Mura, Muri)
Muda: waste — any activity consuming resources without adding value. The 8 wastes described by Toyota are all forms of muda.
Mura: unevenness or irregularity in the production process — variation in workloads that causes some periods to be rushed and others to be idle.
Muri: overburden — pushing workers or machines beyond their natural limits; creates stress, fatigue, breakdowns, and safety issues.
Mura and muri are often the root causes of muda. Fixing muda symptoms without addressing mura/muri leads to recurring waste. Management must address all three.
Lean Philosophy and Definition
Lean (lean production): a philosophy of production that emphasizes the minimization of the amount of all the resources (including time) used in the various activities of the enterprise. It involves identifying and eliminating non-value-adding activities.

8 Lean Objectives:

  • Eliminate waste in business value streams
  • Meet customer demand (pull-based production)
  • Increase velocity (shorten lead times)
  • Reduce need for working capital
  • Increase inventory turns
  • Gain market share through quality and speed
  • Increase profitability by reducing non-value-added costs
  • Develop the workforce through empowerment and training

House of Toyota

The House of Toyota (House of Lean) is a framework explaining the scope of lean. It has four parts:

  • Foundation — operational stability; achieved through load leveling (heijunka), standardized work, and kaizen
  • Left pillar: Just-in-Time (JIT) — right items, right time, right place, right amount; takt time, one-piece flow, pull systems
  • Right pillar: Jidoka — quality at the source; automation with a human touch; stop production when defects occur
  • Roof — best quality, lowest cost, shortest lead time (the goals of the system)
Heijunka: leveling the type and quantity of production over a fixed time period. Enables use of standard work and smooth pull systems by smoothing demand variability on the shop floor.
Kaizen: continuing improvement involving everyone — managers and workers. In manufacturing, kaizen relates to finding and eliminating waste in machinery, labor, or production methods.
Jidoka: automation with a human touch; the ability of a production line to be stopped — by a machine or a worker — when a defect is detected. Prevents defects from flowing to the next process.

Lean Tools and Techniques

Value Stream Mapping (VSM): a paper-and-pencil tool that maps the flow of material and information for a product family from supplier to customer. Reveals where waste exists; used to design future-state lean flows.

Kaizen Event / Kaizen Blitz:

  • Kaizen event: a scheduled, time-boxed improvement event (typically 5 days) involving a cross-functional team; starts and completes a focused improvement
  • Kaizen blitz: a more intense version covering a limited process area; rapid improvement of a production cell; includes basic training, analysis (flowchart), and implementation

Five Ss (5S Workplace Organization):

  • Seiri — Sort: remove unnecessary items from the workplace
  • Seiton — Set in order: organize and label what remains for easy access
  • Seiso — Shine: clean the workplace and equipment
  • Seiketsu — Standardize: create standards for the first three Ss
  • Shitsuke — Sustain: maintain discipline to keep the system working

Setup Time Reduction: 5 steps to reduce changeover time:

  • 1. Classify internal vs. external tasks; 2. Convert internal to external; 3. Streamline internal setup; 4. Eliminate adjustments; 5. Minimize external prep time
Internal tasks must be done while the machine is stopped; external tasks can be done while running. Converting internal to external is the highest-leverage setup reduction step.
Total Productive Maintenance (TPM)
Total Productive Maintenance (TPM): preventive maintenance plus continuing efforts to adapt, modify, and refine equipment to increase flexibility, reduce material handling, and promote continuous flows. It is operator-centered maintenance with the goal of equipment reliability through worker ownership.

TPM aims to eliminate:

  • Equipment breakdowns (unplanned downtime)
  • Setup and adjustment losses
  • Idling and minor stops
  • Reduced speed
  • Quality defects and rework
  • Startup losses

TPM engages all levels and functions — operators perform routine maintenance; maintenance department handles complex repairs. Shifts from reactive to proactive maintenance culture.

Just-in-Time (JIT)
Just-in-Time (JIT): a philosophy of manufacturing based on planned elimination of all waste and on continuous improvement of productivity. It has as its principal objectives having only the required inventory when needed; to improve quality to zero defects; to reduce lead times by reducing setup times, queue lengths, and lot sizes; and to incrementally revise the operations themselves.

3 JIT basics:

  • Waste reduction — eliminate storage, inspections, queues, and defects
  • Variability reduction — reduce demand and supply variability through standardization and supplier development
  • Pull production — items pulled into production only when demanded; not pushed by a preset schedule
Kanban: a system of cards or signals used in JIT to authorize the movement or production of materials. When a work center is ready for more material, it sends a kanban signal upstream. Only the rate of customer demand drives the system.

Poka-yoke: a mistake-proofing device or procedure that prevents a defect from being produced. Supports jidoka by making errors impossible or immediately detectable. Comes from Japanese "poka" (inadvertent errors) + "yoke" (avoid).

Question 1
What is 'kaizen' in the context of lean manufacturing?
A.A Japanese term for quality defect
B.A Japanese philosophy and practice of continuous, incremental improvement — involving all employees in identifying and eliminating waste from processes
C.A production scheduling algorithm
D.A lean manufacturing software system
Kaizen (Japanese for 'change for good') is the practice of continuous incremental improvement, typically involving frontline workers in suggesting and implementing small improvements daily. Small improvements compound over time into significant performance gains.
Question 2
What are the 5S principles of lean manufacturing?
A.Speed, Simplicity, Safety, Sustainability, Standards
B.Sort (eliminate unnecessary items), Set in order (organize), Shine (clean), Standardize (maintain), Sustain (make it habitual) — a systematic approach to creating and maintaining an organized, efficient, and safe work environment
C.Sell, Source, Ship, Store, Serve
D.Strategy, Structure, Systems, Skills, Style
5S creates the foundation for lean: Sort (remove waste/clutter), Set in order (everything has a place), Shine (clean reveals problems), Standardize (create standard work), Sustain (make 5S a habit). A visual, organized workplace enables waste identification and process improvement.
Question 3
What is 'value stream mapping' (VSM) and how is it used?
A.Mapping the financial value of each supply chain activity
B.A lean tool that visually maps the current state of material and information flows through a process — identifying value-added activities, non-value-added activities, waste, and delays — then designing an improved future state
C.A tool for mapping supplier relationships
D.A map of distribution center locations
VSM (from Toyota) maps the entire value stream from raw material to customer. It distinguishes value-added time (what the customer pays for) from non-value-added time (wait, queue, transport, over-processing) — providing a clear picture of where waste exists and how to eliminate it.
Question 4
What is the 'Plan-Do-Check-Act' (PDCA) cycle?
A.A production planning cycle with four phases
B.A continuous improvement cycle: Plan (identify problem, develop solution), Do (implement on a small scale), Check (evaluate results), Act (standardize if successful, or restart cycle) — the foundation of systematic improvement
C.A quality inspection process
D.A strategic planning methodology
PDCA (Deming Cycle) is the structured improvement process: Plan (define the problem and countermeasure), Do (implement the change), Check (measure results against expectation), Act (standardize successful changes or restart if unsuccessful). It creates disciplined, evidence-based improvement.
Question 5
What is the primary difference between 'push' and 'pull' production systems?
A.Push is more expensive; pull is always cheaper
B.Push produces based on forecasts, pushing inventory forward; pull produces only in response to actual downstream demand (kanban signals) — pull minimizes WIP, inventory, and overproduction waste
C.Push is used in services; pull is used in manufacturing only
D.Pull always produces higher quality products than push
Push systems build to forecast (risk of overproduction if demand differs from forecast). Pull systems respond to actual consumption signals (kanban) — producing exactly what is consumed, when it is consumed, eliminating overproduction — lean's most fundamental waste.
Question 6
What is 'standard work' in lean manufacturing?
A.The minimum work an employee is required to perform
B.A documented, agreed-upon best current method for performing each task — specifying exactly what operations to perform, in what sequence, with what materials and tools, in what time — the foundation for consistent quality and continuous improvement
C.A work scheduling standard
D.The standard production speed for a machine
Standard work documents the best current method for performing each task. It: (1) defines how work is done consistently (preventing variation), (2) provides a baseline for improvement, and (3) enables training. Without standard work, improvement is impossible — you can't improve what isn't standardized.
Question 7
What is 'takt time' in lean manufacturing?
A.The time taken by the fastest worker to complete a task
B.Available production time divided by customer demand rate — the rhythm that production must match to meet customer demand without over- or under-producing. Takt time = Available time / Customer demand
C.The cycle time of the bottleneck machine
D.The time required to set up a production line
Takt time (German for 'beat') is the pace of customer demand: if customers buy 100 units/day and 480 minutes are available, takt = 4.8 min/unit. Production must be paced to takt — too fast creates inventory; too slow creates stock-outs.
Question 8
What is 'heijunka' (production leveling) in lean?
A.A Japanese quality control technique
B.Leveling the production schedule by type and volume over a period — spreading different product types and quantities evenly rather than building in large batches. Heijunka enables pull, reduces WIP, and smooths demand on suppliers
C.A visual management system for tracking defects
D.A setup reduction methodology
Heijunka smooths production: instead of making 200 Type A on Monday and 200 Type B on Tuesday, make some of each type every day. This enables pull/kanban, reduces WIP inventory, improves equipment utilization, and sends smoother demand signals to suppliers.
Question 9
What is 'poka-yoke' in lean and continuous improvement?
A.A Japanese philosophy of continuous improvement
B.An error-proofing device or method that prevents errors from occurring, or makes errors immediately obvious before they cause defects — eliminating the need for inspection of that error type
C.A type of visual management board
D.A standard work documentation method
Poka-yoke (mistake-proofing) eliminates the possibility of making a specific error: asymmetric connectors, color-coded components, sensors that won't allow the next step if a part is missing. It builds quality into the process rather than inspecting quality in.
Question 10
What is 'gemba' in lean philosophy?
A.A Japanese term for headquarters
B.'The real place' — where actual work happens (the shop floor, the warehouse, the customer location). Gemba walks involve managers going to the actual workplace to observe processes, understand problems, and support workers directly
C.A lean manufacturing software system
D.A quality certification program
Gemba walk is the practice of going to where work actually happens to observe, understand, and improve. Managers who only rely on reports miss the waste, problems, and opportunities that are visible only at the gemba — the actual workplace.
Question 11
What is 'overall equipment effectiveness' (OEE) and how does it support lean?
A.A measure of employee performance
B.Availability × Performance × Quality — measuring what percentage of planned production time a machine is producing good parts at the designed speed. OEE identifies hidden capacity losses (downtime, speed losses, quality losses) for targeted improvement
C.The ratio of production output to raw material input
D.A machine reliability metric used only for maintenance planning
OEE reveals three types of loss: Availability losses (breakdowns, changeovers), Performance losses (running below design speed), and Quality losses (defects and rework). By measuring all three, OEE guides improvement efforts to their highest-value opportunities.
Question 12
What is 'andon' in lean production?
A.A Japanese performance bonus system
B.A visual and/or audible signal system that allows any worker to stop the production line or alert management when a quality problem or equipment issue is detected — enabling rapid response before defects propagate
C.A lean planning software tool
D.A daily quality review meeting
Andon enables immediate escalation of problems: any worker can pull the andon cord to stop the line or call for help. The principle (jidoka — autonomation) is to stop and fix problems immediately rather than passing defects downstream.
