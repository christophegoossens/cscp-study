Capacity Planning Overview

Capacity planning verifies that the production plan can be executed with available resources. Each MPC planning level has a corresponding capacity check:

  • Resource Planning — long-range; checks S&OP plan against aggregate resource requirements (people, machines, facilities)
  • Rough-Cut Capacity Planning (RCCP) — medium-range; validates MPS against critical work centers using a bill of resources
  • Capacity Requirements Planning (CRP) — short-range; detailed check of MRP-planned orders against all work center capacities
  • Capacity Control / Input-Output Control — execution level; monitors actual vs. planned throughput at work centers
Capacity: the capability of a worker, machine, work center, plant, or organization to produce output per time period. Can be expressed in units, dollars, hours, etc.
Rated capacity: the expected output capability of a resource or system. Determined from historical performance or engineering standards. Rated capacity = demonstrated capacity adjusted for efficiency and utilization.
Capacity Measurement
  • Design capacity — maximum output under ideal conditions (theoretical maximum)
  • Demonstrated capacity — historical average output actually achieved
  • Rated (nominal) capacity — expected output = design capacity × efficiency × utilization
Utilization: a measure of how intensively a resource is being used. Utilization = (actual hours worked / available hours) × 100%.
Efficiency: the ratio of standard hours earned to actual hours worked. Efficiency > 100% means workers are outperforming standard; <100% means underperforming.
Rated capacity = Available hours × Utilization × Efficiency. This is the realistic, achievable capacity used for planning.
Resource Planning (Long-Range)

Resource planning supports the S&OP process by checking aggregate capacity requirements against long-range forecasts:

  • Uses resource requirements profiles — historical ratios of resources needed per unit of output
  • Output: capacity gaps requiring strategic investment (hiring, new equipment, facilities)
  • Horizon: 1–5 years
  • Granularity: product families, not individual items
Rough-Cut Capacity Planning (RCCP)
Rough-cut capacity planning (RCCP): the process of converting the master production schedule into requirements for key resources, often including labor, machinery, warehouse space, suppliers’ capabilities, and money. Compares these requirements to available capacity.

Three RCCP methods (from least to most detailed):

  • Overall factors — uses a single aggregate ratio (e.g., hours per unit of output); fastest but least accurate
  • Capacity bills — uses time per unit at each critical work center; more accurate
  • Resource profiles — time-phased load profiles by resource; most accurate of the three RCCP methods
RCCP checks only key/bottleneck resources — not every work center. It is faster than CRP. If RCCP reveals overload, the master scheduler revises the MPS before MRP is run.
Capacity Requirements Planning (CRP)
Capacity requirements planning (CRP): the function of establishing, measuring, and adjusting limits or levels of capacity. The process of determining the amount of labor and machine resources required to accomplish the tasks of production (planned and released orders) in more detail.

CRP process:

  • Takes planned and released orders from MRP
  • Uses routing data (operations, work centers, setup times, run times) to calculate load at each work center
  • Produces a load profile comparing planned load vs. available capacity by period
  • Identifies overloads (queues build up) and underloads (idle capacity)

Responding to CRP imbalances:

  • Overload options: overtime, subcontracting, split lots, alternate routing, shift orders earlier/later
  • Underload options: pull orders forward, cross-training, preventive maintenance
Capacity Strategies

Organizations choose how to position capacity relative to demand:

  • Lead strategy — add capacity in advance of demand growth; risks excess capacity if demand does not materialize; maximizes service level
  • Lag strategy — add capacity only after demand has materialized; lower risk but may lose sales during ramp-up
  • Match strategy — add capacity in small increments as demand increases; balances risk and service
Load leveling (capacity smoothing): spreading orders out in time or rescheduling operations so that the amount of work to be done in sequential time periods tends to be distributed evenly and is achievable given available capacity.
Input-Output Control

Input-output (I/O) control is a capacity management tool used at the execution level to monitor the queue at a work center:

  • Input rate — work arriving at a work center per period
  • Output rate — work completed at the work center per period
  • Queue (WIP) — work waiting to be processed; grows when input > output
Little’s Law: Queue = Input rate × Lead time. To reduce lead time, reduce the queue by either increasing output or decreasing input.

I/O report shows: planned input, actual input, cumulative deviation; planned output, actual output, cumulative deviation; and resulting queue.

What is Rated Capacity?
ANSWER
The expected output capability of a resource. Rated capacity = Available hours × Utilization × Efficiency. It is the realistic achievable capacity used for planning (vs. design/theoretical max).

What is the difference between Utilization and Efficiency?
ANSWER
Utilization = actual hours worked / available hours. Efficiency = standard hours earned / actual hours worked. Both multiply to give effective output rate relative to design capacity.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 4 capacity planning levels in the MPC hierarchy?
ANSWER
1) Resource Planning (S&OP level, 1–5 year), 2) RCCP (MPS level), 3) CRP (MRP level), 4) Capacity Control / Input-Output Control (execution level).
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Rough-Cut Capacity Planning (RCCP)?
ANSWER
A medium-range process converting the MPS into requirements for key resources and comparing them to available capacity. Checks only critical/bottleneck work centers — faster than CRP but less detailed.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 3 RCCP methods from least to most detailed?
ANSWER
1) Overall factors (single aggregate ratio), 2) Capacity bills (time per unit at each critical work center), 3) Resource profiles (time-phased load by resource — most accurate).

What is Capacity Requirements Planning (CRP)?
ANSWER
The function of establishing, measuring, and adjusting capacity levels by determining the amount of labor and machine resources required to accomplish planned and released orders at each work center.

How does CRP differ from RCCP?
ANSWER
CRP is more detailed: it checks all work centers using routing data for all MRP-planned orders. RCCP checks only key resources at the MPS level using a bill of resources. CRP runs after RCCP.

What is a Load Profile?
ANSWER
A chart showing planned load vs. available capacity at a work center by time period. Used to identify overloads and underloads so planners can take corrective action.

What options exist to resolve a CRP overload?
ANSWER
Overtime, subcontracting, splitting lots, using alternate routings, shifting orders forward or backward in time, increasing staffing.

What is Input-Output (I/O) Control?
ANSWER
An execution-level capacity management tool that monitors work arriving at (input) and leaving (output) a work center. Queue grows when input > output; I/O control helps manage lead times by balancing the two.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Little's Law?
ANSWER
Queue = Input rate × Lead time. To reduce lead time, reduce the queue by either increasing throughput (output rate) or decreasing input rate.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the difference between Design Capacity and Demonstrated Capacity?
ANSWER
Design capacity: maximum output under ideal/theoretical conditions. Demonstrated capacity: historical average actually achieved. Demonstrated capacity is usually lower and more realistic for planning.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Resource Planning?
ANSWER
The long-range capacity check supporting S&OP. Uses resource requirements profiles to compare aggregate demand for resources (people, machines, facilities) against forecasted output volumes. Horizon: 1–5 years.
TERM / CONCEPT
What happens when RCCP reveals an overload?
ANSWER
The master scheduler revises the MPS before running MRP — either by shifting production, reducing quantities, or increasing capacity (overtime, subcontracting). MRP is not run on an infeasible MPS.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a Bill of Resources?
ANSWER
A list of key resources (work centers, labor types) and the time required per unit of a master schedule item. Used in RCCP to quickly estimate load on bottleneck resources.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is CRP more accurate than RCCP?
ANSWER
CRP uses detailed routing data for every planned and released order across all work centers; RCCP uses a bill of resources for only key/bottleneck resources and does not check every operation.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the relationship between queue and lead time?
ANSWER
Longer queues lead to longer lead times. In MRP systems, lead times are often fixed — but in reality they vary with queue length. Reducing queue reduces actual lead time.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is Capacity Control at the execution level?
ANSWER
Monitoring actual vs. planned throughput at work centers during production execution. Uses I/O reports to detect when queues are building and take corrective action to maintain schedule.

Question 1
What is 'capacity' in manufacturing planning?
A.The physical size of a production facility
B.The maximum rate of output that a facility, work center, or resource can achieve in a given time period under normal conditions
C.The total number of employees in the factory
D.The annual production budget
Capacity is the maximum sustainable output rate — the upper limit of what a resource can produce per unit time. It is the foundation for capacity planning, which matches this limit to production requirements.
Question 2
What is the difference between 'demonstrated capacity' and 'rated capacity'?
A.They are identical terms used in different industries
B.Rated capacity is the theoretical maximum based on the number of hours available; demonstrated capacity is what the resource actually produces on average, accounting for efficiency, downtime, and scrap
C.Rated capacity is always lower than demonstrated capacity
D.Demonstrated capacity is calculated from engineering standards only
Rated (or theoretical) capacity is calculated from hours × machines × efficiency rate. Demonstrated capacity is measured from actual historical output — it's what the resource actually produces, not what it theoretically should. It is typically lower than rated.
Question 3
What is 'Capacity Requirements Planning' (CRP)?
A.A rough estimate of capacity needs during S&OP
B.A detailed, work-center-level analysis that calculates the labor and machine capacity required by the MRP-generated production orders — identifying overloads and underloads at each work center in each time period
C.A method for reducing production changeover times
D.A capacity planning tool for the supply chain network
CRP (Level 3 in the planning hierarchy, after RCCP) takes MRP-planned production orders, applies routings and run times, and calculates exact capacity loading at each work center — revealing detailed capacity constraints for the planning horizon.
Question 4
What is the 'theory of constraints' (TOC) applied to capacity planning?
A.A theory that all constraints should be eliminated simultaneously
B.A methodology that identifies the single binding constraint (bottleneck) that limits total system throughput — and focuses all improvement efforts on that constraint first
C.A theory that more machines always improve output
D.A method for calculating economic order quantities
TOC (Goldratt) identifies the system constraint (the weakest link). Improvement anywhere else is wasted — only improving the constraint increases total system output. Once the constraint is resolved, a new constraint emerges and the process repeats.
Question 5
What is 'setup time' and how does it affect effective capacity?
A.The time to set up the computer system used in production
B.The time required to prepare a machine or work center for a new production run (changeover) — during setup, the machine produces nothing, reducing effective output capacity
C.The time for workers to arrive at the factory
D.The time to receive materials from suppliers
Setup time consumes capacity without producing output. High setup times reduce effective capacity (fewer production runs possible) and drive larger batch sizes to amortize the setup. SMED (Single Minute Exchange of Die) targets setup time reduction.
Question 6
What is 'overall equipment effectiveness' (OEE)?
A.A measure of employee productivity
B.A composite metric: Availability × Performance × Quality — measuring what percentage of planned production time a machine is producing good parts at the designed rate. OEE = 100% only when a machine runs all planned time, at full speed, with no defects
C.The percentage of time a machine is running
D.The ratio of actual to theoretical output
OEE measures machine effectiveness comprehensively: Availability (running vs. planned), Performance (actual vs. design speed), and Quality (good units vs. total units). World-class OEE is typically 85%+.
Question 7
What is the 'utilization' of a work center and what is an optimal utilization rate?
A.Utilization should always be maximized to 100%
B.Utilization = actual time used / available time. Optimal utilization is typically 80–85% for non-bottleneck resources — running at 100% eliminates buffer capacity and causes queuing delays everywhere when any variability occurs
C.Utilization above 90% is always desirable
D.Utilization is only relevant for human labor
Queuing theory shows that as utilization approaches 100%, queue times (wait times) increase exponentially. Running at 80–85% provides buffer capacity to absorb variability without creating bottlenecks — critical for maintaining flow.
Question 8
What is 'finite capacity scheduling' vs. 'infinite capacity scheduling'?
A.Finite scheduling is used for small facilities; infinite for large ones
B.Infinite capacity scheduling ignores capacity limits (producing a plan that may be infeasible); finite capacity scheduling respects resource limits and generates only feasible plans — but is more complex to solve
C.Finite scheduling is only used for labor planning
D.Infinite scheduling always produces the better plan
Standard MRP uses infinite capacity (plans requirements without checking feasibility). Finite capacity scheduling constrains the plan to available capacity — producing a feasible schedule but requiring more sophisticated optimization software.
Question 9
What is 'demand management' as a capacity planning strategy?
A.Using pricing and promotions to eliminate demand
B.Using demand shaping tools (pricing, lead time, promotions) to shift demand toward periods of available capacity — evening out demand peaks and valleys to better match production capacity
C.Managing customer orders to prevent demand spikes
D.Using forecasts to plan production capacity
Demand management as a capacity strategy uses pricing incentives, extended lead times, or promotions to shift demand from constrained periods (where capacity is insufficient) to periods of available capacity — smoothing production without adding capital.
Question 10
What is 'surge capacity' and when is it relevant?
A.The maximum capacity during a normal shift
B.Additional capacity activated only during peak demand periods — overtime, temporary workers, subcontracting — that is more expensive than normal capacity but enables the business to handle seasonal peaks or sudden demand surges
C.Emergency capacity used during equipment breakdowns
D.The capacity available on the third production shift
Surge capacity is flex capacity kept in reserve for peaks: weekend overtime, temp workers, outsourced production. It costs more than base capacity but avoids the waste of permanent overcapacity that sits idle most of the year.
Question 11
What is 'lead time' in a production context and why does it matter for capacity planning?
A.The time from order to customer delivery
B.The time required to complete a production operation or entire manufacturing process — lead times determine how far in advance MRP must plan, how much WIP is created, and the maximum responsiveness of the manufacturing system
C.The time required to train a new operator
D.The time between production planning and execution
Manufacturing lead time (queue + setup + run + wait + move time) determines planning horizons and WIP levels. Reducing lead time improves responsiveness, reduces WIP investment, and allows planning horizons to shorten — reducing forecast dependency.
Question 12
What does 'chase strategy' vs. 'level strategy' mean in capacity planning?
A.Chase = maintaining constant inventory; Level = varying production to match demand
B.Chase strategy: adjust production output to match demand each period (varying workforce/hours); Level strategy: maintain constant production output and use inventory to buffer demand fluctuations — each has different cost implications
C.Chase is used in manufacturing; Level is used in services
D.Level strategy always has lower total cost
Chase (exact quantity strategy) varies production rate with demand — low inventory but high capacity change costs (hiring/firing, overtime). Level strategy (constant production) builds inventory in low-demand periods — low change costs but high inventory cost.
