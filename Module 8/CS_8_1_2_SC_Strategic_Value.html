Aligning Business and Supply Chain Strategies

Organizational market/environment analysis → Mission and goals → Organizational strategy → Business strategy → Functional strategies (including supply chain) → SC capabilities → continuous loop.

Exhibit 8-5: Aligning Organizational, Business, and Supply Chain Strategies

Supply chain strategies need the same elements as a business model: value proposition, core competencies, cost structure, and revenue model.

Value Chains and Value Stream Mapping
Value stream (ASCM): The processes of creating, producing, and delivering a good or service to the market. For a good, encompasses the raw material supplier, the manufacturer, and the distributor. For a service, encompasses all processes from concept to delivery.
Value stream mapping (ASCM): A lean production tool to visually understand the flow of materials from supplier to customer that includes the current process and flow as well as the value-added and non-value-added activities.

Types of Value & Stakeholders

Supply chains should create three types of value for both for-profit and non-profit organizations:

  • Financial value: Improved bottom line, stable cash flows, increased sales through customer-desired value
  • Customer value: Meeting customer needs for product/service quality, reliability, availability, and cost
  • Social value: Contributions to community, workers, environment, and broader society

Stakeholders and their value priorities:

  • Companies in SC: Profit margin, market share, revenues, expenses, image/reputation
  • End customers: Affordable and reliable goods/services
  • Investors/lenders: Capital growth, dividends, interest payments, return of capital
  • Communities/governments: Community well-being, environmental protection, jobs, tax base
Supply chain activities that benefit one stakeholder may harm another. Managing these tradeoffs is a core supply chain challenge.
Core Competencies & Excellence

A supply chain strategy builds off its value proposition and core competencies to add financial value. Core competencies are areas where the organization or its SC performs distinctively better than the competition.

Excellence in Customer Service

Customer service (ASCM): The ability of a company to address the needs, inquiries, and requests of customers. Includes availability, operational performance, and customer satisfaction.
  • Availability: Having the product where and when the customer wants it
  • Operational performance: Elapsed time from order to delivery
  • Customer satisfaction: Customer perceptions, expectations, and opinions based on their experience

Effectiveness vs. Efficiency

Efficiency (ASCM): A measurement (usually expressed as a percentage) of the actual output relative to the standard output expected. Inward-focused; measures how well something does what it is supposed to do.
  • Effectiveness: Doing the right things (achieving business objectives with the right resources)
  • Efficiency: Doing things right (maximizing output relative to inputs, increasing capacity)

Resolving Misalignments & Financial Modeling

Types of Misalignment

  • Dynamic alignment: Adapting the SC to expected future changes (pursue cost efficiency without sacrificing flexibility; watch consumer-end trends; design for modularity)
  • Complexity alignment: SC should be only as complex as needed (reduce SKUs, limit product lines to maximize profit, not just revenue)
  • Partner alignment: Use channel master’s influence to promote collaboration over control

Financial Modeling for Supply Chain Strategy

  • Determines financial feasibility and ROI of SC strategy or product/service strategy
  • Uses Net Present Value (NPV): Future cash inflows discounted to present values minus present values of all outflows
  • Uses Discounted payback period: How long to break even, discounting future cash flows to reflect the time value of money
  • Economic value added (EVA): Measures whether the organization is creating more value than the cost of all capital employed

Network Modeling and Operations Research

Operations research (OR) (ASCM): 1) The development and application of quantitative techniques to the solution of problems; 2) theory and methodology in mathematics, statistics, and computing applied to find optimal solutions. Used to find minimum cost or maximum profit/yield/performance across complex tradeoffs.
  • Network modeling (network design): Finds least-cost (efficient) or best-service (responsive) solution for the entire SC network
  • Models adjust for facility locations, supplier choices, inventory levels, transportation costs, and demand scenarios
  • A good model reveals risks and opportunities, enabling both resilience and cost efficiency

Question 1
What is the 'supply chain as competitive advantage' concept?
A.The idea that having a large supply chain creates advantages
B.The recognition that supply chain capabilities — speed, reliability, cost efficiency, responsiveness, sustainability — can differentiate a company in its market and deliver competitive advantage that is difficult for competitors to replicate. Examples: Amazon's delivery speed, Walmart's logistics efficiency
C.The financial value of supply chain assets
D.The competitive value of having many suppliers
Supply chain as competitive advantage: Amazon has turned delivery speed into the primary purchase driver; Walmart's cross-docking and logistics efficiency created a cost advantage that enabled 'Everyday Low Prices'; Apple's supply chain enabled rapid global product launches at scale — these supply chain capabilities are core competitive strengths.
Question 2
What is 'supply chain value creation' and how is it measured?
A.The total value of goods flowing through the supply chain
B.The generation of economic value through supply chain activities — measured through: cost reduction (lower total cost of ownership), revenue enablement (enabling sales through availability, speed, service), working capital optimization (inventory reduction), and risk reduction (business continuity value)
C.The monetary value added by each supply chain step
D.The value created by supply chain software implementations
Supply chain value creation encompasses financial, customer, and strategic dimensions: cost reduction (directly improves margins); revenue enablement (supply availability and service enable sales that would otherwise be lost); working capital improvement (inventory reduction frees cash); resilience (reduces value-at-risk from disruptions).
Question 3
What is 'supply chain finance' (SCF) and how does it create value?
A.The finance department's management of supply chain budgets
B.A set of solutions (reverse factoring, dynamic discounting, inventory financing) that optimize the financing of goods along the supply chain — enabling buyers to extend payment terms while suppliers receive early payment (funded by a bank), improving cash flow for both parties simultaneously
C.The financing of supply chain technology investments
D.A program for providing loans to supply chain companies
Supply chain finance creates working capital value: the buyer extends payment terms (improving their DPO — Days Payable Outstanding); the supplier receives early payment at a rate below their own borrowing cost (improving their DSO — Days Sales Outstanding). The bank earns the spread. All three parties benefit — a genuine win-win-win.
Question 4
What is the 'order-to-cash' cycle and why does it matter strategically?
A.The process of ordering cash from the bank
B.The end-to-end process from customer order receipt through product delivery and invoice payment collection — cycle time and efficiency of this process directly affects: customer satisfaction, revenue recognition timing, working capital (DSO), and cash conversion cycle
C.The process of managing customer credit
D.A financial accounting process for revenue recognition
Order-to-cash is a strategic process: shorter O2C cycle means faster cash receipt (better cash flow); fewer invoice errors mean fewer disputes (lower DSO); better fulfillment means higher customer satisfaction (revenue protection). Optimizing O2C is both a financial and customer service improvement.
Question 5
What is 'supply chain cost-to-serve' analysis?
A.The cost of maintaining the supply chain organization
B.The calculation of the total cost to fulfill a specific customer's orders — including order processing cost, picking cost, delivery cost, and returns cost — revealing that different customers have dramatically different profitability due to varying service requirements and order characteristics
C.The cost of supplying goods to customers
D.The annual budget for supply chain operations
Cost-to-serve analysis reveals true customer profitability: a customer ordering $1M/year in small, frequent orders with special handling, customized packaging, and high return rates may be less profitable than a customer ordering $500K in large standard orders. Cost-to-serve data enables profitable pricing and service differentiation.
Question 6
What is 'supply chain innovation' and how does it drive strategic value?
A.Implementing new supply chain software systems
B.Developing novel supply chain capabilities — new processes (3D printing for spare parts), new technologies (autonomous vehicles for delivery, blockchain for traceability), new business models (direct-to-consumer, platform models) — that create competitive differentiation or unlock new sources of value
C.Using innovative packaging designs in the supply chain
D.Supply chain improvements that are patented by the company
Supply chain innovation creates durable competitive advantage: innovations that work take time for competitors to replicate — Amazon Prime's delivery network took years to build; Zara's fast fashion supply chain required deep organizational and supplier investment. First-mover supply chain innovation often sets new industry standards.
Question 7
What is 'working capital optimization' through supply chain management?
A.Optimizing the working capital of the supply chain finance team
B.Reducing working capital requirements through supply chain improvements — primarily reducing Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) through better demand forecasting and inventory management, reducing Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) through invoice accuracy and faster payment terms, and extending Days Payable Outstanding (DPO) through supplier relationship management
C.Optimizing the investment in supply chain equipment
D.Reducing the number of employees in supply chain finance
Working capital = Inventory + Accounts Receivable - Accounts Payable. Supply chain levers: reduce DIO (better forecasting, VMI, lean practices); reduce DSO (order accuracy, invoice accuracy, faster billing); increase DPO (supplier relationship strength, supply chain finance). Each week of improvement in DIO at $1B revenue = ~$20M cash freed.
Question 8
What is 'supply chain talent management' and why is it strategically important?
A.The management of temporary supply chain workers
B.The strategic acquisition, development, and retention of supply chain professionals — including data analytics, digital technology, sustainability, and global operations expertise. As supply chains become more complex and technology-intensive, talent is increasingly a source of competitive advantage
C.The management of supply chain salaries and benefits
D.A training program for supply chain employees
Supply chain talent is increasingly scarce and valuable: as supply chains require data science, digital technology, global relationship management, and sustainability expertise, companies with superior talent can implement more advanced strategies, respond faster to disruptions, and innovate more effectively than those with skill gaps.
Question 9
What is 'supply chain data analytics' and how does it create strategic value?
A.Using spreadsheets to track supply chain performance
B.The systematic application of data analysis, statistical modeling, machine learning, and AI to supply chain data — enabling: better demand forecasting, optimal inventory positioning, predictive supply disruption detection, dynamic pricing, and network optimization — creating performance advantages over less analytically sophisticated competitors
C.A regulatory reporting system for supply chain data
D.An IT system for storing supply chain data
Supply chain analytics creates compounding advantages: better forecasting → less inventory for the same service level; predictive disruption detection → faster response; network optimization → lower cost at same service; dynamic pricing → better margin management. Data-driven supply chains consistently outperform peers on cost, service, and resilience.
Question 10
What is 'customer-centric supply chain design' and how does it differ from product-centric design?
A.A supply chain design where customers manage their own orders
B.Designing the supply chain starting from customer requirements (service levels, lead times, delivery frequency, flexibility) and working backwards to determine the optimal supply chain configuration — vs. product-centric design which starts from production efficiency and then figures out distribution
C.A supply chain that only serves one customer
D.A design where customers help design the products
Customer-centric design starts with: 'what does the customer need?' (same-day delivery, high availability, mass customization) and designs the supply chain to deliver it. Product-centric design starts with: 'how do we make this efficiently?' and then tries to serve customers within those constraints. Customer-centricity often enables premium pricing that more than offsets additional supply chain cost.
Question 11
What is 'supply chain performance benchmarking' and how is it used strategically?
A.Comparing supply chain employee performance ratings
B.Comparing an organization's supply chain performance metrics (cost, service, inventory, cash flow) against industry peers and best-in-class companies — identifying where the organization lags or leads, informing strategic investment priorities, and setting improvement targets aligned with competitive requirements
C.A process for benchmarking supplier prices
D.Comparing the performance of different supply chain systems
Benchmarking provides strategic context: if your OTIF is 94% and the industry top quartile is 98%, there's a significant service gap requiring strategic investment. If your inventory turns are 4 and best-in-class are 12, there's a working capital and agility gap. Benchmarks set the competitive aspiration level.
Question 12
What is 'platform-based supply chain' and how is it creating new business models?
A.A supply chain built on a physical loading platform
B.A digital platform model where a company provides digital infrastructure connecting suppliers, logistics providers, and customers — enabling network effects, marketplace dynamics, and new value creation beyond the company's own physical supply chain assets. Examples: Alibaba, Amazon Marketplace
C.A supply chain that operates on a fixed cost platform
D.A technology platform for managing supply chain software
Platform models transform supply chain economics: instead of owning supply chain assets, the platform orchestrates third-party assets — Alibaba connects suppliers and buyers globally without owning factories or warehouses; Flexport provides digital freight brokerage connecting shippers to logistics providers. Network effects create defensible competitive positions.
