Emerging Technology Overview

Key emerging technologies for supply chains: cloud computing, artificial intelligence, machine learning, data analytics, sensors, telematics, control towers, quantum computing, 3D printing, wearable technology, augmented reality, robotic process automation, automated guided vehicles, and drones.

Cloud Computing, AI, and Analytics
Cloud computing (ASCM): An emerging way of computing in which data is stored in massive data centers that can be accessed from any location over a network. Eliminates need for in-house hardware and software management.
  • SaaS (Software as a Service): Applications accessed via web browser; leased solutions
  • PaaS (Platform as a Service): Leased platform infrastructure
  • IaaS (Infrastructure as a Service): Leased hardware/network infrastructure
  • Downsides: ongoing lease cost, internet failure risk, security and data privacy concerns
Artificial intelligence (ASCM): Computer programs that can learn and reason in a manner similar to humans. Problem defined in terms of states and operators that can be solved by search or constraint satisfaction.
Machine learning (ASCM): Artificial intelligence software capable of analysis, self-training, and observation to improve its own performance. Often used in AI applications such as pattern detection and predictive analytics.
Expert system (ASCM): A type of AI computer system that mimics human experts by using rules and heuristics rather than deterministic algorithms.
Heuristics (ASCM): A form of problem-solving in which results or rules have been determined by experience or intuition instead of by optimization. Can be rule-based and provide quick near-optimal solutions.
Analytics (ASCM): The review of typically large sets of business data using mathematics, statistics, and computer software to identify meaningful patterns in the data that can be applied to improve business decisions.
  • Predictive analytics: Uses AI/machine learning to predict maintenance requirements, demand patterns, etc.
  • Big data: Very large datasets requiring significant processing power to extract useful information
Sensors, Control Towers & Other Technologies
Sensors (ASCM): Devices that can monitor differences in conditions to control equipment on a dynamic basis.
Supply chain control tower (ASCM): A centralized hub that provides an integrated, complete view of data across the end-to-end supply chain. Allows the supplier to see the requirements of the customer and the customer to see the supplier’s ability to deliver.
  • Telematics: Remote sensors that transmit data; enhance visibility and automation across industries
  • Transportation control towers: Monitor on-time orders, transport costs, track/trace, exception management
  • SC control towers: Monitor on-time in full (OTIF) orders, total cost to serve, 3PL performance
Quantum computing: An entirely different way to build a computer using quantum mechanical phenomena. Can explore many combinations simultaneously, enabling optimization of complex supply chain tradeoffs (routing, facility location, etc.).
3D printing (ASCM): The process of layering materials to make products and components using computer data. Enables on-demand production of 3D objects from digital models.
  • Key application: rapid prototyping (any 3D design can be printed and physically experienced)
Wearable (ASCM): A form of technology worn on the body that allows hands-free work by being voice and/or gesture activated. Used in warehousing, maintenance, and field operations.
Augmented reality (AR) (ASCM): Using holographic imagery alongside the physical environment to provide additional information or guidance about how to carry out a task.
Robotic process automation (RPA): The use of software robots (bots) to emulate human execution of repetitive, pre-defined business processes. Automates high-volume, rule-based tasks without human intervention.
Intelligent Supply Chains
Internet of Things (IoT) (ASCM): An environment in which objects, animals, or people are provided with unique identifiers and the ability to transfer data over a network without requiring human-to-human or human-to-computer interaction.
  • IoT networks non-computer devices (equipment, drones, ASRS, vehicles) that update systems on status, location, sensor readings, and maintenance needs
Blockchain (ASCM): A continuously growing list of records (blocks) linked and secured using cryptography. Each block contains a cryptographic hash of the previous block, a timestamp, and transaction data. Provides irrefutable evidence of transactions.
  • SC applications of blockchain: Chain of custody for goods, automated capture of IoT data (e.g., temperature records), preventing insertion of counterfeits by validating serial numbers
  • Famous as the technology behind cryptocurrencies; also used in TradeLens (IBM + Maersk) for water shipping
Intelligent Asset Management (IAM): Creates a digital version (“digital twin”) of an industrial asset, allowing monitoring, analysis, and optimization of physical assets through their digital counterpart.
Automated guided vehicle system (AGVS) (ASCM): A system that automatically routes one or more material-handling devices (carts, pallet trucks) and positions them at predetermined destinations without operator intervention.
  • Navigation uses markers, wires, vision sensors, or radio frequency; used in manufacturing and warehousing
  • Primary issue: cost (though falling); typical payback period of 2 years

Question 1
What is 'artificial intelligence' (AI) in supply chain management and what applications are emerging?
A.A system that replaces human supply chain managers
B.AI involves machine learning and other techniques enabling systems to perform tasks that traditionally required human intelligence — supply chain applications: demand forecasting (more accurate than statistical methods), dynamic pricing, autonomous replenishment, anomaly detection (fraud, quality), and natural language processing for procurement
C.A type of warehouse automation robot
D.A software system for supply chain data storage
AI supply chain applications are expanding rapidly: ML-based demand forecasting improves accuracy 20-50% vs. statistical methods by incorporating more variables (weather, social media, economic indicators); reinforcement learning optimizes transportation routing dynamically; computer vision automates quality inspection — each reducing cost, improving service, or enabling capabilities previously impossible.
Question 2
What is 'the Internet of Things' (IoT) in supply chains and what data does it provide?
A.The connectivity of supply chain partner companies through the internet
B.A network of physical devices embedded with sensors, connectivity, and software that collect and transmit data — supply chain applications: real-time asset tracking (containers, trucks, pallets), environmental monitoring (temperature, humidity in cold chain), predictive maintenance (sensor data predicting equipment failure), and inventory management (smart shelves)
C.A global communication network for supply chain emails
D.A system for connecting customer ordering systems to supply chain software
IoT creates supply chain visibility from physical world data: a temperature sensor on a pharmaceutical shipment detects excursion (temperature out of range) and triggers alert; a vibration sensor on a conveyor predicts bearing failure before it causes downtime; RFID enables real-time inventory tracking without manual counting — all reducing uncertainty and enabling proactive management.
Question 3
What is 'blockchain' technology and what specific supply chain problems can it solve?
A.A type of heavy-duty pallet for supply chain storage
B.A distributed, immutable ledger where transactions are recorded across multiple nodes — making it impossible to alter records without consensus. Supply chain applications: provenance tracking (verify origin claims), document authentication (bills of lading, certificates of origin), smart contracts (auto-execute payment on delivery confirmation), and multi-party visibility without a central trusted authority
C.A computer system for managing supply chain databases
D.A new type of supply chain encryption technology
Blockchain solves supply chain trust problems: when multiple parties (manufacturer, shipper, customs, buyer) need to share and trust the same data without relying on a single party controlling the database, blockchain provides a shared, tamper-evident record. Food safety traceability (IBM Food Trust) and diamond provenance (Everledger) are proven applications.
Question 4
What is 'robotic process automation' (RPA) and how is it used in supply chain operations?
A.Warehouse robots that automate physical tasks
B.Software robots that automate repetitive, rule-based digital tasks — supply chain applications: automatic purchase order creation (when inventory hits reorder point), invoice matching (PO vs. invoice vs. receipt), shipment tracking updates, reporting compilation, and data migration between systems — freeing humans for higher-value work
C.A physical automation system for manufacturing
D.An AI system that replaces supply chain planners
RPA automates digital grunt work: creating POs, matching invoices (3-way match), updating shipment status, compiling performance reports — tasks that previously required human attention but follow consistent rules. RPA reduces processing time from days to minutes, eliminates data entry errors, and frees supply chain staff for analysis and decision-making.
Question 5
What is '3D printing' (additive manufacturing) and how could it transform supply chains?
A.A printing technique for supply chain documents
B.A manufacturing process that creates objects layer by layer from digital files — supply chain implications: local, on-demand manufacturing (reducing inventory of spare parts), mass customization enablement, reduction of complex supply chains (print at point of need rather than shipping from centralized factory), and tooling elimination
C.A method for printing 3D supply chain maps
D.A process for creating 3D packaging designs
3D printing disrupts traditional supply chain models: instead of manufacturing in one location and shipping globally, digital design files are sent and products printed locally — reducing transportation cost, lead time, and inventory. Spare parts are the near-term application (GE aviation, Rolls-Royce print spare parts on demand rather than stocking globally).
Question 6
What is 'autonomous vehicles and drones' technology and its supply chain applications?
A.Remote-controlled delivery vehicles
B.Self-driving trucks, autonomous warehouse vehicles (AGVs/AMRs), and drones that operate without human drivers — supply chain applications: last-mile delivery (drones and autonomous vans), long-haul trucking (autonomous trucks reducing driver shortage impact), warehouse picking and put-away (AMRs), and yard management
C.A type of advanced GPS navigation system
D.Automated scheduling systems for vehicle fleets
Autonomous vehicles address supply chain constraints: the truck driver shortage (estimated 80,000+ in US) makes autonomous long-haul attractive; drone delivery reduces last-mile cost for light packages; autonomous mobile robots (Amazon Kiva, now Amazon Robotics) dramatically increase warehouse throughput and reduce picking cost.
Question 7
What is 'digital twin' technology and how is it applied in supply chain management?
A.A backup copy of supply chain software systems
B.A virtual replica of a physical supply chain (or facility, or asset) that uses real-time data from the physical system — enabling simulation ('what-if analysis'), optimization, and monitoring without disrupting real operations. Supply chain digital twins simulate network redesign, disruption scenarios, and capacity decisions
C.A digital version of the supply chain organizational chart
D.A system for tracking physical supply chain assets
Digital twins enable risk-free supply chain experimentation: 'what if we close this warehouse and open one in a different city?' — simulate the impact on service levels, cost, and carbon before making the decision. During COVID-19, digital twin models helped companies rapidly simulate alternative supply chain configurations when their networks were disrupted.
Question 8
What is 'predictive analytics' in supply chain management?
A.Analytics that describe past supply chain performance
B.Using historical data, statistical algorithms, and machine learning models to forecast future events — applications: demand forecasting, predictive maintenance (predicting equipment failure before it occurs), supply disruption prediction (monitoring supplier risk signals), and predictive logistics (anticipated delays)
C.Analytics that predict stock market movements
D.A process for predicting future supply chain investments
Predictive analytics shifts supply chain management from reactive to proactive: instead of responding to a stockout after it occurs, ML models predict it 4 weeks in advance; instead of discovering a supplier is failing when they miss a shipment, financial data models predict financial distress months earlier — enabling prevention rather than remediation.
Question 9
What is 'cloud computing' and how has it transformed supply chain technology?
A.Computing using weather cloud patterns for delivery optimization
B.Delivering computing services (servers, storage, software) over the internet — transforming supply chain technology by enabling: rapid deployment of supply chain software (no on-premise hardware), scalable computing for large optimization problems, real-time data sharing across companies (no point-to-point integration), and lower total cost of ownership
C.A data backup system for supply chain files
D.A system for computing transportation costs
Cloud transformed supply chain technology accessibility: SaaS (Software-as-a-Service) supply chain platforms (Kinaxis, o9, Blue Yonder, Coupa) eliminate the need for expensive on-premise infrastructure; cloud computing enables real-time multi-enterprise data sharing; scalable computing makes previously intractable optimization problems (network optimization, large-scale simulation) affordable.
Question 10
What is 'advanced planning systems' (APS) and how do they improve supply chain planning?
A.Upgraded versions of older supply chain planning software
B.Sophisticated planning software that uses optimization algorithms (mathematical programming, simulation, machine learning) to solve complex supply chain planning problems — integrated demand planning, supply planning, capacity planning, and distribution optimization simultaneously, considering all constraints to generate optimal plans
C.Systems that automatically execute supply chain plans
D.A project management tool for supply chain planning projects
APS goes beyond traditional MRP's sequential, unconstrained planning: APS simultaneously considers demand, supply, production capacity, transportation capacity, and inventory constraints to generate feasible, optimized plans. Integrated APS platforms (Kinaxis Maestro, Blue Yonder) dramatically improve plan quality and reduce planning cycle time.
Question 11
What is 'supply chain control tower' and what capabilities does it provide?
A.A physical observation tower at a distribution center
B.A centralized hub with technology, organization, and processes that captures and uses supply chain data to provide enhanced end-to-end supply chain visibility, recommend actions, and enable agile management of the supply chain — integrating data from multiple systems into one actionable view
C.A command center for supply chain security operations
D.An IT system for managing supply chain software systems
The supply chain control tower aggregates data from ERP, WMS, TMS, carrier systems, and supplier systems into a unified view — enabling real-time exception management ('this shipment will be 3 days late'), proactive customer notification, and rapid decision support. It makes end-to-end supply chain visibility operationally actionable.
Question 12
What is 'machine learning' in demand forecasting and how does it outperform traditional methods?
A.A training method for teaching supply chain planners
B.A subset of AI where algorithms learn patterns from historical data to make predictions — in demand forecasting, ML models incorporate more variables (weather, promotions, economic data, social signals, competitor actions) and learn non-linear patterns that traditional statistical methods miss, improving forecast accuracy
C.A machine that learns from customer feedback
D.A software that automatically updates demand plans
ML demand forecasting advantages: handles thousands of variables simultaneously (vs. traditional models with few); learns complex non-linear relationships (promotion + weather + competitor event = multiplicative effect); improves automatically with more data; and processes at SKU-location level without manual parameter tuning — delivering meaningful accuracy improvements that reduce safety stock and stockouts.
