Guidelines vs. Standards vs. Certification
  • Guidelines: Broad, principle-based best practices; lack authority of a standard; may not provide implementation detail
  • Standards: Best practices developed by consensus of numerous practitioners and experts
Accreditation (ASCM): Certification by a recognized body of the facilities, capability, objectivity, competence, and integrity of an agency, service, operational group, or individual to perform specified functions.
Certification (ASCM): Documentation of competency by a supplier or by an organization, such as ISO 9000 certification.

Key standards for CSCP: ISO 9000/9001, ISO 14000/14001, ISO 26000, SA8000, ANSI Z.10, plus international guidelines: UN Global Compact, OECD Guidelines, GRI.

International Guidelines

UN Global Compact

  • 10 key principles across 4 areas: Human Rights (2), Labour (2), Environment (3), Anti-Corruption (1)
  • Two complementary objectives: (1) Incorporate the 10 principles into global business activities; (2) Catalyze actions supporting broader UN goals
  • Recommends reporting sustainability efforts using GRI Sustainability Reporting Guidelines

OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises

OECD Guidelines (ASCM): A set of recommendations about responsible business conduct addressed by governments to multinational enterprises (MNEs) operating in or from adhering countries. Covers employment/labor, environment, antibribery, consumer interests, information disclosure, and science/technology.

Threefold goal: (1) Align enterprises’ operations with government policies; (2) Reinforce trust between enterprises and societies; (3) Strengthen foreign investment climate and sustainable development.

Global Reporting Initiative (GRI)
GRI Reporting Framework (ASCM): The framework that sets out the principles and performance indicators organizations can use to measure and report their human rights, labor, environment, and anti-corruption activities.
Exhibit 8-14: GRI Sustainability Reporting Standards
  • Transitioned from guidelines to standards in 2018 (effective date)
  • Universally applicable: GRI 1 (Foundation — reporting principles), GRI 2 (General Disclosures), GRI 3 (Material Topics)
  • Topic-specific series: GRI 200 (Economic), GRI 300 (Environmental), GRI 400 (Social)

GRI Purposes: Build capacity, foster commitment to sustainability, improve performance and risk management, improve reliable sustainability information flow from supplier to buyer.

10 Reporting Principles (GRI 101): 5 for defining content (Stakeholder Inclusiveness, Sustainability Context, Materiality, Completeness, Accuracy) and 5 for quality (Balance, Comparability, Clarity, Reliability, Timeliness).

GRI 200 Economic topics: Economic performance, market presence, indirect impacts, procurement practices, anti-corruption, anti-competitive behavior, tax.

GRI 300 Environmental topics: Materials, energy, water/effluents, biodiversity, emissions, waste, environmental compliance, supplier assessment.

GRI 400 Social topics: Employment, labor/management, health & safety, training, diversity, non-discrimination, freedom of association, child labor, forced labor, security, indigenous peoples, local communities, supplier social assessment, public policy, customer health/safety, privacy.

ISO Sustainability Standards

ISO Key Characteristics

  • Voluntary: ISO is an NGO with no legal authority to enforce standards
  • Market-driven: Standards developed for which there is a market need, by international cross-section of experts
  • Consensus-based: Widespread applicability; generic management system standards (ISO 9000, ISO 14000) apply to any organization
  • Registration: Audit of implementation and conformance; ISO certification renewed every 3 years

ISO 9000 Series — Quality Standards

ISO 9000 Series Standards (ASCM): A set of international standards about quality management and quality assurance developed to help companies effectively document the quality system elements needed to maintain an efficient quality system.
  • ISO 9001: Certification standard; specifies QMS requirements; focuses on customer requirements, top management commitment, process approach, and continual improvement; renewed every 3 years

ISO 14000 Series — Environmental Standards

ISO 14000 Series Standards (ASCM): A series of generic environmental management standards developed by ISO that provide structure and guidance for a proactive approach to environmental management.
  • ISO 14001: Framework for strategic, holistic environmental policy, plans, and actions; can be certified
  • ISO 14004: Guidelines on elements of an environmental management system (EMS) and implementation

Social and Safety Standards

ISO 26000 (ASCM): An international standard developed by ISO to assist organizations in contributing to sustainable development. Provides guidance for social responsibility (not applicable for certification). Addresses 7 core areas: organizational governance, human rights, labor practices, environment, fair operating practices, consumer issues, community involvement.
SA8000 (ASCM): A widely recognized international standard for managing human rights in the workplace. Provides an auditable framework for social accountability covering child labor, forced labor, health/safety, freedom of association, discrimination, disciplinary practices, working hours, remuneration, and management systems.
ANSI Z.10 (ASCM): A voluntary consensus standard for occupational health and safety management systems. Uses recognized management system principles to be compatible with ISO 9001 and ISO 14001. Focuses on principles rather than detailed specifications.

Question 1
What is the 'Global Reporting Initiative' (GRI) and how is it used?
A.A government agency that regulates sustainability reporting
B.The most widely used global framework for sustainability reporting — providing standardized disclosure standards for environmental (emissions, water, waste), social (labor practices, human rights), and governance topics. Companies use GRI standards to structure and disclose their sustainability performance transparently
C.A sustainability certification for supply chain companies
D.A financial reporting standard for environmental costs
GRI standards enable comparable sustainability disclosure: by using common definitions and metrics (Scope 1, 2, 3 emissions; water withdrawal; waste generated; labor practices), stakeholders (investors, customers, regulators) can compare sustainability performance across companies and verify claims against consistent standards.
Question 2
What are 'Science-Based Targets' (SBTs) and why are they significant?
A.Targets based on scientific research conducted by companies
B.Greenhouse gas emission reduction targets aligned with climate science — specifically, the level of emission reduction needed to limit global warming to 1.5°C or 2°C. Validated by the Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi). Setting SBTs signals credible climate commitment vs. vague 'sustainability goals'
C.Scientific quality targets for supply chain operations
D.Government-mandated emission reduction targets
SBTs are the gold standard for climate commitment: they require companies to set reduction targets covering Scope 1, 2, AND 3 emissions, aligned with the Paris Agreement's 1.5°C pathway. For supply chains, achieving SBTs requires engaging suppliers (Scope 3) — making supplier sustainability programs essential.
Question 3
What is 'ISO 14001' and what does it require of certified organizations?
A.A quality management standard for manufacturing
B.An international standard specifying requirements for an Environmental Management System (EMS) — requiring organizations to: identify environmental impacts, set environmental objectives, implement programs to achieve objectives, monitor performance, and continually improve. Certification demonstrates systematic environmental management
C.An environmental product labeling standard
D.A standard for environmental reporting to governments
ISO 14001 certifies that an organization has a systematic approach to environmental management — not that it has achieved specific environmental performance levels, but that it has the system to identify impacts, set goals, track performance, and improve. Many supply chain companies require ISO 14001 certification from strategic suppliers.
Question 4
What is 'SA8000' and what does it certify?
A.A safety management standard for warehouses
B.A social accountability standard certifying that an organization meets international labor standards — covering: no child labor, no forced labor, safe working conditions, freedom of association, no discrimination, reasonable working hours, and fair wages. One of the leading standards for labor practice certification in global supply chains
C.An accounting standard for supply chain finance
D.A quality standard for social enterprises
SA8000 is the supply chain social responsibility standard: developed by Social Accountability International, it provides a verifiable certification that factories meet ILO labor standards — used by brands and retailers to verify that suppliers are not using child labor, forced labor, or unsafe conditions.
Question 5
What is 'CDP' (formerly Carbon Disclosure Project) and what does it measure?
A.A government carbon tax collection agency
B.A global disclosure system where companies report environmental data — including greenhouse gas emissions, water security, and deforestation risks — enabling investors, customers, and policymakers to assess companies' environmental performance and risks. CDP scores are widely used in sustainability ratings
C.A carbon trading marketplace
D.A certification program for carbon-neutral companies
CDP disclosure enables investor and customer assessment of environmental performance and risk: companies are scored (A through D) on disclosure quality and performance. Major corporations require suppliers to disclose through CDP; investors use CDP scores to assess climate risk in their portfolios. Non-disclosure is itself a signal of poor performance.
Question 6
What is the 'UN Global Compact' and what does signing it commit companies to?
A.A trade agreement between United Nations member countries
B.A voluntary initiative where companies commit to aligning their operations with ten principles covering: human rights (uphold and not abuse), labor standards (eliminate exploitation), environment (precautionary approach, environmental responsibility), and anti-corruption — and to taking action in support of UN goals
C.A United Nations supply chain certification
D.A global standard for financial reporting
The UN Global Compact's ten principles commit signatories to: respect human rights (principles 1-2), eliminate labor abuses including forced and child labor (3-6), take a precautionary approach to environmental challenges and promote environmental responsibility (7-9), and work against corruption (10) — across their own operations and value chains.
Question 7
What is 'Responsible Business Alliance' (RBA) / EICC and what industries does it serve?
A.A government trade association for responsible businesses
B.A coalition of companies in electronics, retail, auto, and other industries committed to a code of conduct for socially responsible supply chains — the RBA Code of Conduct covers: labor (no forced labor, working hours, wages), health and safety, environment (permits, waste), and ethics (anti-corruption, privacy)
C.A private equity alliance for ethical investing
D.A certification body for organic product supply chains
The RBA (formerly Electronic Industry Citizenship Coalition / EICC) created the dominant supply chain sustainability code for electronics: companies like Apple, Samsung, and Dell require suppliers to comply with and be audited against RBA standards — covering labor, health and safety, environmental, and ethics requirements across the supply chain.
Question 8
What is 'conflict minerals' regulation (Dodd-Frank Section 1502)?
A.A regulation prohibiting use of mineral resources in conflict zones
B.A US law requiring public companies to disclose whether their products contain tin, tantalum, tungsten, or gold (3TG) originating from conflict-affected areas of the Democratic Republic of Congo — requiring supply chain due diligence to identify and report the origin of these minerals
C.A regulation about mining conflicts between companies
D.A standard for resolving disputes with mineral suppliers
Conflict minerals regulation requires supply chain traceability: companies must trace 3TG minerals through their supply chain to verify they are not funding armed groups in the DRC. This requires multi-tier supply chain mapping, smelter audits (RMAP), and disclosure in SEC filings — driving deep supply chain transparency requirements.
Question 9
What is the 'EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive' (CSDDD)?
A.An EU environmental tax on corporate supply chains
B.An EU regulation requiring large companies to identify, prevent, and mitigate adverse human rights and environmental impacts across their full value chain — not just their own operations, but including suppliers and downstream partners. Includes director accountability and civil liability for non-compliance
C.An EU standard for corporate sustainability reporting
D.An EU incentive program for sustainable supply chains
CSDDD (once fully implemented) requires companies to conduct due diligence on their entire value chain — identifying risks of human rights violations and environmental damage, preventing and mitigating identified risks, and reporting publicly. It extends responsibility to Tier 2+ suppliers, requiring unprecedented supply chain transparency.
Question 10
What is 'carbon border adjustment mechanism' (CBAM) and how does it affect supply chains?
A.A mechanism for companies to adjust their carbon targets
B.An EU mechanism that imposes a carbon price on imports of certain goods (steel, cement, aluminum, fertilizers, electricity, hydrogen) from countries with less stringent climate policies — preventing 'carbon leakage' (companies relocating production to countries with weaker standards). Effective from 2026
C.A system for measuring carbon emissions at international borders
D.A trade agreement that adjusts carbon standards between countries
CBAM changes supply chain economics for carbon-intensive materials: EU importers must purchase CBAM certificates for embedded carbon in imports, effectively equalizing the carbon cost between domestic and imported goods. This incentivizes sourcing from lower-carbon suppliers, even if they're in higher-wage countries — carbon cost now factors into sourcing decisions.
Question 11
What is 'sustainability-linked financing' and how does it connect to supply chain performance?
A.Financing specifically for sustainability projects
B.Financial instruments (loans, bonds) where the interest rate or terms are linked to the borrower's achievement of sustainability targets — such as emission reduction milestones or supplier sustainability audit scores. Creates financial incentive for supply chain sustainability improvement
C.A financing mechanism for sustainable energy projects only
D.Bank loans restricted to sustainable business activities
Sustainability-linked financing aligns financial incentives with sustainability performance: if a company achieves its supplier sustainability audit score targets, the interest rate on its supply chain finance program decreases — creating a direct financial incentive for supplier sustainability improvement. SCF programs are increasingly structured with sustainability-linked pricing.
Question 12
What is the 'forest stewardship council' (FSC) certification and when is it relevant to supply chains?
A.A government agency managing national forests
B.An international standard certifying that forest products (timber, paper, packaging) come from responsibly managed forests — maintained through chain-of-custody certification that tracks certified material through every step of the supply chain from forest to end product
C.A standard for carbon sequestration in supply chains
D.A certification for sustainable agricultural practices
FSC chain-of-custody certification ensures the sustainability claim survives the supply chain journey: each handler of FSC-certified material (sawmill, paper mill, printer, packaging converter) must be certified to maintain the chain of custody — enabling the brand at the end of the chain to credibly claim FSC-certified packaging or paper.
