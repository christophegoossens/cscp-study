Triple Bottom Line
Sustainability (ASCM): Activities that provide present benefit without compromising the needs of future generations.

The Triple Bottom Line (TBL) has three components that create a sustainable business model:

Exhibit 8-10: Triple Bottom Line — Economic, Environmental, and Social
  • Economic sustainability: Keeping the organization financially viable over the long term; reinvesting profits in market growth
  • Environmental sustainability: Minimizing harmful effects on ecosystems, land, air, and water
  • Social sustainability: Improving labor conditions, human rights, community relations, and worker health
Start with solutions in the “sweet spot” that simultaneously provide economic, environmental, AND social benefits.
Environmental Performance
Environmentally responsible business: A firm that operates in such a way as to minimize deleterious impacts to society.
Environmentally responsible manufacturing: A collection of manufacturing activities that includes design of the product, facility, manufacturing processes, logistics, product use, and end-of-life management.
Environmentally sensitive engineering: Designing with consideration of how a product or its packaging will ultimately be disposed.

Environmental SC management questions by function:

  • Procurement: Can vendors be ISO 14000 certified? Can recycled materials be used? Can nonrenewable resources be substituted?
  • Manufacturing: Is the product designed for the SC? Can toxic/hazardous material use be reduced? What environmental risks arise in production?
  • Warehousing & Transportation: Can environmentally designed facilities be used? Which carrier/mode has the smallest carbon footprint? Can recyclable packaging be used?
  • Reverse logistics: How will products be gathered for recycling/reuse? How will toxic materials be stored and separated safely?

Green SCOR® — environmental component of the SCOR® model — focuses on environmental performance attributes.

Economic Performance Tradeoffs

Common short-term vs. long-term sustainability tradeoffs:

  • Sales department moves orders to meet end-of-period targets by giving customer incentives — harms future demand patterns
  • Using wrong metrics makes customer service appear better than it is (e.g., tracking orders by when shipped, not by delivery promise)
  • Creating large anticipation inventory for seasonal products looks beneficial but requires cash and can result in markdowns
Window dressing: Manipulating customer orders or inventory on a balance sheet in legal ways that harm long-term sustainability but improve short-term appearances.
Long-term economic sustainability requires alignment of financial goals across functions — not just optimizing for end-of-period metrics.
Sustainable SC Compliance

Types of Compliance

  • Voluntary program compliance: Allows measured, cost-effective pace of sustainability implementation (e.g., reuse, recycling, and recovery programs)
  • Organization-specific programs: Self-developed systems (e.g., Timberland’s sustainability label: energy, global warming contribution, material efficiency)
  • Government and regulatory compliance: Vehicle emissions, material content reporting, dangerous goods handling

Material Content Reporting

  • Part of a larger movement toward sustainable trade practices (clean, safe, healthy environment)
  • EU RoHS directive: Restricts certain hazardous substances in electrical/electronic equipment

Dangerous Goods

  • U.S. DOT characteristics of concern: Ignitability, corrosivity, reactivity, toxicity
  • Governed internationally by the UN Recommendations on Transport of Dangerous Goods; ships by the IMDG Code
  • Key regulatory areas: identification/classification, packaging specifications, training certification, documentation (signed statement from shipper)

Conflict Minerals

Conflict minerals (ASCM): Minerals mined in conditions of armed conflict and human rights abuses and that are sold to perpetuate fighting. The U.S. Dodd-Frank Act (2010) requires disclosure of conflict mineral use.

Sustainability Risks from Packaging

  • Wooden pallets: controversy over pest risk and chemical treatment (ISPM15 regulation)
  • Solutions: heat/chemical sterilization, reuse, grinding for recycling, plastic/cardboard pallets, slip sheets

What is Green SCOR®?

What 4 characteristics of concern does the U.S. DOT identify for hazardous materials?
ANSWER
Ignitability, corrosivity, reactivity, and toxicity. Items with these characteristics have different handling, packaging, labeling, training, and documentation requirements.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are conflict minerals (ASCM)?
ANSWER
Minerals mined in conditions of armed conflict and human rights abuses and that are sold to perpetuate fighting. The U.S. Dodd-Frank Act (2010) requires certain companies to disclose their use of conflict minerals.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 3 types of sustainability compliance?
ANSWER
1. Voluntary program compliance (measured, cost-effective pace), 2. Organization-specific programs (self-developed, e.g., Timberland’s sustainability label), 3. Government/regulatory compliance (vehicle emissions, material content, dangerous goods).
TERM / CONCEPT
What key regulations govern dangerous goods transport internationally?
ANSWER
UN Recommendations on the Transport of Dangerous Goods (international framework); International Maritime Dangerous Goods (IMDG) Code (ships); U.S. DOT hazardous materials regulations; Canada has its own “transportation of dangerous goods” laws.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is ISPM15 and why is it relevant to sustainability?
ANSWER
A regulation requiring sterilization of wooden pallets (with heat or chemicals) to prevent pest spread. Pallets are controversial for sustainability due to pest risk, chemical treatment, and waste. Alternatives include recycling, plastic pallets, and slip sheets.
TERM / CONCEPT
What environmental questions should a SC manager ask about transportation?
ANSWER
Which carrier/mode leaves the smallest carbon footprint? Can recyclable packaging and shipping materials be used? How will inbound/outbound traffic impact the local environment? Is the carrier licensed to handle our products?

Question 1
What is 'sustainable supply chain management' (SSCM) and what three dimensions does it encompass?
A.Environmental management of supply chain waste only
B.The management of supply chain material, information, and capital flows, as well as cooperation among companies along the supply chain, while taking goals from all three dimensions of sustainable development — environmental (reducing ecological impact), social (ensuring fair labor, human rights), and economic (creating long-term business value) — into account
C.Sustainable development of supplier relationships only
D.Compliance with environmental regulations in supply chain operations
SSCM is triple-bottom-line supply chain management: environmental (carbon emissions, water use, waste), social (labor standards, human rights, community impact), and economic (long-term profitability, resilience) — recognizing that supply chains that externalize environmental and social costs are not truly efficient.
Question 2
What is 'Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions' and which is most relevant to supply chain?
A.Three levels of air quality regulations
B.Scope 1: direct emissions from company-owned operations. Scope 2: indirect emissions from purchased energy. Scope 3: all other indirect emissions in the value chain (supplier emissions, transportation, product use and disposal). Scope 3 typically represents 70-90% of a company's total emissions and is where supply chain management has the greatest emissions impact
C.Three different greenhouse gas types
D.Three different reporting frameworks for environmental data
Scope 3 emissions dominate: a retailer's Scope 1 and 2 emissions (stores, offices) may be small, but their Scope 3 (manufacturing emissions from suppliers, freight emissions from transportation, consumer use) can be 10-20× larger. Supply chain decarbonization requires engaging suppliers and logistics providers on their emissions.
Question 3
What is the 'circular economy' concept and how does it differ from the linear economy?
A.An economy where goods are delivered in circular routes
B.A regenerative system that aims to eliminate waste and keep materials in use — contrasted with the traditional linear economy (take-make-dispose). In a circular economy: products are designed for reuse/repair/recycling, materials are kept at highest use level, and waste is minimized through closed-loop supply chains
C.An economic system based on circular financial transactions
D.A system where all goods are shared rather than owned
Linear economy: extract raw materials → manufacture → use → dispose. Circular economy closes the loop: design for disassembly, enable reuse and repair, recover materials at end of life, reintroduce into production. For supply chains: take-back programs, remanufacturing, recycled content requirements, packaging reuse.
Question 4
What is 'supplier sustainability assessment' and what does it evaluate?
A.A financial assessment of supplier sustainability as a business
B.A systematic evaluation of suppliers' environmental, social, and governance (ESG) practices — evaluating: environmental management (emissions, water, waste practices), labor standards (wages, working hours, safety), human rights practices, business ethics (anti-corruption, transparency), and supply chain sustainability programs
C.An assessment of how long suppliers have been in business
D.A quality audit of supplier manufacturing processes
Supplier sustainability assessment extends ESG responsibility upstream: companies are held accountable for their suppliers' environmental and labor practices (Rana Plaza collapse in 2013; child labor in cobalt supply chains) — requiring systematic assessment and improvement programs across the supply base, especially in high-risk regions and industries.
Question 5
What is 'sustainable procurement' and what criteria does it consider?
A.Procurement practices that ensure continuous supply
B.Purchasing goods and services in a way that takes into account the environmental and social impacts throughout the supply chain — considering: environmental attributes (recycled content, energy efficiency, emissions), social attributes (fair labor, community impact), and economic value (whole-life cost, not just purchase price)
C.Procurement that ensures supply continuity
D.Procurement of sustainable (long-lasting) products
Sustainable procurement integrates ESG criteria into purchasing decisions: selecting suppliers with strong environmental management, requiring fair labor standards in the supply chain, specifying recycled content or low-carbon materials, and favoring suppliers with credible sustainability programs — using purchasing power to drive supply chain sustainability improvement.
Question 6
What is 'green transportation' and what strategies does it include?
A.Using green-colored transportation vehicles
B.Reducing the environmental impact of freight transportation — strategies include: mode shift (rail/ocean instead of truck/air), fuel efficiency improvement (aerodynamic trailers, route optimization), alternative fuels (electric, hydrogen, biodiesel), load optimization (reducing empty miles), and collaborative shipping (sharing trucks to improve utilization)
C.Transporting only environmentally friendly products
D.Transportation through forested areas
Green transportation strategies reduce the largest source of logistics emissions: mode shift to rail (3-4× more CO2-efficient than truck) is highest impact; electric vehicles eliminate tailpipe emissions for last-mile; route optimization reduces miles driven; collaborative shipping pools multiple shippers' freight to reduce empty vehicle miles.
Question 7
What is 'extended producer responsibility' (EPR) and how does it affect supply chains?
A.Expanding the responsibilities of the procurement team
B.A policy approach that holds manufacturers responsible for the end-of-life management of their products — requiring take-back programs, recycling funding, or eco-design standards. Forces supply chains to design products for recovery and build reverse logistics capabilities for collection and recycling
C.A program extending supplier contracts
D.A responsibility to extend product warranties
EPR (WEEE in EU, state programs in US, packaging EPR globally) shifts end-of-life cost from governments/taxpayers to manufacturers: companies must fund collection and recycling of their products, which incentivizes eco-design (easier to recycle → lower EPR cost) and investment in reverse supply chain capabilities.
Question 8
What is 'social sustainability' in supply chain management?
A.Using social media for supply chain communication
B.Ensuring that supply chain operations treat all workers fairly and contribute positively to communities — including: living wages, safe working conditions, prohibition of child and forced labor, freedom of association, and supplier practices that support community development in sourcing regions
C.A program for social events for supply chain employees
D.Social responsibility programs for supply chain customers
Social sustainability addresses human impacts throughout the supply chain: the fast fashion industry's factory worker conditions, cobalt mining's child labor, and agricultural supply chain's migrant worker treatment are examples of social risks that became public scandals — requiring supply chain transparency and social audit programs.
Question 9
What is 'carbon offsetting' and when is it used in supply chain sustainability?
A.A financial accounting adjustment for carbon tax costs
B.Compensating for emissions that cannot currently be eliminated by funding equivalent carbon reduction or removal elsewhere — such as reforestation, renewable energy projects, or methane capture. Used as a bridge strategy while direct emission reduction investments take time to yield results
C.Reducing emissions by offsetting production with idle time
D.A regulatory mechanism for trading carbon emission rights
Carbon offsets address residual emissions after direct reduction: a company reduces transportation emissions 40% through mode shift and EVs, but the remaining 60% cannot yet be eliminated — high-quality offsets (Gold Standard, Verified Carbon Standard) compensate while technology and infrastructure develop. Offsets are a bridge, not a substitute for direct reduction.
Question 10
What is 'sustainable packaging' and what are the key design principles?
A.Packaging made from sustainable-sourced wood only
B.Packaging designed to minimize environmental impact throughout its lifecycle — principles: minimize material use (right-size packaging), use materials with recycled content or renewable sourcing, design for recyclability (mono-material, labeled for sorting), eliminate single-use plastics, optimize cube (fewer trucks needed), and ensure end-of-life systems exist for recovery
C.Packaging that is durable and long-lasting
D.Packaging that contains environmental marketing messages
Sustainable packaging requires systems thinking: a 'recyclable' label means nothing without collection and recycling infrastructure; compostable packaging in a landfill doesn't compost; paper packaging is only better than plastic in specific scenarios. Design for the actual end-of-life system that exists, not the ideal.
Question 11
What is the 'ASCM Supply Chain Sustainability' framework and what does it address?
A.A regulatory framework for environmental compliance
B.ASCM's approach to sustainability integrates environmental, social, and economic dimensions into supply chain strategy and operations — providing guidance on measuring and reducing environmental impact, ensuring ethical labor practices, enabling transparency through reporting, and building resilience through sustainable supply chain practices
C.A certification program for sustainable supply chains
D.An audit standard for supplier sustainability practices
ASCM's sustainability framework recognizes that supply chains must balance economic performance with environmental stewardship and social responsibility — providing practitioners with tools for sustainability measurement, supplier engagement, risk management, and reporting that integrate with overall supply chain management practice.
Question 12
What is 'traceability' in sustainable supply chains and why is it increasingly required?
A.Tracking the location of delivery vehicles
B.The ability to identify and track the origin and journey of materials, components, and products through the supply chain — enabling verification of sustainability claims (certified sustainable palm oil, conflict-free minerals, fair trade), facilitating recalls, and demonstrating compliance with regulations requiring supply chain transparency
C.Tracking the financial performance of supply chain operations
D.A system for tracing supply chain documents
Traceability is the foundation of supply chain sustainability claims: if you can't trace materials back to their origin, you cannot verify they are deforestation-free, slavery-free, or conflict-free. Regulations (EU Deforestation Regulation, forced labor import bans) and customer requirements are driving investment in end-to-end supply chain traceability systems.
