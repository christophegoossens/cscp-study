Lean Manufacturing
Lean manufacturing: A systematic approach to eliminating waste (non-value-added activities) while delivering exactly what the customer wants, when they want it, in the right quantity. Originated at Toyota (Toyota Production System).
Just-in-Time (JIT): A Lean strategy of producing and delivering exactly what is needed, when it is needed, in the quantity needed — eliminating inventory buffers. Requires reliable suppliers, stable demand, and disciplined processes.
Kanban: A visual pull system where production is triggered by actual consumption — a card (kanban) signals upstream to replenish what was consumed. Implements JIT on the shop floor: nothing is made until the downstream process requests it.
Takt time: The rate at which products must be produced to meet customer demand. Formula: Available production time ÷ Customer demand. Sets the 'heartbeat' of production — the pace every process must match.
Takt Time = Available Production Time ÷ Customer Demand Rate
Poka-yoke: Mistake-proofing — designing processes or devices so that errors are impossible or immediately obvious. Eliminates defects at the source rather than catching them downstream.
Six Sigma
Six Sigma: A data-driven methodology for reducing process variation and defects to near zero — targeting 3.4 defects per million opportunities (DPMO). Uses statistical tools and a structured problem-solving framework.
DMAIC: The Six Sigma improvement framework for existing processes: Define (the problem and customer requirements) → Measure (current performance baseline) → Analyze (root causes of variation/defects) → Improve (implement and test solutions) → Control (sustain the improvement).
DMAIC vs. PDCA: Both are iterative improvement cycles. DMAIC is more rigorous and data-intensive (used for complex problems requiring statistical analysis). PDCA is simpler and faster (used for smaller, everyday improvements).
Belt system: Six Sigma practitioners are certified at levels: White Belt (awareness) → Yellow Belt (team member) → Green Belt (project leader, part-time) → Black Belt (full-time project leader) → Master Black Belt (trainer, strategic deployment).
Six Sigma's key metric: DPMO (Defects Per Million Opportunities). Six Sigma quality = 3.4 DPMO = 99.99966% defect-free. Most organizations operate at 3–4 sigma (66,000–6,200 DPMO).
Theory of Constraints (TOC)
Theory of Constraints (TOC): A management philosophy (Goldratt) that says every system has at least one constraint (bottleneck) limiting its throughput — and the way to improve the system is to identify and manage that constraint.
TOC's 5 Focusing Steps:
  • 1. Identify the constraint — the weakest link limiting system output
  • 2. Exploit the constraint — maximize the constraint's output with current resources
  • 3. Subordinate everything else to the constraint — all other processes feed and protect the bottleneck
  • 4. Elevate the constraint — invest to increase its capacity if still limiting
  • 5. Repeat — once the constraint is resolved, the bottleneck moves; find the new one
Drum-Buffer-Rope (DBR): TOC's production scheduling method. The constraint (drum) sets the pace; a buffer of inventory protects the constraint from disruption; the rope ties release of raw materials to the constraint's rate — preventing overloading the system.
TOC insight: improving a non-constraint does not improve system throughput. Only improving the constraint improves the whole system.

TERM / CONCEPT
Lean vs. Six Sigma — key difference
ANSWER
Lean: focuses on SPEED and eliminating waste (non-value-added activities) — making the process flow faster with less waste. Six Sigma: focuses on QUALITY and reducing variation/defects — making the process more consistent and defect-free. Lean Six Sigma: combines both — eliminate waste AND reduce variation. Most mature organizations use the integrated approach.
TERM / CONCEPT
DMAIC — 5 phases of Six Sigma
ANSWER
Define: problem statement, customer requirements (VOC), project scope Measure: baseline current performance, collect data Analyze: root causes of defects/variation (fishbone, regression, etc.) Improve: develop, test, implement solutions Control: sustain improvement (control chart, SOP, audits) Used for improving EXISTING processes. (DMADV/DFSS = designing NEW processes)
TERM / CONCEPT
Takt time formula and meaning
ANSWER
Takt Time = Available Production Time ÷ Customer Demand Rate Example: 8-hour shift (480 min) ÷ 240 units needed = 2 minutes takt time Meaning: one unit must be completed every 2 minutes to meet demand. Sets the production 'heartbeat' — every process, cell, and station must operate at or faster than takt time. If any step is slower, it's a bottleneck.

TERM / CONCEPT
What is Kanban?
ANSWER
A visual PULL system: production/replenishment is triggered only by actual consumption. How it works: downstream process consumes parts → sends kanban card (or signal) upstream → upstream produces/replenishes exactly what was consumed. Benefits: limits WIP, prevents overproduction, makes inventory visible, self-regulating. Implements JIT: nothing is produced until needed.
TERM / CONCEPT
Poka-yoke — what is it and examples
ANSWER
Poka-yoke = mistake-proofing (Japanese: 'avoiding inadvertent errors') Designing processes so errors are impossible or immediately detectable. Examples: • USB-C connector (only fits one way — can't be inserted backwards) • Assembly jig that won't accept a part installed wrong • Sensor that stops machine if wrong component is loaded • Checklist that must be completed in sequence Zero defect goal: prevent errors at source, not catch them later.

Q1 of 5
In Theory of Constraints, 'subordinating everything else to the constraint' means:
TOC Step 3: subordination means all other processes exist to feed and protect the constraint — even if that means slowing down non-constraint stations (to avoid building inventory ahead of the bottleneck) or dedicating quality checks just before the constraint (to avoid feeding defects to it). The constraint determines system throughput; everything else is secondary.
Q2 of 5
A manufacturing line has available time of 450 minutes/shift and must produce 150 units. The takt time is:
Takt Time = Available Time ÷ Customer Demand = 450 min ÷ 150 units = 3 minutes per unit. This means one unit must be completed every 3 minutes to exactly meet customer demand. Any workstation taking longer than 3 minutes is a bottleneck; stations faster than 3 minutes have excess capacity.
Q3 of 5
DMAIC is the Six Sigma framework for:
DMAIC (Define-Measure-Analyze-Improve-Control) is the Six Sigma roadmap for improving EXISTING processes. It's rigorous and data-driven, requiring statistical analysis to understand root causes. For designing new processes, Six Sigma uses DMADV (Define-Measure-Analyze-Design-Verify).
Q4 of 5
Just-in-Time (JIT) manufacturing requires all of the following EXCEPT:
JIT deliberately eliminates inventory buffers — producing exactly what's needed when needed in the right quantity. Large safety stock is the OPPOSITE of JIT. JIT instead requires: reliable suppliers (parts arrive exactly when needed), stable demand (to plan production precisely), and capable processes (defects can't be absorbed by buffer inventory — they'll shut down the line immediately).
Q5 of 5
What is the key distinction between Lean and Six Sigma?
Lean targets WASTE and SPEED — streamlining processes by removing non-value-added activities, reducing lead times, and improving flow. Six Sigma targets VARIATION and DEFECTS — using statistical methods to make processes more consistent and defect-free. Lean Six Sigma combines both philosophies: faster AND more consistent processes. Both apply to manufacturing and services.
