5.3.2 · Import/Export Regulations
Exam weight: 9% (module) 6 flashcards 5 quiz questions
International Trade Documentation
Export compliance: Ensuring that goods, technology, or services leaving a country comply with all government export laws — including export licenses, embargoed countries/entities, and dual-use goods (items with both commercial and military applications).
Import compliance: Ensuring that goods entering a country meet all customs requirements — correct classification, accurate valuation, payment of applicable duties and taxes, and compliance with product safety/standards regulations.
Key international trade documents:
  • Commercial invoice: Seller's bill showing value, buyer, seller, goods description — basis for customs duties
  • Packing list: Details contents, weight, dimensions of each package
  • Certificate of Origin: Certifies where goods were manufactured — determines tariff rate under trade agreements
  • Export license: Government permission to export controlled goods
  • Letter of Credit (L/C): Bank-backed payment guarantee — seller gets paid when complying documents are presented
Harmonized System (HS) code: A standardized international 6-digit product classification code used in 200+ countries to categorize goods for tariff and statistical purposes. The first 6 digits are universal; countries add digits for national specificity.
Customs Clearance Process
Customs broker: A licensed professional who prepares and files customs entries on behalf of importers — navigating regulations, HS classification, duty calculation, and compliance requirements. Often used because customs law is complex and errors are costly.
Customs duty (tariff): A tax imposed by the importing country on goods entering its territory. Calculated as: Ad Valorem (% of declared value), Specific (per unit/weight), or Compound (combination).
Ad Valorem Duty = Declared Value × Duty Rate
Customs valuation: The process of determining the value of imported goods for duty calculation purposes. Primary method: transaction value (price actually paid or payable). Must include or exclude certain costs (freight, insurance) depending on Incoterms and customs rules.
Free Trade Zones (FTZ) and Bonded Warehouses allow goods to be stored, processed, or assembled without paying customs duties until goods enter domestic commerce — enabling duty deferral and sometimes duty reduction.
Export Controls
Export Administration Regulations (EAR): US Department of Commerce rules governing export of commercial goods, technology, and software — especially dual-use items. Requires exporters to check if a license is needed based on product type, destination country, and end-user.
ITAR (International Traffic in Arms Regulations): US State Department rules governing export of defense articles and services — includes military hardware and technology. Much stricter than EAR — any covered item requires prior approval.
Denied Parties / Sanctioned Entities screening: Before any export, companies must screen buyers and end-users against government lists (OFAC SDN list, BIS Entity List, etc.). Selling to a denied party = severe criminal and civil penalties.
Export compliance is a legal obligation — 'I didn't know' is not a valid defense. Non-compliance consequences: criminal prosecution, fines, loss of export privileges, reputational damage.

TERM / CONCEPT
HS Code — what it is and why it matters
ANSWER
Harmonized System (HS) code: 6-digit international product classification code used in 200+ countries. Why it matters: (1) Determines the tariff rate applied to imported goods — wrong classification = wrong duty (under or overpay); (2) Used for trade statistics; (3) Triggers non-tariff requirements (import permits, certifications). US adds 4 more digits (10-digit HTS code). EU adds 2 more digits. First 6 are globally standardized.
TERM / CONCEPT
Key international trade documents
ANSWER
Commercial invoice: value, buyer, seller, goods — basis for customs duties Packing list: package contents, weight, dimensions Certificate of Origin: where goods were made — determines tariff under trade agreements Export license: government permission for controlled goods Bill of Lading: carrier's receipt + contract of carriage + title document Letter of Credit: bank-backed payment guarantee All must be accurate and consistent — discrepancies cause customs delays.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a Customs Broker?
ANSWER
A licensed specialist who prepares and files customs entries on behalf of importers. Services: HS classification, duty calculation, document preparation, filing with customs authority, handling queries/inspections, advising on compliance. Why use one: customs regulations are complex, HS classification errors are common and costly (wrong duty rate, penalty risk), broker relationships with customs speed clearance.
TERM / CONCEPT
Ad valorem duty — formula and example
ANSWER
Ad Valorem = % of declared customs value Formula: Duty = Declared Value × Duty Rate Example: $10,000 shipment × 5% duty rate = $500 duty Alternatives: • Specific duty: per unit/weight (e.g., $2/kg) • Compound: combination ($1/kg + 3% value) Implication: accurate customs valuation is critical — undervaluation = underpayment + penalty risk; overvaluation = overpayment.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a Letter of Credit (L/C)?
ANSWER
A bank guarantee issued by the buyer's bank promising to pay the seller — provided the seller presents complying documents (BOL, commercial invoice, packing list, etc.) within the specified time. Why it matters: in international trade, buyers and sellers may not know each other well. L/C eliminates credit risk for the seller (bank pays even if buyer defaults) and assures the buyer that payment only happens when goods are shipped per the agreed terms. Documentary compliance is critical — even a typo on one document can cause a bank to reject the L/C presentation.
TERM / CONCEPT
EAR vs. ITAR — key difference
ANSWER
EAR (Export Administration Regulations — Commerce Dept): covers commercial and dual-use goods (technology, software, some chemicals). License requirement depends on product, destination, and end-user. ITAR (International Traffic in Arms Regulations — State Dept): covers defense articles and services. MUCH stricter. Any ITAR item = prior government approval required for export. Even sharing technical data with a foreign national in the US can be an export. Consequence of non-compliance: criminal prosecution, large fines, denial of export privileges.

Q1 of 5
The Harmonized System (HS) code is important in international trade because:
HS codes = universal product classification (6 digits, used in 200+ countries). Customs authorities use HS codes to: (1) determine the applicable duty rate, (2) identify non-tariff requirements (import permits, certifications, inspections), and (3) compile trade statistics. Misclassification = wrong duty rate (underpayment creates penalty risk; overpayment hurts profitability) and potentially missing required permits.
Q2 of 5
A Certificate of Origin is used in international trade to:
Certificate of Origin (COO) certifies the country where goods were produced or substantially transformed. This matters because: (1) Trade agreements (USMCA, EU FTAs) give preferential (lower or zero) duty rates to qualifying goods from specific countries; (2) Some countries have higher tariffs for goods from specific origins (anti-dumping duties, trade sanctions). The COO determines which tariff rate applies.
Q3 of 5
Before exporting goods, a company must screen the buyer against government denied parties lists because:
Denied party/sanctioned entity screening is a legal requirement before ANY export. Key lists: OFAC's SDN (Specially Designated Nationals), BIS Entity List, State Dept Debarred List. Ignorance is NOT a defense — the legal standard is 'knew or should have known.' Penalties: criminal prosecution of individuals, multi-million dollar fines on companies, permanent loss of export privileges. This applies regardless of shipment value.
Q4 of 5
A Free Trade Zone (FTZ) benefits importers by:
FTZ = designated area where goods can be brought in without paying import duties until they leave the zone and enter domestic commerce. Benefits: (1) Defer duty payment until goods are actually sold (cash flow); (2) Manufacture or process goods in the zone — may pay duty on finished goods rate (sometimes lower than components); (3) Re-export goods from FTZ without paying any duty. Common use: import components, assemble in FTZ, pay duty only on finished goods (or re-export duty-free).
Q5 of 5
ITAR (International Traffic in Arms Regulations) differs from EAR primarily in that:
EAR (Commerce Dept): commercial and dual-use goods — some require licenses depending on destination/end-use, many don't. ITAR (State Dept): defense articles and services — EVERYTHING requires prior government approval. The key ITAR trap: 'deemed export' — showing ITAR-controlled technical data to a foreign national, even in the US, is treated as an export and requires authorization. ITAR violations are treated as national security matters — penalties are severe.
