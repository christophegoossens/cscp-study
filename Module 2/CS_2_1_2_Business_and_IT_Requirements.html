Efficiency vs. Responsiveness

Organizations must balance efficiency (least cost) with responsiveness (speed/flexibility to customer needs) while also building resilience.

  • Efficiency-focused SC: stable demand, low uncertainty, long product life cycle, limited variety, infrequent product introductions. Strategy: proactively manage customer demand; minimize cost.
  • Responsive SC: volatile demand, high uncertainty, short product life cycle, many SKUs, rapid change. Strategy: real-time data systems, multiple warehouses, redundant capacity, agile suppliers.
  • Being 100% efficiency-only or responsiveness-only has been fatal for companies — most successful chains balance both.
Service Level: a measure (usually expressed as a percentage) of satisfying demand through inventory or by the current production schedule in time to satisfy the customer’s requested delivery dates and quantities.

Resilience & SC IT Requirements
Information Technology (IT): the technology of computers, telecommunications, and other devices that integrate data, equipment, personnel, and problem-solving methods in planning and controlling business activities.
Information System (IS): interrelated computer hardware and software along with people and processes designed for the collection, processing, and communication of data.
  • Resilience: ability to recover from supply chain disruptions. Requires redundancy, diversification, and contingency planning regardless of efficiency/responsiveness focus.
  • IT roles in SC management: increase velocity/agility/scalability; provide cost-effective global visibility; avoid bullwhip effect; replace inventory with information flows; enable collaborative planning; drive data accuracy and straight-through processing.

TERM / CONCEPT
What is supply chain resilience?
ANSWER
The ability of a supply chain to absorb, adapt to, and recover from disruptions. Built through redundant capacity, geographic diversification, and contingency planning — regardless of efficiency/responsiveness focus.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is purely efficiency-focused dangerous?
ANSWER
It eliminates redundancy and flexibility. Any disruption (supplier failure, demand spike) can halt the chain. Resilience requires some redundancy that conflicts with pure efficiency.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does IT help avoid the bullwhip effect?
ANSWER
By gathering, integrating, and reporting logistical data so that all partners can see real demand — not distorted orders — reducing overreaction and inventory oscillation up the chain.
TERM / CONCEPT
Gap Inc. example: tailored supply chains
ANSWER
Old Navy (cost-conscious) → low-cost manufacturing in Asia. Gap (trend buyers) → Central America. Banana Republic (quality/premium) → Italy/Europe. Three tailored chains with different efficiency/responsiveness profiles.
TERM / CONCEPT
How do you determine the right efficiency/responsiveness balance?
ANSWER
Match the supply chain type to the product characteristics — high-volume, stable-demand products = efficiency; low-volume, unpredictable, short-lifecycle products = responsiveness. Exhibit 2-2 maps this.
TERM / CONCEPT
What variable differentiates efficient vs. responsive SC?
ANSWER
Inventory volume and demand uncertainty (volatility). High-volume, low-uncertainty → efficient. Low-volume, high-uncertainty → responsive.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are 5 IT benefits to SC management?
ANSWER
1. Increase velocity/agility/scalability. 2. Global data visibility at low cost. 3. Avoid bullwhip effect. 4. Replace inventory with information flows (lean). 5. Enable collaborative planning across partners.
TERM / CONCEPT
What does IT do to drive straight-through processing?
ANSWER
Data entered once is stored centrally and used across multiple modules/partners without re-keying. Eliminates errors from duplicate entry and speeds up transaction processing.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is the IS definition's focus on people/processes important?
ANSWER
Technology alone doesn’t deliver value — the combination of hardware, software, people, and processes determines whether an information system achieves its intended benefits.
TERM / CONCEPT
What makes a supply chain agile?
ANSWER
Ability to rapidly scale up or down production, switch suppliers, reroute shipments, or change product mix in response to demand or supply changes. Requires flexible capacity and real-time information.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is scalability in IT context?
ANSWER
The ability to increase or decrease IT capacity (processing power, storage, users) as needed without major redesign. Cloud-based systems typically offer high scalability.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the key question when balancing efficiency and responsiveness?
ANSWER
Does the cost of added responsiveness (redundancy, faster logistics, closer warehouses) justify the competitive benefit? This is assessed product-by-product and segment-by-segment.
TERM / CONCEPT
What SC strategy fits make-to-stock products?
ANSWER
Efficiency-focused SC. MTS products have stable, predictable demand and high volume — characteristics that reward cost minimization over flexibility.
TERM / CONCEPT
What SC strategy fits high-variety, short-lifecycle products?
ANSWER
Responsive SC. High uncertainty, frequent product changes, and customization needs require flexibility — even at higher cost.
TERM / CONCEPT
What does IT replace in a lean supply chain?
ANSWER
Inventory. Real-time information on demand and inventory levels allows partners to reduce buffer stocks and react faster — “replace inventory with information” is a core lean principle.

Question 2
What does 'scalability' mean for a supply chain IT system?
A.The ability to reduce software license costs over time
B.The system's ability to grow with the business — handling more users, data volumes, and transactions without requiring replacement
C.The number of languages the system supports
D.How quickly the system can be deployed
Scalability means the system can handle growth — more SKUs, more users, more transactions, more countries — without performance degradation or the need for a full system replacement.
Question 3
What is an RFP in the context of IT system selection?
A.A 'Request for Payment' submitted to suppliers
B.A 'Request for Proposal' — a document sent to vendors describing business requirements and requesting them to propose a solution and pricing
C.A 'Requirement for Processing' document used in MRP
D.A 'Report of Performance' measuring IT system uptime
An RFP defines business requirements and asks vendors to propose how they would meet them, their implementation approach, timeline, and pricing — enabling structured comparison of vendor solutions.
Question 4
Which of the following is a key 'non-functional' IT requirement?
A.The ability to process purchase orders
B.Integration with existing ERP systems
C.User interface design preferences
D.The ability to generate demand forecasts
Non-functional requirements describe HOW a system works (performance, security, scalability, availability, integration) rather than WHAT it does (functional requirements like processing orders or generating forecasts).
Question 5
What is the 'total cost of ownership' (TCO) of an IT system?
A.The purchase price of the software license
B.All costs over the system's lifetime: license/subscription, implementation, integration, training, maintenance, support, and eventual decommissioning
C.Only the annual maintenance fee
D.The cost of hardware required to run the system
TCO encompasses all costs from acquisition through decommission: upfront license, implementation services, integration, data migration, training, annual maintenance/support, and the eventual cost of replacement.
Question 6
What is the difference between 'best-of-breed' and 'integrated suite' IT strategies?
A.Best-of-breed uses one vendor for everything; integrated suite uses multiple specialized vendors
B.Best-of-breed selects the best specialist system for each function (e.g., best WMS, best TMS); integrated suite uses a single vendor's platform across all functions — trading some specialization for integration simplicity
C.There is no practical difference — both provide the same functionality
D.Best-of-breed is only used by small companies; integrated suites are only for large enterprises
Best-of-breed maximizes functional depth by choosing the best system in each category but creates integration complexity. Integrated suites simplify integration but may not match specialized vendors in specific functional areas.
Question 7
What does 'SaaS' mean for supply chain software?
A.Software as a Service — software accessed via subscription over the internet, hosted by the vendor; no on-premise hardware required
B.Software as a Standard — vendor-managed customization of software to meet all requirements
C.Software and a Service — combines software with implementation consulting
D.System as a Solution — a fully custom-built supply chain system
SaaS (Software as a Service) means the software is hosted and maintained by the vendor and accessed via subscription, typically through a web browser. It eliminates the need for on-premise servers and reduces IT maintenance burden.
Question 8
Why is 'integration' a critical consideration in supply chain IT system selection?
A.Integration is rarely needed since most supply chain systems are standalone
B.Supply chain systems must exchange data with ERP, WMS, TMS, supplier systems, and customer systems — poor integration creates data silos and manual workarounds
C.Integration is only needed for international systems
D.Integration only matters if the company has more than 1,000 employees
Supply chain systems don't operate in isolation — they must exchange data with internal systems (ERP, WMS, TMS) and external partners. Poor integration creates silos, duplicate data entry, and errors.
Question 9
What is 'data governance' and why does it matter for supply chain systems?
A.Governing employee access to company buildings
B.Policies, processes, and accountability for the accuracy, completeness, and consistency of data used across systems — poor data quality undermines all supply chain decisions
C.Managing government regulatory compliance
D.Controlling the number of system users
Poor data quality (wrong lead times, incorrect BOMs, stale customer data) undermines every supply chain decision. Data governance establishes ownership, standards, and processes to maintain data quality.
Question 10
What is a 'phased implementation' approach to supply chain IT deployment?
A.Implementing all modules of the system simultaneously across all sites
B.Rolling out the system in stages — by module, geography, or business unit — reducing risk and allowing learning from early phases to improve later phases
C.Implementing the system only at pilot sites permanently
D.Using different vendors for each phase of implementation
Phased implementation reduces risk by deploying in stages. Early phases provide real-world learning that can be applied to improve subsequent phases — better than a 'big bang' approach for complex supply chain systems.
Question 11
Which business requirement is typically MOST critical for an ERP system in manufacturing?
A.Social media integration
B.Mobile app availability for field sales
C.Integrated MRP/MPS, inventory management, and financial accounting — providing a single source of truth for production planning and financial reporting
D.Advanced analytics and machine learning capabilities
For manufacturing, the core ERP must provide integrated MRP/MPS (materials and production planning), inventory management, and financial accounting in a single system — the foundation of manufacturing operations.
Question 12
What does 'go-live readiness' assessment include before an IT system launch?
A.Signing the software contract
B.Testing data migration, user acceptance testing (UAT), user training completion, integration testing, cutover planning, and rollback plan preparation
C.Writing the system requirements document
D.Selecting the IT project manager
Go-live readiness ensures the system works correctly (UAT), data has been migrated and validated, users are trained, integration points are tested, and a rollback plan exists in case of critical failures.
