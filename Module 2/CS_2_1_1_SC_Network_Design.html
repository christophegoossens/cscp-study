Supply Chain Design: Translating Strategy
Supply Chain Design: the determination of how to structure a supply chain. Design decisions include the selection of partners, the location and capacity of warehouses and production facilities, and the products, services, and flows of information and materials.
  • Design flows from organizational strategy — but not every supply chain decision can be purely strategy-driven; customer needs and partner capabilities also shape design.
  • Customer/market decisions: configure the chain to reflect customer needs and trading partner capabilities.
  • Technology decisions: how frequently to transfer/analyze data, how to cleanse it, leveraging internet/e-commerce, integrating IT into competitive strategy.
  • Process decisions: fine-tune processes per customer segment; manage 4 basic flows — information, primary product, primary funds, and returns.
Sourcing & Network Configuration
Sourcing: the process of identifying a company that provides a needed good or service.
  • Trend: outsourcing and contract manufacturing — initially in manufacturing, now in services too.
  • Network configuration decisions: location of plants (and production capacity), number/location/capacity/capabilities of warehouses, transportation between all facilities.
  • More warehouses = shorter lead time + lower transportation cost per delivery, but higher fixed cost. Optimization finds the right balance.
  • Global expansion requires due diligence on infrastructure (ports, airports, roads), political stability, regulations, and local capabilities.

TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 4 basic flows in a supply chain?
ANSWER
1. Flow of information. 2. Primary product flow. 3. Primary funds flow. 4. Returns (reverse flow). Supply chain design fine-tunes these flows for each customer segment.
TERM / CONCEPT
What decisions are included in network configuration?
ANSWER
Location of plants and production capacities, number/location/capacity/capabilities of warehouses, and transportation links between all facilities.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the trade-off when adding warehouses?
ANSWER
More warehouses = shorter lead time and lower delivery cost per unit (closer to customers), but higher fixed costs. Network optimization finds the point where total cost is minimized.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why does global expansion require due diligence?
ANSWER
Infrastructure quality (ports, roads, airports), political stability, regulations, labor availability, and local partner capabilities all affect whether the global expansion will deliver the expected benefits.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does customer/market needs shape SC design?
ANSWER
The supply chain should be configured to reflect customers' needs and trading partners' capabilities — not just organizational strategy. Customer requirements on speed, flexibility, and service level drive design choices.
TERM / CONCEPT
What technology decisions are part of SC design?
ANSWER
Data transfer frequency, data cleansing and analysis approach, how to leverage internet/e-commerce, IT infrastructure setup with partners, and integrating decision support systems into competitive strategy.
TERM / CONCEPT
What does SC design flow from?
ANSWER
Organizational strategy (business plan) — but tempered by customer/market needs, partner capabilities, technology constraints, and cost realities.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is outsourcing in SC design context?
ANSWER
Contracting a non-core activity to an outside company. Originally focused on manufacturing; now includes services enabled by IT. Allows the firm to focus on core competencies.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are the 4 flows in supply chains?
ANSWER
Information flow, primary product flow (forward), primary funds flow (payment), and returns flow (reverse logistics). Effective SC design optimizes all four.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why is transportation cost a variable to optimize in SC network design?
ANSWER
Transportation cost depends on total distance between production and consumption, shipment frequency, and mode. Warehouse placement directly affects transportation cost — more warehouses reduce distance but increase fixed cost.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the primary goal of network configuration?
ANSWER
To meet customer service requirements (fill rates, lead times) at minimum total cost — balancing plant capacity, warehouse costs, inventory carrying costs, and transportation costs.
TERM / CONCEPT
What are process decisions in SC design?
ANSWER
Deciding how supply chain processes (order management, fulfilment, replenishment, etc.) are structured and optimized for each customer segment. Processes can differ by product line or channel.
TERM / CONCEPT
How does e-commerce affect SC design?
ANSWER
It creates direct-to-consumer channels that bypass traditional distribution, requires faster fulfilment, and changes warehouse location strategy (closer to consumers rather than to retail DCs).
TERM / CONCEPT
What risk does global expansion introduce?
ANSWER
Political instability, currency risk, regulatory complexity, infrastructure gaps (poor roads, unreliable ports), language barriers, and longer lead times that reduce responsiveness.
TERM / CONCEPT
What does SC design determine about partners?
ANSWER
Which partners to include (make-or-buy decisions), their roles, locations, and capabilities. Partner selection affects cost, responsiveness, quality, and risk.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the relationship between SC design and competitive strategy?
ANSWER
SC design should enable the competitive strategy — if competing on cost, design for efficiency; if competing on service/speed, design for responsiveness. Misalignment between strategy and design leads to poor performance.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is the role of IT in SC design?
ANSWER
IT enables visibility across the chain, supports data flow between partners, enables e-commerce and direct channels, and integrates decision support systems. IT decisions are a core part of SC design.
TERM / CONCEPT
Why can't all SC decisions be purely strategy-driven?
ANSWER
Because customers' needs, trading partners' existing capabilities, technology constraints, and practical cost considerations must also be taken into account when structuring the chain.

Question 1
Which factors are typically considered in a supply chain network design study?
A.Employee satisfaction and office locations
B.Facility locations, number of tiers, transportation costs, inventory levels, customer service requirements, and tax implications
C.Only the cost of transportation between facilities
D.Only the number of distribution centers needed
Network design optimizes the total supply chain cost (facility, transportation, inventory) while meeting service level requirements, considering tax structures, duties, and demand geography.
Question 2
What is the primary trade-off in deciding the number of distribution centers?
A.More DCs always reduce total cost
B.More DCs improve delivery speed and reduce transportation costs but increase facility costs and inventory requirements — the optimal number minimizes total cost
C.Fewer DCs always provide better customer service
D.The number of DCs has no impact on inventory levels
Adding DCs reduces outbound transportation but increases facility and inventory costs (more locations = more safety stock needed due to risk pooling loss). Network design finds the cost-minimizing number.
Question 3
What is 'total landed cost' in network design?
A.The price paid to the supplier for goods
B.All costs associated with getting a product to its final destination: purchase price plus transportation, duties, insurance, handling, and inventory carrying costs
C.Only the freight cost for a shipment
D.The manufacturing cost plus profit margin
Total landed cost is the comprehensive cost of acquiring and delivering goods — including purchase price, freight, customs duties, insurance, port fees, and in-transit inventory carrying costs.
Question 4
Why does inventory 'safety stock' increase when the number of distribution centers increases?
A.Safety stock is the same regardless of the number of locations
B.When inventory is split across more locations, the risk-pooling benefit is lost — each location must hold safety stock independently, increasing total system safety stock
C.More DCs allow safety stock to be eliminated
D.Safety stock only exists at manufacturing facilities, not DCs
Risk pooling: when inventory is centralized, demand variability aggregates and total safety stock is lower. Splitting inventory across many DCs means each location holds safety stock independently — increasing total system inventory.
Question 5
What is the purpose of a 'make-vs-buy' analysis in network design?
A.Deciding whether to manufacture products in-house or outsource them to suppliers
B.Choosing between two raw material suppliers
C.Determining the optimal safety stock level
D.Selecting the best transportation carrier
A make-vs-buy analysis evaluates whether it is more cost-effective and strategically appropriate to produce a product or perform a service in-house vs. outsourcing to a supplier or 3PL.
Question 6
Which analysis is used to identify the optimal facility location given customer locations, demand, and transportation costs?
A.ABC analysis
B.Center-of-gravity analysis — locating the facility at the weighted center of demand to minimize total transportation cost
C.SWOT analysis
D.Critical path analysis
Center-of-gravity (centroid) analysis calculates the weighted geographic center of customer demand, providing a starting-point location that minimizes total transportation cost.
Question 7
What is a 'greenfield analysis' in supply chain network design?
A.An environmental impact study for a new factory
B.Starting network design from scratch — ignoring existing assets — to determine the theoretically optimal network, then comparing it to the current state
C.A study of sustainable supply chain practices
D.An analysis of agricultural supply chains only
A greenfield analysis designs the ideal network without being constrained by existing assets. It reveals the gap between the optimal and current state, helping prioritize network restructuring investments.
Question 8
Which supply chain network design approach uses mathematical optimization to find the minimum cost solution?
A.Center-of-gravity heuristic only
B.Linear programming / operations research optimization models that minimize total cost (facility + transportation + inventory) subject to service level constraints
C.Simple spreadsheet comparison of two alternatives
D.Management judgment and experience alone
Operations research optimization (linear programming, mixed-integer programming) finds the mathematically optimal solution across all facility, transportation, and inventory cost trade-offs — not possible with heuristics alone.
Question 9
'Nearshoring' as a network design strategy refers to:
A.Locating all distribution centers near the ocean for port access
B.Moving production or sourcing from distant low-cost countries to nearby countries, trading some cost savings for shorter lead times and greater supply chain resilience
C.Opening retail stores near customer homes
D.Partnering with nearby companies to share warehouse space
Nearshoring (e.g., shifting manufacturing from Asia to Mexico for U.S. companies) sacrifices some labor cost savings in exchange for shorter lead times, lower transit inventory, and reduced geopolitical risk.
Question 10
How does demand uncertainty affect supply chain network design?
A.Demand uncertainty has no impact on network design decisions
B.Higher demand uncertainty increases the need for safety stock and may favor a more flexible, responsive network (more locations, smaller DCs) over a lean, centralized network
C.Higher demand uncertainty always leads to fewer distribution centers
D.Demand uncertainty only affects forecasting, not network design
Higher uncertainty requires more safety stock and faster response capability. Network designers must balance the cost of flexibility (more DCs, responsive suppliers) against the cost of uncertainty (safety stock, lost sales, expediting).
Question 11
What role does IT play in modern supply chain network design?
A.IT has no role — network design is a purely strategic activity
B.IT systems provide the data (demand, costs, transportation rates, lead times) needed for optimization models and enable the real-time visibility required to execute the designed network
C.IT systems automatically design the network without human input
D.IT only handles the execution of orders, not strategic design
Network design models require large amounts of data (demand by location, transportation costs, facility costs, lead times), and executing the designed network requires IT systems for visibility and coordination.
Question 12
What is a 'hub-and-spoke' distribution network?
A.A network where all shipments go directly from manufacturer to customer
B.A centralized network where goods flow from multiple origin points to a central hub (cross-dock or DC), then out to multiple destinations — improving consolidation and reducing total transportation cost
C.A network with equal-sized distribution centers in every city
D.A transportation network using only air freight
Hub-and-spoke consolidates inbound flows at a central hub, enabling full truckload consolidation, then distributes to destinations. It reduces per-unit transportation costs but adds handling at the hub.
