What is Master Data?
Master data: Core, relatively static data that defines the key business entities in a supply chain — customers, suppliers, products, locations, and units of measure. Unlike transactional data (which changes with every order), master data changes infrequently.

Types of data in supply chain systems:

  • Master data: Static reference data (customer master, product master, supplier master, location master)
  • Transactional data: Dynamic event data (orders, shipments, invoices, receipts)
  • Unstructured data: Documents, emails, images — not in a structured database format
  • Metadata: Data about data — describes the attributes of other data
  • Hierarchical data: Data organized in parent-child relationships (e.g., product family → subfamily → SKU)
Master data is the foundation of every supply chain transaction — if master data is wrong (wrong unit of measure, wrong price, wrong supplier address), every transaction using it will also be wrong.
Master Data Management (MDM)
Master Data Management (MDM): A discipline that coordinates the creating, updating, and archiving of master data — ensuring it is accurate, consistent, and available across all systems and business functions.

The master data life cycle:

  • Plan: Determine what master data is needed, how it will be created, and who owns it
  • Create: Enter or load master data into systems — with validation and approval workflows
  • Use: Master data is used to support transactions, analysis, and reporting
  • Maintain: Ongoing cleansing, correction, deduplication, and security
  • Archive/Delete: Data no longer needed is archived (for compliance/history) or purged (to keep systems clean)
The expected USE of data should drive what data is collected — not the other way around. Over-collecting unused data creates maintenance burden and compliance risk.
Key Types of Master Data for Supply Chain
Critical master data files in supply chain:
  • Customer master: Customer name, address, payment terms, credit limit, shipping instructions, contact
  • Supplier/vendor master: Supplier name, address, payment terms, lead time, certifications, contacts
  • Item/product master: Product description, unit of measure, weight, dimensions, price, lead time, classification
  • Location master: Warehouse addresses, storage zone assignments, dock doors
  • Bill of Materials (BOM): Product structure — components and quantities needed to make each finished product
Item master (ASCM): A computerized file that contains descriptive and inventory data for each item — the foundation for ordering, planning, and reporting.

Poor master data quality is a leading cause of supply chain failures: incorrect BOMs cause production errors; wrong lead times cause missed deliveries; duplicate supplier records cause payment errors.

Q1 of 5
A purchase order is sent to a supplier with the wrong unit of measure (cases instead of pallets). The most likely root cause is:
Unit of measure is a master data field in the item master. If the item master has the wrong UOM, every PO generated for that item will use the wrong UOM — a systemic error affecting all transactions until the master data is corrected.
Q2 of 5
Master Data Management (MDM) is responsible for:
MDM governs master data throughout its life cycle — ensuring it is accurate, consistent, and available across all systems. It is a data governance discipline, not a transactional or infrastructure function.
Q3 of 5
Which of the following is an example of MASTER data (not transactional data)?
Product weight, dimensions, and UOM in the item master are static reference data — they describe the item and rarely change. POs, receipts, and invoices are transactional data — they capture events that change constantly.
Q4 of 5
The master data life cycle stage where duplicate records are identified and merged, and incorrect data is corrected, is:
Maintain = ongoing data stewardship activities: cleansing (removing errors), deduplication (merging duplicate records), validation (checking data quality), and security. Without maintenance, master data quality degrades over time.
Q5 of 5
A Bill of Materials (BOM) is a type of master data that specifies:
A BOM is the product structure master — it lists every component and the quantity needed to make one unit of a finished product. Incorrect BOMs directly cause production errors and material shortages.
