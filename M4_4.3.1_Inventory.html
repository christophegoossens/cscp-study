Inventory Types and Functions
Inventory: Items waiting for future use — raw materials, WIP, finished goods, and MRO supplies.
4 types of inventory by function:
  • Cycle stock: Inventory that results from replenishing in batches (order quantities) — the average inventory between replenishments
  • Safety stock: Buffer inventory held to protect against demand variability and supply uncertainty
  • Anticipation inventory: Built up in advance of a known future spike — seasonal demand, planned shutdown, promotion
  • Pipeline (in-transit) inventory: Goods moving through the supply chain — in transit from supplier or between facilities
Work in process (WIP): Inventory that has entered the production process but is not yet finished goods.
Inventory has value (it can be sold) but also has cost. The goal is the MINIMUM inventory needed to meet service level requirements — not zero inventory.
Inventory Costs
4 categories of inventory cost:
  • Ordering/setup cost: Cost to place an order or set up a production run — clerical cost, tooling changeover, transportation of first shipment. Fixed per order.
  • Carrying/holding cost: Cost of holding inventory — capital cost (opportunity cost), storage, insurance, obsolescence, damage. Typically 20–30% of inventory value per year.
  • Shortage/stockout cost: Cost when inventory runs out — lost sales, emergency procurement, expediting, customer service damage.
  • Unit cost (purchase or production cost): The variable cost per item.
Annual Carrying Cost = Average Inventory × Carrying Cost Rate Example: €100,000 avg inventory × 25% rate = €25,000/year
There is a fundamental tradeoff between ordering cost and carrying cost — larger orders reduce ordering cost but increase carrying cost. EOQ finds the minimum total cost.
EOQ (Economic Order Quantity): The order quantity that minimizes total annual inventory cost (ordering + carrying).
EOQ = √(2 × Annual Demand × Ordering Cost / Carrying Cost per Unit) At EOQ: Annual ordering cost = Annual carrying cost

TERM / CONCEPT
What is cycle stock?
ANSWER
Inventory that results from replenishing in batches. Average cycle stock = Order Quantity / 2. Larger order quantities → more cycle stock → higher carrying cost. Smaller orders → less cycle stock → more ordering cost.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is safety stock and what determines its size?
ANSWER
Buffer inventory protecting against demand variability and supply uncertainty (lead time variation). Sized using: SS = Z × σ × √LT Z = service level factor, σ = demand standard deviation, LT = lead time. Higher service level → more safety stock.
TERM / CONCEPT
Annual carrying cost formula
ANSWER
Annual Carrying Cost = Average Inventory Value × Carrying Cost Rate Carrying cost rate typically 20–30%/year (includes capital opportunity cost, storage, insurance, obsolescence, damage).

Q1 of 5
A retailer builds up extra inventory of sunscreen in March before the summer season peak. This is an example of:
Anticipation inventory is built in advance of a KNOWN future spike — seasonal demand (summer), a planned promotion, or a supplier shutdown. The retailer knows summer demand is coming and pre-builds stock to meet it.
Q2 of 5
Which inventory cost increases as order quantity increases?
Carrying cost increases with order quantity because larger batches mean more average inventory (Avg Inventory = Order Qty / 2). Ordering cost DECREASES with larger quantities (fewer orders per year). EOQ balances these two opposing costs.
Q3 of 5
The Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) is the point where:
At EOQ, the total cost curve is at its minimum — which occurs exactly where annual ordering cost = annual carrying cost. This is the mathematical optimum. Any smaller or larger order quantity increases total cost.
Q4 of 5
A company has average inventory of €500,000 and a carrying cost rate of 24% per year. Annual carrying cost is:
Annual Carrying Cost = Average Inventory × Rate = €500,000 × 24% = €120,000 per year. This represents the cost of capital tied up, plus storage, insurance, obsolescence, and damage.
Q5 of 5
Pipeline inventory is best described as:
Pipeline (in-transit) inventory = goods physically moving through the supply chain — in transit from supplier, between DCs, or to customer. This inventory is 'owned' but not yet available for use or sale.
