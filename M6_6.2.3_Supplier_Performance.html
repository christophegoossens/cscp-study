Supplier Performance Measurement
Supplier performance management (SPM): A systematic process for measuring, monitoring, and developing supplier performance — using defined metrics, regular reviews, and structured feedback to hold suppliers accountable and drive improvement.
The purpose of SPM is not punishment — it is to identify gaps, drive improvement, and develop the supply base to support business objectives. Measurement without development is punitive; development without measurement is unfocused.
Supplier scorecard: A structured performance evaluation tool measuring suppliers across multiple dimensions — quality, delivery, cost, service, and innovation. Provides an objective, consistent basis for performance conversations, ranking, and business allocation decisions.
Key Supplier Performance Metrics
Common supplier performance dimensions (QCDS framework):
  • Quality: Defect rate (PPM — parts per million), rejection rate, corrective action response time
  • Cost: Price competitiveness, cost reduction trend, total cost of ownership
  • Delivery: On-time delivery %, lead time accuracy, flexibility/responsiveness
  • Service: Communication responsiveness, corrective action quality, innovation proposals
PPM (Parts Per Million defects): The number of defective parts per one million units received. Used for high-volume manufacturing supply. Lower PPM = better quality. Six Sigma quality = 3.4 PPM.
Corrective Action Request (CAR): A formal request to a supplier to investigate a quality problem, identify root cause, and implement corrective actions to prevent recurrence. The supplier's quality management system and CAR response quality are themselves performance metrics.
Supplier Review Process
Business review (QBR — Quarterly Business Review): A structured meeting between buyer and supplier leadership to review performance against scorecard metrics, discuss market trends, plan improvement initiatives, and align on strategic priorities.
QBR agenda typically includes: performance scorecard review (actuals vs. targets), root cause discussion for misses, improvement action plan status, business outlook (volume forecast, upcoming changes), and strategic discussion (new opportunities, technology roadmap).
Supplier tiering: Classifying suppliers by their strategic importance and performance — determining how much management attention, development investment, and business allocation each receives. Top-tier suppliers receive preferred business; poor performers face risk of reduced business or disqualification.
Supplier rationalization: periodically reducing the total number of suppliers to focus volume and relationship investment on fewer, better-performing suppliers — increasing leverage and reducing complexity. Counter-balanced by risk of over-concentration.

QCDS supplier performance framework
ANSWER
Quality: defect rate (PPM), rejection rate, corrective action response time Cost: price vs. market, cost reduction trend, TCO Delivery: on-time delivery %, lead time consistency, flexibility/responsiveness Service: responsiveness, innovation, corrective action quality Scorecard weights each dimension based on category importance. Combined score drives: preferred/approved/probation/disqualified status and business allocation.
TERM / CONCEPT
What is a Corrective Action Request (CAR)?
ANSWER
Formal request to supplier after a quality failure: 1. Contain the problem (stop further defective product from reaching production) 2. Investigate root cause (5 Whys, fishbone, data analysis) 3. Implement corrective action (permanent fix) 4. Verify effectiveness (confirm problem doesn't recur) 5. Document and submit to buyer CAR response quality and speed are themselves performance metrics — a supplier who responds slowly or superficially gets a poor service score.
TERM / CONCEPT
Supplier scorecard — purpose and content
ANSWER
Purpose: objective, consistent measurement of supplier performance across multiple dimensions — basis for performance discussions, ranking, business allocation. Typical content: • Quality score (PPM, rejection rate) • Delivery score (OTD%, lead time accuracy) • Cost score (price competitiveness, savings) • Service score (responsiveness, CAR quality) • Overall weighted score • Trend vs. prior period • Ranking vs. peer suppliers Output drives: preferred vs. probation status, next business award decision.
TERM / CONCEPT
Quarterly Business Review (QBR) — what it is and why it matters
ANSWER
A structured review meeting between buyer and supplier leadership (both sides, including executives for strategic suppliers). Typical agenda: 1. Scorecard performance review (actuals vs. targets) 2. Root cause discussion for misses 3. Improvement action plan status 4. Volume forecast and business outlook 5. Strategic discussion (technology, innovation, market trends) Value: converts metrics from a report into a conversation → alignment on priorities, relationship investment, and mutual accountability.
TERM / CONCEPT
PPM — what it means and why it's used
ANSWER
PPM = Parts Per Million defects Formula: (Defective units ÷ Total units received) × 1,000,000 Example: 5 defects in 100,000 units = 50 PPM Why PPM (not %): for high-volume manufacturing, % is too coarse. A 0.01% defect rate sounds tiny — but means 100 defective parts per million. With automotive or electronics assembly running millions of parts, even 100 PPM causes significant production disruption. Six Sigma target: 3.4 PPM.
TERM / CONCEPT
Supplier rationalization — benefits and risks
ANSWER
Benefits of rationalization (fewer suppliers): • Higher volume per supplier → better pricing leverage • Deeper relationships → better performance • Lower management cost (fewer QBRs, scorecards, contracts) • Preferred customer status with remaining suppliers Risks: • Less supplier diversity → supply concentration risk • Single/sole source risk • Less competitive pressure on remaining suppliers Balance: rationalize to an optimal number — enough competition for accountability, few enough for deep relationships.

Q1 of 5
A supplier with 150 PPM defect rate is receiving:
PPM = Parts Per Million. 150 PPM = 150 defective parts per one million units received. This sounds small (0.015%), but for a high-volume manufacturer receiving millions of parts, it represents thousands of defects annually. Six Sigma quality = 3.4 PPM. Most industries target 50–500 PPM depending on criticality. Automotive typically requires <10 PPM for critical components.
Q2 of 5
The primary purpose of a supplier performance scorecard is:
Supplier scorecard: structured, objective measurement across Quality, Cost, Delivery, and Service. Purpose: (1) Create shared understanding of performance gaps; (2) Provide objective basis for performance conversations (rather than subjective impressions); (3) Drive improvement by making gaps visible; (4) Inform business allocation (preferred suppliers get more business); (5) Identify suppliers at risk of disqualification. Scorecard without development conversation is just data — the conversation is where value is created.
Q3 of 5
A Quarterly Business Review (QBR) between a buyer and strategic supplier serves to:
QBR = structured meeting that converts scorecard data into action. Executives from both companies attend — signaling strategic importance. Agenda: review actuals vs. targets (accountability), root cause and corrective action discussion (problem-solving), business outlook (alignment), and strategic discussion (innovation, market trends). The QBR relationship investment is what separates a strategic partnership from a transactional buy-sell — it builds trust, alignment, and mutual commitment.
Q4 of 5
Supplier rationalization is beneficial primarily because:
Rationalization benefits: more volume per remaining supplier → better pricing and preferred customer status; deeper relationships → better performance and access to supplier innovation; lower management cost (fewer scorecards, QBRs, contracts, quality audits). Risk: concentration. Balance: rationalize to the minimum number that still provides competition, resilience, and market coverage — not to zero or one.
Q5 of 5
A Corrective Action Request (CAR) is issued to a supplier when:
CAR = formal quality response mechanism. When a quality problem is discovered (incoming inspection failure, production defect, customer complaint traced to supplier component), the buyer issues a CAR requiring: (1) immediate containment (stop defective product from moving forward), (2) root cause investigation (8D, 5 Whys), (3) permanent corrective action implementation, (4) verification of effectiveness. The speed and quality of the supplier's CAR response is itself a service performance metric — it reveals the maturity of their quality management system.
